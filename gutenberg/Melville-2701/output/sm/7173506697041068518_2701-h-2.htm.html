O LE MATAUPU 4. O le Counterpane.

Ina ua ou ala a'e i le taeao na sosoo ai i le malamalama o le ao, sa ou maua atu le lima o Queequeg o fuli mai i ou luga i se uiga sili ona alofa ma le alofa. Na toetoe a e manatu o aʻu o lona toʻalua. O le fata fa'asaga sa fai fa'ama'i, tumu i sikuea laiti felanulanua'i ma tafatolu; ma o le lima lenei o lona peʻa na taia uma i se labyrinth e le mafaitaulia o Kereta, e leai ni vaega se lua e tasi le paolo saʻo—ona ou te manatu o lona taofia o lona lima i le sami i se auala le talafeagai i le la ma le paolo, o lona ofutino sa masani ona taai i luga. i taimi eseese—o lona lima lava lea, ou te fai atu ai, sa vaavaai mo le lalolagi atoa e pei o se fasi pepa o lena lava ie soosoo. O le mea moni, o se vaega o le taoto i luga e pei ona faia e le lima i le taimi muamua na ou ala ai, sa tau le mafai ona ou iloa mai le ie soosoo, sa latou fefiloi faatasi o latou lanu; ma na o le lagona o le mamafa ma le mamafa na mafai ai ona ou iloa o loo fusi mai a'u e Queequeg.

Sa ese o'u lagona. Se'i ou taumafai e faamatala atu. A o ou laitiiti, ou te manatua lelei se tulaga faapea na oo ia te au; pe o se mea moni pe o se miti, e le mafai lava ona ou atoatoa. O le tulaga lea. Sa ou tipiina sina kape po o se isi mea—ou te manatu o loo taumafai e tolotolo i luga o le alaasu, e pei ona ou vaai i sina salu laitiiti o faia i nai aso ua mavae; ma lo’u tina fai, o le sa sasaina a’u i taimi uma, po o le auina atu o a’u e moe e aunoa ma se taumafataga,—sa toso a’e e lo’u tina o’u vae i fafo o le alaasu ma teu a’u e momoe, e ui ua na o le lua i le afiafi. i le aoauli o le aso 21 o Iuni, o le aso sili ona umi o le tausaga i lo tatou lalolagi. Sa ou lagona le fefe. Ae sa leai se fesoasoani mo lena mea, o lea na ou alu ai i luga o faasitepu i loʻu tamai potu i le fogafale tolu, tatala ese aʻu lava ia i le tele e mafai ai ina ia faʻaumatia le taimi, ma faʻatasi ma se mapuea tiga i le va o ie.

Sa ou taoto ai iina ma le le mautonu e fuafua e tatau ona mavae atu le sefuluono itula ao lei mafai ona ou faamoemoe mo se toetutu. E sefuluono itula i le moega! ua tiga le tamai o lo'u tua pe a mafaufau i ai. Ma sa matua mama foi; o le susulu mai o le la i le faamalama, ma le taalili tele o faiaoga i auala, ma le leo o leo fiafia i le fale atoa. Sa ou lagona le sili atu ona leaga—na iu ina ou tu i luga, fai ou lavalava, ma alu ifo lemu i lalo i o’u vae, saili lo’u tina fai, ma faafuase’i ona faapa’u ifo i ona vae, ma aioi atu ia te ia o se fesoasoani faapitoa e tuu mai ia te a’u se se’e lelei mo a’u. amio leaga; soo se mea lava ae na o le taʻusalaina o aʻu e taoto i se taimi umi e le mafaitaulia. Ae o ia o le sili ma sili ona magafagafa o tina fai, ma i tua sa tatau ona ou alu i loʻu potu. Mo le tele o itula sa ou taoto ai iina ma le mataala, ma lagona le sili atu le leaga nai lo le mea na ou faia talu mai lena taimi, e oo lava i le sili atu o mala na mulimuli mai. Mulimuli ane, masalo na ou pau i se miti taufaafefe o le toafilemu; ma ua ala lemu mai ai—ua afatia i miti—na pupula oʻu mata, ma o le potu e leʻi susulu i le la ua afifi nei i le pogisa i fafo. O le taimi lava lena na ou lagonaina ai se te'i o tafe mai i lo'u tino atoa; e leai se mea e iloa, ma e leai se mea e lagona; ae na foliga mai na tuu mai i lo'u lima se lima uiga ese. O loʻu lima na tautau i luga o le fata, ma o le leai o se igoa, le mafaufauina, le leoa poʻo le faʻailoga, lea e iai le lima, na foliga mai o nofo vavalalata i tafatafa o loʻu moega. Ona o le tele o tausaga na foliga mai ua faatupulaia i le tele o tausaga, sa ou taoto ai iina, ua aisaina ma le fefe sili ona matautia, ma le le fefe e toso ese lo'u lima; ae o lo'o mafaufau pea a na o le tasi le inisi e mafai ona ou fa'aosoina, o le a motusia le sipela mata'utia. Ou te le iloa pe na faapefea ona alu ese atu lenei malamalama mai ia te au; ae o le ala a’e i le taeao, sa ou manatua ai ma le gatete na mea uma, ma mo aso ma vaiaso ma masina mulimuli ane sa ou leiloa ai a’u lava i taumafaiga faafememeaiina e faamatala le mealilo. E leai, e oo mai lava i lenei lava itula, e masani ona ou le mautonu ia te au lava ia.

Ia, aveese le fefe mata'utia, ma o'u lagona i le lagonaina o le lima faalelagi i lo'u lima na matua tutusa lava, i lo latou uiga ese, ma mea na ou oo i ai ina ua ou ala i luga ma vaai atu i le lima faapaupau o Queequeg o fusi mai ia te au. Ae i se taimi mulimuli ane, o mea uma na tutupu i le po ua mavae na toe tupu mai, taitoatasi, i se tulaga moni, ona ou taoto ai lea na o le ola i le tulaga malie. Aua e ui ina ou taumafai e faagaioi lona lima—tatala le fusi o lona faatoafaiava—ae, a o moe o ia, sa ia fusi mai pea a’u, e peiseai e leai se mea ae o le oti e tatau ona tuueseeseina ai i maua. Ua ou taumafai nei e fafagu o ia—“Queequeg!”—ae na o le pau lana tali o le taagulu. Ona ou ta'avale ai lea, ua lagona lo'u ua e pei o se kola solofanua; ma faafuase'i ona lagonaina sina maosi laitiiti. I le togi ese o le fata, o iina o lo o taoto ai le tomawk o moe i tafatafa o le feai, e pei o se pepe foliga to'i. O se piko aulelei, e moni, sa ou manatu; abed iinei i se fale ese i le aso lautele, ma le 'ai ma le tomahawk! “Queequeg!—i le igoa o le lelei, Queequeg, ala mai!” I se taimi umi, e ala i le leo tele o le mimio, ma le leo tele ma le faifai pea i le le talafeagai o lona fusiina o se alii i lena ituaiga sitaili faa-faaipoipoga, sa faamanuiaina ai au i le aveesea o se muimui; ma i le taimi nei, na ia toe toso i tua lona lima, lulu atoa o ia e pei o se maile Newfoundland mai le vai, ma nofo i luga i luga o le moega, malo e pei o se tootoo, tilotilo mai ia te aʻu, ma mimilo ona mata e pei na te le manatua atoatoa. pe na faapefea ona ou i ai iina, e ui na foliga lemu le malamalama o le iloa o se mea e uiga ia te aʻu ia te ia. I le taimi nei, sa ou taoto lemu ma tilotilo atu ia te ia, ua leai se masalosalo tele i le taimi nei, ma punou i luga o le vaʻai vaʻai i se meaola fiailoa. Ina ua i'u lava, na foliga mai ua tonu lona mafaufau i uiga o lana uo moe, ma ua foliga mai ua toe faalelei ma le mea moni; sa ia oso i fafo i luga o le fola, ma e ala i ni faailoga ma ni leo patino na ou malamalama ai, afai ou te fiafia i ai, o le a ia fai muamua ona ofu ona tuu ai lea o au e fai ou lavalava mulimuli ane, ae tuua le potu atoa mo au lava. Ou te manatu o a'u, Queequeg, i lalo o le tulaga, o se mea e sili ona malamalama; ae, o le mea moni, o nei tagata ulavavale ei ai se lagona masani o le maaleale, fai mai mea e te manao ai; e ofoofogia lo latou faaaloalo. Ou te tuuina atu lenei faamalo faapitoa ia Queequeg, ona sa ia faia ia te au i le tele o le faaaloalo ma le magafagafa, a o ou nofosala i le le faaaloalo tele; sioa atu ia te ia mai le moega, ma matamata i ana gaioiga uma i le faleuila; mo le taimi ua sili atu loʻu fia iloa o laʻu faʻatoʻaga. Ae ui i lea, o se tagata e pei o Queequeg e te le o vaʻai i ai i aso uma, o ia ma ona ala sa matua taua lava.

Na amata ona fai ona ofu pito i luga e ala i le ofuina o lona pulou piupiu, o se pulou umi tele, i tafatafa ane, ona toe toesea ai lea o ona ofuvae—na ia tuli i luga ona seevae. O le a le mea na ia faia ai i lalo o le lagi, ou te le iloa, ae o lana gaioiga na sosoo ai o le tuʻimomomoina o ia lava—seevae i lima, ma pulou i lalo—i lalo o le moega; ina ua, mai le tele o le manava ma le tiga, sa ou faapea atu sa faigata o ia i lana galuega e fa'aosoina o ia lava; e ui lava e leai se tulafono o le tatau ua ou faalogo i ai, e manaomia ai e soo se tagata ona nofo toatasi pe a ofuina ona seevae. Ae o Queequeg, e te vaʻai atu, o se meaola i le tulaga o suiga - e le o se anufe poʻo se pepe. Sa lava lona malamalama e fa'aalia ai lona uiga ese i uiga uiga ese. E le'i mae'a lana a'oga. Sa avea o ia ma se tamaititi aoga. Ana le na o sina tulaga atamai, semanu e le popole lava o ia i seevae; ae ana faapea e le'i avea pea o ia ma se tagata feai, semanu e le moemiti lava o ia e alu ifo i lalo o le moega e faaofu ai. Mulimuli ane, na ia oso aʻe ma lona pulou ua mago tele ma nutimomoia i luga o ona mata, ma amata ona tetete ma setete solo i totonu o le potu, e peiseai, e le masani i seevae, o lana pea o paʻu povi maanuminumi-atonu e leʻi faia. a le o le fa'atonu—nai lo o le pipi'i ma fa'atiga ia te ia i le amataga o le taeao malulu.

I le vaai atu nei, e leai ni pupuni i le faamalama, ma o le auala e matua vaapiapi, o le fale faafeagai na faatonuina ai se vaaiga manino i totonu o le potu, ma matauina atili pea le foliga mataga na faia e Queequeg, o loʻo fetolofi solo i se isi mea seʻi vagana ai. lona pulou ma ona seevae; Sa ou aioi atu ia te ia i le mea sili ou te mafaia, ia faatelevaveina lona faleuila, aemaise lava ia alu i totonu o ona ofuvae i se taimi vave e mafai ai. Na ia usitaʻi, ona alu ai lea e taele o ia lava. O le taimi lena i le taeao e mulumulu e soo se Kerisiano ona mata; ae o Queequeg, i lo'u ofo, na faamalieina o ia lava i le taofiofia o ana vaiinu i lona fatafata, lima, ma lima. Ona ia ofuina lea o lona ofuvae, ma ave i luga se fasi fasimoli malo i luga o le laulau taele ogatotonu, fufui i le vai ma amata ona fufulu ona foliga. Sa ou vaavaai atu e vaai po o fea na tuu ai lana sele, faauta ma faauta, ua ia aveese mai le sele mai le tulimanu o le moega, ma se'e ese le laau umi, tatala le ulu, sasaa teisi i luga o lona seevae, ma savali aʻe i luga. o le fa'ata i le puipui, e amata ai se solo malosi, po'o le sapoina o ona alafau. Ou te manatu o aʻu, Queequeg, o loʻo faʻaaogaina le meaʻai sili a Rogers ma le tauimasui. Mulimuli ane, sa ou mafaufau i le itiiti ifo i lenei taotoga ina ua ou iloa po o le a le u'amea lelei e fai ai le ulu o se sapo, ma le matua maai o pito sa'o uumi i taimi uma.

O le isi vaega o lona faleuele na vave ona ausia, ma sa ia savali atu ma le mitamita i fafo o le potu, ma afifi i lona peleue manuki sili, ma taʻalo lana faufau e pei o le tootoo a le fitafita.





O LE MATAUPU 5. O le taeao.

Sa vave ona ou mulimuli atu i ai, ma ou alu ifo i totonu o le potu pa ma faafeiloaia le matai o le fale ma le fiafia tele. Ou te lei faapelepele ia te ia, e ui lava sa ia felafolafoa'i ia te au e le o sina mea itiiti i le mataupu o lo'u uo moe.

Ae ui i lea, o le ata malie o se mea lelei tele, ae o se mea itiiti lava o se mea lelei; o le tele o le alofa. O le mea lea, afai e avatu e se tagata i lona lava tagata se mea e fai ma tausuaga lelei i se tasi, aua neʻi solomuli o ia, a ia tuu atu ma le fiafia o ia lava e faʻaalu ma faʻaaluina i lena auala. Ma o le tagata o loʻo i ai se mea faʻatauvaʻa e uiga ia te ia, ia mautinoa e tele atu mea i totonu o lena tagata nai lo le mea e te mafaufau i ai.

O lea ua tumu le potu pa i tagata nofonofo ia na pa'u ifo i le po ua mavae, ma ou te le'i vaai lelei i ai. Toeitiiti lava o i latou uma o ni tafola; o alii sili, ma lona lua, ma lona tolu, ma kamuta, ma tufuga, ma tufuga, ma tautai, ma leoleo vaa; o se kamupani lanu enaena ma le pa'u, ma 'ava pupuu; o se seti e lei seleina, mafuefu, e fai uma lava peleue manuki mo ofu taeao.

E mafai ona e iloa lelei le umi na i ai i uta. O le alafau maloloina o lenei taulealea e pei o se pea i le la i le lanu, ma e foliga mai e manogi e pei o le musky; e le mafai ona tolu aso na tulaueleele ai o ia mai lana malaga Initia. O le tamaloa lea e sosoo ma ia e foliga mai i ni nai lanu mama; atonu e te faapea mai o loo i totonu ia te ia se pa'i o laau satini. I le pa'u o le lona tolu o lo'o tumau pea se lanu enaena, ae ua sinasina; e lē taumatea sa nofo o ia i vaiaso uma i uta. Ae o ai e mafai ona faʻaalia se alafau e pei o Queequeg? lea, e pupuni i lanu eseese, e foliga mai e pei o le malifa i sisifo o Andes, e faʻaalia i se faasologa e tasi, eseese tau, sone ma sone.

“Grub, ho!” o le tagi lea a le pule o le fale, ma tatala le faitotoa, ma matou o atu i le taeao.

Latou te fai mai o tagata ua vaʻai i le lalolagi, o le a avea ma tagata toʻafimalie i uiga, e fai lava si faʻatasi i le mafutaga. Ae e le o taimi uma: Ledyard, le tagata malaga maoae Niu Egelani, ma Mungo Park, le Scotch; o tagata uma, sa latou maua le mautinoa aupito itiiti i le potu malolo. Ae masalo o le na o le sopoia o Siberia i se taavale toso e taifau e pei ona faia e Ledyard, po o le alu umi na o le savali i se manava gaogao, i totonu o le negro loto o Aferika, o le aofaiga lea o faatinoga leaga a Mungo-o lenei ituaiga o malaga, Ou te fai atu, atonu e le o se auala sili lea ona lelei e maua ai se tulaga maualuga lautele. Ae, mo le tele o vaega, o lena ituaiga mea e tatau ona i ai i soo se mea.

O nei manatunatuga na o iinei e mafua ona o le tulaga ina ua uma ona matou nonofo uma i le laulau, ma sa ou sauni e faalogo i ni tala lelei e uiga i le fagotaina; sa ou te'i lava, e toetoe lava o tagata uma sa tumau le filemu. Ma e le gata i lea, ae sa latou foliga maasiasi. Ioe, sa i ai se seti o maile sami, o le toatele o i latou e aunoa ma sina matamuli na feosofi atu i tafola tetele i luga o le sami—o tagata ese uma ia i latou—ma taufetuli ma feoti e aunoa ma le faanenefu; ae ui i lea, o iinei sa latou saofafai ai i se laulau o le malu taeao—o le valaauga e tasi, o ituaiga uma e fiafia i ai—na vaavaai solo ma le matamuli le tasi i le isi e peiseai e lei misia lava i latou i se lotoā mamoe i totonu o Mauga Green. O se vaaiga fia iloa; o nei urosa matamuli, o nei tagata tau tafola!

Ae o Queequeg—aisea, sa nofo ai Queequeg faatasi ma i latou—i le ulu foi o le laulau, sa tupu fua; e malu pei o se aisa. E mautinoa lava e le mafai ona ou fai atu se tala tele mo lana faʻaleleia. Sa le mafai e le tagata faamemelo sili ona tauamiotonuina ma le alofa lona aumaia o lana nauna i le taeao faatasi ma ia, ma faaaoga ai iina e aunoa ma se sauniga; ma aapa atu i luga o le laulau ma ia, i se tulaga lamatia o le tele o ulu, ma tauivi ma fasi povi ia te ia. Ae e mautinoa lava sa matua lelei lava na ia faia, ma e iloa e tagata uma i le tele o tagata, o le faia o se mea malie o le faia lea ma le agamalu.

O le a tatou le talanoa i uiga uiga ese uma o Queequeg iinei; le auala na ia aloese ai mai le kofe ma lola vevela, ma faaaoga lona gauai atoatoa i pisupo, e seasea faia. Ua lava, ina ua uma le taumafataga o le taeao na ia alu ese e pei o isi i totonu o le potu lautele, tutu lana paipa tomahawk, ma sa nofo ai iina ma le leoa ma ulaula ma lona pulou e le mavavaeeseina, ina ua ou oso i fafo mo se savaliga.





O LE MATAUPU 6. O le Ala.

Ana faapea na ou ofo i le taimi muamua i le mauaina o se vaaiga o se tagata ese e pei o Queequeg o feoai solo i totonu o le sosaiete faaaloalo o se taulaga malamalama, e lei umi ae alu ese lena maofa ina ua ou savalivali muamua i le ao i magaala o New Bedford.

I auala e lata ane i uafu, so'o se uafu tele e masani ona ofo mai e matamata ai i fa'amatalaga e le fa'ailoa mai fafo. E oo lava i auala o Broadway ma Chestnut, o le au tautai Metitirani o nisi taimi o le a taufetuli i tamaitai fefefe. Regent Street e le o iloa e Lascars ma Malays; ma i Bombay, i le Apollo Green, e nonofo ai Yankees e masani ona fefefe i tagata o le atunuu. Ae na manumalo New Bedford i le Water Street ma Wapping. I nei nofoaga ua ta'ua mulimuli e te vaai ai na o seila; ae i New Bedford, e tutu tagata 'ai tino ma talatalanoa i tulimanu o le auala; tagata feai; o le toatele o i latou o loo tauaveina pea o latou ivi aano leaga. E sioa ai se tagata ese.

Ae, e ese mai le Feegeeans, Tongatobooarrs, Erromanggoans, Pannangians, ma Brighggians, ma, e ese mai i faʻataʻitaʻiga vao o le vaʻa fagota e leʻi faʻalogo i luga o auala, o le a e vaʻai i isi vaaiga e sili atu le fia iloa, e sili atu le malie. E taunuu mai i vaiaso taitasi i lenei taulaga le anoanoai o tagata lanumeamata o Vermonters ma New Hampshire, e galala uma i le manuia ma le mamalu i faigafaiva. O le tele lava o i latou e laiti, o fa'avaa malolosi; o tagata ua taia le vaomatua, ae o lea ua saili e tuu le to'i ma se'e le tao tafola. O le tele e lanu meamata e pei o Mauga Green na o mai ai. I nisi mea e te manatu i ai ae na o ni nai itula le matutua. Vaai iina! o lena tagata o lo'o ta'amilo i le tulimanu. E fai lona pulou pipi ma se ofu talaloa si'usi'u, fusia i le fusipa'u ma le naifi faaofuofu. O lo'o sau le isi ma se sou'-wester ma se 'ofu pomu.

E leai se tama'i tama'i tama'i taulaga e fa'atusalia ma se tama'i tama'i-nu'u—o le uiga o la'u tala o se tama'i tama'i tama'i-se tagata, i aso o ta'ifau, na te moaina lona lua eka i totini lima ona o le fefe ne'i pa'u ona lima. O lea la a oo i se atunuu e pei o lea ua manatu i lona ulu e faia ai se igoa iloga, ma auai i faigafaiva tafola tele, e tatau ona e vaai i mea malie na te faia pe a taunuu i le uafu. I le ta'u atu o lona ofu sami, na ia faatonuina ai logo logo i ona ofuvae; fusi i lona ofu tapoleni. Aue, leaga Hay-Seed! e matua motusia lava na maea i le afa muamua o le agi, pe a tulia oe, fusi, faamau, ma mea uma, i lalo o le faai o le afā.

Ae aua ne'i manatu o lenei taulaga ta'uta'ua e iai na'o harpooneers, 'ai tagata, ma pa'u e fa'aali atu i ana tagata asiasi. E leo mea uma. Ae o New Bedford o se nofoaga e le masani ai. Ana le seanoa i matou le au tafolā, semanu o le aso nei o le fanua o le fanua atonu e pei o le aue o le talafatai o Labrador. E pei ona i ai, o vaega o lona atunuu i tua ua lava e faʻafefe ai se tasi, latou te foliga matuaʻi. O le taulaga lava ia atonu o le nofoaga sili ona pele e nofo ai, i Niu Egelani atoa. O le nu‘u o le suāu‘u, ‘ua tonu lava, ‘ae lē pei o Kanana; o se laueleele foi, o saito ma uaina. E lē taufetuli ala i le suāsusu; pe i le tau e totogo ai mea latou te saunia ai i fuamoa fou. Ae ui i lea, e ui lava i lea, e leai se mea i Amerika uma e te maua ai nisi fale pei o patrician; paka ma togalaau e sili atu ona matagofie, nai lo New Bedford. O fea na latou o mai ai? Na fa'afefea ona totoina i luga o lenei scoria tuai o se atunu'u?

Alu ma tilotilo i luga o le u'amea fa'atusa o lo'o taamilo i le maota maualuga, ma o le a taliina lau fesili. Ioe; o nei fale totoa uma ma togalaau fuga na sau mai le Vasa Atalani, Pasefika, ma Initia. O le tasi ma i latou uma, sa saisai i latou ma toso mai i inei mai le pito i lalo o le sami. E mafai e Herr Alexander ona faia se mea faapena?

I New Bedford, o tama, latou te fai mai, e avatu ni tafolā mo a latou fanau teine, ma tufatufa atu a latou tuagane ma ni nai popoise i le fasi vaega. “Shipmates, this book, containing only four chapters—four yarns—is one of the smallest strands in the mighty cable of the Scriptures. Yet what depths of the soul does Jonah's deep sealine sound! what a pregnant lesson to us is this prophet! What a noble thing is that canticle in the fish's belly! How billow-like and boisterously grand! We feel the floods surging over us; we sound with him to the kelpy bottom of the waters; sea-weed and all the slime of the sea is about us! But what is this lesson that the book of Jonah teaches? Shipmates, it is a two-stranded lesson; a lesson to us all as sinful men, and a lesson to me as a pilot of the living God. As sinful men, it is a lesson to us all, because it is a story of the sin, hard-heartedness, suddenly awakened fears, the swift punishment, repentance, prayers, and finally the deliverance and joy of Jonah. As with all sinners among men, the sin of this son of Amittai was in his wilful disobedience of the command of God—never mind now what that command was, or how conveyed—which he found a hard command. But all the things that God would have us do are hard for us to do—remember that—and hence, he oftener commands us than endeavors to persuade. And if we obey God, we must disobey ourselves; and it is in this disobeying ourselves, wherein the hardness of obeying God consists.

“With this sin of disobedience in him, Jonah still further flouts at God, by seeking to flee from Him. He thinks that a ship made by men will carry him into countries where God does not reign, but only the Captains of this earth. He skulks about the wharves of Joppa, and seeks a ship that's bound for Tarshish. There lurks, perhaps, a hitherto unheeded meaning here. By all accounts Tarshish could have been no other city than the modern Cadiz. That's the opinion of learned men. And where is Cadiz, shipmates? Cadiz is in Spain; as far by water, from Joppa, as Jonah could possibly have sailed in those ancient days, when the Atlantic was an almost unknown sea. Because Joppa, the modern Jaffa, shipmates, is on the most easterly coast of the Mediterranean, the Syrian; and Tarshish or Cadiz more than two thousand miles to the westward from that, just outside the Straits of Gibraltar. See ye not then, shipmates, that Jonah sought to flee world-wide from God? Miserable man! Oh! most contemptible and worthy of all scorn; with slouched hat and guilty eye, skulking from his God; prowling among the shipping like a vile burglar hastening to cross the seas. So disordered, self-condemning is his look, that had there been policemen in those days, Jonah, on the mere suspicion of something wrong, had been arrested ere he touched a deck. How plainly he's a fugitive! no baggage, not a hat-box, valise, or carpet-bag,—no friends accompany him to the wharf with their adieux. At last, after much dodging search, he finds the Tarshish ship receiving the last items of her cargo; and as he steps on board to see its Captain in the cabin, all the sailors for the moment desist from hoisting in the goods, to mark the stranger's evil eye. Jonah sees this; but in vain he tries to look all ease and confidence; in vain essays his wretched smile. Strong intuitions of the man assure the mariners he can be no innocent. In their gamesome but still serious way, one whispers to the other—“Jack, he's robbed a widow;” or, “Joe, do you mark him; he's a bigamist;” or, “Harry lad, I guess he's the adulterer that broke jail in old Gomorrah, or belike, one of the missing murderers from Sodom.” Another runs to read the bill that's stuck against the spile upon the wharf to which the ship is moored, offering five hundred gold coins for the apprehension of a parricide, and containing a description of his person. He reads, and looks from Jonah to the bill; while all his sympathetic shipmates now crowd round Jonah, prepared to lay their hands upon him. Frighted Jonah trembles, and summoning all his boldness to his face, only looks so much the more a coward. He will not confess himself suspected; but that itself is strong suspicion. So he makes the best of it; and when the sailors find him not to be the man that is advertised, they let him pass, and he descends into the cabin.

“'Who's there?' cries the Captain at his busy desk, hurriedly making out his papers for the Customs—'Who's there?' Oh! how that harmless question mangles Jonah! For the instant he almost turns to flee again. But he rallies. 'I seek a passage in this ship to Tarshish; how soon sail ye, sir?' Thus far the busy Captain had not looked up to Jonah, though the man now stands before him; but no sooner does he hear that hollow voice, than he darts a scrutinizing glance. 'We sail with the next coming tide,' at last he slowly answered, still intently eyeing him. 'No sooner, sir?'—'Soon enough for any honest man that goes a passenger.' Ha! Jonah, that's another stab. But he swiftly calls away the Captain from that scent. 'I'll sail with ye,'—he says,—'the passage money how much is that?—I'll pay now.' For it is particularly written, shipmates, as if it were a thing not to be overlooked in this history, 'that he paid the fare thereof' ere the craft did sail. And taken with the context, this is full of meaning.

“Now Jonah's Captain, shipmates, was one whose discernment detects crime in any, but whose cupidity exposes it only in the penniless. In this world, shipmates, sin that pays its way can travel freely, and without a passport; whereas Virtue, if a pauper, is stopped at all frontiers. So Jonah's Captain prepares to test the length of Jonah's purse, ere he judge him openly. He charges him thrice the usual sum; and it's assented to. Then the Captain knows that Jonah is a fugitive; but at the same time resolves to help a flight that paves its rear with gold. Yet when Jonah fairly takes out his purse, prudent suspicions still molest the Captain. He rings every coin to find a counterfeit. Not a forger, any way, he mutters; and Jonah is put down for his passage. 'Point out my state-room, Sir,' says Jonah now, 'I'm travel-weary; I need sleep.' 'Thou lookest like it,' says the Captain, 'there's thy room.' Jonah enters, and would lock the door, but the lock contains no key. Hearing him foolishly fumbling there, the Captain laughs lowly to himself, and mutters something about the doors of convicts' cells being never allowed to be locked within. All dressed and dusty as he is, Jonah throws himself into his berth, and finds the little state-room ceiling almost resting on his forehead. The air is close, and Jonah gasps. Then, in that contracted hole, sunk, too, beneath the ship's water-line, Jonah feels the heralding presentiment of that stifling hour, when the whale shall hold him in the smallest of his bowels' wards.

“Screwed at its axis against the side, a swinging lamp slightly oscillates in Jonah's room; and the ship, heeling over towards the wharf with the weight of the last bales received, the lamp, flame and all, though in slight motion, still maintains a permanent obliquity with reference to the room; though, in truth, infallibly straight itself, it but made obvious the false, lying levels among which it hung. The lamp alarms and frightens Jonah; as lying in his berth his tormented eyes roll round the place, and this thus far successful fugitive finds no refuge for his restless glance. But that contradiction in the lamp more and more appals him. The floor, the ceiling, and the side, are all awry. 'Oh! so my conscience hangs in me!' he groans, 'straight upwards, so it burns; but the chambers of my soul are all in crookedness!'

“Like one who after a night of drunken revelry hies to his bed, still reeling, but with conscience yet pricking him, as the plungings of the Roman race-horse but so much the more strike his steel tags into him; as one who in that miserable plight still turns and turns in giddy anguish, praying God for annihilation until the fit be passed; and at last amid the whirl of woe he feels, a deep stupor steals over him, as over the man who bleeds to death, for conscience is the wound, and there's naught to staunch it; so, after sore wrestlings in his berth, Jonah's prodigy of ponderous misery drags him drowning down to sleep.

“And now the time of tide has come; the ship casts off her cables; and from the deserted wharf the uncheered ship for Tarshish, all careening, glides to sea. That ship, my friends, was the first of recorded smugglers! the contraband was Jonah. But the sea rebels; he will not bear the wicked burden. A dreadful storm comes on, the ship is like to break. But now when the boatswain calls all hands to lighten her; when boxes, bales, and jars are clattering overboard; when the wind is shrieking, and the men are yelling, and every plank thunders with trampling feet right over Jonah's head; in all this raging tumult, Jonah sleeps his hideous sleep. He sees no black sky and raging sea, feels not the reeling timbers, and little hears he or heeds he the far rush of the mighty whale, which even now with open mouth is cleaving the seas after him. Aye, shipmates, Jonah was gone down into the sides of the ship—a berth in the cabin as I have taken it, and was fast asleep. But the frightened master comes to him, and shrieks in his dead ear, 'What meanest thou, O, sleeper! arise!' Startled from his lethargy by that direful cry, Jonah staggers to his feet, and stumbling to the deck, grasps a shroud, to look out upon the sea. But at that moment he is sprung upon by a panther billow leaping over the bulwarks. Wave after wave thus leaps into the ship, and finding no speedy vent runs roaring fore and aft, till the mariners come nigh to drowning while yet afloat. And ever, as the white moon shows her affrighted face from the steep gullies in the blackness overhead, aghast Jonah sees the rearing bowsprit pointing high upward, but soon beat downward again towards the tormented deep.

“Terrors upon terrors run shouting through his soul. In all his cringing attitudes, the God-fugitive is now too plainly known. The sailors mark him; more and more certain grow their suspicions of him, and at last, fully to test the truth, by referring the whole matter to high Heaven, they fall to casting lots, to see for whose cause this great tempest was upon them. The lot is Jonah's; that discovered, then how furiously they mob him with their questions. 'What is thine occupation? Whence comest thou? Thy country? What people? But mark now, my shipmates, the behavior of poor Jonah. The eager mariners but ask him who he is, and where from; whereas, they not only receive an answer to those questions, but likewise another answer to a question not put by them, but the unsolicited answer is forced from Jonah by the hard hand of God that is upon him.

“'I am a Hebrew,' he cries—and then—'I fear the Lord the God of Heaven who hath made the sea and the dry land!' Fear him, O Jonah? Aye, well mightest thou fear the Lord God then! Straightway, he now goes on to make a full confession; whereupon the mariners became more and more appalled, but still are pitiful. For when Jonah, not yet supplicating God for mercy, since he but too well knew the darkness of his deserts,—when wretched Jonah cries out to them to take him and cast him forth into the sea, for he knew that for his sake this great tempest was upon them; they mercifully turn from him, and seek by other means to save the ship. But all in vain; the indignant gale howls louder; then, with one hand raised invokingly to God, with the other they not unreluctantly lay hold of Jonah.

“And now behold Jonah taken up as an anchor and dropped into the sea; when instantly an oily calmness floats out from the east, and the sea is still, as Jonah carries down the gale with him, leaving smooth water behind. He goes down in the whirling heart of such a masterless commotion that he scarce heeds the moment when he drops seething into the yawning jaws awaiting him; and the whale shoots-to all his ivory teeth, like so many white bolts, upon his prison. Then Jonah prayed unto the Lord out of the fish's belly. But observe his prayer, and learn a weighty lesson. For sinful as he is, Jonah does not weep and wail for direct deliverance. He feels that his dreadful punishment is just. He leaves all his deliverance to God, contenting himself with this, that spite of all his pains and pangs, he will still look towards His holy temple. And here, shipmates, is true and faithful repentance; not clamorous for pardon, but grateful for punishment. And how pleasing to God was this conduct in Jonah, is shown in the eventual deliverance of him from the sea and the whale. Shipmates, I do not place Jonah before you to be copied for his sin but I do place him before you as a model for repentance. Sin not; but if you do, take heed to repent of it like Jonah.”

While he was speaking these words, the howling of the shrieking, slanting storm without seemed to add new power to the preacher, who, when describing Jonah's sea-storm, seemed tossed by a storm himself. His deep chest heaved as with a ground-swell; his tossed arms seemed the warring elements at work; and the thunders that rolled away from off his swarthy brow, and the light leaping from his eye, made all his simple hearers look on him with a quick fear that was strange to them.

There now came a lull in his look, as he silently turned over the leaves of the Book once more; and, at last, standing motionless, with closed eyes, for the moment, seemed communing with God and himself.

But again he leaned over towards the people, and bowing his head lowly, with an aspect of the deepest yet manliest humility, he spake these words:

“Shipmates, God has laid but one hand upon you; both his hands press upon me. I have read ye by what murky light may be mine the lesson that Jonah teaches to all sinners; and therefore to ye, and still more to me, for I am a greater sinner than ye. And now how gladly would I come down from this mast-head and sit on the hatches there where you sit, and listen as you listen, while some one of you reads me that other and more awful lesson which Jonah teaches to me , as a pilot of the living God. How being an anointed pilot-prophet, or speaker of true things, and bidden by the Lord to sound those unwelcome truths in the ears of a wicked Nineveh, Jonah, appalled at the hostility he should raise, fled from his mission, and sought to escape his duty and his God by taking ship at Joppa. But God is everywhere; Tarshish he never reached. As we have seen, God came upon him in the whale, and swallowed him down to living gulfs of doom, and with swift slantings tore him along 'into the midst of the seas,' where the eddying depths sucked him ten thousand fathoms down, and 'the weeds were wrapped about his head,' and all the watery world of woe bowled over him. Yet even then beyond the reach of any plummet—'out of the belly of hell'—when the whale grounded upon the ocean's utmost bones, even then, God heard the engulphed, repenting prophet when he cried. Then God spake unto the fish; and from the shuddering cold and blackness of the sea, the whale came breeching up towards the warm and pleasant sun, and all the delights of air and earth; and 'vomited out Jonah upon the dry land;' when the word of the Lord came a second time; and Jonah, bruised and beaten—his ears, like two sea-shells, still multitudinously murmuring of the ocean—Jonah did the Almighty's bidding. And what was that, shipmates? To preach the Truth to the face of Falsehood! That was it!

“This, shipmates, this is that other lesson; and woe to that pilot of the living God who slights it. Woe to him whom this world charms from Gospel duty! Woe to him who seeks to pour oil upon the waters when God has brewed them into a gale! Woe to him who seeks to please rather than to appal! Woe to him whose good name is more to him than goodness! Woe to him who, in this world, courts not dishonor! Woe to him who would not be true, even though to be false were salvation! Yea, woe to him who, as the great Pilot Paul has it, while preaching to others is himself a castaway!”

He dropped and fell away from himself for a moment; then lifting his face to them again, showed a deep joy in his eyes, as he cried out with a heavenly enthusiasm,—“But oh! shipmates! on the starboard hand of every woe, there is a sure delight; and higher the top of that delight, than the bottom of the woe is deep. Is not the main-truck higher than the kelson is low? Delight is to him—a far, far upward, and inward delight—who against the proud gods and commodores of this earth, ever stands forth his own inexorable self. Delight is to him whose strong arms yet support him, when the ship of this base treacherous world has gone down beneath him. Delight is to him, who gives no quarter in the truth, and kills, burns, and destroys all sin though he pluck it out from under the robes of Senators and Judges. Delight,—top-gallant delight is to him, who acknowledges no law or lord, but the Lord his God, and is only a patriot to heaven. Delight is to him, whom all the waves of the billows of the seas of the boisterous mob can never shake from this sure Keel of the Ages. And eternal delight and deliciousness will be his, who coming to lay him down, can say with his final breath—O Father!—chiefly known to me by Thy rod—mortal or immortal, here I die. I have striven to be Thine, more than to be this world's, or mine own. Yet this is nothing: I leave eternity to Thee; for what is man that he should live out the lifetime of his God?”

He said no more, but slowly waving a benediction, covered his face with his hands, and so remained kneeling, till all the people had departed, and he was left alone in the place.
