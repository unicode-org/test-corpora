A o ou savali ese, sa tumu au i le mafaufau; o le mea na fa'afuase'i ona fa'aali mai ia te a'u e uiga ia Kapeteni Aapo, na faatumulia ai a'u i se uiga le manino o le tiga e uiga ia te ia. Ma i se isi itu, i lena taimi, sa ou lagonaina se tigaalofa ma se faanoanoa mo ia, ae ou te le iloa po o le a, sei vagana o le tigaina o lona vae. O LE MATAUPU 17. O Ramadan.

A o Queequeg's Ramadan, po o le Anapogi ma le Faalumaina, o le a faaauau i le aso atoa, ou te lei filifili e faalavelave ia te ia seia oo i le po; aua ou te faatāuaina le faaaloalo silisili i matafaioi faalelotu a tagata uma, aua lava nei o se mea malie, ma ou te le maua i lo'u loto le manatu faatauvaa e oo lava i se faapotopotoga o loi o loo tapuai i se nofoa tod; po o isi meafaiola i nisi vaega o lo tatou lalolagi, o e e i ai se tikeri o le vae e matua le mafaatusalia i isi paneta, ua ifo i luma o le tino o se tagata ua maliu e ona fanua ona o le tele o mea totino o loo umia ma mautotogi i lona suafa.

Ou te fai atu, o i tatou o Kerisiano Peresipeteriana lelei e tatau ona alofa i nei mea, ae aua le manatu ia i tatou lava e sili atu nai lo isi tagata, faapaupau ma isi mea, ona o lo latou faʻamaualuga faavalevalea i nei mataupu. Sa i ai Queequeg, i le taimi nei, e mautinoa lava le fiafia i manatu sili ona valea e uiga ia Yojo ma lana Ramadan; - ae o le a lena mea? Sa manatu Queequeg na te iloa le uiga o ia, ou te manatu; sa foliga ua faamalieina o ia; ona malolo ai lea o ia. E lē aogā a tatou finauga uma ma ia; ia tuu pea o ia, ou te fai atu ai: ma ia e alofa mai le Lagi ia te i matou uma—Pepereane ma le au faapaupau—aua o i tatou uma lava ua matua mata'utia le ta'ei o le ulu, ma ua manaomia se faaleleiga.

I le afiafi, ina ua ou lagona le mautinoa ua tatau ona uma ana faafiafiaga ma sauniga, sa ou alu i luga i lona potu ma tuitui i le faitotoa; ae leai se tali. Sa ou taumafai e tatala, ae sa mau i totonu. “Queequeg,” na ou fai atu lemu ai i totonu o le pu ki:—e lemu uma. “Ou te fai atu, Queequeg! aisea e te le tautala ai? O a’u—Isamaeli.” Ae sa tumau pea mea uma e pei o le taimi muamua. Sa amata ona ou popole. Sa ou tuu atu ia te ia se taimi tele; Sa ou manatu atonu na ia maua se apoplectic fit. Sa ou tilotilo atu i le pu ki; ae o le faitotoa na matala atu i se tulimanu uiga ese o le potu, o le avanoa o le pu ki sa na o se pi'opi'o ma leaga. Sa na o se vaega o le laupapa vae o le moega ma se laina o le puipui na mafai ona ou iloa atu, ae leai se isi mea. Na ou te'i ina ua ou vaai atu i le puipui o loo taatitia ai i luga o le puipui le la'au o le sapo a Queequeg, lea na aveese mai e le tamaitai o le fale i le afiafi ua mavae, a'o le'i matou o atu i le potu. E ese lena, na ou manatu ai; ae po o le a lava le tulaga, talu ai ona o loo tu i luga le mea lena, ma e seasea pe le alu foi i fafo e aunoa ma lea mea, o lea e tatau ai ona i ai o ia i totonu iinei, ma e leai se mea sese.

Ou te lei vaai lava talu ona ou tuu iina.” Na tamoe atu i se tamai kapoti i lalo o le tulaueleele o le faasitepu, na ia autilo i totonu, ma toe foi mai, ma taʻu mai ia te aʻu o loʻo misi le faufau a Queequeg. “Ua ia fasioti ia te ia lava,” o lana tagi lea. “O se mea leaga ua toe faia e Stiggs-ua toe alu foi le isi fa’atusa-e alofa le Atua i lona tina mativa!-o le a faaleagaina ai lo’u fale. Faatasi ai ma se leo tele na matala ai le faitotoa, ma le ki o le puipui, ma lafo ai le sima i luga o le taualuga; ma o iina, lagi lelei! sa nofo ai iina Queequeg, e to'afilemu ma fa'atauva'a; i le ogatotonu tonu o le potu; squatting on his hams, ma uu Yojo i luga o lona ulu. “E le'i nofonofo lava o ia i le aso atoa, a ea?” o le tala lea a le fafine nofofale.

Ae o mea uma na matou fai atu ai, e leai se upu na mafai ona matou toso ese mai ia te ia; Na toetoe a ou lagona le fia tuleia o ia i luga, ina ia suia lona tulaga, aua sa toetoe lava a le mafai ona onosaia, sa foliga mai e matua tiga ma le masani ai; aemaise lava, e foliga mai sa nofo lava o ia mo le silia i le valu pe sefulu itula, e alu foi e aunoa ma ana meaai masani.

“Mrs. Hussey,” na ou fai atu ai, “o loo ola o ia i mea uma e tutupu; o lea, tu‘u mai ‘iā te i matou, pe a e finagalo i ai, ‘ona ‘ou va‘ai ai lea i lenei mea uiga ‘ese.”

I le tapunia o le faitotoa i le tamaitai e ona le fale, sa ou taumafai e manumalo ia Queequeg e nofo i se nofoa; ae le aoga. Sa nofo ai o ia; ma o mea uma na te mafaia—mo a’u faatufugaga faaaloalo uma ma le taufaaleaga—na te le gaioi se pine, pe fai mai se upu e tasi, pe tilotilo mai ia te au, pe matauina lo’u i ai i sina auala itiiti.

Ou te mafaufau, ou te manatu, pe mafai ona avea lenei mea ma se vaega o lana Ramadan; e anapopogi ea i latou i o latou puaa faapena i lona motu. E le mafai ona tumau e faavavau, faafetai i le Atua, ma lona Ramadan na o le faatasi i le tausaga; ma ou te le talitonu e matua'i vave lava i lena taimi.

Ina ua umi se taimi o nofonofo ma faalogologo i tala uumi a nisi o seila na faatoa o mai mai se malaga pudding plum, e pei ona latou taʻua ai (o lona uiga, o se folauga fagota puʻupuʻu i totonu o se sikola po o se brig, e gata i le itu i matu o le laina. , na'o le Vasa Atalani); ina ua uma ona ou faalogo i nei pulu-puddingers seia oo i le lata i le sefulutasi, sa ou alu i luga o faasitepu ou te moe, ma le lagona mautinoa e oo mai i le taimi lea e mautinoa lava ua uma ona faamuta e Queequeg lana Ramadan. Ae leai; sa i ai o ia i le mea na ou tuua ai o ia; na te le'i lagaina se inisi. “Ona o le lagi, Queequeg, tu ia i luga ma lulu oe; tula'i ma fai se talisuaga. E te fia aai; e te fasioti oe lava, Queequeg.” Ae e leai se upu na ia tali mai ai.

O le faanoanoa ia te ia, o lea na ou filifili ai ou te alu e moe ma moe; ma e mautinoa lava, e lei umi se taimi, o le a ia mulimuli mai ia te au. Ae ae ou te lei liliu atu i totonu, sa ou tago i lo’u peleue mamafa o le pa’u urosa, ma togi i ona luga, ona sa folafola mai o se po malulu lava; ma sa leai se mea na ia ofuina ae na o lona peleue lapotopoto masani. Mo sina taimi, fai mea uma ou te manao ai, sa le mafai ona ou oo atu i se moe vaivai. Sa ou iliina le moligao; ma o le na o le mafaufau lava ia Queequeg—e le o se fa futu le mamao—o nofo ai iina i lena tulaga le mautonu, na o ia lava i le malulu ma le pogisa; o lenei mea na ou matua leaga ai lava. Mafaufau i ai; moe i le po atoa i totonu o le potu e tasi ma se faapaupau lautele ala ala i luga o ona hams i lenei faanoanoa, le mafai ona tali atu Ramadan!

Ae i se isi itu, na iu lava ina ou alu ese, ma ou le toe iloa se mea seia oo i le tafa o ata; a'o tilotilo atu i tafatafa o le moega, o lo'o to'oto'o Queequeg, e peisea'i ua fa'atete i lalo i le fola. Ae o le taimi lava na ulu atu ai le vaaiga muamua o le la i le faamalama, na ia oso aʻe i luga, ma soʻoga malo ma malō, ae ma se foliga fiafia; fa'asetete mai ia te a'u i le mea na ou taoto ai; toe oomi mai lona muaulu i lo’u muaulu; ma fai mai ua uma lana Ramadan.

O lenei, e pei ona ou taʻua muamua, e leai soʻu tetee i le tapuaiga a soo se tagata, po o le a lava, pe afai e le fasioti pe faatiga lena tagata i se isi tagata, aua e le talitonu foi lena tagata. O LE MATAUPU 18. O Lana Mareko.

A o matou savavali atu i le pito o le uafu agai atu i le vaa, o loo tauaveina e Queequeg lana sapelu, o Kapeteni Peleg i lona leo tiga ma le leotele na valaau mai ia i matou mai lana wigwam, fai mai na te leʻi masalomia laʻu uo o se tagata 'ai, ma sa ia faʻailoa mai e leai. 'ai tagata i luga o lena va'a, se'i vagana ua latou faia muamua a latou pepa.

Na mavae se aso pe lua, ma sa i ai se gaoioiga tele i luga o le Pequod. Aua e le gata i le umi tele o le folauga fagota, ae o le tele o mataupu e uiga ese i le itu a leoleo o faiga faiva, ma le le mafai ona toe suia i uafu mamao e masani ona masani ai, e ao ona manatua, o vaa uma, o vaa fagota e sili ona aliali mai. i fa'alavelave fa'afuase'i o so'o se ituaiga, aemaise o le fa'aleagaina ma le leiloa o mea tonu lava e fa'amoemoe i ai le manuia o le folauga. O le mea lea, o va'a fa'apolopolo, fa'apolopolo fa'apolopolo, ma laina fa'apolopolo ma harpoons, ma fa'asaoina mea uma, toetoe lava, ae o se Kapeteni fa'apolopolo ma se va'a fa'alua.

I le taimi na matou taunuu ai i le Motu, ua toetoe lava uma le teuina o le Pequod sili ona mamafa; e aofia ai ana povi, falaoa, vai, fafie, ma u'amea ma tootoo. Peita'i, e pei ona ta'ua muamua, sa i ai pea le fe'ai ma le fe'avea'i i luga o le va'a o mea 'ese'ese ma fa'ai'uga o mea, tetele ma laiti.

O le sili ia i latou na faia lenei mea o le siiina ma le amoina o le tuafafine o Kapeteni Pilitati, o se tina matua paʻu o se agaga e sili ona naunau ma e le vaivai, ae e matua agalelei lava, o le na foliga mai na filifili, afai e mafai ona ia fesoasoani i ai, e leai se mea e maua i le Pequod. , ina ua uma ona oo atu i le sami. E i ai se taimi e sau ai o ia i luga o le vaa ma se fagu piko mo le faleoloa a le auauna pule; i le isi taimi ma le fusi o le kesi a le paaga sili, lea na ia teuina ai lana ogalaau; a third time with a roll of flannel for the small of some one's rheumatic back. Never did any woman better deserve her name, which was Charity—Aunt Charity, as everybody called her. And like a sister of charity did this charitable Aunt Charity bustle about hither and thither, ready to turn her hand and heart to anything that promised to yield safety, comfort, and consolation to all on board a ship in which her beloved brother Bildad was concerned, and in which she herself owned a score or two of well-saved dollars.

But it was startling to see this excellent hearted Quakeress coming on board, as she did the last day, with a long oil-ladle in one hand, and a still longer whaling lance in the other. Nor was Bildad himself nor Captain Peleg at all backward. As for Bildad, he carried about with him a long list of the articles needed, and at every fresh arrival, down went his mark opposite that article upon the paper. Every once in a while Peleg came hobbling out of his whalebone den, roaring at the men down the hatchways, roaring up to the riggers at the mast-head, and then concluded by roaring back into his wigwam.

During these days of preparation, Queequeg and I often visited the craft, and as often I asked about Captain Ahab, and how he was, and when he was going to come on board his ship. To these questions they would answer, that he was getting better and better, and was expected aboard every day; meantime, the two captains, Peleg and Bildad, could attend to everything necessary to fit the vessel for the voyage. If I had been downright honest with myself, I would have seen very plainly in my heart that I did but half fancy being committed this way to so long a voyage, without once laying my eyes on the man who was to be the absolute dictator of it, so soon as the ship sailed out upon the open sea. But when a man suspects any wrong, it sometimes happens that if he be already involved in the matter, he insensibly strives to cover up his suspicions even from himself. And much this way it was with me. I said nothing, and tried to think nothing.

At last it was given out that some time next day the ship would certainly sail. So next morning, Queequeg and I took a very early start.





CHAPTER 21. Going Aboard.

It was nearly six o'clock, but only grey imperfect misty dawn, when we drew nigh the wharf.

“There are some sailors running ahead there, if I see right,” said I to Queequeg, “it can't be shadows; she's off by sunrise, I guess; come on!”

“Avast!” cried a voice, whose owner at the same time coming close behind us, laid a hand upon both our shoulders, and then insinuating himself between us, stood stooping forward a little, in the uncertain twilight, strangely peering from Queequeg to me. It was Elijah.

“Going aboard?”

“Hands off, will you,” said I.

“Lookee here,” said Queequeg, shaking himself, “go 'way!”

“Ain't going aboard, then?”

“Yes, we are,” said I, “but what business is that of yours? Do you know, Mr. Elijah, that I consider you a little impertinent?”

“No, no, no; I wasn't aware of that,” said Elijah, slowly and wonderingly looking from me to Queequeg, with the most unaccountable glances.

“Elijah,” said I, “you will oblige my friend and me by withdrawing. We are going to the Indian and Pacific Oceans, and would prefer not to be detained.”

“Ye be, be ye? Coming back afore breakfast?”

“He's cracked, Queequeg,” said I, “come on.”

“Holloa!” cried stationary Elijah, hailing us when we had removed a few paces.

“Never mind him,” said I, “Queequeg, come on.”

But he stole up to us again, and suddenly clapping his hand on my shoulder, said—“Did ye see anything looking like men going towards that ship a while ago?”

Struck by this plain matter-of-fact question, I answered, saying, “Yes, I thought I did see four or five men; but it was too dim to be sure.”

“Very dim, very dim,” said Elijah. “Morning to ye.”

Once more we quitted him; but once more he came softly after us; and touching my shoulder again, said, “See if you can find 'em now, will ye?

“Find who?”

“Morning to ye! morning to ye!” he rejoined, again moving off. “Oh! I was going to warn ye against—but never mind, never mind—it's all one, all in the family too;—sharp frost this morning, ain't it? Good-bye to ye. Shan't see ye again very soon, I guess; unless it's before the Grand Jury.” And with these cracked words he finally departed, leaving me, for the moment, in no small wonderment at his frantic impudence.

At last, stepping on board the Pequod, we found everything in profound quiet, not a soul moving. The cabin entrance was locked within; the hatches were all on, and lumbered with coils of rigging. Going forward to the forecastle, we found the slide of the scuttle open. Seeing a light, we went down, and found only an old rigger there, wrapped in a tattered pea-jacket. He was thrown at whole length upon two chests, his face downwards and inclosed in his folded arms. The profoundest slumber slept upon him.

“Those sailors we saw, Queequeg, where can they have gone to?” said I, looking dubiously at the sleeper. But it seemed that, when on the wharf, Queequeg had not at all noticed what I now alluded to; hence I would have thought myself to have been optically deceived in that matter, were it not for Elijah's otherwise inexplicable question. But I beat the thing down; and again marking the sleeper, jocularly hinted to Queequeg that perhaps we had best sit up with the body; telling him to establish himself accordingly. He put his hand upon the sleeper's rear, as though feeling if it was soft enough; and then, without more ado, sat quietly down there.

“Gracious! Queequeg, don't sit there,” said I.

“Oh! perry dood seat,” said Queequeg, “my country way; won't hurt him face.”

“Face!” said I, “call that his face? very benevolent countenance then; but how hard he breathes, he's heaving himself; get off, Queequeg, you are heavy, it's grinding the face of the poor. Get off, Queequeg! Look, he'll twitch you off soon. I wonder he don't wake.”

Queequeg removed himself to just beyond the head of the sleeper, and lighted his tomahawk pipe. I sat at the feet. We kept the pipe passing over the sleeper, from one to the other. Meanwhile, upon questioning him in his broken fashion, Queequeg gave me to understand that, in his land, owing to the absence of settees and sofas of all sorts, the king, chiefs, and great people generally, were in the custom of fattening some of the lower orders for ottomans; and to furnish a house comfortably in that respect, you had only to buy up eight or ten lazy fellows, and lay them round in the piers and alcoves. Besides, it was very convenient on an excursion; much better than those garden-chairs which are convertible into walking-sticks; upon occasion, a chief calling his attendant, and desiring him to make a settee of himself under a spreading tree, perhaps in some damp marshy place.

While narrating these things, every time Queequeg received the tomahawk from me, he flourished the hatchet-side of it over the sleeper's head.

“What's that for, Queequeg?”

“Perry easy, kill-e; oh! perry easy!”

He was going on with some wild reminiscences about his tomahawk-pipe, which, it seemed, had in its two uses both brained his foes and soothed his soul, when we were directly attracted to the sleeping rigger. The strong vapor now completely filling the contracted hole, it began to tell upon him. He breathed with a sort of muffledness; then seemed troubled in the nose; then revolved over once or twice; then sat up and rubbed his eyes.

“Holloa!” he breathed at last, “who be ye smokers?”

“Shipped men,” answered I, “when does she sail?”

“Aye, aye, ye are going in her, be ye? She sails to-day. The Captain came aboard last night.”

“What Captain?—Ahab?”

“Who but him indeed?”

I was going to ask him some further questions concerning Ahab, when we heard a noise on deck.

“Holloa! Starbuck's astir,” said the rigger. “He's a lively chief mate, that; good man, and a pious; but all alive now, I must turn to.” And so saying he went on deck, and we followed.

It was now clear sunrise. Soon the crew came on board in twos and threes; the riggers bestirred themselves; the mates were actively engaged; and several of the shore people were busy in bringing various last things on board. Meanwhile Captain Ahab remained invisibly enshrined within his cabin.
