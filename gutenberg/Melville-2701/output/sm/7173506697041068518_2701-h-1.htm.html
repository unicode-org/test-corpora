O LE MATAUPU 1. Fa'ata'ita'iga.

Valaau a'u o Isamaeli. I ni nai tausaga ua mavae—le afaina pe o le a le umi tonu—o le i ai o se tupe itiiti pe leai foi i la’u ato, ma e leai se mea faapitoa e fiafia i ai a’u i luga o le matafaga, sa ou manatu ai o le a ou folau teisi ma vaai i le vai o le lalolagi. O se auala ou te alu ese ai mai le toto ma faatonutonu le taamilosaga. Soo se taimi lava ou te maua ai lo'u tuputupu a'e leaga i le gutu; soo se taimi lava e susū ai, timuga o Novema i lo'u agaga; soo se taimi lava ou te iloa ai ua ou tu ma le le loto i ai i luma o faleteuoloa pusa maliu, ma aumai i tua o falelauasiga uma ou te feiloai i ai; aemaise lava i soo se taimi lava e sili ai aʻu e loʻu mafaufau, e manaʻomia ai se mataupu faavae malosi e taofia ai aʻu mai le laa atu ma le loto i ai i le auala, ma tuʻituʻi ese pulou o tagata - ona ou manatu ai lea ua oʻo i le taimi e alu ai i le sami i se taimi vave. e pei ona ou mafaia. O lo'u sui lea mo le fana ma le polo. Faatasi ai ma se filosofia lauolaola Cato o ia lava i luga o lana pelu; Ou te ave filemu i le vaa. E leai se mea e ofo ai i lenei mea. Afai latou te iloaina, e toetoe lava o alii uma i o latou tikeri, i se taimi po o se isi, e toetoe lava o lagona tutusa i le vasa ma au.

O lea la ua i ai lou aai insular o Manhattoes, o loo fusi faataamilo i uafu e pei o atumotu o Initia i amu—o loo siomia ai e pisinisi i ana faase'e. Taumatau ma agavale, e ave oe e ala i vai. O lona pito i totonu o le taulaga o le maa, lea e fufuluina ai lena mole taua i galu, ma maalili i le savili, lea na le iloa atu e le eleele i ni nai itula talu ai. Vaai i le motu o tagata matamata vai iina.

Taamilomilo i le aai o se aoauli o le Sapati miti. Alu mai Corlears Hook i Coenties Slip, ma mai iina, i Whitehall, agai i matu. O le a se mea ua e vaʻaia?—Ua faʻapipiʻiina e pei o leoleo le leoa i le taulaga atoa, o loʻo tutu i le afe ma afe o tagata ola o loʻo faʻamautu i le vasa. O nisi e faalagolago i faupuega; o nisi sa nonofo i luga o le pou; o nisi e vaavaai i luga o pa o vaa mai Saina; o nisi e maualuga atu i luga o le uaea, e pei o loo taumafai ia maua se vaaiga sili atu i le sami. Ae o tagata uma ia o fanua; o aso o le vaiaso o lo'o fa'apipi'i i u'amea ma fa'ama'i— nonoa i fata, tutu'i i nofoa fa'amau, u'u i kesi. E faapefea la lenei mea? Ua leai ea fanua lanulauava? O a mea latou te faia iinei?

Ae vaai! lea ua o mai ai le toatele o le motu o tagata, o loo feosofi sa'o mo le vai, ma e foliga mai ua fusifusia mo se maulu. uiga ese! E leai se mea e faamalieina ai i latou, na o le tu'itu'au tupito o le nu'u; e le lava le feoai solo i lalo o le paolo o faleteuoloa. Leai. E tatau ona latou latalata atu i le vai i le mea latou te mafaia e aunoa ma le pauu i totonu. Ma o iina latou te tutu ai—maila o latou—liki. Ilanders uma, latou te o mai mai auala ma ala, auala ma auala—matū, sasaʻe, saute, ma sisifo. Ae o iinei latou te lotogatasi uma. Ta'u mai ia te a'u, pe ua tosina atu ea i latou i le malosi maneta o nila o tapasa o na vaa uma?

Toe tasi. Fai mai o loo e i le atunuu; i nisi o laueleele maualuluga o vaituloto. Toe ui i soo se ala e te fiafia i ai, ma e sefulu i le tasi e ave ai oe i lalo i se vanu, ma tuua ai oe iina i tafatafa o se vaitaele i le vaitafe. O loo i ai se togafiti faataulaitu. Tuu atu i tagata e sili ona le mautonu o tagata ia punou i ana moomooga sili ona loloto - ia tu i luga o lena tagata i luga o ona vae, tuʻu ona vae, ma o le a ia taʻitaʻia oe i le vai, pe a iai vai i lena itulagi uma. Afai e te fia inu i le toafa tele o Amerika, fa'ata'ita'i le fa'ata'ita'iga lenei, pe a fa'apea o lo'o tu'uina atu lau karavani se polofesa fa'atusa. Ioe, e pei ona iloa e tagata uma, o le mafaufau loloto ma le vai e faaipoipo e faavavau.

Ae o se tusiata lenei. Na te manaʻo e valiina oe i le miti sili ona manaia, sili ona paolo, sili ona filemu, sili ona faʻataʻitaʻiina o laufanua alofa i le vanu uma o le Saco. O le a le elemene autu na te fa'aaogaina? O iina e tutu ai ona laau, e tofu ma le ogalaau omo, e peiseai o se hermit ma se faasatauro i totonu; o iinei foi e momoe ai lona fanua, ma o iina e momoe ai ana manu; ae alu a'e mai le fale i luga le asu moe. O'oo i togavao mamao e savili ai i se auala faalavefau, e o'o atu i tumutumu o mauga o lo'o ta'ele i le lanu moana i le itu mauga. Ae e ui lava ina taoto le ata faapea, ma e ui ina luluina e lenei laau paina ona mapuea e pei o lau i luga o le ulu o lenei leoleo mamoe, ae e le aoga mea uma, sei vagana ua taulai atu le mata o le leoleo mamoe i le vaitafe faataulaitu i ona luma. Alu e asiasi i Fa'ato'aga ia Iuni, pe a o'o i le tele o maila e te savali ai i tulivae i totonu o lili-Taka-o le a le mea o lo'o mana'omia e le aulelei? Pe o Niagara na o se fata oneone, pe e te malaga i lau afe maila e matamata ai? Aisea na fa'afuase'i ai ona maua e le tusisolo mativa o Tennessee ni lu'u siliva se lua, pe fa'atau se peleue mo ia, lea na ia mana'omia ma le fa'anoanoa, pe teu lana tupe i se malaga savali i Rockaway Beach? Aisea e toetoe lava o tama malolosi uma soifua maloloina ma se agaga malosi malosi i totonu ia te ia, i se taimi poʻo isi valea e alu i le sami? Aisea i lau malaga muamua i le avea ai ma se pasese, na e lagona ai e oe lava ia se tete'e uiga ese, ina ua ta'u muamua atu o oe ma lau vaa ua le iloa atu nei i le laueleele? Aiseā na fa'apa'iaina ai e tagata Peresia tuai le sami? Aisea na tuuina atu ai e tagata Eleni se atua ese, ma lona uso o Jove? E mautinoa o nei mea uma e leai se uiga. Ae e loloto atu le uiga o lena tala ia Narcissus, o le na le mafai ona ia malamalama i le faatusa tiga ma le agamalu na ia vaaia i totonu o le vaipuna, ma oso i totonu ma malemo ai. Ae o le ata lava lea e tasi, tatou te vaai i ai i vaitafe uma ma vasa. O le ata lea o le ata le mafaamatalaina o le olaga; ma o le ki lea i mea uma.

Ia, pe a ou fai atu ua ou masani i le alu i le sami i soo se taimi lava e amata ai ona ou faanenefu i mata, ma amata ona ou soona mafaufau i oʻu mama, ou te le o manatu e faapea ou te alu i le sami se pasese. Aua o le alu o se pasese e tatau ona i ai sau ato tupe, ma o se ato e na o se ieie sei vagana ua i ai sau mea i totonu. E le gata i lea, o pasese e mama’i i le sami—tupu misa—e le momoe i po—e le fiafia tele, e pei o se mea masani;—leai, ou te le alu lava o se pasese; pe, e ui lava o aʻu o se masima, ou te alu i le sami o se Commodore, po o se Kapeteni, po o se Kuki. Ou te lafoai le mamalu ma le tulaga ese o ia tofiga ia i latou e fiafia i ai. Mo la’u vaega, ou te inoino i galuega mamalu uma, tofotofoga, ma puapuaga o soo se ituaiga. E pei lava o le mea e mafai ona ou faia e tausia ai aʻu lava, e aunoa ma le tausia o vaʻa, barques, brigs, schooners, ma isi mea. Ae o le alu e fai kuka,—e ui ina ou ta’uta’u atu e tele le mamalu i lena mea, o se kuka o se ituaiga o tagata ofisa i luga o le vaa—ae, i se isi itu, ou te le’i manao lava i ni moa tunu;—e ui ina sa kukaina, faapagota ma le faautauta, ma le faamasinotonu. ua faamasima ma faasusuina, e leai se tasi e tautala atu ma le faaaloalo, e le fai atu ma le migao, e uiga i se moa tunua nai lo au. O le tu'inanau ifo i tupua a tagata Aikupito tuai i luga o ipi tunu ma solofanua o le vaitafe tunu, e te va'aia ai mami o na manu i totonu o latou fale tao tao tetele o pyramid.

Leai, pe a ou alu i le sami, ou te alu e pei o se tautai faigofie, i luma tonu o le pou, e paʻu ifo i lalo i le vaʻa, i luga aʻe i luga i le ulu o le tupu. E moni, latou te fa'atonu a'u e uiga i nisi, ma fa'aoso a'u mai lea va'a i lea va'a, e pei o se sē i le vao o Me. Ma i le taimi muamua, o lenei ituaiga o mea e le lelei. E pāʻia ai le lagona o se tasi o le mamalu, aemaise lava pe afai e te sau i se aiga tuai i le atunuu, o le Van Rensselaers, poʻo Randolphs, poʻo Hardicanutes. Ma e sili atu i lo mea uma, afai na'o le'i tu'uina lou lima i totonu o le ulo ta, ua e pule i ai o se faiaoga o le atunu'u, ma e maofa ai tama maualuluga ia te oe. O le suiga o se mea mataʻutia, ou te taʻutino atu ia te oe, mai se faiaoga i se seila, ma e manaʻomia ai le malosi o Seneca ma le Stoics e mafai ai e oe ona ataata ma tauaveina. Ae e oo lava i lenei mea e mou atu i le taimi.

Ae faapefea, pe a faatonu a'u e ni tama tuai o se kapeteni o le sami e aumai se salu ma salu i lalo o le fogafale? O le a le mea e tutusa ai lena uiga mataga, mamafa, o le uiga o la'u tala, i le fua o le Feagaiga Fou? E te manatu e manatu le agelu sili o Kaperielu i se mea e itiiti ifo ia te aʻu, aua ou te vave usitai ma le faaaloalo i na tagata matutua i lena tulaga faapitoa? O ai e le o se pologa? Ta'u mai lena mea. Ia, o lea la, po o le a lava le faatonuga a kapeteni tuai o le sami—po o le a lava le latou tu'i ma tu'i mai ia te a'u, ua ou maua le faamalieina i le iloaina e le afaina; o isi tagata uma o se tasi auala po o se isi e auauna atu i le auala lava e tasi—pe i se vaaiga faaletino pe faafaatusa, o lona uiga; ma o le mea lea e fealua'i solo ai le tu'i o le lalolagi, ma ia mililima uma lima o le tasi i le tau'au o le isi, ma ia faamalieina.

Ou te toe alu foi i le vasa i taimi uma o se seila, aua latou te faia se mea e totogi ai aʻu mo loʻu faalavelave, ae latou te le totogia pasese se sene e tasi ou te faalogo i ai. I se isi itu, o pasese lava latou e tatau ona totogi. Ma o loʻo i ai uma le eseesega i le lalolagi i le va o le totogiina ma le totogiina. O le gaioiga o le totogiina atonu o se faʻalavelave sili ona le lelei na oʻo mai ia i matou e le au faomea e toʻalua. Ae a totogi ,-o le a le mea e faʻatusatusa i ai? O le galuega fa'alenu'u lea e maua ai e se tagata tupe e matua ofoofogia lava, pe a manatu tatou te matua talitonu o tupe o le a'a lea o ma'i uma o le lalolagi, ma e leai se mea e mafai ai e se tagata mautotogi ona ulu atu i le lagi. Ah! e maeu lo tatou tuuina atu ma le fiafia i tatou lava i le malaia!

Mulimuli ane, ou te alu pea i le sami e pei o se seila, ona o le faamalositino lelei ma le ea mama o le fogafale i luma o le maota. Aua e pei ona i ai i lenei lalolagi, o matagi ulu e sili atu ona taatele nai lo matagi mai i le itu i sasae (o lona uiga, afai e te le solia le Pythagorean maxim), o le tele o vaega o le Commodore i luga o le kuata-foa e maua lona siosiomaga i le lona lua mai le auvaa. i luga o le vavalo. E manatu o ia na te manava muamua; ae le faapea. O le auala lava lea e tasi e ta'ita'iina ai e le au ta'ita'i o latou ta'ita'i i le tele o isi mea, i le taimi lava lea e tau le masalomia ai e ta'ita'i. Ae o le mea lea ina ua uma ona sogisogi soo i le sami o se tautai faatauoloa, ua tatau nei ona ou ave i loʼu ulu ou te alu i se folauga fagota; o le leoleo le vaaia lea o Fates, o lē e mataʻituina pea aʻu, ma faʻalilolilo aʻu, ma faʻaosofia aʻu i se auala e le mafai ona tali atu - e sili atu lana tali nai lo se isi lava. Ma, e le taumateina, o lo'u alu i lenei malaga fagota, o se vaega o le polokalama tele a Providence lea na tusia i se taimi ua leva. Na sau i totonu o se ituaiga o faʻasalalauga puʻupuʻu ma solo i le va o faʻaaliga sili atu. Ou te manatu o le vaega lenei o le pili e tatau ona faʻapea e pei o lenei:

“ Faiga Palota Tele Tauvaga mo le Au Peresitene o le Iunaite Setete. “O LE TAUSAGA TAFOGA A LE TASI O ISAMELI. “TAUA TOTO I AAFGHANISTAN.”

E ui lava e le mafai ona ou iloa pe aisea na tuu ai aʻu e na pule o le tulaga, o le Fates, mo lenei vaega leaga o le folauga fagota, ae o isi na tuu i lalo mo vaega matagofie i faalavelave maualuluga, ma vaega pupuu ma faigofie i tala malie malie, ma o vaega fiafia i fa'amuaga—e ui e le mafai ona ou iloa pe aisea ua tonu ai; ae, o lenei ua ou toe manatua tulaga uma, ou te manatu e mafai ona ou vaai teisi i punavai ma faamoemoega na tuuina mai ma le poto ia te aʻu i ni faafoliga eseese, na faaosofia ai aʻu e amata ona faatino le vaega na ou faia, e ese mai le faatosinaina o aʻu i le taufaasese lea. o se filifiliga e mafua mai i la'u lava filifiliga saoloto ma le faailoga tagata.

O le autu o nei mafuaaga o le manatu lofituina o le tafola tele lava ia. O se sau'ai mata'utia ma le lilo na faaosofia ai lo'u fia iloa uma. Ona sosoo ai lea ma le sami vao ma le mamao lea na ia taamilo ai lona motu tele; o fa'alavelave e le mafai ona fa'aolaina, e leai ni igoa o le tafola; o nei mea, faatasi ai ma mea ofoofogia uma na auai o le afe o vaaiga ma leo o Patagonian, na fesoasoani e faaosofia aʻu i loʻu manaʻoga. I isi tagata, atonu, o na mea semanu e le o ni faatosinaga; a o a‘u, ‘ua puapuagātia a‘u i le mageso e fa‘avavau i mea mamao. Ou te fiafia e folau i sami faasāina, ma tulaueleele i gataifale. O le le amanaiaina o le mea lelei, e vave ona ou iloa se mea mataʻutia, ma e mafai lava ona fegalegaleai ma ia-pe latou te faatagaina aʻu-talu ai e lelei le faauo ma pagota uma o le nofoaga e nofo ai.

Ona o ia mea, na talia ai le folauga fagota; na matala mai faitotoa tetele o lologa o le lalolagi ofoofogia, ma i le faasausili valevalenoa na faaseseina ai au i lo’u faamoemoe, e lua ma le lua na opeopea i totonu o lo’u loto, solo e le gata o le tafola, ma, i le ogatotonu o i latou uma, e tasi. fa'alilo tele, pei o se mauga kiona i le ea.





O LE MATA'upu 2. O le Taga-Kapeta.

Sa ou togiina se ofutino pe lua i totonu o la'u ato kapeta tuai, tuu i lalo o lo'u lima, ma amata loa mo Cape Horn ma le Pasefika. O le tuua o le aai lelei o Manhatto tuai, na ou taunuu ai i New Bedford. O se po o le Aso Toonai ia Tesema. Sa ou faanoanoa tele ina ua ou iloa ua uma ona folau le tama'i taga mo Nantucket, ma e leai se auala e taunuu atu ai i lena nofoaga e ofo mai, seia oo i le Aso Gafua e sosoo ai.

Ona o le toatele o sui tauva talavou mo tiga ma faasalaga o le fagotaina e tutu i lea lava New Bedford, e amata atu ai i le latou folauga, atonu e faapea foi, o a'u, mo se tasi, sa leai se manatu e faia faapea. Aua ua tonu lo'u mafaufau e folau i se va'a Nantucket, aua sa i ai se mea lelei ma le pisapisao e uiga i mea uma e feso'ota'i ma lena motu tuai lauiloa, lea na matua'i fiafia ai a'u. E le gata i lea, e ui lava ua leva ona faasolosolo malie ona pulea e New Bedford le pisinisi o le fagotaina o tafola, ma e ui o lea ua i tua atu le toeaina mativa o Nantucket ia te ia, ae o Nantucket o lana uluai tusi sili lea—le Turo o lenei Karefasi;—le nofoaga lea na muamua maliliu ai Amerika. ua pa'ulia le tafola. O fea se isi mea ae na o Nantucket na muamua feosofi atu ai na tagata tafolā, o Tagata Ulaula, e tuliloa le Leviatana? Ae o fea foi ae mai Nantucket, na tuu mai ai lena tama'i tama'i fagota muamua, o se vaega o lo'o tumu i ma'a fa'aulufale mai-e fa'apea fo'i le tala-e togi atu i tafola, ina ia iloa ai po'o le a le taimi e latalata atu ai e lamatia ai se sapo mai le aufana. ?

O lea ua i ai se po, se aso, ma se isi po na sosoo mai ai i o’u luma i New Bedford, ae ou te lei alu atu i lo’u uafu faamoemoeina, sa avea ma se popolega i le mea ou te ai ma moe ai i le taimi nei. O se po e matua masalosalo lava, e leai, o se po pogisa ma le le fiafia, malulu ma le fiafia. Ou te le iloa se tasi i lea nofoaga. Na ou iliina la'u taga, ma na o ni nai tupe siliva na ou aumaia,—O lea, po o fea lava e te alu i ai, Isamaeli, na ou faapea ifo ai ia te au lava, a o ou tu i le ogatotonu o se auala pogisa ma tau'u mai la'u ato, ma faatusatusa le pogisa agai i matu ma le pogisa agai i saute—po o fea lava i lou poto e te manatu e te momoe ai i le po, la’u pele o Isamaeli, ia mautinoa e fesili i le tau, ma aua le faapitoa.

Faatasi ai ma laa tutu sa ou savalia le auala, ma pasia le faailo o le “The Crossed Harpoons”—ae sa foliga taugata tele ma fiafia iina. E le gata i lea, mai i faamalama mumu sesega o le “Sword-Fish Inn,” na oo mai ai ni ave malolosi, e foliga mai na liusuavai ai le kiona ma le aisa mai luma o le fale, aua o soo se mea lava o lo o taatia ai le aisa mafiafia e sefulu inisi le mafiafia. o se fola malō, mafolafola,—ua fai si vaivai mo a'u, pe a ou tu'ia lo'u vae i luga o mea maaa, aua o le galue malosi ma le le salamo sa i ai alofivae o o'u seevae i se tulaga pagatia. O le taugata tele ma le fiafia, na ou toe mafaufau ai, ma tu mo sina taimi e matamata i le susulu lautele o le auala, ma faalogo i leo o tioata o loo i totonu. Ae alu pea, Isamaeli, na ou fai atu mulimuli ane; e te le fa'alogo mai? alu ese mai luma o le faitotoa; o ou seevae teuteu ua tu le ala. Ona ou alu ai lea. O lea ua ou mulimuli ai i auala na ou agai atu ai i vai, aua o iina, e mautinoa lava, sa sili ona taugofie, pe a le o fale talimalo sili ona fiafia.

O na auala pogisa! poloka o le pogisa, e le o fale, i itu uma e lua, ma o iinei ma iina o se moligao, e pei o se moligao o loo fealuai i totonu o se tuugamau. I lenei itula o le po, o le aso mulimuli o le vaiaso, o lena kuata o le taulaga na faamaonia ai le leai o se mea. Ae o le taimi nei na ou oo atu ai i se moli asu o sau mai se fale maualalo, lautele, o lona faitotoa na tu matala ma le fiafia. Sa i ai se vaaiga faatamala, e peiseai o se mea e faaaoga e tagata lautele; o lea, i totonu, o le mea muamua na ou faia o le tautevateva i luga o se pusa lefulefu i le faapaologa. Ha! Na ou manatu, ha, ina ua toetoe a fetinaiina aʻu e mea lele, o lefulefu ea mai lena aai ua faatafunaina, o Komoro? Ae o le “The Crossed Harpoons,” ma le “The Sword-Fish?”—o le mea lea e tatau ona iai le faailoga o le “The Mailei.” Ae peitai, sa ou tu i luga ma faalogoina se leo tele i totonu, tulei i luga ma tatala le faitotoa lona lua i totonu.

Na foliga mai o le Palemene Black sili sa nofo i Tofeta. O foliga uliuli e selau na liliu mai i o latou laina e matamata i ai; ma i tua atu, o se Agelu uliuli o le malaia sa sasaina se tusi i luga o le pulelaa. O se lotu negro; ma o le tusi a le failauga e uiga i le pogisa o le pogisa, ma le tagi ma le tagiauē ma le lilivau o nifo iina. Ha, Isamaeli, na ou mumumu, solomuli ese, Faafiafia leaga i le faailoga o 'O le Mailei!'

I le agai atu i luma, na iu ina ou tau atu i se malamalama pogisa e le mamao mai le uafu, ma ou faalogoina ai se tagitue i le ea; ma tepa a’e i luga, ma iloa atu ai se faailoilo o loo sasau i luga o le faitotoa o loo i ai se atavali papae i ona luga, e foliga lemu mai o se vaalele sa’o umi o le puao, ma o upu nei i lalo—“The Spouter Inn:—Peter Coffin.”

O le pusa oti?—Spouter?—E ese le leaga i lena sootaga faapitoa, ou te manatu ai. Ae o se igoa masani i Nantucket, latou te fai mai ai, ma ou te manatu o Peteru iinei o se tagata malaga mai iina. A'o foliga mupumu le moli, ma o le nofoaga, mo le taimi, na foliga filemu, ma o le tamai fale laupapa leaga lava ia na foliga mai atonu na aumai iinei mai le faaleagaina o se itumalo mu, ma o le faailo o le sasau sa i ai se O le ma'i-mativa o lo'o i ai, na ou manatu o le nofoaga tonu lea mo fale taugofie, ma le kofe sili ona lelei.

O se nofoaga uiga ese—o se fale tuai e i'u i le gable, e pei ua pe le isi itu, ma faalagolago i luga ma le faanoanoa. Na tu i luga o se tulimanu pogisa matuitui, lea na atili ai ona tagiauē le matagi malosi o Euroclydon nai lo se isi lava taimi e uiga i le vaa felafoaʻi leaga a Paulo. Ae ui i lea, o le Euroclydon, o se zephyr manaia tele i soʻo se tasi i totonu o le faitotoʻa, ma ona vae i luga o le ogaumu faʻafefe filemu mo le moega. “I le faamasinoina o lena matagi afā e igoa ia Euroclydon,” o le tala lea a se tusitala tuai—o ana galuega e na o le pau lea o le kopi o loo i ai—“e faia ai se eseesega ofoofogia, pe e te vaai atu i ai mai se faamalama tioata o loo i ai le aisa i luga o le eleele. i fafo, pe e te vaʻai i ai mai le faʻamalama e leai ni fusi, o loʻo i ai le aisa i itu uma e lua, ma e na o le pau lea o le pupula o le Oti." E moni lava, sa ou manatu, a o oo mai lenei fuaitau i loʻu mafaufau-mataitusi tuai-uliuli, ua e mafaufau lelei. Ioe, o mata ia o faamalama, ma o loʻu tino lenei o le fale. Ae paga lea, latou te le'i tu'u le u'amea, ma sasa'i i totonu o sina laina i lea mea ma lea mea. Ae ua tuai tele ona fai so o se faaleleiga i le taimi nei. Ua uma le atulaulau; ua i ai le copestone, ma o tupe meataalo na ave ese i le miliona tausaga talu ai. Le mativa o Lasalo iina, e lili ona nifo i le maa o lona aluga, ma lulu ona masaesae ma lona tetete, e mafai ona pupuni taliga e lua i ieie, ma tuu se atigi saito i lona gutu, a e le mafai ona taofia ai le gutu. Euroclydon matagi. Euroclydon! o le tala lea a le toeaina o Dives, i lana afifi siliki mumu—(na i ai lona ofu mumu mulimuli ane) pooh, pooh! Oka se po aisa manaia; pe faapefea ona feilafi Orion; ma'ea moli i matu! Tuu atu ia i latou e talanoa e uiga ia latou tau mafanafana i sasae o fale faasao e faavavau; tuu mai ia te au le avanoa e fai ai la'u lava taumafanafana i a'u lava malala.

Ae o le ā le manatu o Lasalo? E mafai ona ia faamafanafanaina ona lima lanumoana e ala i le uuina i luga o moli tetele i matu? Pe le sili atu ea ona iai Lasalo i Sumatra nai lo o iinei? Pe le sili atu ea ia te ia e tuu o ia i lalo i le umi o le laina o le ekueta; ioe, outou atua! alu ifo i le lua afi, ina ia sao ai lenei aisa?

O lea la, e tatau ona taoto Lasalo iina i luga o le ma'a fa'ama'a i luma o le faitoto'a o Dives, e sili atu le matagofie nai lo le fa'amauina o se aisa i se tasi o Moluccas. Ae o Dives lava ia, e ola foi o ia e pei o se Czar i totonu o se maota aisa e faia i le mapuea aisa, ma o se peresitene o se sosaiete e le gaoia, na o le inu lava o loimata o tamaiti matuaoti.

Ae e le toe oo i lenei fagogo, o lea o le a tatou faia se tafola, ma e tele na mea o le a oo mai. Se'i o tatou vele ese le aisa mai o tatou vae ua aisa, ma vaai po o le a le ituaiga nofoaga e iai lenei “Spouter”.





O LE MATAUPU 3. O le Spouter-Inn.

O le ulufale atu i lena Spouter-Inn e fa'ai'u i le gable, na e maua ai oe i totonu o se avanoa lautele, maualalo, fa'afefeteina fa'atasi ma vainscots tuai, fa'amanatuina se tasi o pa puipui o se va'a tuai fa'asalaina. I le tasi itu o loʻo tautau ai se vali suauu lapoʻa na matua asu, ma faʻaleagaina auala uma, i le le tutusa o moli na e vaʻaia ai, na na o le suʻesuʻeina ma le filiga ma se faasologa o asiasiga masani i ai, ma le suʻesuʻeina ma le totoa o tuaoi, ina ia mafai ona e maua i so o se auala se malamalama i lona faamoemoega. O ia ituaiga o ata ma ata lafoia e le mafaitaulia, lea na toetoe lava a e manatu o se tusiata talavou mataʻutia, i le taimi o New England hags, na taumafai e faʻamalamalamaina le vevesi. Ae e ala i le tele ma le naunautai o manatunatuga loloto, ma manatunatuga faifai pea, aemaise lava o le togiina o le tamai faamalama agai i tua o le faitotoa, e iu ai ina e oo i le faaiuga o sea manatu, e tusa lava po o le a le leaga, atonu e le o se mea e le talafeagai.

Ae o le mea e sili ona le mautonu ma faafememeaiina ai oe, o se vaega umi, lamolemole, mata'utia, uliuli o se mea o lo'o faapepepepe i le ogatotonu o le ata i luga o laina lanu moana e tolu, faanenefu, fa'atutusa o lo'o opeopea i se fefete leai se igoa. O se ata gaogao, susu, squitchy e moni lava, ua lava e tuliesea ai se tagata popolevale. Ae sa i ai se ituaiga e le tumau, afa-maua, le mafaufauina le mamalu e uiga i ai na matua fa'amalo ai oe i ai, seia oʻo ina e faia se tautoga ma oe lava e te iloa ai le uiga o lena atavali ofoofogia. E masani lava o se mea manaia, ae, talofa e, o se manatu taufaasesē o le a taia oe i totonu.—O le Sami Uliuli i le vaeluaga o le po.—O le taua e le masani ai o elemene muamua e fa.—O se mea ua pala.—O se vaaiga i le taumalulu Hyperborean .—O le malepe o le tafe o le aisa o Taimi. Ae mulimuli ane, o nei mafaufauga uma na iu atu i lena mea mataʻutia o loʻo i totonu o le ata. O lena taimi na iloa ai, ma o isi mea uma sa manino. Ae taofi; e lē tutusa ‘ea ma se i‘a tetele? e oo lava i le leviatana sili?

O le mea moni, o le mamanu a le tusiata e foliga mai o lenei: o se manatu mulimuli o aʻu lava, o se vaega e faavae i luga o le tuufaatasiga o manatu o le tele o tagata matutua na matou talanoa i ai i le mataupu. O le ata o loʻo faʻatusalia se Cape-Horner i se afa tele; o le va'a afa fa'avae o lo'o fa'afefeteina iina ma ona fa'amau e tolu na malepe na'o iloa atu; ma o se tafola ita tele, ma le faamoemoe e puna i luga o le vaa, o loo i ai i se gaoioiga tele o le tutu'i o ia lava i luga o ulu pou e tolu.

O le puipui fa'afeagai o lenei ulufale sa tautau uma i le fa'ailoga fa'apaupau o uatogi ma tao. O nisi sa mafiafia ma nifo feilafi e pei o ili nifo elefane; o isi sa fusifusia i nonoa o lauulu o tagata; ma o le tasi e pei o se selesaito, ma se au tele e salu faataamilo e pei o le vaega na faia i le mutia fou na moaina e se moa ua faaumiumi. Na e gatete a'o e sioa, ma mafaufau pe o le a se manu mata'utia ma le fe'ai na mafai ona o atu i se seleselega oti ma se mea taufaaleaga, mata'utia. Ua fefiloi faatasi ma tao elea tuai fagota ma sapo na gaui uma ma faaletonu. O nisi o ni auupega faagaeeina. O lenei tao umi, lea ua matua tulilima, i le limasefulu tausaga talu ai na fasiotia ai e Nathan Swain tafola se sefululima i le va o le oso a'e o le la ma le goto o le la. Ma o lena harpoon—ua pei o se tui i le taimi nei—na lafo i le sami Iava, ma sola ese ai e se tafola, i tausaga mulimuli ane na fasiotia ai i luga o le Cape of Blanco. O le u'amea muamua na ulu latalata i le si'usi'u, ma, e pei o se nila le mapu o lo'o nofo i totonu o le tino o se tagata, na malaga atoa i le fasefulu futu, ma i'u ai ina maua i totonu o le pu.

O le sopoia atu o lenei auala pogisa, ma agai atu ai i le ala maualalo-o le tipi i le mea i aso anamua atonu o se alaasu tutotonu tele ma magalafu faataamilo uma-e te ulu atu i le potu lautele. O se nofoaga e sili atu le pogisa o lenei, faatasi ai ma ala maualalo maualalo i luga, ma ni laupapa maanuminumi tuai i lalo, e toetoe a e manaʻo e te solia ni paipa tuai o vaʻa, ae maise o se po tagi, ina ua luluina ma le ita tele lenei vaa tuai na taula i tulimanu. . I le tasi itu o loʻo tu ai se laulau umi, maualalo, pei o se fata e ufiufi i pusa tioata taʻe, e tumu i pefu seasea maua mai i tulimanu pito mamao o lenei lalolagi. O lo'o va'ai atu i le isi itu o le potu o lo'o tu ai se lua e foliga pogisa—le pa—o se taumafaiga fa'atauva'a i le ulu o se tafola taumatau. Po o le a lava le tulaga, o loo tu ai le ponaivi lautele faaofuofu o le auvae o le tafola, e matua lautele lava, e toetoe lava a alu ai se taavale solofanua i lalo. O totonu o lo'o iai fata leaga, fa'ata'amilomilo fa'atasi ma fagu tuai, fagu, fagu; ma i na auvae o le faatafunaga vave, e pei ona fetuuina e le isi Iona (lea na latou faaigoa ai o ia), o loo vevesi ai se toeaina matua mamae, o le, mo a latou tupe, na faatau atu ai ma le taugata i le auvaa le taufaaleaga ma le oti.

‘Ua ‘inosia fo‘i mea e sasaa i ai lona ‘ona. E ui ina fa'ato'a fa'ata'oto i fafo—i totonu, ae fa'a'ole'ole le matatioata matatio lanu meamata leaga i lalo i le pito i lalo ole kopi. O meridian tutusa e tutu fa'atauva'a i totonu o le tioata, fa'ata'amilo i ipu vaevae nei. Faatumu i lenei faailoga, ma o lou totogi e na o se pene; e sili atu i lenei se pene; ma faapena ai lava i le ipu tioata atoa—le fua Cape Horn, e mafai ona e folo i lalo mo le seleni.

Ina ua ou ulu atu i lea nofoaga, sa ou maua atu ai se numera o taulelea talavou o faapotopoto i luga o se laulau, ma su'esu'e e se moli mu'omo'o ituaiga eseese o skrimshander . Sa ou sailia le pule o le fale, ma ta’u atu ia te ia ou te fia nofo i se potu, ma maua mai le tali ua tumu lona fale—ae le o se moega e leai se nofo. “Ae o avast,” sa ia faaopoopo mai ai, ma patipati lona muaulu, “e te le i ai se tetee i le faasoaina atu o se palanikeke tautumu, a ea? Ou te fa'apea o le a e alu i se tafola, o lea e sili ai ona e masani i na ituaiga mea."

Sa ou fai atu ia te ia ou te le fiafia lava e momoe toalua i se moega; afai ou te faia, e faalagolago lava po o ai le tautuupu, ma afai o ia (le pule o le fale) e matua leai lava se isi nofoaga mo aʻu, ma e leʻi faʻafitia le faʻaupu, aisea nai lo le feoai solo i se mea ese. aai i se po oona, ou te onosaia le afa o le palanikeke a se tagata lelei.

“Sa ou manatu faapena. Ua lelei; nofo i se nofoa. Talisuaga?—e te mana'o i le afiafi? O le a saunia sa'o lava le taumafataga.”

Sa ou nofo i lalo i luga o se nofoa laupapa tuai, vaneina uma e pei o se nofoa i luga o le Maa. I le tasi itu, o lo'o toe teuteu atili ai e se ta fa'atamu i lana naifi toka, punou i luga ma galue ma le filiga i le va o ona vae. Sa taumafai o ia i se vaa i lalo o le folauga atoa, ae e leʻi tele se agaʻi i luma, sa ou mafaufau ai.

Mulimuli ane, pe ā ma le toʻafā pe toʻalima o i matou na valaaulia i la matou taumafataga i se potu e sosoo ai. Sa malulu e pei o Iceland—e leai lava se afi—na fai mai le pule o le fale e le gafatia. E leai se mea ae na'o ni moliga'o tallow leaga se lua, o lo'o i totonu o se laupepa taamilo. Sa matou fiafia e faamau i luga o matou peleue manuki, ma uu i o matou laugutu ipu lauti vevela ma o matou tamatamailima afa aisa. Ae o le pasese o se ituaiga sili ona taua—e le gata o aano o manufasi ma pateta, ae o dumplings; lagi lelei! dumplings mo le talisuaga! O se tasi o alii talavou i se peleue pusa lanu meamata, sa ia talanoa atu ia te ia lava i nei dumplings i se uiga sili ona leaga.

“Lo’u tama,” o le tala lea a le tagata e ona le fale, “o le a e maua se miti taufaafefe i se sainai oti.”

“Le pule o le fale,” sa ou musumusu atu ai, “e le o le tautuupu lena?”

“Oi, e leai,” o lana tala lea, e foliga mai o se uiga malie mataga, “o le tautuupu o se tagata lanu uliuli. E le 'ai lava o ia i dumplings, na te le'i ai—na te le 'ai i se mea ae na o steak, ma e seasea ona ia fiafia i ai."

“O le tiapolo na te faia,” o le tala lea a a'u. “O fea o i ai lena tautuupu? O iinei o ia?”

“E le'i leva ona i ai o ia iinei,” o le tali lea.

Sa le mafai ona ou taofia, ae na amata ona ou lagona le masalosalo i lenei tagata "pouliuli" harpooneer. Po o le a lava le tulaga, sa tonu i lo'u mafaufau afai e oo mai e tatau ona ma momoe faatasi, e tatau ona ia tatala ofu ma moe ae ou te lei moe.

Ina ua uma le taumafataga o le afiafi, na toe foi atu le kamupani i le potu pa, ae ou te le iloa po o le a se isi mea e fai ia te au lava ia, na ou filifili ai e faaalu le afiafi atoa e matamata ai.

O le taimi nei na faalogoina ai le leo o le vevesi. Ina ua amata, sa tagi le pule o le fale, “O le auvaa lena a le Grampus. Na ou vaai ia te ia na lipotia mai i le taeao nei; o le malaga e tolu tausaga, ma le vaa atoa. Hura, tama; o lea o le a matou maua tala fou mai le Feegees.”

Na fa'alogoina le taia o seevae o le sami i totonu; na matala le faitotoa, ma ua lava ma totoe se seti vao o tautai. Ua ufitia i o latou ofu talaloa mata'utia, ma ua pupuni o latou ulu i ofu mafanafana fulufulu mamoe, ua pala uma ma masaesae, ma o latou 'ava ua malo i aisa, ua foliga mai o se urosa mai Labrador. Na faatoʻā tulaʻi mai la latou vaa, ma o le fale muamua lea na latou ulu atu i ai. E leitioa la, ona latou fafagu sa'o atu i le gutu o le tafola—le pa—ina ua le pine ae sasaa atu e Iona la'itiiti maanuminumi, o loo fai le sauniga, ia i latou i le gutu atoa. Na faitio se tasi i le malulu leaga i lona ulu, lea na palu ai e Iona ia te ia se vai inu e pei o le pitch o gin ma molasses, lea na ia tauto ai o se vaifofo sili mo le malulu ma le maʻi uma soo se mea, e le afaina pe o le a le umi e tu ai, pe maua mai. le talafatai o Labrador, poʻo luga o le itu o le tau o se motu aisa.

E le'i umi ae oo atu le ava io latou ulu, e pei ona masani ai e oo lava i topers fa'ato'a tulaueleele mai le sami, ma amata ai loa ona latou fa'ata'ata'i i le mea e sili ona leaga.

Ae peitai, sa ou matauina, o se tasi o i latou sa fai si taumamao, ma e ui ina sa foliga mai sa ia manao e aua nei faaleagaina le fiafia o ana uo i ona lava foliga vaaia, ae i lona tulaga atoa sa ia taofia mai le pisapisao tele e pei o isi. Na fiafia mai lenei tagata ia te au i le taimi lava lena; ma talu ai ona ua faauuina e atua o le sami e le o toe umi ona avea o ia ma a’u soa vaa (e ui lava o se paaga momoe, e tusa ai ma lenei tala), o le a ou taumafai ai iinei i se faamatalaga itiiti e uiga ia te ia. Sa tu o ia i le ono futu le maualuga, ma tauau mamalu, ma se fatafata e pei o se pusa-faatanoa. E seasea ona ou vaai i se tagata faapena. O ona foliga e matua'i lanu enaena ma mu, ma pupula ai ona nifo sinasina i le eseesega; a'o opeopea i lalo ifo o ona mata nisi o manatuaga e foliga mai e le'i maua ai se fiafia tele. O lona siufofoga i le taimi lava lena na faailoa mai ai o ia o se tagata i Saute, ma mai lona tino aulelei, na ou manatu ai o ia o se tasi o na tagata aʻe mauga maualuga mai le Alleghanian Ridge i Virginia. Ina ua oo ina maualuga le fiafia o ana soa, na se'e ese lenei tagata e aunoa ma se iloa, ma sa ou le toe vaai ia te ia seia oo ina avea o ia ma a'u uo i luga o le sami. Ae peitai, i ni nai minute, na misia o ia e ana uo, ma o le avea ai, e foliga mai, mo se mafuaaga e sili ona fiafia i ai i latou, na latou alalaga ai “Bulkington! Bulkington! o fea o iai Bulkington?” ma oso atu i fafo o le fale e tuliloa ia te ia.

It was now about nine o'clock, and the room seeming almost supernaturally quiet after these orgies, I began to congratulate myself upon a little plan that had occurred to me just previous to the entrance of the seamen.

No man prefers to sleep two in a bed. In fact, you would a good deal rather not sleep with your own brother. I don't know how it is, but people like to be private when they are sleeping. And when it comes to sleeping with an unknown stranger, in a strange inn, in a strange town, and that stranger a harpooneer, then your objections indefinitely multiply. Nor was there any earthly reason why I as a sailor should sleep two in a bed, more than anybody else; for sailors no more sleep two in a bed at sea, than bachelor Kings do ashore. To be sure they all sleep together in one apartment, but you have your own hammock, and cover yourself with your own blanket, and sleep in your own skin.

The more I pondered over this harpooneer, the more I abominated the thought of sleeping with him. It was fair to presume that being a harpooneer, his linen or woollen, as the case might be, would not be of the tidiest, certainly none of the finest. I began to twitch all over. Besides, it was getting late, and my decent harpooneer ought to be home and going bedwards. Suppose now, he should tumble in upon me at midnight—how could I tell from what vile hole he had been coming?

“Landlord! I've changed my mind about that harpooneer.—I shan't sleep with him. I'll try the bench here.”

“Just as you please; I'm sorry I can't spare ye a tablecloth for a mattress, and it's a plaguy rough board here”—feeling of the knots and notches. “But wait a bit, Skrimshander; I've got a carpenter's plane there in the bar—wait, I say, and I'll make ye snug enough.” So saying he procured the plane; and with his old silk handkerchief first dusting the bench, vigorously set to planing away at my bed, the while grinning like an ape. The shavings flew right and left; till at last the plane-iron came bump against an indestructible knot. The landlord was near spraining his wrist, and I told him for heaven's sake to quit—the bed was soft enough to suit me, and I did not know how all the planing in the world could make eider down of a pine plank. So gathering up the shavings with another grin, and throwing them into the great stove in the middle of the room, he went about his business, and left me in a brown study.

I now took the measure of the bench, and found that it was a foot too short; but that could be mended with a chair. But it was a foot too narrow, and the other bench in the room was about four inches higher than the planed one—so there was no yoking them. I then placed the first bench lengthwise along the only clear space against the wall, leaving a little interval between, for my back to settle down in. But I soon found that there came such a draught of cold air over me from under the sill of the window, that this plan would never do at all, especially as another current from the rickety door met the one from the window, and both together formed a series of small whirlwinds in the immediate vicinity of the spot where I had thought to spend the night.

The devil fetch that harpooneer, thought I, but stop, couldn't I steal a march on him—bolt his door inside, and jump into his bed, not to be wakened by the most violent knockings? It seemed no bad idea; but upon second thoughts I dismissed it. For who could tell but what the next morning, so soon as I popped out of the room, the harpooneer might be standing in the entry, all ready to knock me down!

Still, looking round me again, and seeing no possible chance of spending a sufferable night unless in some other person's bed, I began to think that after all I might be cherishing unwarrantable prejudices against this unknown harpooneer. Thinks I, I'll wait awhile; he must be dropping in before long. I'll have a good look at him then, and perhaps we may become jolly good bedfellows after all—there's no telling.

But though the other boarders kept coming in by ones, twos, and threes, and going to bed, yet no sign of my harpooneer.

“Landlord!” said I, “what sort of a chap is he—does he always keep such late hours?” It was now hard upon twelve o'clock.

The landlord chuckled again with his lean chuckle, and seemed to be mightily tickled at something beyond my comprehension. “No,” he answered, “generally he's an early bird—airley to bed and airley to rise—yes, he's the bird what catches the worm. But to-night he went out a peddling, you see, and I don't see what on airth keeps him so late, unless, may be, he can't sell his head.”

“Can't sell his head?—What sort of a bamboozingly story is this you are telling me?” getting into a towering rage. “Do you pretend to say, landlord, that this harpooneer is actually engaged this blessed Saturday night, or rather Sunday morning, in peddling his head around this town?”

“That's precisely it,” said the landlord, “and I told him he couldn't sell it here, the market's overstocked.”

“With what?” shouted I.

“With heads to be sure; ain't there too many heads in the world?”

“I tell you what it is, landlord,” said I quite calmly, “you'd better stop spinning that yarn to me—I'm not green.”

“May be not,” taking out a stick and whittling a toothpick, “but I rayther guess you'll be done brown if that ere harpooneer hears you a slanderin' his head.”

“I'll break it for him,” said I, now flying into a passion again at this unaccountable farrago of the landlord's.

“It's broke a'ready,” said he.

“Broke,” said I—“ broke , do you mean?”

“Sartain, and that's the very reason he can't sell it, I guess.”

“Landlord,” said I, going up to him as cool as Mt. Hecla in a snow-storm—“landlord, stop whittling. You and I must understand one another, and that too without delay. I come to your house and want a bed; you tell me you can only give me half a one; that the other half belongs to a certain harpooneer. And about this harpooneer, whom I have not yet seen, you persist in telling me the most mystifying and exasperating stories tending to beget in me an uncomfortable feeling towards the man whom you design for my bedfellow—a sort of connexion, landlord, which is an intimate and confidential one in the highest degree. I now demand of you to speak out and tell me who and what this harpooneer is, and whether I shall be in all respects safe to spend the night with him. And in the first place, you will be so good as to unsay that story about selling his head, which if true I take to be good evidence that this harpooneer is stark mad, and I've no idea of sleeping with a madman; and you, sir, you I mean, landlord, you , sir, by trying to induce me to do so knowingly, would thereby render yourself liable to a criminal prosecution.”

“Wall,” said the landlord, fetching a long breath, “that's a purty long sarmon for a chap that rips a little now and then. But be easy, be easy, this here harpooneer I have been tellin' you of has just arrived from the south seas, where he bought up a lot of 'balmed New Zealand heads (great curios, you know), and he's sold all on 'em but one, and that one he's trying to sell to-night, cause to-morrow's Sunday, and it would not do to be sellin' human heads about the streets when folks is goin' to churches. He wanted to, last Sunday, but I stopped him just as he was goin' out of the door with four heads strung on a string, for all the airth like a string of inions.”

This account cleared up the otherwise unaccountable mystery, and showed that the landlord, after all, had had no idea of fooling me—but at the same time what could I think of a harpooneer who stayed out of a Saturday night clean into the holy Sabbath, engaged in such a cannibal business as selling the heads of dead idolators?

“Depend upon it, landlord, that harpooneer is a dangerous man.”

“He pays reg'lar,” was the rejoinder. “But come, it's getting dreadful late, you had better be turning flukes—it's a nice bed; Sal and me slept in that ere bed the night we were spliced. There's plenty of room for two to kick about in that bed; it's an almighty big bed that. Why, afore we give it up, Sal used to put our Sam and little Johnny in the foot of it. But I got a dreaming and sprawling about one night, and somehow, Sam got pitched on the floor, and came near breaking his arm. Arter that, Sal said it wouldn't do. Come along here, I'll give ye a glim in a jiffy;” and so saying he lighted a candle and held it towards me, offering to lead the way. But I stood irresolute; when looking at a clock in the corner, he exclaimed “I vum it's Sunday—you won't see that harpooneer to-night; he's come to anchor somewhere—come along then; do come; won't ye come?”

I considered the matter a moment, and then up stairs we went, and I was ushered into a small room, cold as a clam, and furnished, sure enough, with a prodigious bed, almost big enough indeed for any four harpooneers to sleep abreast.

“There,” said the landlord, placing the candle on a crazy old sea chest that did double duty as a wash-stand and centre table; “there, make yourself comfortable now, and good night to ye.” I turned round from eyeing the bed, but he had disappeared.

Folding back the counterpane, I stooped over the bed. Though none of the most elegant, it yet stood the scrutiny tolerably well. I then glanced round the room; and besides the bedstead and centre table, could see no other furniture belonging to the place, but a rude shelf, the four walls, and a papered fireboard representing a man striking a whale. Of things not properly belonging to the room, there was a hammock lashed up, and thrown upon the floor in one corner; also a large seaman's bag, containing the harpooneer's wardrobe, no doubt in lieu of a land trunk. Likewise, there was a parcel of outlandish bone fish hooks on the shelf over the fire-place, and a tall harpoon standing at the head of the bed.

But what is this on the chest? I took it up, and held it close to the light, and felt it, and smelt it, and tried every way possible to arrive at some satisfactory conclusion concerning it. I can compare it to nothing but a large door mat, ornamented at the edges with little tinkling tags something like the stained porcupine quills round an Indian moccasin. There was a hole or slit in the middle of this mat, as you see the same in South American ponchos. But could it be possible that any sober harpooneer would get into a door mat, and parade the streets of any Christian town in that sort of guise? I put it on, to try it, and it weighed me down like a hamper, being uncommonly shaggy and thick, and I thought a little damp, as though this mysterious harpooneer had been wearing it of a rainy day. I went up in it to a bit of glass stuck against the wall, and I never saw such a sight in my life. I tore myself out of it in such a hurry that I gave myself a kink in the neck.

I sat down on the side of the bed, and commenced thinking about this head-peddling harpooneer, and his door mat. After thinking some time on the bed-side, I got up and took off my monkey jacket, and then stood in the middle of the room thinking. I then took off my coat, and thought a little more in my shirt sleeves. But beginning to feel very cold now, half undressed as I was, and remembering what the landlord said about the harpooneer's not coming home at all that night, it being so very late, I made no more ado, but jumped out of my pantaloons and boots, and then blowing out the light tumbled into bed, and commended myself to the care of heaven.

Whether that mattress was stuffed with corn-cobs or broken crockery, there is no telling, but I rolled about a good deal, and could not sleep for a long time. At last I slid off into a light doze, and had pretty nearly made a good offing towards the land of Nod, when I heard a heavy footfall in the passage, and saw a glimmer of light come into the room from under the door.

Lord save me, thinks I, that must be the harpooneer, the infernal head-peddler. But I lay perfectly still, and resolved not to say a word till spoken to. Holding a light in one hand, and that identical New Zealand head in the other, the stranger entered the room, and without looking towards the bed, placed his candle a good way off from me on the floor in one corner, and then began working away at the knotted cords of the large bag I before spoke of as being in the room. I was all eagerness to see his face, but he kept it averted for some time while employed in unlacing the bag's mouth. This accomplished, however, he turned round—when, good heavens! what a sight! Such a face! It was of a dark, purplish, yellow colour, here and there stuck over with large blackish looking squares. Yes, it's just as I thought, he's a terrible bedfellow; he's been in a fight, got dreadfully cut, and here he is, just from the surgeon. But at that moment he chanced to turn his face so towards the light, that I plainly saw they could not be sticking-plasters at all, those black squares on his cheeks. They were stains of some sort or other. At first I knew not what to make of this; but soon an inkling of the truth occurred to me. I remembered a story of a white man—a whaleman too—who, falling among the cannibals, had been tattooed by them. I concluded that this harpooneer, in the course of his distant voyages, must have met with a similar adventure. And what is it, thought I, after all! It's only his outside; a man can be honest in any sort of skin. But then, what to make of his unearthly complexion, that part of it, I mean, lying round about, and completely independent of the squares of tattooing. To be sure, it might be nothing but a good coat of tropical tanning; but I never heard of a hot sun's tanning a white man into a purplish yellow one. However, I had never been in the South Seas; and perhaps the sun there produced these extraordinary effects upon the skin. Now, while all these ideas were passing through me like lightning, this harpooneer never noticed me at all. But, after some difficulty having opened his bag, he commenced fumbling in it, and presently pulled out a sort of tomahawk, and a seal-skin wallet with the hair on. Placing these on the old chest in the middle of the room, he then took the New Zealand head—a ghastly thing enough—and crammed it down into the bag. He now took off his hat—a new beaver hat—when I came nigh singing out with fresh surprise. There was no hair on his head—none to speak of at least—nothing but a small scalp-knot twisted up on his forehead. His bald purplish head now looked for all the world like a mildewed skull. Had not the stranger stood between me and the door, I would have bolted out of it quicker than ever I bolted a dinner.

Even as it was, I thought something of slipping out of the window, but it was the second floor back. I am no coward, but what to make of this head-peddling purple rascal altogether passed my comprehension. Ignorance is the parent of fear, and being completely nonplussed and confounded about the stranger, I confess I was now as much afraid of him as if it was the devil himself who had thus broken into my room at the dead of night. In fact, I was so afraid of him that I was not game enough just then to address him, and demand a satisfactory answer concerning what seemed inexplicable in him.

Meanwhile, he continued the business of undressing, and at last showed his chest and arms. As I live, these covered parts of him were checkered with the same squares as his face; his back, too, was all over the same dark squares; he seemed to have been in a Thirty Years' War, and just escaped from it with a sticking-plaster shirt. Still more, his very legs were marked, as if a parcel of dark green frogs were running up the trunks of young palms. It was now quite plain that he must be some abominable savage or other shipped aboard of a whaleman in the South Seas, and so landed in this Christian country. I quaked to think of it. A peddler of heads too—perhaps the heads of his own brothers. He might take a fancy to mine—heavens! look at that tomahawk!

But there was no time for shuddering, for now the savage went about something that completely fascinated my attention, and convinced me that he must indeed be a heathen. Going to his heavy grego, or wrapall, or dreadnaught, which he had previously hung on a chair, he fumbled in the pockets, and produced at length a curious little deformed image with a hunch on its back, and exactly the colour of a three days' old Congo baby. Remembering the embalmed head, at first I almost thought that this black manikin was a real baby preserved in some similar manner. But seeing that it was not at all limber, and that it glistened a good deal like polished ebony, I concluded that it must be nothing but a wooden idol, which indeed it proved to be. For now the savage goes up to the empty fire-place, and removing the papered fire-board, sets up this little hunch-backed image, like a tenpin, between the andirons. The chimney jambs and all the bricks inside were very sooty, so that I thought this fire-place made a very appropriate little shrine or chapel for his Congo idol.

I now screwed my eyes hard towards the half hidden image, feeling but ill at ease meantime—to see what was next to follow. First he takes about a double handful of shavings out of his grego pocket, and places them carefully before the idol; then laying a bit of ship biscuit on top and applying the flame from the lamp, he kindled the shavings into a sacrificial blaze. Presently, after many hasty snatches into the fire, and still hastier withdrawals of his fingers (whereby he seemed to be scorching them badly), he at last succeeded in drawing out the biscuit; then blowing off the heat and ashes a little, he made a polite offer of it to the little negro. But the little devil did not seem to fancy such dry sort of fare at all; he never moved his lips. All these strange antics were accompanied by still stranger guttural noises from the devotee, who seemed to be praying in a sing-song or else singing some pagan psalmody or other, during which his face twitched about in the most unnatural manner. At last extinguishing the fire, he took the idol up very unceremoniously, and bagged it again in his grego pocket as carelessly as if he were a sportsman bagging a dead woodcock.

All these queer proceedings increased my uncomfortableness, and seeing him now exhibiting strong symptoms of concluding his business operations, and jumping into bed with me, I thought it was high time, now or never, before the light was put out, to break the spell in which I had so long been bound.

But the interval I spent in deliberating what to say, was a fatal one. Taking up his tomahawk from the table, he examined the head of it for an instant, and then holding it to the light, with his mouth at the handle, he puffed out great clouds of tobacco smoke. The next moment the light was extinguished, and this wild cannibal, tomahawk between his teeth, sprang into bed with me. I sang out, I could not help it now; and giving a sudden grunt of astonishment he began feeling me.

Stammering out something, I knew not what, I rolled away from him against the wall, and then conjured him, whoever or whatever he might be, to keep quiet, and let me get up and light the lamp again. But his guttural responses satisfied me at once that he but ill comprehended my meaning.

“Who-e debel you?”—he at last said—“you no speak-e, dam-me, I kill-e.” And so saying the lighted tomahawk began flourishing about me in the dark.

“Landlord, for God's sake, Peter Coffin!” shouted I. “Landlord! Watch! Coffin! Angels! save me!”

“Speak-e! tell-ee me who-ee be, or dam-me, I kill-e!” again growled the cannibal, while his horrid flourishings of the tomahawk scattered the hot tobacco ashes about me till I thought my linen would get on fire. But thank heaven, at that moment the landlord came into the room light in hand, and leaping from the bed I ran up to him.

“Don't be afraid now,” said he, grinning again, “Queequeg here wouldn't harm a hair of your head.”

“Stop your grinning,” shouted I, “and why didn't you tell me that that infernal harpooneer was a cannibal?”

“I thought ye know'd it;—didn't I tell ye, he was a peddlin' heads around town?—but turn flukes again and go to sleep. Queequeg, look here—you sabbee me, I sabbee—you this man sleepe you—you sabbee?”

“Me sabbee plenty”—grunted Queequeg, puffing away at his pipe and sitting up in bed.

“You gettee in,” he added, motioning to me with his tomahawk, and throwing the clothes to one side. He really did this in not only a civil but a really kind and charitable way. I stood looking at him a moment. For all his tattooings he was on the whole a clean, comely looking cannibal. What's all this fuss I have been making about, thought I to myself—the man's a human being just as I am: he has just as much reason to fear me, as I have to be afraid of him. Better sleep with a sober cannibal than a drunken Christian.

“Landlord,” said I, “tell him to stash his tomahawk there, or pipe, or whatever you call it; tell him to stop smoking, in short, and I will turn in with him. But I don't fancy having a man smoking in bed with me. It's dangerous. Besides, I ain't insured.”

This being told to Queequeg, he at once complied, and again politely motioned me to get into bed—rolling over to one side as much as to say—“I won't touch a leg of ye.”

“Good night, landlord,” said I, “you may go.”

I turned in, and never slept better in my life.
