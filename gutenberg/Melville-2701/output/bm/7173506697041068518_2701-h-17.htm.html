SABABU 86. A ka kùnkolo.

Poyikɛla wɛrɛw ye wuluwulu ɲɛ nɔgɔlen tanuli fɔ, ani kɔnɔnin min tɛ jigin abada, o ɲɛda kanulenw; less celestial, n bɛ seli kɛ wulu dɔ la.

Ni an y’a jate Minɛ ko Balema-kɔnɔ-fɛn min bonya ka bon kosɛbɛ k’a da Minɛ jirisunba yɔrɔ la a bɛ Dɔgɔya fo ka Se cɛ lamini ma, a bɛ Kɛ a sanfɛla dɔrɔn kan, a yɔrɔ min bɛ Se mɛtɛrɛ kɛrɛnkɛrɛnnen bi duuru ma a dɔgɔyalenba la. A ju farikolo lamininen min bɛ ɲɔgɔn kan, o bɛ bonya ka kɛ bolokɔni fila ye minnu janya ka bon, minnu ka gɛlɛn, minnu ka fla, u bɛ wuli dɔɔni dɔɔni fo ka se santimɛtɛrɛ kelen ma. Kɔnɔbara walima ɲɔgɔndan na, o flukew bɛ ɲɔgɔn Da ɲɔgɔn kan dɔɔnin, o kɔ fɛ u bɛ kɔsegin ɲɔgɔn fɛ kɛrɛfɛ i n’a fɔ wuluw, ka yɔrɔba dɔ To u ni ɲɔgɔn cɛ. Fɛn ɲɛnama si la, cɛɲi siraw ɲɛfɔlen don ka ɲɛ ka tɛmɛ o fɛnɲɛnɛmaw dancɛw kan. A bonya laban na balema dafalen na, a kùnkolo bɛna Tɛmɛ kosɛbɛ mɛtɛrɛ mugan kan.

O tɔnden bɛɛ bɛ i n’a fɔ a ye dilan dense ye min bɛ wele ko webbed bed of welded sinews; nka tigɛ a kɔnɔ, i b’a ye ko sɛrɛ saba danfaralenw b’a da:—sanfɛ, cɛmancɛ ani duguma. Fɛnɲɛnɛma minnu bɛ sanfɛla ni duguma-dakun na, olu ka jan ani u bɛ jɛgɛn; cɛmancɛ ta ta, u ka surun kosɛbɛ, wa u bɛ boli ka kɛɲɛ ni kɛnɛma layiniw ni ɲɔgɔn cɛ. O sabatilen in, i n’a fɔ fɛn tɔw bɛɛ, o bɛ fanga Di kɔ ma. Romɛkaw ka kogo kɔrɔw kalanden fɛ, cɛmancɛ-yɔrɔ bɛna ɲɔgɔndɛmɛ kabakoma dɔ di ni tilew sira finman ye min bɛ ɲɔgɔn falen-falen tuma bɛɛ ni kabakurun ye o fɛn kɔrɔw kabakomaw kɔnɔ, wa siga t’a la ko olu bɛ dɔ fara kosɛbɛ kabakurun dilanni fanga bonya kan.

Nka i n’a fɔ nin sigida fanga belebele in min bɛ tendinous tail kɔnɔ, o ma se, leviathan fanba bɛɛ sirilen bɛ ni warp ni woof ye min bɛ kɛ ni muscle fibres ni filaments ye, minnu bɛ tɛmɛ kɔkolo fan fila bɛɛ fɛ ka boli ka jigin ka don flukes kɔnɔ, k’a sɔrɔ u ma faamuya ka fara u kan, ka dɛmɛba don u fanga la; walasa a kɔ la, balema bɛɛ fanga sumanikɛbali min bɛ ɲɔgɔn sɔrɔ, o bɛ i n’a fɔ a bɛ ɲɔgɔn sɔrɔ fo ka se yɔrɔ dɔ ma. Yala halakili bɛ se ka kɛ ka kɛ fɛn ye, nin tun ye fɛn ye min ka kan k’a kɛ.

O fana tɛ—a fanga kabakoma, hali dɔɔnin, o b’a fɛ k’a lamaga-lamagaliw kɔrɔtacogo ɲuman nalo; yɔrɔ min na nɔgɔya denmisɛnninya bɛ wuli ka tɛmɛn fanga Titanisme dɔ fɛ. O kɔfɛ, o lamaga-lamagaliw bɛ u cɛɲi siranninba sɔrɔ o de la. Fanga lakika tɛ cɛɲi walima bɛnkan tiɲɛ abada, nka tuma caman na, a bɛ o di; ani fɛn o fɛn cɛ ka ɲi kosɛbɛ, fanga bɛ caman kɛ ni subagaya ye. Aw ye sɔgɔsɔgɔninjɛ sirilenw bɔ yen, a bɛ iko u bɛɛ bɛ pɛrɛn ka bɔ marbre la min bɛ Hercules jalen kɔnɔ, o la, a cɛɲi tun bɛna ban. Eckerman min tun bɛ bato kɛ kosɛbɛ, o tun bɛ ka finimugu kɔrɔta ka bɔ Goethe su farilankolon na tuma min na, o cɛ disi belebeleba ye a degun, o min tun bɛ iko Romɛkaw ka se sɔrɔli jirisunba. Ni Angelo bɛ hali Fa Ala ja hadamaden cogo la, i ka taamasiyɛn kɛ barika min bɛ yen. Wa u bɛ se ka fɛn o fɛn jira Ala ka kanuya la Denkɛ kɔnɔ, Itali ja nɔgɔlenw, kurulen, cɛɲɔgɔnɲini, a hakilina kɛra minnu kɔnɔ ka ɲɛ kosɛbɛ; nin ja ninnu, hali n’u ka fanga bɛɛ dɛsɛlen don, u tɛ fanga si jira, nka musoya jugu dɔrɔn de don, n’o ye kololi ni muɲuli ye, min bɛ sɔn a ma bolo bɛɛ fɛ, o de bɛ a ka kalansiraw kɛcogo ɲuman danmadɔw kɛ.

Ne bɛ farikolo yɔrɔ min furakɛ, o ka sɔgɔsɔgɔninjɛ nɔgɔlen ye ten, fo a kɛra farikoloɲɛnajɛ la wo, walima ni timinandiya ye wo, walima dimi la wo, a kɛra dusukunnata suguya o suguya ye, a jiginniw bɛ taamasiyɛn kɛ tuma bɛɛ ni nɛɛma tɛmɛnen ye. O kɔnɔ, nsiirin bolo si tɛ se ka tɛmɛ a kan.

Lamaga-lamagaliba duuru ye a danma ye. A fɔlɔ, ni a kɛra fin ye walasa ka ɲɛtaa sɔrɔ; Filanan, ni a kɛra mace ye kɛlɛ la; Sabanan, bɔgɔdaga la; Naaninan, lobtailing (lobtailing) la; Duurunan, a bɛ kɛ peaking flukes kɔnɔ.

Fɔlɔ: Ikomi Leviatan kɔ bɛ jɔ a jɔyɔrɔ la, a bɛ baara kɛ cogo wɛrɛ la ni kɔgɔji kɔnɔfɛn tɔw bɛɛ kɔ ye. A tɛ wuli abada. Cɛ walima jɛgɛ la, sɔgɔsɔgɔninjɛ ye dɔgɔya taamasyɛn ye. Balema fɛ, a kùnkolo kelenpe de b'a Laɲini. Scroll-wise coiled forwards beneath the body, ka sɔrɔ ka teliya ka sprung kɔ, o de bɛ o darting kelen-kelen, panni lamaga di waraba ma ni a bɛ naji kɛ ni dimi ye. A kɛrɛfɛ-fiɲɛw bɛ Kɛ dɔrɔn ka bolifɛn Tɛmɛ.

Filanan: A nafa ka bon dɔɔnin, ko hali n’a y’a sɔrɔ ko balema cɛya kelen bɛ balema cɛya wɛrɛ kɛlɛ dɔrɔn n’a kunkolo n’a dawolo ye, o bɛɛ n’a ta, a ni hadamaden ka kɛlɛw la, a bɛ baara kɛ n’a kɔ ye kosɛbɛ ani ni danbe ye. Kurun dɔ gosi la, a b’a ka flukew kuru joona ka bɔ a la, wa o gosili bɛ kɛ kɔsegin dɔrɔn de fɛ. Ni a dilannen don fiɲɛ na min ma bali, kɛrɛnkɛrɛnnenya la n’a jigira ka se a taamasiɲɛ ma, o tuma na fɛ, kirinni tɛ se ka bali dɔrɔn. Mɔgɔ walima kurun si tɛ se k’o muɲu. I ka kisili kelenpe de bɛ i yɛrɛ tanga o ma; nka n’a nana kɛrɛ fɛ ji kɛrɛfɛ, o tuma na, a yɔrɔ dɔ bɛ bɔ balema-kurun ka yeelen wulicogo la, ani a fɛnw ka wulicogo la, kɔkili karilen walima pankurun tigɛlen walima fila, sɔgɔli sugu dɔ kɛrɛ fɛ, o bɛ kɛ caman na o nɔ min ka jugu kosɛbɛ. O kɛrɛfɛ-bɔgɔdaga minnu bɛ ji la, olu bɛ Sɔrɔ tuma caman na mɔni na, fo u bɛ Jate denmisɛnninw ka tulonkɛ dɔrɔn ye. Dɔ bɛ fini dɔ bɔ, ani dingɛ bɛ jɔ.

Sabanan : N tɛ se k’a jira, nka a bɛ iko n bolo, ko balema na, magali hakilina bɛ ɲɔgɔn sɔrɔ a kɔ la; sabu o siratigɛ la, dumuni duman dɔ bɛ a kɔnɔ min bɛ bɛn waraba jiribolo duman dɔrɔn ma. O dumuni duman in bɛ ye kosɛbɛ sɛgɛsɛgɛli kɛcogo la, n’o ye sunguru dususuman na, balema ni sumaya nɔgɔman dɔ b’a ka bɔgɔdaga belebelebaw lamaga ka bɔ fan dɔ la ka taa fan wɛrɛ fɛ kɔgɔji sanfɛ ani n'a y'a ye ko kurunbolila kunsigi dɔrɔn don, bɔnɛ bɛ o kuruntigi, wuluwuluw ni bɛɛ. O magan fɔlɔ in na, dususuma don dɛ! Ni nin kɔ in tun bɛ ni fanga ye min bɛ se ka minɛ, ne tun ka kan ka n miiri o yɔrɔnin bɛɛ la Darmonodes ka waraba la min tun bɛ to ka taa falenfɛnw feereyɔrɔ la ten, ani ni foli dɔgɔmanw ye ka nuguw di npogotigininw ma, ka sɔrɔ k’u ka zonew lamaga. Jatebla caman kan ka tɛmɛ kelen kan, makari don ko balema tɛ o jogo ɲuman minɛcogo in sɔrɔ a kɔ la; sabu ne ye waraba wɛrɛ ko mɛn, n’a jogin na kɛlɛ la, a y’a kuru k’a ka jiribolo lamini ka nɛgɛso bɔ.

Naaninan: Ka sonyali kɛ balema kan k’a sɔrɔ i ma a dɔn kɔgɔji kelenw cɛmancɛ lakanani miirilen na, i b’a sɔrɔ a ma biri a ka bonya farikoloba la, wa a bɛ i n’a fɔ wuludennin, a bɛ tulon kɛ kɔgɔji kan i n’a fɔ tasumabɔyɔrɔ. Nka hali bi i b'a ka fanga Ye a ka tulonkɛ la. A kùnkolo-yɔrɔ janw bɛ 'sèn Bɔ fiɲɛ na ka Taa san fɛ; o kɔfɛ, ka dugukolo gosi, sanpɛrɛn bɛ mankan bɔ kilomɛtɛrɛ caman kɔnɔ. I bɛna a miiri ko marifaba dɔ bɔra; ani n’i ye lafiɲɛbɔlan nɔgɔlen kɔlɔsi min bɛ bɔ jirisunba la a dakun tɔ kelen na, i b’a miiri ko o de ye sisi ye min bɛ bɔ maga-maga-dingɛ kɔnɔ.

Duurunan: I n’a fɔ leviatan jɔcogo gansan na, flukew bɛ da a kɔ hakɛ jukɔrɔ kosɛbɛ, o kɔfɛ, u tɛ ye pewu a sanfɛla jukɔrɔ; nka n’a bɛ ɲini ka Dòn ‘kɔnɔna na, a ka bɔgɔdaga bɛɛ n’a farikolo mɛtɛrɛ bisaba ni kɔ bɛ Dòn fiɲɛ na, wa o cogo la, u bɛ To ka Yɛrɛyɛrɛ dɔɔnin, fo k’u Jigin ka Fɔ ka Bɔ mɔgɔw ɲɛ na. N’a ma fɔ o tiɲɛniba ye —yɔrɔ wɛrɛ la min bɛ se ka ɲɛfɔ — o balema ka bɔgɔbɔgɔli kuncɛlen in ye laala yecogo belebeleba ye min bɛ ye fɛnɲɛnamaw bɛɛ la. Ka bɔ dugukolo jugumanw na minnu tɛ duguma, a bɛ iko a kɔba in bɛ ka sɔgɔsɔgɔ sankolo sanfɛla la. O la sa, sugo la, yala ne ye Sitanɛ belebeleba ye k’a ka nɛgɛ belebeleba tɔɔrɔlen bɔ ka bɔ Jahannama Baltik tasuma na wa. Nka o ɲɛnajɛ suguw filɛli la, i bɛ cogo min na, o bɛɛ bɛ kɛ; ni dankan na , jinɛw bɛna na i hakili la ; ni Esayi ta la, mɛlɛkɛ kuntigiw. N jɔlen n ka kurun kuncɛ la tilebɔ waati la min ye sankolo ni kɔgɔji bilen, n ye balema kuluba dɔ ye siɲɛ kelen kɔrɔn fɛ, u bɛɛ bɛ ka taa tile fan fɛ, wa waati dɔɔnin kɔnɔ u bɛ yɛrɛyɛrɛ ɲɔgɔn fɛ ni flukes kuncɛlenw ye. I n’a fɔ a tun bɛ ne ɲɛ na o waati la cogo min na, o ala batocogo belebeleba sugu ma ye abada, hali Pɛrɛsi jamana na, tasuma batobagaw ka so. I n’a fɔ Ptolémée Filopater ye seereya kɛ cogo min na Afiriki waraba ko la, o kɔfɛ, ne ye seereya kɛ balema in ko la, k’a fɔ ko a ye fɛn ye min bɛ Ala bato ka tɛmɛ danfɛnw bɛɛ kan. Sabu masakɛ Juba ka fɔ la, kɔrɔlen sɔrɔdasi waraw tun bɛ to ka sɔgɔma tanu n’u ka jirisunw kɔrɔtalen ye makun juguba kɔnɔ.

Nin tilayɔrɔba in kɔnɔ, balema ni waraba cɛ, fo ka se kelen kɔ ni tɔ kelen kɔkolo fan dɔw ma, o man kan ka kɛ sababu ye k’o farikolo yɔrɔ fila minnu bɛ ɲɔgɔn sɔsɔ, olu bila bɛnkan kan, a ka dɔgɔ kosɛbɛ danfɛn minnu bɛ u kelen-kelen bɛɛ la. Sabu, i n’a fɔ waraba barikamaba ye Leviatan ka wuluwulu ye cogo min na, o cogo kelen na, n’i y’a suma ni Leviatan kɔ ye, a jiribolo ye lili ye dɔrɔn. Gosili min ka jugu kosɛbɛ ka bɔ waraba ka jiribolo la, o kɛra i n’a fɔ fiɲɛbɔlan tulonkɛ-pompi, n’i y’a suma ni balema-sɔgɔsɔgɔninjɛ ka fɛn gɛlɛnw sɔgɔsɔgɔli ni u bincogo ye min tɛ se ka suma, minnu bɛ segin-ka-bɔnyew kɛ, minnu bɛ segin-ka-bɔnye kɛ, kelen kɔfɛ, u bɛ kurun kuuru bɛɛ fili u kɔnɔ n’u ka kurunw n’u ka baarakɛlaw bɛɛ ye fiɲɛ, kosɛbɛ i n’a fɔ Ɛndu jugler dɔ b’a ka bolow wuli cogo min na.*

*Hali n’a y’a sɔrɔ ko ɲɔgɔndan bɛɛ bɛ kɛ balema ni waraba cɛ camanba siratigɛ la, o ye fɛn ye min tɛ fosi ye, bawo o kɛrɛnkɛrɛnnen na, waraba jɔlen bɛ balema siratigɛ la cogo kelen na, wulu bɛ min kɛ waraba la o bɛɛ n’a ta, fɛn dɔw tɛ ɲini minnu bɛ tali kɛ ɲininkaliw la; o dɔw ye sɔgɔsɔgɔninjɛ ye. A dɔnna kosɛbɛ ko tuma caman na, waraba bɛna ji walima buguri sama a ka jiribolo la, ka sɔrɔ k’a kɔrɔta, k’a wuli ka taa baji dɔ kɔnɔ.

Ni n bɛ nin kɔ barikama in jateminɛ ka taa a fɛ, n bɛ n dusu kasi ka taa a fɛ n ka sebaliya k’a fɔ. Tuma dɔw la, bololabaara dɔw bɛ kɛ a kɔnɔ, hali n’u bɛna hadamaden bolo diya kosɛbɛ, u bɛ to u ɲɛfɔbali ye pewu. Sagakuluba dɔ kɔnɔ, min bɛ mɔgɔ kabakoya kosɛbɛ, tuma dɔw la, nin gundolafɛnw bɛ yen, fo n ye kungokɔnɔfɛnw ɲininikɛlaw mɛn minnu y’a jira k’u ni Free-Mason taamasiyɛnw ni taamasiyɛnw bɛ tali kɛ ɲɔgɔn na; ko balema in, tiɲɛ na, o fɛɛrɛw fɛ, a ye baro kɛ ni hakili ye ni diɲɛ ye. Balema lamaga-lamagali wɛrɛw fana t’a fɛ a farikolo bɛɛ lajɛlen na, min falen bɛ kabako la, wa a tɛ jate a binkannikɛla ye min ka ko dɔn kosɛbɛ. Dissect a cogo min na ne bɛ se, o tuma, ne nka taa fari juguman; Ne t’a dɔn, wa ne tɛna a dɔn abada. Nka ni ne tɛ hali balema in kɔ dɔn, a kunkolo faamuya cogo di? caman wɛrɛ, a ɲɛda faamuya cogo di, k'a sɔrɔ ɲɛda tɛ a la? I na ne kɔ yɔrɔw ye, ne kɔ, a bɛ iko a b’a fɔ, nka ne ɲɛda tɛna ye. Nka n tɛ se k’a kɔ yɔrɔw dɔn pewu; ani ka hint a bɛna min kɛ a ɲɛda ko la, n b'a fɔ tugun ko ɲɛda tɛ a la.





SABABU 87. Armada belebeleba.

Malaka gun jan ni surun, min bɛ taa saheli-kɔrɔn fɛ ka bɔ Birmah maraw la, o bɛ kɛ Azi jamana bɛɛ saheli yɔrɔ ye. Layini dɔ la min tɛ ban ka bɔ o gun in na, Sumatra, Java, Bally ani Timor gun janw bɛ wuli; minnu ni caman wɛrɛw bɛ kɛ mɔli belebeleba ye, walima kogoba dɔ, min bɛ Azi ni Ɔsitarali cɛ janya la, ani ka Ɛndu kɔgɔji jan min ma kari, o tila ka bɔ kɔrɔnfɛ gunw na minnu falen bɛ jirisunba la. O kogo in bɛ sɔgɔ ni sally-port caman ye walasa kurunw ni balemaw ka nɔgɔya; minnu bɛ ye minnu cɛma, olu ye Sunda ni Malacca bajiw ye. Sunda kɔgɔjida la, kɛrɛnkɛrɛnnenya la, kurun minnu bɛ Taa Sinuwa ka Bɔ tlebin fɛ, olu bɛ Bɔ Sinuwa kɔgɔjiw la.

O Sunda kɔji misɛnninw bɛ Sumatra ni Java cɛ; ani a jɔlen bɛ cɛmancɛ la o gunw kogo belebeleba in kɔnɔ, o kɔji jɛman jagɛlɛn min bɛ a sabati, kɔgɔjilamɔgɔw bɛ min dɔn ko Java Head; u tɛ bɛn dɔɔnin cɛmancɛda la min bɛ da wuli ka don mansamara belebeleba dɔw kɔnɔ minnu kogolen don: wa n’i y’a jateminɛ ko nafolo min tɛ ban, n’o ye tulumafɛnw, ni silikiw, ani kabakurunw, ani sanu, ani nɛgɛso ye, o kɔrɔnfɛla kɔgɔji gun ba caman bɛ nafolo sɔrɔ ni minnu ye, a bɛ iko siginiden ka sariyaba dɔ, ko o nafolo suguw, dugukolo jɔli yɛrɛ fɛ, a dɔgɔyalenba ka kan ka kɛ i n’a fɔ u bɛ kɔlɔsi cogo o cogo, hali ni nafa tɛ u la, k’u kɔlɔsi ka bɔ tilebin diɲɛ kɔnɔ min bɛ fɛn bɛɛ minɛ. Sunda kɔgɔjida kɔgɔjida la, o kɛlɛbolo fangatigiw tɛ sɔrɔ minnu bɛ Mediterane, Baltik ani Propontis dondaw kɔlɔsi. O kɔrɔnyanfan mɔgɔw tɛ i n’a fɔ Danmakɛw, u tɛ bonya jugu ɲini sanfɛ-kurunw jiginlenw na ka bɔ kurunw ka taama banbali la fiɲɛ ɲɛfɛ, minnu tɛmɛna san kɛmɛ caman kɔnɔ, su fɛ ani tile fɛ, Sumatra ni Java gunw cɛ, doni bɛ minnu na kɔrɔnfɛla doni minnu sɔngɔ ka gɛlɛn kosɛbɛ. Nka hali n’u bɛ ban hɔrɔnya la seli dɔ la min bɛ i n’a fɔ nin, u tɛ ban u ka fɔta la cogo si la ko u bɛ se ka kɛ ni jatebɔ barikama ye ka tɛmɛ o kan.

Waati bɔlen kɔ an hakili la, Malaykaw ka jagokɛlaw ka proas, minnu bɛ u dogo Sumatra kɔw ni gunw cɛma, minnu sumayalen don kosɛbɛ, olu ye kurunw kan minnu bɛ tɛmɛ kɔjiw fɛ, ka jago ɲini kosɛbɛ u ka tamaw kun na. A whale wounded (as we afterwards learned) in this part, but not effectually, as it seemed, had broken away from the boat, carrying along with him half of the harpoon line; and in the extraordinary agony of the wound, he was now dashing among the revolving circles like the lone mounted desperado Arnold, at the battle of Saratoga, carrying dismay wherever he went.

But agonizing as was the wound of this whale, and an appalling spectacle enough, any way; yet the peculiar horror with which he seemed to inspire the rest of the herd, was owing to a cause which at first the intervening distance obscured from us. But at length we perceived that by one of the unimaginable accidents of the fishery, this whale had become entangled in the harpoon-line that he towed; he had also run away with the cutting-spade in him; and while the free end of the rope attached to that weapon, had permanently caught in the coils of the harpoon-line round his tail, the cutting-spade itself had worked loose from his flesh. So that tormented to madness, he was now churning through the water, violently flailing with his flexible tail, and tossing the keen spade about him, wounding and murdering his own comrades.

This terrific object seemed to recall the whole herd from their stationary fright. First, the whales forming the margin of our lake began to crowd a little, and tumble against each other, as if lifted by half spent billows from afar; then the lake itself began faintly to heave and swell; the submarine bridal-chambers and nurseries vanished; in more and more contracting orbits the whales in the more central circles began to swim in thickening clusters. Yes, the long calm was departing. The boat was now all but jammed between two vast black bulks, leaving a narrow Dardanelles between their long lengths. After many similar hair-breadth escapes, we at last swiftly glided into what had just been one of the outer circles, but now crossed by random whales, all violently making for one centre. Further pursuit was useless; but the boats still lingered in their wake to pick up what drugged whales might be dropped astern, and likewise to secure one which Flask had killed and waifed. The waif is a pennoned pole, two or three of which are carried by every boat; and which, when additional game is at hand, are inserted upright into the floating body of a dead whale, both to mark its place on the sea, and also as token of prior possession, should the boats of any other ship draw near.

The result of this lowering was somewhat illustrative of that sagacious saying in the Fishery,—the more whales the less fish. Of all the drugged whales only one was captured. The rest contrived to escape for the time, but only to be taken, as will hereafter be seen, by some other craft than the Pequod.





CHAPTER 88. Schools and Schoolmasters.

The previous chapter gave account of an immense body or herd of Sperm Whales, and there was also then given the probable cause inducing those vast aggregations.

Now, though such great bodies are at times encountered, yet, as must have been seen, even at the present day, small detached bands are occasionally observed, embracing from twenty to fifty individuals each. Such bands are known as schools. They generally are of two sorts; those composed almost entirely of females, and those mustering none but young vigorous males, or bulls, as they are familiarly designated.

In cavalier attendance upon the school of females, you invariably see a male of full grown magnitude, but not old; who, upon any alarm, evinces his gallantry by falling in the rear and covering the flight of his ladies. In truth, this gentleman is a luxurious Ottoman, swimming about over the watery world, surroundingly accompanied by all the solaces and endearments of the harem. The contrast between this Ottoman and his concubines is striking; because, while he is always of the largest leviathanic proportions, the ladies, even at full growth, are not more than one-third of the bulk of an average-sized male. They are comparatively delicate, indeed; I dare say, not to exceed half a dozen yards round the waist. Nevertheless, it cannot be denied, that upon the whole they are hereditarily entitled to en bon point .

It is very curious to watch this harem and its lord in their indolent ramblings. Like fashionables, they are for ever on the move in leisurely search of variety. You meet them on the Line in time for the full flower of the Equatorial feeding season, having just returned, perhaps, from spending the summer in the Northern seas, and so cheating summer of all unpleasant weariness and warmth. By the time they have lounged up and down the promenade of the Equator awhile, they start for the Oriental waters in anticipation of the cool season there, and so evade the other excessive temperature of the year.

When serenely advancing on one of these journeys, if any strange suspicious sights are seen, my lord whale keeps a wary eye on his interesting family. Should any unwarrantably pert young Leviathan coming that way, presume to draw confidentially close to one of the ladies, with what prodigious fury the Bashaw assails him, and chases him away! High times, indeed, if unprincipled young rakes like him are to be permitted to invade the sanctity of domestic bliss; though do what the Bashaw will, he cannot keep the most notorious Lothario out of his bed; for, alas! all fish bed in common. As ashore, the ladies often cause the most terrible duels among their rival admirers; just so with the whales, who sometimes come to deadly battle, and all for love. They fence with their long lower jaws, sometimes locking them together, and so striving for the supremacy like elks that warringly interweave their antlers. Not a few are captured having the deep scars of these encounters,—furrowed heads, broken teeth, scolloped fins; and in some instances, wrenched and dislocated mouths.

But supposing the invader of domestic bliss to betake himself away at the first rush of the harem's lord, then is it very diverting to watch that lord. Gently he insinuates his vast bulk among them again and revels there awhile, still in tantalizing vicinity to young Lothario, like pious Solomon devoutly worshipping among his thousand concubines. Granting other whales to be in sight, the fishermen will seldom give chase to one of these Grand Turks; for these Grand Turks are too lavish of their strength, and hence their unctuousness is small. As for the sons and the daughters they beget, why, those sons and daughters must take care of themselves; at least, with only the maternal help. For like certain other omnivorous roving lovers that might be named, my Lord Whale has no taste for the nursery, however much for the bower; and so, being a great traveller, he leaves his anonymous babies all over the world; every baby an exotic. In good time, nevertheless, as the ardour of youth declines; as years and dumps increase; as reflection lends her solemn pauses; in short, as a general lassitude overtakes the sated Turk; then a love of ease and virtue supplants the love for maidens; our Ottoman enters upon the impotent, repentant, admonitory stage of life, forswears, disbands the harem, and grown to an exemplary, sulky old soul, goes about all alone among the meridians and parallels saying his prayers, and warning each young Leviathan from his amorous errors.

Now, as the harem of whales is called by the fishermen a school, so is the lord and master of that school technically known as the schoolmaster. It is therefore not in strict character, however admirably satirical, that after going to school himself, he should then go abroad inculcating not what he learned there, but the folly of it. His title, schoolmaster, would very naturally seem derived from the name bestowed upon the harem itself, but some have surmised that the man who first thus entitled this sort of Ottoman whale, must have read the memoirs of Vidocq, and informed himself what sort of a country-schoolmaster that famous Frenchman was in his younger days, and what was the nature of those occult lessons he inculcated into some of his pupils.

The same secludedness and isolation to which the schoolmaster whale betakes himself in his advancing years, is true of all aged Sperm Whales. Almost universally, a lone whale—as a solitary Leviathan is called—proves an ancient one. Like venerable moss-bearded Daniel Boone, he will have no one near him but Nature herself; and her he takes to wife in the wilderness of waters, and the best of wives she is, though she keeps so many moody secrets.

The schools composing none but young and vigorous males, previously mentioned, offer a strong contrast to the harem schools. For while those female whales are characteristically timid, the young males, or forty-barrel-bulls, as they call them, are by far the most pugnacious of all Leviathans, and proverbially the most dangerous to encounter; excepting those wondrous grey-headed, grizzled whales, sometimes met, and these will fight you like grim fiends exasperated by a penal gout.

The Forty-barrel-bull schools are larger than the harem schools. Like a mob of young collegians, they are full of fight, fun, and wickedness, tumbling round the world at such a reckless, rollicking rate, that no prudent underwriter would insure them any more than he would a riotous lad at Yale or Harvard. They soon relinquish this turbulence though, and when about three-fourths grown, break up, and separately go about in quest of settlements, that is, harems.

Another point of difference between the male and female schools is still more characteristic of the sexes. Say you strike a Forty-barrel-bull—poor devil! all his comrades quit him. But strike a member of the harem school, and her companions swim around her with every token of concern, sometimes lingering so near her and so long, as themselves to fall a prey.
