HAADSTIK 1. Loomings.

Neam my Ismael. Guon jierren lyn - it makket neat út hoe lang krekt - mei in bytsje of gjin jild yn myn beurs, en neat spesjaal om my oan 'e wâl te ynteressearjen, tocht ik dat ik in bytsje sile soe en it wetterige diel fan' e wrâld sjen soe. It is in manier dy't ik haw om de milt ôf te riden en de sirkulaasje te regeljen. Elke kear as ik mysels grimmitich oer de mûle fyn; as it in fochtige, drizige novimber is yn myn siel; elke kear as ik mysels ûnfrijwillich ophâlde foar kistepakhuzen, en de efterkant fan elke begraffenis dy't ik moetsje ophelje; en foaral as myn hypo's sa'n oerhân fan my krije, dat it in sterk moreel prinsipe freget om foar te kommen dat ik mei opsetsin de strjitte opstappe, en de minsken metodysk de hoed ôfslaan - dan fyn ik it heech tiid om sa gau yn see te kommen. as ik kin. Dit is myn ferfanger foar pistoal en bal. Mei in filosofyske bloei smyt Cato him op syn swurd; Ik gean rêstich nei it skip. D'r is neat ferrassend yn dit. As se it mar wisten, koesterje hast alle manlju yn har graad, ien of oare tiid, hast deselde gefoelens foar de oseaan mei my.

D'r is no jo insulêre stêd fan 'e Manhatto's, omsletten troch wâlen as Yndiaaske eilannen troch koraalriffen - hannel omfettet it mei har surf. Rjochts en lofts, de strjitten bringe jo wetter. Syn ekstreme binnenstêd is de batterij, dêr't dy eale mol wurdt wosken troch weagen, en ôfkuolle troch bries, dy't in pear oeren earder wiene út it sicht fan lân. Sjoch nei de mannichte wettersjoggers dêr.

Circumambulate de stêd fan in dreamlike sabbat middei. Gean fan Corlears Hook nei Coenties Slip, en fan dêrwei troch Whitehall, noardlik. Wat sjochsto? - As stille wachtposten rûn de stêd hinne, steane tûzenen der tûzenen stjerlike manlju fêst yn 'e see-reveries. Guon dy't tsjin 'e spjalten oanleunen; guon sitten op 'e pierkoppen; guon sjogge oer de bolwurken fan skippen út Sina; wat heech yn 'e riging, as stribbet nei in noch better seespegel. Mar dat binne allegearre lânslju; fan wike dagen opboud yn latten en gips - bûn oan balken, spikere oan banken, oan buro's beknypt. Hoe is dit dan? Binne de griene fjilden fuort? Wat dogge se hjir?

Mar sjoch! hjir komme mear mannichte, dy't rjocht op it wetter rinne, en blykber op 'e dûk gean. Frjemd! Neat sil har ynhâlde as de ekstreme limyt fan it lân; slûgjen ûnder it skaad fan 'e oare pakhuzen sil net genôch wêze. Nee, se moatte krekt sa ticht by it wetter komme as se kinne sûnder yn te fallen. En dêr steane se - kilometers fan - kompetysjes. Binnenlanners allegear komme se út leanen en stegen, strjitten en lanen - noard, east, súd en west. Doch hjir ferienigje se allegear. Sis my, lûkt de magnetyske deugd fan 'e naalden fan 'e kompassen fan al dy skippen har dêrhinne?

Nochris. Sis do bist yn it lân; yn guon hege lân fan marren. Nim hast elk paad dat jo wolle, en tsien tsjin ien draacht it jo del yn in dal, en lit jo dêr by in poel yn 'e stream. Der sit magy yn. Lit de meast ôfwêzichste minsken yn syn djipste earbiedigingen ûnderdompele wurde - stean dy man op 'e skonken, set de fuotten op 'e gong, en hy sil jo ûnfeilber nei it wetter liede, as der wetter is yn al dy streek. Mochten jo oait toarst wêze yn 'e grutte Amerikaanske woastyn, besykje dit eksperimint, as jo karavan tafallich wurdt foarsjoen fan in metafysyske professor. Ja, lykas elkenien wit, meditaasje en wetter binne foar altyd troud.

Mar hjir is in keunstner. Hy wol jo it dreamlikste, skaadste, stilste, meast betsjoenende bytsje romantysk lânskip skilderje yn 'e hiele delling fan' e Saco. Wat is it haadelemint dat hy brûkt? Dêr steane syn beammen, elk mei in holle stam, as sieten in kluzener en in krús yn; en hjir sliept syn greide, en dêr sliept syn fee; en fan boppen bûthús giet in slieperige reek. Djip yn fiere bosken slingert in mazy manier, en berikt nei oerlappende spoaren fan bergen baden yn har heuvelsideblau. Mar hoewol't it byld sa trance leit, en al skoddet dizze pinebeam syn suchten as blêden op 'e holle fan dizze herder, dochs wiene allegear idel, útsein as it each fan 'e hoeder op 'e magyske stream foar him rjochte wie. Gean yn juny de Prairies te besykjen, as jo foar skoares fan tsientallen kilometers knibbeldjip waadje tusken Tiger-lelies - wat wol de iene sjarme? - Wetter - d'r is gjin drip wetter! Wiene Niagara mar in katarakt fan sân, soene jo jo tûzen kilometer reizgje om it te sjen? Wêrom hat de earme dichter fan Tennessee, doe't er ynienen twa hânfol sulver krige, besletten om him in jas te keapjen, dy't er spitigernôch nedich hie, of syn jild ynvestearje yn in kuiertocht nei Rockaway Beach? Wêrom is hast elke robúste sûne jonge mei in robúste sûne siel yn him, op ien of oare stuit gek om nei see te gean? Wêrom fielde jo sels op jo earste reis as passazjier sa'n mystike trilling, doe't jo earst fertelden dat jo en jo skip no út it sicht fan lân wiene? Wêrom holden de âlde Perzen de see hillich? Wêrom joegen de Griken it in aparte godheid, en eigen broer fan Jove? Dit alles is wis net sûnder betsjutting. En noch djipper de betsjutting fan dat ferhaal fan Narcissus, dy't, om't er it pineige, mylde byld dat er yn 'e fontein seach, net bigripe koe, dêryn stoarte en ferdronken waard. Mar datselde byld sjogge wy sels yn alle rivieren en oseanen. It is it byld fan it ûngrypbere libbensfantoom; en dit is de kaai ta it allegear.

No, as ik sis dat ik de gewoante haw om nei see te gean as ik dizich begjint te wurden oer de eagen en begjin te bewust te wêzen fan myn longen, bedoel ik net dat ik derfan ôflei dat ik oait nei see gean as in passazjier. Want om as passazjier te gean moatte jo in beurs hawwe, en in beurs is mar in doek, útsein as jo wat yn hawwe. Boppedat wurde passazjiers seesiik - wurde rûzje - sliepe net fan nachten - genietsje har net folle, as in algemien ding; - nee, ik gean noait as passazjier; ek, hoewol ik wat sâlt bin, gean ik ea nei see as Commodore, of in kaptein, of in kok. Ik lit de gloarje en ûnderskieding fan sokke amten oer oan dyjingen dy't se graach hawwe. Foar myn part ôfskriuw ik alle eare respektabele muoite, besikingen en ferdrukkingen fan elke soart dan ek. It is sa folle as ik kin om foar mysels te soargjen, sûnder skippen, barken, briggen, skûtsjes, en wat net te fersoargjen. En as it gean as kok, - hoewol ik beken dat d'r in protte gloarje yn is, in kok dy't in soarte fan offisier oan board is - dochs, op ien of oare manier, hie ik noait fan 'e broadende fûgels; gezouten en pepere, der is gjinien dy't earbiediger, om net earbiedich te sizzen, oer in broeide fûgel sprekt as ik. It is út 'e ôfgoaden fan' e âlde Egyptners op broiled ibis en roastere rivier hynder, dat jo sjogge de mummies fan dy skepsels yn harren enoarme bakhuzen de piramiden.

Nee, as ik nei see gean, gean ik as in ienfâldige seeman, krekt foar de mêst, plomp del yn 'e foarstek, dêr omheech nei de keninklike mêstkop. Wier, se bestelle my leaver oer guon, en meitsje my springe fan spar nei spar, as in sprinkhaan yn in maaie greide. En yn 't earstoan is dit soarte ding onaangenaam genôch. It rekket jins earegefoel oan, benammen as jo út in âlde fêstige famylje yn it lân komme, de Van Rensselaers, of Randolphs, of Hardicanutes. En mear as alles, as just foarôfgeand oan it stekken fan jo hân yn 'e teerpot, hawwe jo it as lânsskoallemaster beheard, wêrtroch't de langste jonges yn 'e eangst foar jo steane. De oergong is in skerpe, ik fersekerje jo, fan in skoalmaster nei in seeman, en freget om in sterke decoction fan Seneca en de Stoïsyen om jo yn steat te meitsjen om it te gnizen en te dragen. Mar ek dit ferrint yn 'e tiid.

Wat der mei, as guon âlde hunken fan in seekaptein my befelje om in biezem te heljen en de dekken del te feijen? Wat is dy ûnweardichheid, weage, ik bedoel, yn 'e skaal fan it Nije Testamint? Tinksto dat de aartsingel Gabriël wat minder fan my tinkt, om't ik yn dat bysûndere gefal prompt en earbiedich oan dy âlde hunken hear? Wa is gjin slaaf? Fertel my dat. Nou, dan, hoe't de âlde seekapteins my ek befelje meie - hoe't se my ek klopje en slaan meie, ik haw de foldwaning om te witten dat it goed is; dat alle oaren op ien of oare manier op in protte deselde wize tsjinne wurde - itsij yn in fysyk as metafysysk eachpunt, dat is; en sa wirdt de universele stomp rûn, en alle hannen moatte inoars skouderblêden wrijven, en tofreden wêze.

Nochris gean ik altyd nei see as matroas, om't se der in punt fan meitsje om my te beteljen foar myn problemen, wylst se passazjiers noait in inkele penny betelje wêr't ik ea fan heard haw. Krektoarsom, passazjiers moatte sels betelje. En d'r is al it ferskil yn 'e wrâld tusken beteljen en betelle wurde. De hanneling fan beteljen is faaks de meast ûngemaklike tafoeging dy't de twa boomgaarddieven ús meimakke hawwe. Mar betelle wurde , - wat sil dêrmei fergelykje? De stedske aktiviteit wêrmei't in man jild ûntfangt, is echt wûnderlik, yn betinken nommen dat wy sa earnstich leauwe dat jild de woartel is fan alle ierdske kwalen, en dat in man mei jild yn gjin gefal de himel yngean kin. Ah! hoe fleurich jouwe wy ússels ta it ferneatiging!

Einliks gean ik altyd as seeman nei see, fanwegen de sûne oefening en de suvere lucht fan it foarkastieldek. Want lyk as yn dizze wrâld komt kopwyn folle mear foar as wyn út efterút (dat is, as jo de Pythagoreeske maksimaal nea oertrêde), dus foar it grutste part krijt de Kommodore op it kertier syn sfear fan twaddehâns fan de seelju op 'e foarsizzing. Hy tinkt dat er it earst sykhellet; mar net sa. Op folle deselde wize liedt de mienskip har lieders yn in protte oare dingen, tagelyk dat de lieders it net folle fertinke. Mar hwerom wie it, dat ik, nei't ik as keapman as keapman mear as ien kear de see smolten hie, it nou yn 'e holle nimme soe op in walfisktocht to gean; dit de ûnsichtbere plysjeman fan 'e Fates, dy't it konstante tafersjoch op my hat, en my temûk hûnet en my op ien of oare ûnferantwurdlike wize beynfloedet - hy kin better antwurdzje as in oar. En, sûnder mis, myn gean op dizze walfiskfeart, makke diel út fan it grutte programma fan Providence dat lang lyn opsteld is. It kaam yn as in soarte fan koarte tuskenspul en solo tusken wiidweidigere optredens. Ik nim oan dat dit diel fan 'e rekken sa'n ding moat hawwe útfierd:

" Grutte bestriden ferkiezings foar it presidintskip fan 'e Feriene Steaten. “WALFJING REIS DOOR IEN ISHMAEL. "BLOEDIGE BATTLE IN AFFHANISTAN."

Al kin ik net sizze wêrom't it krekt wie dat dy toaniellieders, de Fates, my delsetten foar dit sjofele diel fan in walfiskfeart, doe't oaren waarden set foar prachtige dielen yn hege trageedzjes, en koarte en maklike dielen yn sêfte komeedzjes, en fleurige dielen yn farsen - al kin ik net sizze wêrom't dat krekt wie; Dochs, no't ik my alle omstannichheden herinner, tink ik dat ik in bytsje sjen kin yn 'e boarnen en motiven dy't my listich foarlein ûnder ferskate ferklaaiïng, my oantrúnje ta it útfieren fan it diel dat ik dien hat, neist my yn 'e waan te bringen dat it wie in kar dy't ûntstie út myn eigen unbiased freewill en diskriminearjend oardiel.

Haad ûnder dizze motiven wie it oerweldigjende idee fan 'e grutte walfisk sels. Sa'n skriklike en mysterieuze meunster wekker al myn nijsgjirrigens. Dan de wylde en fiere see dêr't er syn eilân bulk rôle; de net te leverjen, nammeleaze gefaren fan 'e walfisk; dizze, mei alle oanwêzige wûnders fan tûzen Patagonyske sights en lûden, holpen my te swaaien nei myn winsk. By oare manlju soene sokke dingen miskien gjin oantrún west hawwe; mar wat my oanbelanget, ik wurdt martele mei in ivige jeuk foar dingen op ôfstân. Ik hâld fan ferbeane seeën te farren, en lân op barbaarske kusten. Net negearje wat goed is, ik fernimme gau in ôfgriis, en soe der noch sosjaal mei wêze kinne - soene se my litte - om't it mar goed is om freonskip te wêzen mei alle finzenen fan it plak dêr't men yn ferbliuwt.

Troch dizze dingen wie dan de walfisktocht wolkom; de grutte oerstreamingspoarten fan 'e wûnderwrâld swaaiden iepen, en yn 'e wylde oermoeden dy't my nei myn doel swaaiden, sweefden twa en twa dêr yn myn binnenste siel, einleaze optochten fan 'e walfisk, en, midden yn 'e measten, ien grand hooded fantom, as in snieheuvel yn 'e loft.





HAADSTIK 2. The Carpet-Bag.

Ik stopte in shirt of twa yn myn âlde tapyttas, stuts it ûnder myn earm en begon nei Kaap Hoarn en de Stille Oseaan. Nei it ferlitten fan 'e goede stêd fan it âlde Manhatto, kaam ik krekt yn New Bedford oan. It wie in sneontejûn yn desimber. In protte wie ik teloarsteld doe't ik learde dat it lytse pakket foar Nantucket al fearn wie, en dat gjin manier om dat plak te berikken soe biede, oant de folgjende moandei.

Om't de measte jonge kandidaten foar de pine en de straffen fan 'e walfiskjacht op deselde New Bedford stopje, om dêrwei op har reis te begjinnen, kin it likegoed relatearre wurde dat ik, foar ien, gjin idee hie fan dat te dwaan. Want myn miening wie der foar makke om net oars te farren as in Nantucket-ambacht, om't der in moai, boeiend wat wie oer alles dat ferbûn wie mei dat ferneamde âlde eilân, dat my ongelooflijk bliid makke. Neist al hat New Bedford de lêste tiid stadichoan it bedriuw fan de walfiskfangst monopolisearre, en hoewol't de earme âlde Nantucket no folle efter har is, wie Nantucket har grutte orizjineel - de bân fan dizze Kartago; - it plak dêr't de earste deade Amerikaan walfisk wie strâne. Wêr oars as fan Nantucket kamen dy aboriginal walfiskfarders, de Reade-Mannen, earst yn kano's út om de Leviathan te efterfolgjen? En wêr oars as fan Nantucket, ek, die dat earste aventoerlike lytse sloep, foar in part beladen mei ymportearre kasseien - sa giet it ferhaal - nei de walfisken te goaien, om te ûntdekken wannear't se tichtby genôch wiene om in harpoen út 'e boegspriet te riskearjen ?

No't ik in nacht, in dei en noch in nacht foar my yn New Bedford folge, ear't ik nei myn bestimde haven oanstappe koe, waard it in kwestje fan soarch wêr't ik ûnderwilens ite en sliepe soe. It wie in tige twifelich útseach, nee, in tige tsjustere en sombere nacht, bytend kâld en fleurich. Ik koe nimmen op it plak. Mei eangstige grapnels hie ik myn bûse slein en mar in pear sulverstikken opbrocht, - Dus, wêr't jo hinnegeane, Ismael, sei ik tsjin mysels, wylst ik midden yn in drege strjitte stie en myn tas skoudere en de tsjuster nei it noarden mei it tsjuster nei it suden - wêr't jo yn jo wiisheid ek ôfslute kinne om te ferbliuwen foar de nacht, myn leave Ismael, wês wis om de priis te freegjen, en wês net te bysûnder.

Mei haltende stappen rûn ik de strjitten troch, en passearre it teken fan "The Crossed Harpoons" - mar it like dêr te djoer en fleurich. Fierderop, út 'e knalreade ruten fan 'e "Swurdfiskherberch", kamen sokke fûle strielen, dat it de ynpakte snie en iis fan foar it hûs like opsmolten te hawwen, want oeral lei de stiele froast tsien sintimeter dik yn in hurde, asfaltige bestrating, - my nochal wurch, doe't ik mei de foet tsjin 'e flintige útsteksels sloech, om't fan hurde, ûnberouleaze tsjinst de soallen fan myn learzens yn 'e meast ellendige need wiene. Te djoer en fleurich, tocht ik wer, in momint stilhâldend om de brede glâns yn 'e strjitte te sjen en de lûden fan 'e tinteljende glêzen binnen te hearren. Mar gean fierder, Ismaël, sei ik op 't lêst; hearst it net? gean fuort foar de doar; dyn lapke laarzen stopje de wei. Sa gong ik fierder. Ik folge no troch ynstinkt de strjitten dy't my nei it wetter leine, want dêr wiene sûnder mis de goedkeapste, as net de fleurichste herbergen.

Sokke drege strjitten! blokken swart, gjin huzen, oan beide kanten, en hjir en dêr in kears, as in kears dy't yn in grêf beweecht. Op dit oere fan 'e nacht, fan 'e lêste dei fan 'e wike, wie dat kertier fan 'e stêd hast ferlitten. Mar op 't heden kaam ik yn in smûk ljocht út in leech, breed gebou, wêrfan de doar útnoegjend iepen stie. It hie in achteleas uterlik, as wie it bedoeld foar it gebrûk fan it publyk; sa, it binnenkommen, it earste wat ik die wie om te stroffeljen oer in jiskebak yn 'e feranda. Ha! tocht ik, ha, doe't de fleanende dieltsjes my hast ferstikten, binne dizze jiske út dy ferwoaste stêd, Gomorra? Mar "The Crossed Harpoons," en "The Sword-Fish?" - dit moat dan it teken wêze fan "The Trap." Ik helle mysels lykwols op en hearde in lûde stim fan binnen, triuwde op en die in twadde binnendoar iepen.

It like it grutte Swarte Parlemint te sitten yn Tophet. Hûndert swarte gesichten draaiden har yn har rigen om om te sjen; en fierder, in swarte Angel of Doom sloech in boek yn in preekstoel. It wie in negertsjerke; en de tekst fan de predikant gie oer it swart fan it tsjuster, en it gûlen en janken en it toskenkerzen dêr. Ha, Ismael, mompele ik, achterút, Eltstich fermaak by it teken fan 'De Trap!'

Trochgean, kaam ik op 't lêst by in dimmen soarte ljocht net fier fan 'e dokken, en hearde in forlitten kreakjen yn 'e loft; en seach omheech, seach in swaaiend teken oer de doar mei in wyt skilderij derop, wat flau foarstelde fan in hege rjochte straal mistige spray, en dizze wurden derûnder - "The Spouter Inn: - Peter Coffin."

Coffin? - Spouter? - Noch wat onheilspellend yn dat bysûndere ferbân, tocht ik. Mar it is in gewoane namme yn Nantucket, sizze se, en ik nim oan dat dizze Piter hjir in emigrant is. Om't it ljocht sa swak seach, en it plak foar de tiid stil genôch wie, en it ferfallen houten hûs sels like as soe it hjir út 'e ruïnes fan ien of oare ôfbaarnde wyk west hawwe kinnen, en as it slingerjende teken in earmoedich soartsje kreake der oan, ik tocht, dat hjir it plak wie foar goedkeap ferbliuw, en it bêste fan eartekoffie.

It wie in raar soarte plak - in âld hûs mei gevelbeein, ien kant sa't it wie ferlamme, en fertrietlik oerhinne. It stie op in skerpe sombere hoeke, dêr't dy stoarmige wyn Euroclydon in slimmer janking ophâldde as ea oer it slingere ambacht fan earme Paul. Euroclydon, lykwols, is in machtich noflike zephyr foar elkenien yn 'e doar, mei syn fuotten op' e kookplaat rêstich toastend foar bêd. "Om te oardieljen nei dy stoarmige wyn dy't Euroclydon neamd wurdt," seit in âld skriuwer - fan waans wurk ik it iennichste eksimplaar besit - "makket it in prachtich ferskil, oft jo der út in glêzen rút útsjogge wêr't de froast op 'e bûtendoar, of oft jo it beoardielje fan dat sjerpleaze rút, dêr't de froast oan beide kanten is en dêr't de wight Dea de iennichste gletsjer is." Wier genôch, tocht ik, doe't dizze passaazje my yn 't sin kaam - âlde swarte letter, do redenest goed. Ja, dizze eagen binne finsters, en dit lichem fan my is it hûs. Wat spitich dat se de knibbels en kroanen dochs net tsjinhâlden en hjir en dêr in bytsje plús yn stutsen. Mar it is te let om no ferbetteringen oan te bringen. It hielal is klear; de copestone is oan, en de chips waarden carted off in miljoen jier lyn. Earme Lazarus dêr, mei de tosken tsjin 'e stoepe foar syn kessen, en mei syn huverjen syn sleatten ôfskodde, koe er beide earen mei lappen stekke en in maïskolf yn 'e mûle stekke, en dat scoe him dochs net úthâlde. stoarmige Euroclydon. Euroclydon! seit âlde Dûken, yn syn reade siden omslach - (hy hie dernei in reader) poeh, poeh! Wat in moaie froastnacht; hoe't Orion glitters; wat noarderljocht! Lit se prate oer har oriïntaalske simmerklimaten fan ivige konservatoaren; jou my it foarrjocht om mei myn eigen koalen myn eigen simmer te meitsjen.

Mar wat tinkt Lazarus? Kin er syn blauwe hannen waarmje troch se tsjin it grutte noarderljocht te hâlden? Soe Lazarus net leaver yn Sumatra wêze as hjir? Soe er him net fier leaver yn 'e lingte by de line fan 'e evener dellizze; ja, goaden! del gean nei de fjoerput sels, om dizze froast út te hâlden?

No, dat Lazarus dêr strâne lizze soe op de stoep foar de doar fan Dives, dit is prachticher as dat der in iisberch oan ien fan de Molukken oanlein wurde moat. Dochs Dives sels, hy libbet ek as in tsaar yn in iispaleis makke fan beferzen suchten, en as presidint fan in matigingsmaatskippij drinkt hy allinich de lauwe triennen fan weesbern.

Mar no net mear fan dit blubberjen, wy geane op walfiskfangst, en dêr moat noch in soad fan komme. Lit ús it iis fan ús frostige fuotten skrabe, en sjen wat foar in plak dizze "Spouter" kin wêze.





HAADSTIK 3. De Spouter-Inn.

By it ynfieren fan dy gevel-einige Spouter-Inn, do fûn dysels yn in brede, lege, straggling yngong mei âlderwetske wainscots, tinken ien fan de bolwurken fan guon feroardiele âlde ambacht. Oan 'e iene kant hong in hiel grut oaljeferve, sa bot smookt en yn alle wizen fersmiten, dat it yn 'e ûngelikense krúsljochten wêrmei't jo it seagen, allinich wie troch iverige stúdzje en in rige systematyske besites deroan, en soarchfâldich ûndersyk fan 'e buorlju, dat jo op elke manier kinne komme ta in begryp fan it doel. Sokke ûnferantwurdlike massa's fan skaden en skaden, dat jo earst hast tochten dat ien of oare ambisjeuze jonge keunstner, yn 'e tiid fan' e New England hags, besocht hie om gaos behekse ôf te tekenjen. Mar troch in protte en earnstich neitinken, en faak werhelle betinken, en benammen troch it lytse rút nei de efterkant fan de yngong iepen te smiten, komme je op 't lêst ta de konklúzje dat sa'n idee, hoe wyld ek, miskien net hielendal ûnrjochtfeardich is.

Mar wat jo it meast fernuvere en ferbjustere wie in lange, limber, ferrifeljende, swarte massa fan iets dat yn it sintrum fan 'e foto sweefde oer trije blauwe, dimmen, loodrjochte linen dy't yn in nammeleaze gist sweven. In boggy, soggy, squitchy foto wirklik, genôch om in senuweftige man ôf te lieden. Dochs siet der in soarte fan ûnbepaalde, heal berikte, ûnfoarstelbere ferhevenheid oer, dy't jin der aardich oan befrieze, oant jo ûnwillekeurich in eed mei jin ôfslepen om út te finen wat dat wûnderlike skilderij betsjutte. Altyd en anon soe in helder, mar, helaas, misleidend idee jo trochrinne.—It is de Swarte See yn in middernachtske storm.—It is de ûnnatuerlike bestriding fan de fjouwer oereleminten.—It is in blaze heide.—It is in Hyperboreaansk wintersêne. .—It is it opbrekken fan de iisbûne stream fan de Tiid. Mar op 't lêst joegen al dy fantasyen oan dat iene wrede wat yn 'e foto. Dat fûn ienris út, en al de rest wie gewoan. Mar stopje; liket it net in swakke oerienkomst mei in gigantyske fisk? sels de grutte Leviatan sels?

Yn feite, it ûntwerp fan 'e keunstner like dit: in lêste teory fan myn eigen, foar in part basearre op de aggregearre mieningen fan in protte âldere persoanen mei wa't ik praat oer it ûnderwerp. De foto stiet foar in Kaap-Hoarner yn in grutte orkaan; it healboude skip dat dêr mei syn trije ôfbrutsen mêsten allinnich sichtber is; en in fergriemde walfisk, dy't fan doel is skjin te springen oer it ambacht, is yn 'e enoarme died om himsels op' e trije mêstkoppen te spitsen.

De tsjinoerstelde muorre fan dizze yngong waard oeral ophongen mei in heidenske array fan meunsterlike klubs en spearen. Guon wiene dik set mei glinsterjende tosken dy't lykje op ivoarenseagen; oaren wiene tufted mei knopen minsklik hier; en ien wie sikkelfoarmich, mei in grutte hân dy't rûnom sweevje as it segmint makke yn it nij-maaide gers troch in langarmige maaier. Jo huveren doe't jo seagen, en jo fregen jo ôf hokker meunsterlike kannibaal en wyld oait in dea-rêsting koe hawwe gien mei sa'n hackende, ôfgryslike ynstrumint. Mei dizze mingde wiene roestige âlde walfisklansen en harpoenen allegear brutsen en misfoarme. Guon wiene ferneamd wapens. Mei dizze eartiids lange lans, no wyld mei elbow, fyftich jier lyn deade Nathan Swain fyftjin walfisken tusken in sinne-op- en in sinneûndergong. En dy harpoen - sa as in kurketrekker no - waard yn 'e Javaanske see slingere, en troch in walfisk fuortrûn, jierren letter fermoarde foar de Kaap fan Blanco. It oarspronklike izer kaam tichtby de sturt yn, en, as in ûnrêstige naald dy't yn it lichem fan in man wenne, reizge folle fjirtich fuotten, en waard op it lêst fûn ynbêde yn 'e bult.

Troch dizze tsjustere yngong oer te stekken, en troch jo leechbôge wei - trochsnien wat yn âlde tiden in geweldige sintrale skoarstien wêze moat mei kachels rûnom - komme jo de iepenbiere keamer yn. In noch tsjusterder plak is dit, mei sa'n lege swiere balken boppe en sokke âlde rimpelige planken derûnder, dat jo hast graach wolle dat jo de cockpits fan âlde ambachten trape, benammen fan sa'n gûlende nacht, doe't dizze yn 'e hoeke ferankere âlde arke sa fûleindich skommele . Oan de iene kant stie in lange, lege, planke-achtige tafel bedutsen mei barsten glêzen kasten, fol mei stoffige rariteiten sammele út de fierste hoeken fan dizze wide wrâld. Ut 'e fierdere hoeke fan 'e keamer projeksje, stiet in donkere útsjende hoale - de bar - in rûch besykjen op 'e holle fan in rjochte walfisk. Hoe dan ek, dêr stiet de grutte bôgebonke fan 'e kaak fan 'e walfisk, sa breed, dat in koets der suver ûnder ride kin. Binnen binne armoedige planken, rûnom mei âlde karaffen, flessen, flessen; en yn dy kaken fan fluch ferneatiging, as in oare ferflokte Jona (mei hokker namme se him nammentlik neamden), brûst in bytsje ferwoaste âld man, dy't foar har jild de matrozen deliriums en dea ferkeapet.

Afgryslik binne de tumblers dêr't er syn gif yn giet. Hoewol't wiere silinders sûnder-binnen, de skansearre griene goggling glêzen ferrifeljend tapsed nei ûnderen ta in falsk spul boaiem. Parallelle meridianen dy't grof yn it glês pikke, omfetsje de bekers fan dizze footpads. Folje nei dit teken, en jo lading is mar in penny; hjirfoar in penny mear; en sa fierder nei it folle glês - de Kaaphoarnmaat, dy't jo foar in shilling opslokje meie.

By it binnenkommen fan it plak fûn ik in oantal jonge seelju gearkommen om in tafel, ûndersocht troch in swak ljocht dûkers eksimplaren fan skrimshander . Ik socht de lânhear, en fertelde him dat ik woe wurde ûnderbrocht mei in keamer, krige foar antwurd dat syn hûs wie fol - net in bêd ûnbewenne. "Mar avast," foege er ta, en tikte op 'e foarholle, "jo hawwe gjin beswieren om in harpoonier's tekken te dielen, hawwe jo? Ik stel dat jo in walfisk geane, dus jo kinne better wenne oan dat soarte dingen.

Ik fertelde him, dat ik noait graach twa sliepe op in bêd; dat as ik dat ea dwaan soe, it der fan ôfhingje soe wa't de harpoonier wêze soe, en dat as hy (de lânhear) eins gjin oar plak foar my hie, en de harpoonier wie net beslist beswierlik, wêrom leaver as fierder te swalkjen oer in frjemde stêd op sa'n bittere nacht, ik soe mei de helte fan elke fatsoenlike man syn tekken ophâlde.

"Dat tocht ik. Oke; Gean sitten. Jûnsmiel? - wolle jo iten? It jûnsmiel sil direkt klear wêze. ”

Ik siet op in âlde houten set, útsnijd as in bankje op de Batterij. Oan 'e iene ein siet noch fierder in rommeljende tar mei syn jack-mes, bûgd oer en wurke fleurich fuort by de romte tusken syn skonken. Hy besocht syn hân op in skip ûnder folle seil, mar hy makke net folle foarút, tocht ik.

Op 't lêst waarden sa'n fjouwer of fiif fan ús oproppen foar ús miel yn in neistlizzende keamer. It wie kâld as Yslân - hielendal gjin fjoer - de lânhear sei dat er it net betelje koe. Neat oars as twa sombere talgkearsen, elk yn in kronkeljend blêd. Wy wiene flaaks om ús aapjassen op te knopjen, en mei ús heal beferzen fingers kopkes ferbrandende tee tsjin ús lippen te hâlden. Mar de fare wie fan 'e meast substansjele soarte - net allinne fleis en ierappels, mar dumplings; goede himel! dumplings foar it iten! Ien jonge keardel yn in griene boksjas, rjochte him op in heulende manier ta dizze dumplings.

"Myn jonge," sei de lânhear, "dû silst de nachtmerje hawwe nei in deade siel."

"Hânhear," flústere ik, "dat is net de harpoonier?"

"Och nee," sei er, en seach in soarte fan diabolysk grappich út, "de harpoonier is in donkere teint. Hy yt noait knoedels, hy net - hy yt neat oars as steaks, en hy hâldt fan 'e seldsume."

"De duvel docht er," seit ik. "Wêr is dy harpoonier? Is hy hjir?"

"Hy sil hjir noch lang wêze," wie it antwurd.

Ik koe it net helpe, mar ik begon fertocht te fielen oer dizze "donkere teint" harpoonier. Ik ha my yn elts gefal besletten dat as it sa blykt dat wy tegearre sliepe moatte, hy him útklaaie moat en op bêd foardat ik dat die.

It jûnsmiel foarby gong it selskip werom nei de kroechkeamer, doe't ik, net wittende wat ik oars mei mysels dwaan moast, besleat om de rest fan 'e jûn troch te bringen as sjenner.

Op it stuit waard in oproer lûd heard sûnder. Opstartend rôp de lânhear: "Dat is de bemanning fan 'e Grampus. Ik sied har fannemoarn rapportearre yn 'e ôffal; in reis fan trije jier, en in folslein skip. Hoera, jonges; no krije wy it lêste nijs fan de Feegees.”

By de yngong hearde men in tramperjen fan seelearzen; de doar waerd iepenslingere, en der rôle in wyld stel seelju genôch yn. Yn har skuorre horloazjejassen en mei de hollen dempe yn wollen dekbeammen, allegear bedarre en raffele, en har burd stiif fan ispegels, liken se in útbarsting fan bearen út Labrador. Se wiene krekt fan har boat telâne kommen, en dit wie it earste hûs dat se binnenkamen. Gjin wûnder dus, dat se in rjochte wekker makken foar de mûle fan 'e walfisk - de bar - doe't de rimpelige lytse âlde Jona, dêr't offisiearjende, har al gau brimmers útgie. Ien klage oer in slimme kjeld yn 'e holle, dêr't Jona him in pyk-achtige drank fan jenever en melasse op mingde, dy't er swarde wie in soevereine remedie foar alle verkoudheid en catarrhs ​​dan ek, nettsjinsteande hoe lang stean, of oft fongen de kust fan Labrador, of oan de waarkant fan in iis-eilân.

De drank kaam har gau yn 'e holle, lykas it yn 't algemien docht sels mei de meast foarkommende topers dy't nij fan 'e see lâne binne, en se begûnen it meast obstreperously om te rinnen.

Ik konstatearre lykwols dat ien fan harren wat ôfhâlden hold, en hoewol't er like begearde om de hilariteit fan syn skipsgenoaten net te bedjerren troch syn eigen sobere gesicht, dochs bleau er yn 't gehiel ôf fan it meitsjen fan likefolle lûd as de rest. Dizze man ynteressearre my yn ien kear; en om't de seegoaden ornearre hiene dat er meikoarten myn skipsgenoat wurde soe (hoewol mar in slieppartner ien, wat dit ferhaal oanbelanget), sil ik my hjir weagje oan in lytse beskriuwing fan him. Hy stie fol seis foet yn 'e hichte, mei eale skouders en in kiste as in kofferdam. Ik haw selden sa'n brawn yn in man sjoen. Syn gesicht wie djip brún en ferbaarnd, wêrtroch't syn wite tosken skitterend troch it kontrast; wylst yn 'e djippe skaden fan syn eagen wat oantinkens sweven dy't him net folle wille liken te jaan. Syn stim kundige daliks oan dat er in Súderling wie, en fan syn moaie statuer tocht ik dat er ien fan dy hege berchbeklimmers wêze moast fan de Alleghanian Ridge yn Firginia. Doe't de brulloft fan syn maten op syn hichte wie, glide dizze man sûnder waarnommen fuort, en ik seach net mear fan him oant hy myn kammeraat op see waard. Yn in pear minuten waard hy lykwols mist troch syn skipsgenoaten, en as, it liket, om ien of oare reden in grutte favoryt by har, rôpen se in gjalp fan "Bulkington! Bulkington! wêr is Bulkington?” en raasde it hûs út op 'e efterfolging fan him.

It wie no om njoggen oere hinne, en de keamer like nei dizze orgyen hast boppenatuerlik stil, ik begûn mysels te lokwinskjen mei in lyts plan dat my krekt foar de yngong fan de seelju opkommen wie.

Gjin minske sliept leaver twa op in bêd. In fact, you would a good deal rather not sleep with your own brother. I don't know how it is, but people like to be private when they are sleeping. And when it comes to sleeping with an unknown stranger, in a strange inn, in a strange town, and that stranger a harpooneer, then your objections indefinitely multiply. Nor was there any earthly reason why I as a sailor should sleep two in a bed, more than anybody else; for sailors no more sleep two in a bed at sea, than bachelor Kings do ashore. To be sure they all sleep together in one apartment, but you have your own hammock, and cover yourself with your own blanket, and sleep in your own skin.

The more I pondered over this harpooneer, the more I abominated the thought of sleeping with him. It was fair to presume that being a harpooneer, his linen or woollen, as the case might be, would not be of the tidiest, certainly none of the finest. I began to twitch all over. Besides, it was getting late, and my decent harpooneer ought to be home and going bedwards. Suppose now, he should tumble in upon me at midnight—how could I tell from what vile hole he had been coming?

“Landlord! I've changed my mind about that harpooneer.—I shan't sleep with him. I'll try the bench here.”

“Just as you please; I'm sorry I can't spare ye a tablecloth for a mattress, and it's a plaguy rough board here”—feeling of the knots and notches. “But wait a bit, Skrimshander; I've got a carpenter's plane there in the bar—wait, I say, and I'll make ye snug enough.” So saying he procured the plane; and with his old silk handkerchief first dusting the bench, vigorously set to planing away at my bed, the while grinning like an ape. The shavings flew right and left; till at last the plane-iron came bump against an indestructible knot. The landlord was near spraining his wrist, and I told him for heaven's sake to quit—the bed was soft enough to suit me, and I did not know how all the planing in the world could make eider down of a pine plank. So gathering up the shavings with another grin, and throwing them into the great stove in the middle of the room, he went about his business, and left me in a brown study.

I now took the measure of the bench, and found that it was a foot too short; but that could be mended with a chair. But it was a foot too narrow, and the other bench in the room was about four inches higher than the planed one—so there was no yoking them. I then placed the first bench lengthwise along the only clear space against the wall, leaving a little interval between, for my back to settle down in. But I soon found that there came such a draught of cold air over me from under the sill of the window, that this plan would never do at all, especially as another current from the rickety door met the one from the window, and both together formed a series of small whirlwinds in the immediate vicinity of the spot where I had thought to spend the night.

The devil fetch that harpooneer, thought I, but stop, couldn't I steal a march on him—bolt his door inside, and jump into his bed, not to be wakened by the most violent knockings? It seemed no bad idea; but upon second thoughts I dismissed it. For who could tell but what the next morning, so soon as I popped out of the room, the harpooneer might be standing in the entry, all ready to knock me down!

Still, looking round me again, and seeing no possible chance of spending a sufferable night unless in some other person's bed, I began to think that after all I might be cherishing unwarrantable prejudices against this unknown harpooneer. Thinks I, I'll wait awhile; he must be dropping in before long. I'll have a good look at him then, and perhaps we may become jolly good bedfellows after all—there's no telling.

But though the other boarders kept coming in by ones, twos, and threes, and going to bed, yet no sign of my harpooneer.

“Landlord!” said I, “what sort of a chap is he—does he always keep such late hours?” It was now hard upon twelve o'clock.

The landlord chuckled again with his lean chuckle, and seemed to be mightily tickled at something beyond my comprehension. “No,” he answered, “generally he's an early bird—airley to bed and airley to rise—yes, he's the bird what catches the worm. But to-night he went out a peddling, you see, and I don't see what on airth keeps him so late, unless, may be, he can't sell his head.”

“Can't sell his head?—What sort of a bamboozingly story is this you are telling me?” getting into a towering rage. “Do you pretend to say, landlord, that this harpooneer is actually engaged this blessed Saturday night, or rather Sunday morning, in peddling his head around this town?”

“That's precisely it,” said the landlord, “and I told him he couldn't sell it here, the market's overstocked.”

“With what?” shouted I.

“With heads to be sure; ain't there too many heads in the world?”

“I tell you what it is, landlord,” said I quite calmly, “you'd better stop spinning that yarn to me—I'm not green.”

“May be not,” taking out a stick and whittling a toothpick, “but I rayther guess you'll be done brown if that ere harpooneer hears you a slanderin' his head.”

“I'll break it for him,” said I, now flying into a passion again at this unaccountable farrago of the landlord's.

“It's broke a'ready,” said he.

“Broke,” said I—“ broke , do you mean?”

“Sartain, and that's the very reason he can't sell it, I guess.”

“Landlord,” said I, going up to him as cool as Mt. Hecla in a snow-storm—“landlord, stop whittling. You and I must understand one another, and that too without delay. I come to your house and want a bed; you tell me you can only give me half a one; that the other half belongs to a certain harpooneer. And about this harpooneer, whom I have not yet seen, you persist in telling me the most mystifying and exasperating stories tending to beget in me an uncomfortable feeling towards the man whom you design for my bedfellow—a sort of connexion, landlord, which is an intimate and confidential one in the highest degree. I now demand of you to speak out and tell me who and what this harpooneer is, and whether I shall be in all respects safe to spend the night with him. And in the first place, you will be so good as to unsay that story about selling his head, which if true I take to be good evidence that this harpooneer is stark mad, and I've no idea of sleeping with a madman; and you, sir, you I mean, landlord, you , sir, by trying to induce me to do so knowingly, would thereby render yourself liable to a criminal prosecution.”

“Wall,” said the landlord, fetching a long breath, “that's a purty long sarmon for a chap that rips a little now and then. But be easy, be easy, this here harpooneer I have been tellin' you of has just arrived from the south seas, where he bought up a lot of 'balmed New Zealand heads (great curios, you know), and he's sold all on 'em but one, and that one he's trying to sell to-night, cause to-morrow's Sunday, and it would not do to be sellin' human heads about the streets when folks is goin' to churches. He wanted to, last Sunday, but I stopped him just as he was goin' out of the door with four heads strung on a string, for all the airth like a string of inions.”

This account cleared up the otherwise unaccountable mystery, and showed that the landlord, after all, had had no idea of fooling me—but at the same time what could I think of a harpooneer who stayed out of a Saturday night clean into the holy Sabbath, engaged in such a cannibal business as selling the heads of dead idolators?

“Depend upon it, landlord, that harpooneer is a dangerous man.”

“He pays reg'lar,” was the rejoinder. “But come, it's getting dreadful late, you had better be turning flukes—it's a nice bed; Sal and me slept in that ere bed the night we were spliced. There's plenty of room for two to kick about in that bed; it's an almighty big bed that. Why, afore we give it up, Sal used to put our Sam and little Johnny in the foot of it. But I got a dreaming and sprawling about one night, and somehow, Sam got pitched on the floor, and came near breaking his arm. Arter that, Sal said it wouldn't do. Come along here, I'll give ye a glim in a jiffy;” and so saying he lighted a candle and held it towards me, offering to lead the way. But I stood irresolute; when looking at a clock in the corner, he exclaimed “I vum it's Sunday—you won't see that harpooneer to-night; he's come to anchor somewhere—come along then; do come; won't ye come?”

I considered the matter a moment, and then up stairs we went, and I was ushered into a small room, cold as a clam, and furnished, sure enough, with a prodigious bed, almost big enough indeed for any four harpooneers to sleep abreast.

“There,” said the landlord, placing the candle on a crazy old sea chest that did double duty as a wash-stand and centre table; “there, make yourself comfortable now, and good night to ye.” I turned round from eyeing the bed, but he had disappeared.

Folding back the counterpane, I stooped over the bed. Though none of the most elegant, it yet stood the scrutiny tolerably well. I then glanced round the room; and besides the bedstead and centre table, could see no other furniture belonging to the place, but a rude shelf, the four walls, and a papered fireboard representing a man striking a whale. Of things not properly belonging to the room, there was a hammock lashed up, and thrown upon the floor in one corner; also a large seaman's bag, containing the harpooneer's wardrobe, no doubt in lieu of a land trunk. Likewise, there was a parcel of outlandish bone fish hooks on the shelf over the fire-place, and a tall harpoon standing at the head of the bed.

But what is this on the chest? I took it up, and held it close to the light, and felt it, and smelt it, and tried every way possible to arrive at some satisfactory conclusion concerning it. I can compare it to nothing but a large door mat, ornamented at the edges with little tinkling tags something like the stained porcupine quills round an Indian moccasin. There was a hole or slit in the middle of this mat, as you see the same in South American ponchos. But could it be possible that any sober harpooneer would get into a door mat, and parade the streets of any Christian town in that sort of guise? I put it on, to try it, and it weighed me down like a hamper, being uncommonly shaggy and thick, and I thought a little damp, as though this mysterious harpooneer had been wearing it of a rainy day. I went up in it to a bit of glass stuck against the wall, and I never saw such a sight in my life. I tore myself out of it in such a hurry that I gave myself a kink in the neck.

I sat down on the side of the bed, and commenced thinking about this head-peddling harpooneer, and his door mat. After thinking some time on the bed-side, I got up and took off my monkey jacket, and then stood in the middle of the room thinking. I then took off my coat, and thought a little more in my shirt sleeves. But beginning to feel very cold now, half undressed as I was, and remembering what the landlord said about the harpooneer's not coming home at all that night, it being so very late, I made no more ado, but jumped out of my pantaloons and boots, and then blowing out the light tumbled into bed, and commended myself to the care of heaven.

Whether that mattress was stuffed with corn-cobs or broken crockery, there is no telling, but I rolled about a good deal, and could not sleep for a long time. At last I slid off into a light doze, and had pretty nearly made a good offing towards the land of Nod, when I heard a heavy footfall in the passage, and saw a glimmer of light come into the room from under the door.

Lord save me, thinks I, that must be the harpooneer, the infernal head-peddler. But I lay perfectly still, and resolved not to say a word till spoken to. Holding a light in one hand, and that identical New Zealand head in the other, the stranger entered the room, and without looking towards the bed, placed his candle a good way off from me on the floor in one corner, and then began working away at the knotted cords of the large bag I before spoke of as being in the room. I was all eagerness to see his face, but he kept it averted for some time while employed in unlacing the bag's mouth. This accomplished, however, he turned round—when, good heavens! what a sight! Such a face! It was of a dark, purplish, yellow colour, here and there stuck over with large blackish looking squares. Yes, it's just as I thought, he's a terrible bedfellow; he's been in a fight, got dreadfully cut, and here he is, just from the surgeon. But at that moment he chanced to turn his face so towards the light, that I plainly saw they could not be sticking-plasters at all, those black squares on his cheeks. They were stains of some sort or other. At first I knew not what to make of this; but soon an inkling of the truth occurred to me. I remembered a story of a white man—a whaleman too—who, falling among the cannibals, had been tattooed by them. I concluded that this harpooneer, in the course of his distant voyages, must have met with a similar adventure. And what is it, thought I, after all! It's only his outside; a man can be honest in any sort of skin. But then, what to make of his unearthly complexion, that part of it, I mean, lying round about, and completely independent of the squares of tattooing. To be sure, it might be nothing but a good coat of tropical tanning; but I never heard of a hot sun's tanning a white man into a purplish yellow one. However, I had never been in the South Seas; and perhaps the sun there produced these extraordinary effects upon the skin. Now, while all these ideas were passing through me like lightning, this harpooneer never noticed me at all. But, after some difficulty having opened his bag, he commenced fumbling in it, and presently pulled out a sort of tomahawk, and a seal-skin wallet with the hair on. Placing these on the old chest in the middle of the room, he then took the New Zealand head—a ghastly thing enough—and crammed it down into the bag. He now took off his hat—a new beaver hat—when I came nigh singing out with fresh surprise. There was no hair on his head—none to speak of at least—nothing but a small scalp-knot twisted up on his forehead. His bald purplish head now looked for all the world like a mildewed skull. Had not the stranger stood between me and the door, I would have bolted out of it quicker than ever I bolted a dinner.

Even as it was, I thought something of slipping out of the window, but it was the second floor back. I am no coward, but what to make of this head-peddling purple rascal altogether passed my comprehension. Ignorance is the parent of fear, and being completely nonplussed and confounded about the stranger, I confess I was now as much afraid of him as if it was the devil himself who had thus broken into my room at the dead of night. In fact, I was so afraid of him that I was not game enough just then to address him, and demand a satisfactory answer concerning what seemed inexplicable in him.

Meanwhile, he continued the business of undressing, and at last showed his chest and arms. As I live, these covered parts of him were checkered with the same squares as his face; his back, too, was all over the same dark squares; he seemed to have been in a Thirty Years' War, and just escaped from it with a sticking-plaster shirt. Still more, his very legs were marked, as if a parcel of dark green frogs were running up the trunks of young palms. It was now quite plain that he must be some abominable savage or other shipped aboard of a whaleman in the South Seas, and so landed in this Christian country. I quaked to think of it. A peddler of heads too—perhaps the heads of his own brothers. He might take a fancy to mine—heavens! look at that tomahawk!

But there was no time for shuddering, for now the savage went about something that completely fascinated my attention, and convinced me that he must indeed be a heathen. Going to his heavy grego, or wrapall, or dreadnaught, which he had previously hung on a chair, he fumbled in the pockets, and produced at length a curious little deformed image with a hunch on its back, and exactly the colour of a three days' old Congo baby. Remembering the embalmed head, at first I almost thought that this black manikin was a real baby preserved in some similar manner. But seeing that it was not at all limber, and that it glistened a good deal like polished ebony, I concluded that it must be nothing but a wooden idol, which indeed it proved to be. For now the savage goes up to the empty fire-place, and removing the papered fire-board, sets up this little hunch-backed image, like a tenpin, between the andirons. The chimney jambs and all the bricks inside were very sooty, so that I thought this fire-place made a very appropriate little shrine or chapel for his Congo idol.

I now screwed my eyes hard towards the half hidden image, feeling but ill at ease meantime—to see what was next to follow. First he takes about a double handful of shavings out of his grego pocket, and places them carefully before the idol; then laying a bit of ship biscuit on top and applying the flame from the lamp, he kindled the shavings into a sacrificial blaze. Presently, after many hasty snatches into the fire, and still hastier withdrawals of his fingers (whereby he seemed to be scorching them badly), he at last succeeded in drawing out the biscuit; then blowing off the heat and ashes a little, he made a polite offer of it to the little negro. But the little devil did not seem to fancy such dry sort of fare at all; he never moved his lips. All these strange antics were accompanied by still stranger guttural noises from the devotee, who seemed to be praying in a sing-song or else singing some pagan psalmody or other, during which his face twitched about in the most unnatural manner. At last extinguishing the fire, he took the idol up very unceremoniously, and bagged it again in his grego pocket as carelessly as if he were a sportsman bagging a dead woodcock.

All these queer proceedings increased my uncomfortableness, and seeing him now exhibiting strong symptoms of concluding his business operations, and jumping into bed with me, I thought it was high time, now or never, before the light was put out, to break the spell in which I had so long been bound.

But the interval I spent in deliberating what to say, was a fatal one. Taking up his tomahawk from the table, he examined the head of it for an instant, and then holding it to the light, with his mouth at the handle, he puffed out great clouds of tobacco smoke. The next moment the light was extinguished, and this wild cannibal, tomahawk between his teeth, sprang into bed with me. I sang out, I could not help it now; and giving a sudden grunt of astonishment he began feeling me.

Stammering out something, I knew not what, I rolled away from him against the wall, and then conjured him, whoever or whatever he might be, to keep quiet, and let me get up and light the lamp again. But his guttural responses satisfied me at once that he but ill comprehended my meaning.

“Who-e debel you?”—he at last said—“you no speak-e, dam-me, I kill-e.” And so saying the lighted tomahawk began flourishing about me in the dark.

“Landlord, for God's sake, Peter Coffin!” shouted I. “Landlord! Watch! Coffin! Angels! save me!”

“Speak-e! tell-ee me who-ee be, or dam-me, I kill-e!” again growled the cannibal, while his horrid flourishings of the tomahawk scattered the hot tobacco ashes about me till I thought my linen would get on fire. But thank heaven, at that moment the landlord came into the room light in hand, and leaping from the bed I ran up to him.

“Don't be afraid now,” said he, grinning again, “Queequeg here wouldn't harm a hair of your head.”

“Stop your grinning,” shouted I, “and why didn't you tell me that that infernal harpooneer was a cannibal?”

“I thought ye know'd it;—didn't I tell ye, he was a peddlin' heads around town?—but turn flukes again and go to sleep. Queequeg, look here—you sabbee me, I sabbee—you this man sleepe you—you sabbee?”

“Me sabbee plenty”—grunted Queequeg, puffing away at his pipe and sitting up in bed.

“You gettee in,” he added, motioning to me with his tomahawk, and throwing the clothes to one side. He really did this in not only a civil but a really kind and charitable way. I stood looking at him a moment. For all his tattooings he was on the whole a clean, comely looking cannibal. What's all this fuss I have been making about, thought I to myself—the man's a human being just as I am: he has just as much reason to fear me, as I have to be afraid of him. Better sleep with a sober cannibal than a drunken Christian.

“Landlord,” said I, “tell him to stash his tomahawk there, or pipe, or whatever you call it; tell him to stop smoking, in short, and I will turn in with him. But I don't fancy having a man smoking in bed with me. It's dangerous. Besides, I ain't insured.”

This being told to Queequeg, he at once complied, and again politely motioned me to get into bed—rolling over to one side as much as to say—“I won't touch a leg of ye.”

“Good night, landlord,” said I, “you may go.”

I turned in, and never slept better in my life.
