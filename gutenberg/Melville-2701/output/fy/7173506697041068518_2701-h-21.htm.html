HAADSTIK 107. De timmerman.

Sit dysels sultanysk tusken de moannen fan Saturnus, en nim allinne de hege abstrahearre minske; en hy liket in wûnder, in grutheid en in wee. Mar fan itselde punt, nim it minskdom yn 'e massa, en foar it grutste part lykje se in skare fan ûnnedige duplikaten, sawol hjoeddeistich as erflik. Mar it meast nederich wie er, en fier fan in foarbyld fan 'e hege, minsklike abstraksje; de timmerman fan de Pequod wie gjin duplikaat; dêrfandinne, hy komt no yn persoan op dit poadium.

Lykas alle seegeande skippentimmerlju, en benammen dy dy't ta de walfiskfeart hearden, wie er, ta in beskate off-handed, praktyske mjitte, likegoed belibbe yn tal fan hannel en roppingen ûnderpân oan syn eigen; de timmermansstriid is de oerâlde en útsûnderlike stam fan al dy tal fan ambachten dy't min of mear te krijen hawwe mei hout as helpstof. Mar, neist de tapassing op him fan 'e generyske opmerking hjirboppe, wie dizze timmerman fan' e Pequod bûtengewoan effisjint yn dy tûzen nammeleaze meganyske needgefallen dy't hieltyd weromkamen yn in grut skip, op in trije of fjouwer jier reis, yn ûnbeskaafde en fiere see . Om net te sprekken fan syn reewilligens yn gewoane plichten: - kachelboaten reparearje, spjirren spjirren, de foarm fan ûnhandich blêdden herfoarmje, bolle-eagen yn it dek stekke, of nije beamsikers yn 'e sydplanken, en oare ferskate saken mear direkt oangeande syn bysûndere bedriuw; hy wie boppedat sûnder twifel saakkundich yn allerhande tsjinstridige oanlegenheden, sawol nuttich as grillich.

It iene grutte poadium dêr't er al syn ferskate ûnderdielen sa mannichfâldich opfierde, wie syn vice-bank; in lange rûge swiere tafel ynrjochte mei ferskate ûndeugden, fan ferskillende grutte, en sawol fan izer as fan hout. Op alle tiden, útsein as walfisken njonken wiene, waard dizze bank feilich tsjin de efterkant fan 'e Try-works slein.

In fêstlizzende pin wurdt te grut fûn om maklik yn syn gat te stekken: de timmerman klapt it yn ien fan syn altyd klearebare ûndeugden, en slacht it daliks lytser. In ferlerne lânfûgel fan frjemde plumage dwaalt oan board, en wurdt in finzene makke: út skjin skearde stokken fan rjochte walfiskbonke, en dwersbalken fan sperma-ifoaren, makket de timmerman der in pagoade-sykjende koai foar. In oarman ferstuiket syn pols: de timmerman makket in kalmerend lotion. Stubb socht nei fermiljoenstjerren dy't op it blêd fan syn elk oar skildere waarden; skroef elke oar yn syn grutte ûndeugd fan hout, de timmerman symmetrysk leveret it stjerrebyld. In seeman fynt it sin om earringen fan haaiebonken te dragen: de timmerman boar yn syn earen. In oar hat de kiespijn: de timmerman lûkt knyntsjes út, en klapt mei de iene hân op syn bank, freget him dêr te sitten; mar de earme keardel winkt ûnbehannelelik ûnder de net ôfsletten operaasje; dwarreljend om 'e stâle fan syn houten skroef, tekenet de timmerman him dêr yn 'e kaak te klappen, as er him de tosk lûke wol.

Sa waard dizze timmerman op alle punten taret, en likegoed ûnferskillich en sûnder respekt yn alles. Tosken hy rekkene stikjes ivoar; hollen achte er mar topblokken; manlju sels hold er licht foar capstans. Mar wylst no op sa'n wiid in fjild sa ferskillend folbrocht en mei sa'n libbenens fan saakkundigens yn him ek; dit alles soe lykje te pleitsjen wat ûngewoane vivacity fan yntelliginsje. Mar net krekt sa. Want neat wie dizze man opmerkliker as foar in beskate ûnpersoanlike stûlheit as it wie; ûnpersoanlik, sis ik; om't it sa ôfskaad yn 'e omlizzende ûneinige dingen, dat it ien like mei de algemiene stevichheid te waarnimmen yn 'e hiele sichtbere wrâld; dy't, wylst se pauzeleas aktyf is yn ûntelde modi, noch altyd har frede hâldt, en jo negearret, hoewol jo fûneminten grave foar katedralen. Dochs wie dy heal ôfgryslike stûlheit yn him, dy't ek, sa't it bliek, in alles-ferrifeljende herteleazens befette; - dochs wie it soms raar oerstutsen, mei in âlde, krukachtige, antediluviaanske, piepende humoristyk, no net ûnstreken en dan mei in beskate grize witness; sokken dy't tsjinne kinnen hawwe om de tiid troch te bringen tidens de middernachtwacht op it bebaarde foarstek fan 'e arke fan Noach. Wie it dat dizze âlde timmerman in libbenslange swalker west hie, hwaens folle rôljen, hinne en wer, net allinne gjin moas sammele hie; mar hwat mear is, hie ôfskuorre hokker lytse uterlike kloften him oarspronklik oangien hawwe kinnen? Hy wie in stript abstrakt; in unfractioned yntegraal; kompromiseloos as in nijberne poppe; libje sûnder foarbedoelde ferwizing nei dizze wrâld of de folgjende. Jo soene hast sizze kinne, dat dy frjemde kompromisleazens yn him in soarte fan ûnferstân behelle; want yn syn mannich ambachten like er net sa folle te wurkjen troch ferstân of ynstinkt, of gewoan om't er dêr leard wie, of troch hokker ferminging fan al dizze, sels of ûnjildich; mar allinne troch in soarte fan dôf en stom, spontaan letterlik proses. Hy wie in suvere manipulator; syn harsens, as er der oait ien hân hie, moast betiid yn de spieren fan syn fingers sûpe. Hy wie as ien fan dy ûnferstannige, mar dochs tige brûkbere, multum in parvo , Sheffield-ûntwerpen, oannommen dat it eksterieur - hoewol in bytsje opswolle - fan in gewoan bûsmes; mar befetsje, net allinnich blêden fan ferskate maten, mar ek screw-drivers, cork-screws, pincet, awls, pennen, linialen, nail-filers, countersinkers. Dus, as syn superieuren de timmerman brûke woene foar in skroefdraaier, moasten se allinich dat diel fan him iepenje, en de skroef wie fluch: of as foar pincet, nim him by de skonken, en dêr wiene.

Dochs, lykas earder oanjûn, wie dizze omnitoolde, iepen-en-sluten timmerman ommers gjin inkelde masine fan in automaat. As er gjin mienskiplike siel yn him hie, hie er in subtyl eat dat op ien of oare manier anomaly syn plicht die. Wat dat wie, of essensje fan kwiksulver, of in pear drippen hertshoarn, is net te sizzen. Mar dêr wie it; en dêr hie it no sa'n sechstich jier of mear bleaun. En dit wie it, datselde ûnferantwurdlike, slûchslimme libbensbeginsel yn him; dit wie it, dat hâldde him in grut part fan 'e tiid te soliloquiing; mar allinne as in ûnferstannich tsjil, dat ek brommerjend soliloquizes; of leaver, syn liif wie in wachtkiste en dizze soliloquizer dêre op wacht, en praette de hiele tiid om himsels wekker te hâlden.

HAADSTIK 110. Queequeg yn syn kiste.

By sykaksje die bliken dat de lêsten yn it laad sleine fûten hielendal deugd wiene, en dat it lek fierder ôf moast. Sa, it wie rêstich waar, se bruts út djipper en djipper, fersteurend de sliep fan 'e grutte grûn-tier konten; en fan dy swarte middernacht ôf stjoerde dy gigantyske mollen yn it deiljocht boppe. Sa djip gyngen se; en sa âld, en fersmoarge, en weed it aspekt fan 'e ûnderste puncheons, dat jo hast neist sochten nei wat skimmele hoekstiennen vat mei munten fan kaptein Noach, mei kopyen fan' e pleatste plakkaten, omdôch de fereale âlde wrâld warskôgje foar de floed . Tierce nei tierce, ek, ek wetter, en brea, en fleis, en skodden fan stielden, en izeren bondels hoopt, oant einlings oan 'e lêste depels lestich wiene om oer te kommen; en de holle romp galmde ûnder de foet, as stiene jo oer lege katakomben, en rôle en rôle yn 'e see as in loftfrachte demijohn. Topswier wie it skip as in dinerleaze studint mei alle Aristoteles yn 'e holle. Goed wie it dat de Typhoons har doe net besochten.

No, op dit stuit wie it dat myn earme heidenske maat, en snelle boezemfreon, Queequeg, waard beslach nommen mei in koarts, dy't him tichtby syn einleaze ein brocht.

Mei it sein, dat yn dizze ropping fan de walfiskfangst, sinecures binne ûnbekend; weardichheid en gefaar geane hân yn hân; oant jo kaptein wurde, hoe heger jo opkomme, hoe hurder jo wurkje. Sa mei de earme Queequeg, dy't as harpoonier net allinne de hiele grime fan 'e libbene walfisk tsjinkomme moat, mar - sa't wy earne oars sjoen hawwe - syn deaden werom yn 'e rôljende see opstekke; en op it lêst del yn 'e tsjusterens fan 'e skuorre, en de hiele dei bitter swittend yn dy ûndergrûnske opsluting, resoluut omgean mei de ûnhandichste fusten en soargje foar har opslach. Om koart te wêzen, ûnder walfiskfarders binne de harpooniers de hâlders, sa neamd.

Arme Queequeg! doe't it skip omtrint heal ûntboarst wie, hiene jo oer it lûkke bûgd moatten en dêr op him delsjoen hawwe; dêr't de tatoeëerde wyld, ôfstutsen oan syn wollen laden, tusken dat focht en slym hinne krûpte, as in griene flekke hagedis ûnder in put. En in put, of in iishûs, it bliek him op ien of oare manier, earme heiden; dêr't er, frjemd om te sizzen, troch alle waarmte fan syn switten, in skriklike kjeld krige dy't yn koarts ferrûn; en op 't lêst lei him, nei in pear dagen lijen, yn syn hangmat, tichteby de doar fan 'e dea. Hoe fergriemde er en fergriemde er yn dy pear lange dagen, oant der mar net folle mear fan him like as syn ramt en tatoeaazje. Mar as al it oare yn him dûnker en syn wangbonken skerper waerden, liken syn eagen dochs foller en foller te wurden; hja waerden fan in frjemde sêftens fan glâns; en myld mar djip seach út nei dy fan syn sykte, in wûnderlik tsjûgenis fan dy ûnstjerlike sûnens yn him dy't koe net stjerre, of ferswakke wurde. En as sirkels op it wetter, dy't, as se swakker wurde, útwreidzje; sa liken syn eagen rûn en rûn, as de ringen fan 'e ivichheid. In eangst dy't net neamd wurde kin, soe oer jo stelle as jo oan 'e kant fan dizze ôfnimmende wyld sieten, en sa frjemde dingen yn syn gesicht seach, lykas elk seach dy't omstanners wiene doe't Zoroaster stoar. Want hwat wier wûnderlik en freeslik yn 'e minske is, is noch nea yn wurden of boeken brocht. En it tichterby kommen fan 'e Dea, dy't likegoed alles nivelleret, likegoed makket yndruk op allegearre mei in lêste iepenbiering, dy't allinnich in skriuwer út 'e deaden adekwaat fertelle koe. Dat - lit ús it nochris sizze - gjin stjerrende Chaldeeër of Gryk hie hegere en hilliger tinzen as dy, waans geheimsinnige skaden jo oer it antlit fan 'e earme Queequeg seagen krûpen, wylst er rêstich yn syn swaaiende hangmat lei, en de rôljende see wie sêft skommele him nei syn lêste rêst, en it ûnsichtbere wetterfloed fan 'e oseaan tilde him heger en heger nei syn bestimde himel.

Net in man fan de bemanning mar joech him op; en, as foar Queequeg sels, wat er tocht fan syn saak waard mei geweld sjen litten troch in nijsgjirrich geunst er frege. Hy rôp ien nei him yn 'e grize moarnswacht, doe't de dei krekt oanbriek, en naam syn hân, sei dat hy wylst yn Nantucket kâns hie om guon lytse kano's fan donker hout te sjen, lykas it rike oarlochshout fan syn berte-eilân ; en op ûndersiik hie er leard dat alle walfiskfarders dy't yn Nantucket stoaren, yn dy selde tsjustere kano's lein wiene, en dat de ferbylding om sa lein te wurden him in protte wille hie; want it wie net oars as de gewoante fan syn eigen ras, dy't, nei it balsemjen fan in deade strider, him útstrekte yn syn kano en him sa liet om fuort te sweevjen nei de stjerlike arsjipels; want net allinne leauwe se dat de stjerren eilannen binne, mar dat fier foarby alle sichtbere hoarizons har eigen mylde, ûnkontinintearre see mei de blauwe himel oerstreame; en sa foarmje de wite brekers fan de molkwei. Hy foege der oan, dat er huvere by de gedachte om yn syn hangmat begroeven te wurden, neffens de gewoane see-gewoante, as wat ferfelends nei de dea-fertearjende haaien slingere. Nee: hy woe in kano lykas dy fan Nantucket, des te nofliker foar him, as in walfiskman, dat as in walfiskboat dizze kistkano's sûnder kiel wiene; al wie dat mar ûnwisse stjoering, en in protte leeway nei de dimmene ieu.

No, doe't dizze nuvere omstannichheid efteren bekend makke waerd, krige de timmerman daliks befel om Queequeg's bod te dwaan, hwat it ek koe. D'r wie wat heidensk, kistkleurich âld timmerhout oan board, dat, op in lange foarige reis, ôfsnien wie út 'e aboriginal boskjes fan 'e Lackaday-eilannen, en fan dizze donkere planken waard oanrikkemandearre om de kiste te meitsjen. Net earder waard de timmerman op 'e hichte brocht fan 'e oarder, as hy syn hearskippij naam, hy fuortendaliks mei al de ûnferskillige promptiteit fan syn karakter, gie de foarsizzing yn en naam Queequeg's mjitte mei grutte krektens, en regelmjittich kalke Queequeg's persoan wylst hy de regel ferskowe.

"Ach! earme keardel! hy sil no stjerre moatte,” ejakulearre de seeman fan Long Island.

Geande nei syn vice-bank, de timmerman foar gemak en algemiene ferwizing, no oerdragend op mjitten de krekte lingte fan de kiste soe wêze, en doe makke de oerdracht permanint troch snije twa kerven oan syn úteinen. Dit dien, hy sette de planken en syn ark gear, en oan it wurk.

Doe't de lêste spiker slein wie, en it deksel behoarlik ôfskaafd en oanbrocht wie, skoudere er de kiste licht en gyng der mei nei foaren, freegjend oft se der noch yn dy rjochting klear foar wiene.

By it hearren fan 'e ferûntrêste, mar heal humoristyske gjalp, dêr't de lju op it dek begûnen de kiste fuort te driuwen, gebea Queequeg, ta elk syn ferbjustering, dat it ding daliks by him brocht wurde moast, en der waard him ek net ûntkenne; sjoen dat, fan alle stjerliken, guon stjerrende minsken de meast tirannike binne; en wis, om't se ús ynkoarten foar altyd sa'n bytsje lestich falle sille, moatte de earme keardels oerlitte wurde.

Queequeg bûgde yn syn hangmat, seach lang de kiste mei in opmerksum each. Hy rôp doe om syn harpoen, liet der de houten stok derút lûke en liet doe it izeren diel yn 'e kiste pleatse tegearre mei ien fan 'e peddels fan syn boat. Allegearre op eigen fersyk waarden dan ek biskuiten rûn de kanten fan binnen: in flesse swiet wetter waard oan 'e kop pleatst, en in lyts pûdsje houtige ierde skreaude yn' e hold oan 'e foet; en in stik sylbêd dat oprôle waard foar in kessen, smeekte Queequeg no om yn syn lêste bêd ophelle te wurden, dat er syn treasten besykje mocht, as der ien wie. Hy lei sûnder bewegen in pear minuten, doe fertelde ien om nei syn tas te gean en syn lytse god, Yojo, út te heljen. Doe krúste er syn earms op syn boarst mei Yojo der tusken, hy rôp om it kistdeksel (luik neamde hy it) om oer him te pleatsen. It hollediel draaide om mei in learen skarnier, en dêr lei Queequeg yn syn kiste mei in bytsje mar syn gearstalde gesicht yn it each. "Rarmai" (it sil dwaan; it is maklik), murmelde hy op 't lêst, en tekene om yn syn hammat te ferfangen.

Mar ear't dat dien wie, kaam Pip, dy't al dy tiid slûchslimme yn 'e buert west hie, tichter by him dêr't er lei, en mei sêft snikken, naam him by de hân; yn 'e oare, mei syn tamboeryn.

"Earme rover! sille jo noait dien hawwe mei al dit wurge sweevjen? wêr gean jo no hinne? Mar as de streamen jim drage nei dy swiete Antillen dêr’t de strannen allinnich mar mei wetterlelies slein wurde, sille jim dan ien lyts boadskip foar my dwaan? Sykje ien Pip, dy't no al lang fermist is: ik tink dat er yn dy fiere Antillen is. As jimme him fine, treast him den; want hy moat tige tryst wêze; foar sjo! hy hat syn tamboeryn efterlitten; - ik fûn it. Rig-a-dig, dig, dig! No, Queequeg, stjerre; en ik scil jimme dyn stjerrende mars slaan."

"Ik haw heard," mompele Starbuck, en seach nei de skûtel, "dat yn heftige koarts, manlju, alle ûnwittendheid, yn âlde tongen praten hawwe; en dat as it mystearje ûndersocht wurdt, it altyd bliken docht dat yn har alhiel fergetten bernetiid dy âlde tongen wier yn har harken sprutsen wiene troch guon ferhevene gelearden. Sa, nei myn leaf leauwe, bringt earme Pip, yn dizze frjemde swietens fan syn waansin, himelske bonnen fan al ús himelske huzen. Wêr learde er dat, mar dêr? - Hark! hy praat wer: mar no wylder.”

"Formulier twa en twa! Lit ús in generaal fan him meitsje! Ho, wêr is syn harpoen? Lege it hjir oer. - Rig-a-dig, dig, dig! huzza! Och foar in spielpik dy't no op 'e holle sit en kraait! Queequeg stjert spul! - tink der om; Queequeg stjert spul! - sjoch dêr goed op; Queequeg dies spultsje! ik sis; spultsje, spultsje, spultsje! mar basis lytse Pip, hy stoar in leffe; stoar al in rilling; - op Pip! Harkje jo; as jim Pip fine, sis dan oan alle Antillen dat er in flecht is; in leffe, in leffe, in leffe! Fertel har dat er fan in walfiskboat sprong! Ik soe myn tamboeryn noait oer basis Pip slaan, en him generaal wolkom, as er hjir nochris stjerre soe. Nee nee! skamje alle leffe - skamje harren! Lit se fersûpe as Pip, dy't fan in walfiskboat sprong. Skamte! skamte!"

By dit alles lei Queequeg mei sletten eagen, as yn in dream. Pip waard fuortfierd, en de sike waard ferfongen yn syn hangmat.

Mar no't er blykber alle tarieding op de dea makke hie; no't syn kiste in goede fit wie, kaam Queequeg ynienen byinoar; al gau like der gjin ferlet fan de timmermanskiste: en doe, doe't guon harren bliid ferrassing útsprutsen, sei er, yn wêzen, dat de oarsaak fan syn hommels herstel wie dit; - op in kritysk momint hie er krekt in bytsje plicht weromroppen. oan de wâl, dy't er ûngedien liet; en wie dêrom fan gedachten feroare oer it stjerren: hy koe noch net stjerre, sei er. Se fregen him dan, oft te libjen of te stjerren wie in kwestje fan syn eigen soevereine wil en wille. Hy antwurde, wiswier. Yn ien wurd, it wie Queequeg syn eigensinnigens, dat as in man syn miening makke om te libjen, gewoane sykte him net deadzje koe: neat oars as in walfisk, of in storm, of ien of oare gewelddiedige, ûnbestjoerbere, ûnyntelligente ferneatiger fan dat soarte.

No, der is dit opmerklike ferskil tusken wyld en beskaafd; that while a sick, civilized man may be six months convalescing, generally speaking, a sick savage is almost half-well again in a day. So, in good time my Queequeg gained strength; and at length after sitting on the windlass for a few indolent days (but eating with a vigorous appetite) he suddenly leaped to his feet, threw out his arms and legs, gave himself a good stretching, yawned a little bit, and then springing into the head of his hoisted boat, and poising a harpoon, pronounced himself fit for a fight.

With a wild whimsiness, he now used his coffin for a sea-chest; and emptying into it his canvas bag of clothes, set them in order there. Many spare hours he spent, in carving the lid with all manner of grotesque figures and drawings; and it seemed that hereby he was striving, in his rude way, to copy parts of the twisted tattooing on his body. And this tattooing had been the work of a departed prophet and seer of his island, who, by those hieroglyphic marks, had written out on his body a complete theory of the heavens and the earth, and a mystical treatise on the art of attaining truth; so that Queequeg in his own proper person was a riddle to unfold; a wondrous work in one volume; but whose mysteries not even himself could read, though his own live heart beat against them; and these mysteries were therefore destined in the end to moulder away with the living parchment whereon they were inscribed, and so be unsolved to the last. And this thought it must have been which suggested to Ahab that wild exclamation of his, when one morning turning away from surveying poor Queequeg—“Oh, devilish tantalization of the gods!”





CHAPTER 111. The Pacific.

When gliding by the Bashee isles we emerged at last upon the great South Sea; were it not for other things, I could have greeted my dear Pacific with uncounted thanks, for now the long supplication of my youth was answered; that serene ocean rolled eastwards from me a thousand leagues of blue.

There is, one knows not what sweet mystery about this sea, whose gently awful stirrings seem to speak of some hidden soul beneath; like those fabled undulations of the Ephesian sod over the buried Evangelist St. John. And meet it is, that over these sea-pastures, wide-rolling watery prairies and Potters' Fields of all four continents, the waves should rise and fall, and ebb and flow unceasingly; for here, millions of mixed shades and shadows, drowned dreams, somnambulisms, reveries; all that we call lives and souls, lie dreaming, dreaming, still; tossing like slumberers in their beds; the ever-rolling waves but made so by their restlessness.

To any meditative Magian rover, this serene Pacific, once beheld, must ever after be the sea of his adoption. It rolls the midmost waters of the world, the Indian ocean and Atlantic being but its arms. The same waves wash the moles of the new-built Californian towns, but yesterday planted by the recentest race of men, and lave the faded but still gorgeous skirts of Asiatic lands, older than Abraham; while all between float milky-ways of coral isles, and low-lying, endless, unknown Archipelagoes, and impenetrable Japans. Thus this mysterious, divine Pacific zones the world's whole bulk about; makes all coasts one bay to it; seems the tide-beating heart of earth. Lifted by those eternal swells, you needs must own the seductive god, bowing your head to Pan.

But few thoughts of Pan stirred Ahab's brain, as standing like an iron statue at his accustomed place beside the mizen rigging, with one nostril he unthinkingly snuffed the sugary musk from the Bashee isles (in whose sweet woods mild lovers must be walking), and with the other consciously inhaled the salt breath of the new found sea; that sea in which the hated White Whale must even then be swimming. Launched at length upon these almost final waters, and gliding towards the Japanese cruising-ground, the old man's purpose intensified itself. His firm lips met like the lips of a vice; the Delta of his forehead's veins swelled like overladen brooks; in his very sleep, his ringing cry ran through the vaulted hull, “Stern all! the White Whale spouts thick blood!”





CHAPTER 112. The Blacksmith.

Availing himself of the mild, summer-cool weather that now reigned in these latitudes, and in preparation for the peculiarly active pursuits shortly to be anticipated, Perth, the begrimed, blistered old blacksmith, had not removed his portable forge to the hold again, after concluding his contributory work for Ahab's leg, but still retained it on deck, fast lashed to ringbolts by the foremast; being now almost incessantly invoked by the headsmen, and harpooneers, and bowsmen to do some little job for them; altering, or repairing, or new shaping their various weapons and boat furniture. Often he would be surrounded by an eager circle, all waiting to be served; holding boat-spades, pike-heads, harpoons, and lances, and jealously watching his every sooty movement, as he toiled. Nevertheless, this old man's was a patient hammer wielded by a patient arm. No murmur, no impatience, no petulance did come from him. Silent, slow, and solemn; bowing over still further his chronically broken back, he toiled away, as if toil were life itself, and the heavy beating of his hammer the heavy beating of his heart. And so it was.—Most miserable!

A peculiar walk in this old man, a certain slight but painful appearing yawing in his gait, had at an early period of the voyage excited the curiosity of the mariners. And to the importunity of their persisted questionings he had finally given in; and so it came to pass that every one now knew the shameful story of his wretched fate.

Belated, and not innocently, one bitter winter's midnight, on the road running between two country towns, the blacksmith half-stupidly felt the deadly numbness stealing over him, and sought refuge in a leaning, dilapidated barn. The issue was, the loss of the extremities of both feet. Out of this revelation, part by part, at last came out the four acts of the gladness, and the one long, and as yet uncatastrophied fifth act of the grief of his life's drama.

He was an old man, who, at the age of nearly sixty, had postponedly encountered that thing in sorrow's technicals called ruin. He had been an artisan of famed excellence, and with plenty to do; owned a house and garden; embraced a youthful, daughter-like, loving wife, and three blithe, ruddy children; every Sunday went to a cheerful-looking church, planted in a grove. But one night, under cover of darkness, and further concealed in a most cunning disguisement, a desperate burglar slid into his happy home, and robbed them all of everything. And darker yet to tell, the blacksmith himself did ignorantly conduct this burglar into his family's heart. It was the Bottle Conjuror! Upon the opening of that fatal cork, forth flew the fiend, and shrivelled up his home. Now, for prudent, most wise, and economic reasons, the blacksmith's shop was in the basement of his dwelling, but with a separate entrance to it; so that always had the young and loving healthy wife listened with no unhappy nervousness, but with vigorous pleasure, to the stout ringing of her young-armed old husband's hammer; whose reverberations, muffled by passing through the floors and walls, came up to her, not unsweetly, in her nursery; and so, to stout Labor's iron lullaby, the blacksmith's infants were rocked to slumber.

Oh, woe on woe! Oh, Death, why canst thou not sometimes be timely? Hadst thou taken this old blacksmith to thyself ere his full ruin came upon him, then had the young widow had a delicious grief, and her orphans a truly venerable, legendary sire to dream of in their after years; and all of them a care-killing competency. But Death plucked down some virtuous elder brother, on whose whistling daily toil solely hung the responsibilities of some other family, and left the worse than useless old man standing, till the hideous rot of life should make him easier to harvest.

Why tell the whole? The blows of the basement hammer every day grew more and more between; and each blow every day grew fainter than the last; the wife sat frozen at the window, with tearless eyes, glitteringly gazing into the weeping faces of her children; the bellows fell; the forge choked up with cinders; the house was sold; the mother dived down into the long church-yard grass; her children twice followed her thither; and the houseless, familyless old man staggered off a vagabond in crape; his every woe unreverenced; his grey head a scorn to flaxen curls!

Death seems the only desirable sequel for a career like this; but Death is only a launching into the region of the strange Untried; it is but the first salutation to the possibilities of the immense Remote, the Wild, the Watery, the Unshored; therefore, to the death-longing eyes of such men, who still have left in them some interior compunctions against suicide, does the all-contributed and all-receptive ocean alluringly spread forth his whole plain of unimaginable, taking terrors, and wonderful, new-life adventures; and from the hearts of infinite Pacifics, the thousand mermaids sing to them—“Come hither, broken-hearted; here is another life without the guilt of intermediate death; here are wonders supernatural, without dying for them. Come hither! bury thyself in a life which, to your now equally abhorred and abhorring, landed world, is more oblivious than death. Come hither! put up thy gravestone, too, within the churchyard, and come hither, till we marry thee!”

Hearkening to these voices, East and West, by early sunrise, and by fall of eve, the blacksmith's soul responded, Aye, I come! And so Perth went a-whaling.





CHAPTER 113. The Forge.

With matted beard, and swathed in a bristling shark-skin apron, about mid-day, Perth was standing between his forge and anvil, the latter placed upon an iron-wood log, with one hand holding a pike-head in the coals, and with the other at his forge's lungs, when Captain Ahab came along, carrying in his hand a small rusty-looking leathern bag. While yet a little distance from the forge, moody Ahab paused; till at last, Perth, withdrawing his iron from the fire, began hammering it upon the anvil—the red mass sending off the sparks in thick hovering flights, some of which flew close to Ahab.

“Are these thy Mother Carey's chickens, Perth? they are always flying in thy wake; birds of good omen, too, but not to all;—look here, they burn; but thou—thou liv'st among them without a scorch.”

“Because I am scorched all over, Captain Ahab,” answered Perth, resting for a moment on his hammer; “I am past scorching; not easily can'st thou scorch a scar.”

“Well, well; no more. Thy shrunk voice sounds too calmly, sanely woeful to me. In no Paradise myself, I am impatient of all misery in others that is not mad. Thou should'st go mad, blacksmith; say, why dost thou not go mad? How can'st thou endure without being mad? Do the heavens yet hate thee, that thou can'st not go mad?—What wert thou making there?”

“Welding an old pike-head, sir; there were seams and dents in it.”

“And can'st thou make it all smooth again, blacksmith, after such hard usage as it had?”

“I think so, sir.”

“And I suppose thou can'st smoothe almost any seams and dents; never mind how hard the metal, blacksmith?”

“Aye, sir, I think I can; all seams and dents but one.”

“Look ye here, then,” cried Ahab, passionately advancing, and leaning with both hands on Perth's shoulders; “look ye here— here —can ye smoothe out a seam like this, blacksmith,” sweeping one hand across his ribbed brow; “if thou could'st, blacksmith, glad enough would I lay my head upon thy anvil, and feel thy heaviest hammer between my eyes. Answer! Can'st thou smoothe this seam?”

“Oh! that is the one, sir! Said I not all seams and dents but one?”

“Aye, blacksmith, it is the one; aye, man, it is unsmoothable; for though thou only see'st it here in my flesh, it has worked down into the bone of my skull— that is all wrinkles! But, away with child's play; no more gaffs and pikes to-day. Look ye here!” jingling the leathern bag, as if it were full of gold coins. “I, too, want a harpoon made; one that a thousand yoke of fiends could not part, Perth; something that will stick in a whale like his own fin-bone. There's the stuff,” flinging the pouch upon the anvil. “Look ye, blacksmith, these are the gathered nail-stubbs of the steel shoes of racing horses.”

“Horse-shoe stubbs, sir? Why, Captain Ahab, thou hast here, then, the best and stubbornest stuff we blacksmiths ever work.”

“I know it, old man; these stubbs will weld together like glue from the melted bones of murderers. Quick! forge me the harpoon. And forge me first, twelve rods for its shank; then wind, and twist, and hammer these twelve together like the yarns and strands of a tow-line. Quick! I'll blow the fire.”

When at last the twelve rods were made, Ahab tried them, one by one, by spiralling them, with his own hand, round a long, heavy iron bolt. “A flaw!” rejecting the last one. “Work that over again, Perth.”

This done, Perth was about to begin welding the twelve into one, when Ahab stayed his hand, and said he would weld his own iron. As, then, with regular, gasping hems, he hammered on the anvil, Perth passing to him the glowing rods, one after the other, and the hard pressed forge shooting up its intense straight flame, the Parsee passed silently, and bowing over his head towards the fire, seemed invoking some curse or some blessing on the toil. But, as Ahab looked up, he slid aside.

“What's that bunch of lucifers dodging about there for?” muttered Stubb, looking on from the forecastle. “That Parsee smells fire like a fusee; and smells of it himself, like a hot musket's powder-pan.”

At last the shank, in one complete rod, received its final heat; and as Perth, to temper it, plunged it all hissing into the cask of water near by, the scalding steam shot up into Ahab's bent face.

“Would'st thou brand me, Perth?” wincing for a moment with the pain; “have I been but forging my own branding-iron, then?”

“Pray God, not that; yet I fear something, Captain Ahab. Is not this harpoon for the White Whale?”

“For the white fiend! But now for the barbs; thou must make them thyself, man. Here are my razors—the best of steel; here, and make the barbs sharp as the needle-sleet of the Icy Sea.”

For a moment, the old blacksmith eyed the razors as though he would fain not use them.

“Take them, man, I have no need for them; for I now neither shave, sup, nor pray till—but here—to work!”

Fashioned at last into an arrowy shape, and welded by Perth to the shank, the steel soon pointed the end of the iron; and as the blacksmith was about giving the barbs their final heat, prior to tempering them, he cried to Ahab to place the water-cask near.

“No, no—no water for that; I want it of the true death-temper. Ahoy, there! Tashtego, Queequeg, Daggoo! What say ye, pagans! Will ye give me as much blood as will cover this barb?” holding it high up. A cluster of dark nods replied, Yes. Three punctures were made in the heathen flesh, and the White Whale's barbs were then tempered.

“Ego non baptizo te in nomine patris, sed in nomine diaboli!” deliriously howled Ahab, as the malignant iron scorchingly devoured the baptismal blood.

Now, mustering the spare poles from below, and selecting one of hickory, with the bark still investing it, Ahab fitted the end to the socket of the iron. A coil of new tow-line was then unwound, and some fathoms of it taken to the windlass, and stretched to a great tension. Pressing his foot upon it, till the rope hummed like a harp-string, then eagerly bending over it, and seeing no strandings, Ahab exclaimed, “Good! and now for the seizings.”

At one extremity the rope was unstranded, and the separate spread yarns were all braided and woven round the socket of the harpoon; the pole was then driven hard up into the socket; from the lower end the rope was traced half-way along the pole's length, and firmly secured so, with intertwistings of twine. This done, pole, iron, and rope—like the Three Fates—remained inseparable, and Ahab moodily stalked away with the weapon; the sound of his ivory leg, and the sound of the hickory pole, both hollowly ringing along every plank. But ere he entered his cabin, light, unnatural, half-bantering, yet most piteous sound was heard. Oh, Pip! thy wretched laugh, thy idle but unresting eye; all thy strange mummeries not unmeaningly blended with the black tragedy of the melancholy ship, and mocked it!
