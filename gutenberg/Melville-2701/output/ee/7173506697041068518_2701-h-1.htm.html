TA 1. Nusiwo le ŋgɔ yim.

Yɔm be Ismael. Ƒe aɖewoe nye esi va yi—ɣeyiɣi didi si tututu metsɔ ɖeke le eme o—esi ga sue aɖe alo naneke menɔ asinye le nye gakotoku me o, eye naneke tɔxɛ aɖeke menɔ asinye si ado dzidzɔ nam le ƒuta o ta la, mesusu be maɖo tɔdziʋu anɔ abe vie ene akpɔ xexeame ƒe akpa si tsi le. Enye mɔ aɖe si le asinye si dzi mato aku ʋu le aɖuɖɔtoea ŋu eye mawɔ ɖoɖo ɖe ʋu ƒe sisi ŋu. Ɣesiaɣi si mekpɔ be nye dzi le nu ƒom le nu me ŋu; ɣesiaɣi si wònye November si me tsi dzana eye tsi dzana le nye luʋɔ me; ɣesiaɣi si mekpɔ be metɔ vie le aɖakadzraɖoƒewo ŋkume le nye lɔlɔ̃nu me, eye metsɔ kunuwɔwɔ ɖesiaɖe si medo goe ƒe megbe va; eye vevietɔ ɣesiaɣi si nye hypos ɖu dzinye ale gbegbe, be ebia agbenyuinɔnɔ ƒe gɔmeɖose sesẽ aɖe be wòaxe mɔ nam be nyemaɖoe koŋ aɖo afɔ ablɔdzi o, eye matsɔ mɔnu aƒo amewo ƒe kposɔwo ɖa o—ekema, mebunɛ be ɣeyiɣia de be maɖo ƒua dzi kaba abe alesi mate ŋui ene. Esiae nye nye nusi woatsɔ aɖɔli tu kple bɔl. Cato tsɔ xexemenunya ƒe dzidzedzekpɔkpɔ ƒu gbe ɖe eƒe yi dzi; Metsɔa kpoo yia melia gbɔ. Naneke mewɔ nuku le esia me o. Ne wonyae ko la, ŋutsu siwo katã kloe le woƒe ɖaseɖigbalẽ me, ɣeyiɣi aɖe alo bubu, dea asixɔxɔ seselelãme ɖeka ma ke kloe ɖe atsiaƒu ŋu kplim.

Fifia miaƒe ƒukpodzidu si nye Manhattoes, si melidzeƒewo ƒo xlãe abe India-ƒukpowo ene kple koral-towo le—asitsatsa ƒo xlãe kple eƒe ƒutsotsoewo. Le ɖusime kple miame la, ablɔwo kplɔa wò yia tsi gbɔ. Eƒe dua ƒe titina si gbɔ emee nye batri, afisi ƒutsotsoewo klɔa lãkle bubume ma le, eye yaƒoƒo siwo gaƒoƒo ʋɛ aɖewo do ŋgɔ la megakpɔa anyigba o la nana wòfana le. Kpɔ ameha siwo le tsi kpɔm le afima ɖa.

Miƒo xlã du si me Sabat ŋdɔ si me drɔ̃e le. Yi tso Corlears Hook yi Coenties Slip, eye tso afima, to Whitehall, yi dziehe gome. Nukae nèkpɔ?—Woɖo ame kuku akpe geɖe siwo le tsitre ɖe atsiaƒu me drɔ̃ewo me abe dzɔla siwo meƒoa nu o ene. Ame aɖewo nɔ te ɖe tsikpeawo ŋu; ame aɖewo bɔbɔ nɔ tɔdziʋudzeƒewo ƒe tawo dzi; ame aɖewo le ŋku lém ɖe meli siwo tso China ƒe mɔ̃ sesẽwo dzi; ɖewo le kɔkɔƒe le rigging la me, abe ɖe wole agbagba dzem be yewoakpɔ atsiaƒu ƒe dzedzeme si nyo wu ene. Gake amesiawo katã nye anyigbadzitɔwo; ƒe kwasiɖanuwuwu siwo woɣla ɖe lath kple plaster me—wobla wo ɖe kplɔ̃wo ŋu, woƒo wo ɖe zikpuiwo ŋu, wobla wo ɖe kplɔ̃wo ŋu. Ekema aleke esia le? Ðe agble damawo nu yia? Nukae wowɔna le afisia?

Gake kpɔ ɖa! afisiae ameha geɖe wu va, siwo le zɔzɔm tẽe yina tsia gbɔ, eye edze abe wobla wo be yewoaƒu tsi ene. Si womekpᴐ kpᴐ o! Naneke madze wo ŋu o negbe anyigba la ƒe seɖoƒe si gbɔ eme wu; tsaɖiɖi le nudzraɖoƒe siwo le afima ƒe ya si vɔvɔli le te masɔ gbɔ o. Ao, ele be woate ɖe tsia ŋu alesi woate ŋui evɔ womage ɖe eme o. Eye afimae wotsi tre ɖo—kilometa geɖe tso wo gbɔ—leaguƒewo. Inlanders katã, wotsoa mɔwo kple mɔdodowo, ablɔwo kple mɔdodowo dzi—dziehe, ɣedzeƒe, dzigbe, kple ɣetoɖoƒe. Ke hã le afisia la, wo katã wowɔ ɖeka. Gblɔe nam be ɖe magnet ƒe nɔnɔme nyui si le meli mawo katã ƒe kɔmpasi ƒe aŋewo ŋu hea wo yia afima?

Zi ɖeka ake. Gblɔ be yele dukɔa me; le ta kɔkɔ aɖewo dzi. Mito mɔ ɖesiaɖe kloe si nèdi, eye ewo va ɖo ɖeka la, etsɔa wò yia anyime le bali aɖe me, eye wògblẽa wò ɖe afima le ta aɖe to le tɔsisia me. Akunyawɔwɔ le eme. Na ame siwo ƒe susu mebu o wu la nanyrɔ ɖe eƒe drɔ̃e goglotɔwo kekeake me—tsi tre ɖe ŋutsu ma ŋu ɖe eƒe afɔwo dzi, tsɔ eƒe afɔwo nayi, eye wòakplɔ wò ayi tsi gbɔ vodadamanɔmee, ne tsi le nuto ma katã me. Ne tsikɔ ​​wu wò le Amerika-dzogbe gã la dzi gbeɖeka la, ke te dodokpɔ sia kpɔ, ne edzɔ be wotsɔ nukpɔsusuŋutinunyafialagã aɖe na wò mɔzɔha la. Ẽ, abe alesi amesiame nyae ene la, ŋugbledede kple tsi nye srɔ̃ɖeɖe tegbee.

Gake nutala aɖee nye esi. Edi be yeata lɔlɔ̃ ƒe nɔnɔme si me drɔ̃e le wu, vɔvɔlitɔ kekeake, tomefafa wu, si doa dzidzɔ na ame wu le Saco balia katã me la wò. Nukae nye nu vevitɔ si wòzãna? Eƒe atiwo le tsitre ɖe afima, eye wo dometɔ ɖesiaɖe ƒe ke me do le, abe ɖe saɖagaxɔmenɔla kple atitsoga le eme ene; eye afi sia mlɔ eƒe gbedadaƒo, eye afi ma eƒe nyiwo mlɔna; eye dzudzɔ aɖe si le alɔ̃ dɔm la tso aƒe sue si le afima la dzi. Ya ƒona yia avetsu didiwo me goglo, eye wòɖoa to siwo le wo nɔewo dzi siwo wotsɔ woƒe blɔ si le togbɛa to la me. Gake togbɔ be nɔnɔmetata la mlɔ anyi alea, eye togbɔ be pine-ti sia ʋuʋua eƒe hũɖeɖewo abe aŋgba siwo le alẽkplɔla sia ƒe ta dzi ene hã la, wo katã nye dzodzro, negbe ɖe alẽkplɔla la ƒe ŋku nɔ akunyawɔwɔ ƒe tɔsisi si le eŋgɔ la ŋu ko hafi. Yi ɖasrã Prairies kpɔ le June me, esime nèƒua tsi kilometa gbogbo aɖewo le Tiger-lilies dome vaseɖe klo dzi—nukae adzewɔwɔ ɖeka dim?—Tsi—tsi ɖeka pɛ hã mele afima o! Ðe Niagara nye ke ƒe ŋkumeʋuʋu ko la, ɖe nàzɔ mɔ wò kilometa akpe ɖeka aɖakpɔea? Nukatae Tennessee hakpanyaŋlɔla dahe la, esi wòxɔ klosalo asi eve zi ɖeka la, ɖoe be yeaƒle awudziwui aɖe nɛ, si wòhiã nublanuitɔe, alo atsɔ eƒe ga ade afɔtoƒe yi Rockaway Beach? Nukatae ŋutsuvi ɖesiaɖe kloe si le lãmesẽ me sesẽ si si luʋɔ sesẽ si le lãmesẽ me le, le ɣeyiɣi aɖe me alo bubu me la nye tagbɔsesẽtɔ be wòayi ƒua dzi? Nukatae le wò mɔzɔzɔ gbãtɔ abe mɔzɔla ene me la, wò ŋutɔ nèse ʋuʋudedi ɣaɣla sia tɔgbe, esi wogblɔ na wò zi gbãtɔ be wò kple wò melia megale anyigba dzi fifia o? Nu ka tae Persiatɔ xoxoawo bu atsiaƒua nu kɔkɔe? Nukatae Helatɔwo tsɔ mawu tɔxɛ aɖe nɛ, eye wònye Jove ŋutɔ nɔviŋutsu? Kakaɖedzitɔe la, esiawo katã menye nusi ŋu gɔmesese aɖeke mele o. Eye gɔmesese si le Narcissus ƒe ŋutinya ma ŋu gade to ɖe edzi, amesi esi mete ŋu se legba si le funyafunya wɔm, si fa miamiamia si wòkpɔ le vudoa me la gɔme o ta la, enyrɔ ɖe eme eye wònyrɔe. Gake nɔnɔmetata ma ke, mía ŋutɔwo míekpɔna le tɔsisiwo kple atsiaƒuwo katã me. Enye agbe ƒe gbɔgbɔ si gɔme womate ŋu ase o la ƒe nɔnɔmetata; eye esiae nye nu vevitɔ le wo katã me.

Fifia, ne megblɔ be ezu numame nam be mayi ƒua dzi ɣesiaɣi si medze ŋkuwo ƒe ʋuʋu gɔme, eye medze nye lãkusiwo ƒe ŋudzedzekpɔkpɔ gɔme akpa la, nyemedi be mana woaƒo nya ta be meyina ƒua dzi gbeɖe abe o ene mɔzɔla aɖe. Elabena hafi nàyi abe mɔzɔla ene la, ele be gakotoku nanɔ asiwò, eye gakotoku nye avɔ ko negbe ɖe nane le eme hafi. Gakpe ɖe eŋu la, mɔzɔlawo dzea ƒudzidɔ—wova nɔa dzre wɔm—womedɔa alɔ̃ zã bliboa o—womekpɔa dzidzɔ boo o, abe nusi bɔ ene;—ao, nyemeyina abe mɔzɔla ene gbeɖe o; eye togbɔ be dze aɖee menye hã la, nyemeyia ƒua dzi abe Commodore, alo Captain, alo Cook ene gbeɖe o. Meɖea asi le ɖoƒe mawo ƒe ŋutikɔkɔe kple vovototodedeameme ŋu na amesiwo lɔ̃a wo. Le nye gome la, meƒoa ŋu ɖe dɔ sesẽ siwo ŋu bubu le, dodokpɔwo, kple xaxa ƒomevi ɖesiaɖe si ŋu bubu le la katã ŋu. Enye nusi mate ŋu awɔ be malé be na ɖokuinye, evɔ nyemelé be na meliwo, barques, brigs, schooners, kple nusiwo menye nenema o. Eye le nuɖala yiyi gome la,—togbɔ be meʋu eme be ŋutikɔkɔe geɖe le ema me hã la, nuɖala nye asrafomegã ƒomevi aɖe si le meli me—ke hã, le mɔ aɖe nu la, nyemesusu xevi siwo wotɔ dzoe kpɔ o;—togbɔ be woɖae tsã, wotsɔ bɔta ƒo wo nunyatɔe, eye le ʋɔnudɔdrɔ̃ nu hã wotsɔ dze kple atadi de eme la, ame aɖeke meli si aƒo nu bubutɔe, ne míagblɔe bubutɔe o, tso xevi si wotɔ dzoe ŋu wu nye o. Egiptetɔ xoxoawo ƒe trɔ̃subɔsubɔ ƒe lɔlɔ̃ ɖe ibis si wotɔ dzoe kple tɔsisi me sɔ si wotɔ dzoe dzi mee nèkpɔa nuwɔwɔ mawo ƒe ame kukuwo le woƒe abolomeƒe gã siwo nye piramidwo me.

Ao, ne meyi ƒua dzi la, meyina abe ƒudzidela bɔbɔe ene, do ŋgɔ na tɔdziʋua ƒe ʋuƒoa tututu, metsɔa tsi ƒua du yia ŋgɔgbexɔa me, yia dzi le afima va ɖoa fia ƒe tɔdziʋua ƒe ta gbɔ. Nyateƒee, woɖea gbe nam boŋ tso ɖewo ŋu, eye wonana meti kpo tso spar yi spar, abe ʋetsuvi si le May ƒe gbedadaƒo ene. Eye le gɔmedzedzea me la, nu sia ƒomevi mevivina o ale gbegbe. Ewɔa dɔ ɖe ame ƒe bubu ƒe seselelãme dzi, vevietɔ ne ƒome xoxo aɖe si woɖo anyi le anyigbaa dzi, si nye Van Rensselaers, alo Randolphs, alo Hardicanutes, mee nètso. Eye wu wo katã la, ne do ŋgɔ teti na wò asi tsɔtsɔ de aŋɔka me la, ke èɖu edzi abe kɔƒenutome sukutatɔ ene, si na ŋutsuvi kɔkɔtɔwo tsi tre ɖe vɔvɔ̃ na wò. Meka ɖe edzi na wò be tɔtrɔa nye esi me dzo le, tso sukutatɔ dzi va zu ƒudzidela, eye ebia Seneca kple Stoatɔwo ƒe ʋeʋẽ sesẽ aɖe si ana nàte ŋu ako alɔgbɔnu ahado dzi. Gake esia gɔ̃ hã nu yina le ɣeyiɣi aɖe megbe.

Ke ne ƒudzisrafomegã aɖe ƒe akpa xoxo aɖewo ɖe gbe nam be matsɔ ʋuʋudedi aɖe atutu ʋua ƒe akpatawo ya ɖe? Nukae bubumademade ame ŋu ma nye, ne wodae, mebe, le Nubabla Yeyea ƒe nudanu me? Èsusu be mawudɔla gã Gabriel bua naneke le ŋunye wu, elabena meɖoa to hunk xoxo mawo enumake kple bubutɔe le nɔnɔme ma koŋ mea? Amekae menye kluvi o? Gblɔ nya ma nam. Enyo, ekema aleke kee ƒudzisrafomegã xoxoawo ate ŋu aɖe gbe nam be magblɔe o—aleke kee woɖaƒom kple atam o, edzɔa dzi nam be menya be enyo; be wosubɔa ame bubu ɖesiaɖe le mɔ ɖeka alo bubu nu le mɔ ma ke nu—le ŋutilã alo nu ŋutɔŋutɔ ƒe nukpɔsusu nu, si nye; eye aleae wotsɔa xexeame katã ƒe ƒoƒoa ƒoa xlãe, eye ele be asiwo katã natutu wo nɔewo ƒe abɔta, eye woƒe dzi nadze eme.

Le afisia hã la, meyina ƒua dzi ɣesiaɣi abe ƒudzidela ene, elabena woɖoe be yewoaxe fe nam ɖe nye kuxia ta, evɔ womexea gaku ɖeka pɛ hã na mɔzɔlawo si ŋu mese kpɔ o. Kura o, ele be mɔzɔlawo ŋutɔ naxe fe. Eye vovototo katã le xexeame le fexexe kple fexexe dome. Ðewohĩ fexexe ƒe nuwɔnae nye nusi me dziɖeleameƒo le wu si atikutsetsebɔdzifila eveawo he vɛ na mí. Gake esi woxe fe na ,—nukae asɔ kplii? Dugã me dɔwɔna si ame tsɔ xɔa ga la wɔ nuku ŋutɔŋutɔ, ne míebu eŋu be míexɔe se vevie ale gbegbe be gae nye anyigbadzidɔlélewo katã ƒe ke, eye be le mɔ aɖeke nu la, ame si si ga le mate ŋu age ɖe dziƒo o. Ah! aleke gbegbe míetsɔa mía ɖokui dea tsɔtsrɔ̃ me kple dzidzɔ enye si!

Mlɔeba la, meyina ƒua dzi ɣesiaɣi abe ƒudzidela ene, le kamedede nyui kple ya dzadzɛ si le ŋgɔgbexɔa ƒe akpata me ta. Elabena abe alesi wòle le xexe sia me ene la, ta ƒe ya bɔ sã wu ya si tso megbe (si fia be ne mèda Pythagoras ƒe nyagbɔgblɔ dzi gbeɖe o), eyata le akpa gãtɔ me la, Commodore si le ʋua ƒe akpa enelia la xɔa eƒe yame ƒe nɔnɔme le mɔ evelia nu tso ƒudzidelawo gbɔ le nyagblɔɖigbalẽa dzi. Esusu be yegbɔnɛ gbã; gake menye nenemae o. Mɔ ma ke nue ame ɖekawo kplɔa woƒe ŋgɔnɔlawo le nu bubu geɖe me, le ɣeyiɣi ma ke me la, kplɔlawo mesusunɛ boo o. Gake esia tae wònye be esi mese atsiaƒua ƒe ʋeʋẽ enuenu abe asitsala ƒudzidela ene vɔ la, ele be matsɔe ade ta me azɔ be mayi tɔmelãwo léle ƒe mɔzɔzɔ dzi; esiae nye Fates ƒe kpovitɔ makpɔmakpɔ, amesi léa ŋku ɖe ŋunye ɣesiaɣi, eye wònaa avuwo nɔa ŋunye le adzame, eye wòkpɔa ŋusẽ ɖe dzinye le mɔ aɖe si ŋu womate ŋu abu akɔnta le o nu—ate ŋu aɖo eŋu nyuie wu ame bubu ɖesiaɖe. Eye ɖikeke mele eme o be nye tɔmelãwo léle ƒe mɔzɔzɔ sia yiyi, nye Providence ƒe ɖoɖo gã si wowɔ ɣeyiɣi didi aɖee nye sia la ƒe akpa aɖe. Eva abe dometsotso kpui aɖe ƒomevi kple ame ɖeka ko le fefe siwo keke ta wu dome ene. Mebui be sea ƒe akpa sia anya ƒu du nane si le abe esia ene:

“ United States ƒe Dukplɔla ƒe Tiatia Gã si Woʋli Ho. “ISMAEL ÐEKA ƑE ƲUƲU ƑE ƲUƲU. “ƲUƲUƲU LE AFFGHANISTAN.”

Togbɔ be nyemate ŋu agblɔ nusita tututu wònye be fefewɔƒedzikpɔla mawo, Fates, tsɔm ɖo anyi ɖe tɔmelãwo léle ƒe mɔzɔzɔ ƒe akpa gbegblẽ sia ta, esime woɖo ame bubuwo ɖe akpa wɔnukuwo dzi le nublanuinya gãwo me, kple akpa kpui siwo le bɔbɔe le nukokoedonamenya siwo me wowɔa nu le ame ŋkutawo me, kple akpa siwo doa dzidzɔ na ame le fefenyawo me—togbɔ be nyemate ŋu agblɔ nusita tututue nye esia o hã; ke hã, fifia si meɖo ŋku nɔnɔmeawo katã dzi la, mesusu be mate ŋu akpɔ tsidzɔƒewo kple susu siwo wotsɔ ayedzedze gblɔ nam le atsyɔ̃ɖoɖo vovovowo me, ʋãm be madze akpa si mewɔ la wɔwɔ gɔme, hekpe ɖe alesi wòƒoe ɖe nunye be mawɔ alakpatɔe be enye tiatia si do tso nye ŋutɔ nye tiatiawɔblɔɖe si me ameŋkumemakpɔmakpɔ le kple ʋɔnudɔdrɔ̃ si me vovototodedeameme le me.

Susu siawo dometɔ vevitɔe nye tɔmelã gã la ŋutɔ ƒe susu si ƒo ɖesiaɖe ta. Lã wɔadã aɖe si nye dzesi eye wònye nya ɣaɣla alea nyɔ nye didi be manya nu geɖe. Emegbe atsiaƒu gbemelãwo kple didiƒe ʋĩ siwo me wòmli eƒe ƒukpoa ƒe akpa gãtɔ le; afɔku siwo womate ŋu atsɔ ana o, siwo ŋkɔ mele o, siwo le tɔmelã si woyɔna be whale me; esiawo, kple Patagoniatɔwo ƒe nukpɔkpɔ kple gbeɖiɖi akpe ɖeka ƒe nukunu siwo katã va la kpe ɖe ŋunye meʋuʋu ɖe nye didi ŋu. Ðewohĩ le ŋutsu bubuwo gome la, nu mawo manye ameʋãŋusẽ o; ke nye ya la, wotsɔa ʋuʋudedi mavɔ le fu ɖem nam le nu siwo le didiƒe ʋĩ ta. Melɔ̃a tɔdziʋukuku le atsiaƒu siwo ŋu wode se ɖo dzi, eye meɖina ɖe ƒuta siwo me wowɔa nu manyatalenuwo le. Ne nyemeŋe aɖaba ƒu nusi nyo dzi o la, mekpɔa nu dziŋɔ aɖe dzea sii kaba, eye mate ŋu anɔ hadome kplii kokoko—ɖe woaɖe mɔ nam—elabena enyo ŋutɔ be manɔ xɔlɔ̃wɔwɔ me kple teƒe si ame dzena le ƒe gamenɔlawo katã.

Eyata le nusiawo ta la, woxɔ tɔmelãwo léle ƒe mɔzɔzɔa nyuie; nukuxexea ƒe tsiɖɔɖɔ-gbo gãwo ʋu, eye le dada gbegblẽ siwo ʋãm yi nye tameɖoɖo gbɔ me la, ame eve kple eve nɔ tsia dzi yi nye luʋɔ ememetɔ me, tɔmelã siwo woyɔna be whale ƒe mɔzɔzɔ siwo nuwuwu meli na o, eye le wo dometɔ akpa gãtɔ dome la, ɖeka grand hooded phantom, abe sno togbɛ si le yame ene.





TA 2. Kapeti-Akplo.

Metsɔ awudziwui ɖeka alo eve de nye modzakaɖekotoku xoxoa me, tsɔe de nye alɔnu te, eye medze mɔ yi Cape Horn kple Pacific. Esi medzo le du nyui si nye Manhatto xoxoa me la, meva ɖo New Bedford wòsɔ. Dzi ɖe le ƒonye ŋutɔ esi mese be agbalẽvi sue si wotsɔ yi Nantucket la ɖo tɔdziʋu xoxo, eye be mɔ aɖeke si dzi mato aɖo teƒe ma manɔ anyi o, vaseɖe Memleɖa si kplɔe ɖo dzi.

Exɔ asi akpa eye wònye dzidzɔ, megasusui ake, eye metɔ vie be makpɔ keklẽ gbadzaa si le ablɔa dzi, eye mase ŋkumeɖobolo siwo le ɖiɖim le eme ƒe gbeɖiɖiwo. Ablɔ siwo dzi nu te ɖe ame dzi alea! xɔtunu yibɔwo, ke menye aƒewo o, le axa eveawo dzi, eye akaɖi le afisia kple afima, abe bosomikaɖi si le tsatsam le yɔdo me ene. Le zã ƒe gaƒoƒo sia, si nye kwasiɖaa ƒe ŋkeke mamlɛtɔ dzi la, dua ƒe akpa enelia ma zu ame aɖeke kura o. Gake fifia la, meva ɖo akaɖi aɖe si me dzudzɔ le dodom tso xɔ aɖe si le bɔbɔe eye wòkeke, si ƒe ʋɔtru le ʋuʋu ɖi si kpena la gbɔ. Eƒe dzedzeme ŋumaɖɔɖotɔe nɔ, abe ɖe woɖoe na dukɔa ƒe zazã ene; eyata, esi mege ɖe eme la, nu gbãtɔ si mewɔe nye be mekli aŋɔɖaka aɖe le akpata me. Ha! mesusu be, ha, esi nu suesuesue siwo le dzodzom la ƒo nye nu kloe la, ɖe afi siawo tso du ma si wogbã, si nye Gomora, mea? Gake “Akpa Siwo Wotso Atitsoga,” kple “Yi-Tlã?”—ekema ele be esia nanye “Mɔ̃” ƒe dzesi. Gake mefɔ ɖokuinye eye mese gbe sesẽ aɖe le ememe, metu yi ŋgɔ heʋu ʋɔtru evelia si le eme.

Edze abe Ameyibɔwo ƒe Sewɔtakpekpe gã si bɔbɔ nɔ Tophet ene. Ameyibɔ mo alafa ɖeka trɔ le woƒe fli me be yewoakpɔ ŋkume; eye le go kemɛ dzi la, Ameyibɔ aɖe si nye Ameyibɔ si le tsɔtsrɔ̃ ƒe Mawudɔla aɖe nɔ agbalẽ aɖe ƒom le nuƒolanɔƒe aɖe. Ameyibɔwo ƒe sɔlemexɔe wònye; eye mawunyagblɔla la ƒe mawunyakpukpui la ku ɖe viviti ƒe viviti, kple avifafa kple avifafa kple aɖututu le afima ŋu. Ha, Ismael, meƒo nu le ɣlidodo me, hegbugbɔ yi megbe, Modzaka wɔnublanui le dzesi si nye ‘Mɔ̃a!’ la nu.

Esi menɔ yiyim la, meva ɖo kekeli aɖe si me mekɔ tututu o si medidi tso melidzeƒeawo gbɔ o gbɔ mlɔeba, eye mese ame aɖe si tsi dzodzodzoe ƒe ɣlidodo le yame; eye esi wòfɔ mo dzi la, ekpɔ dzesi aɖe si le ʋuʋum le ʋɔtrua tame si dzi wota nu ɣi aɖe ɖo, si tsi tre ɖi na aŋɔ si le dzɔdzɔe kɔkɔ aɖe si me kuɖiɖi le, eye nya siawo le ete—“The Spouter Inn:—Peter Coffin.”

Aɖaka?—Spouter?—Mesusu be enye nusi vɔ̃ɖi boŋ le kadodo ma koŋ me. Gake wogblɔ be enye ŋkɔ si bɔ le Nantucket, eye mesusu be Petro sia si le afisia nye ʋuʋula tso afima. Esi kekelia dze abe ɖe mekɔ tututu o ene, eye teƒea, le ɣeyiɣi ma me la, edze abe tomefafa ene, eye atixɔ sue si gbã la ŋutɔ dze abe ɖe woate ŋu atsɔe tso nuto aɖe si wotɔ dzoe ƒe glikpowo me va afisia ene, eye abe alesi dzesi si le ʋuʋum la ƒe a poverty-stricken sort of creak to it, mesusu be afisia tututue nye teƒe si dzeƒe siwo mexɔ asi o le, kple pea kɔfi nyuitɔ kekeake.

Enye teƒe si mewɔ nuku o—xɔ xoxo aɖe si ƒe nuwuwu nye agbo, le kpɔɖeŋunyagbɔgblɔ nu la, eƒe akpa ɖeka ƒo ƒu, eye wòbɔbɔ nɔ anyi nublanuitɔe. Etsi tre ɖe dzogoe ɖaɖɛ aɖe si me do viviti dzi, afisi ya sesẽ ma si woyɔna be Euroclydon la nɔ ɣli dom vɔ̃ɖi wu tsã le Paulo dahe si wòtsɔ ƒu gbe la ŋu. Ke hã, Euroclydon nye zephyr vivi sesẽ aɖe na amesiame si le xɔa me, eye eƒe afɔwo le dzodoƒea nɔa toast kpoo hena aba. Agbalẽŋlɔla tsitsi aɖe—amesi ƒe agbalẽwo ƒe kɔpi ɖeka kolia si gakpɔtɔ le asinye—gblɔ be: “Le ʋɔnudɔdrɔ̃ yaƒoƒo sesẽ ma si woyɔna be Euroclydon me la, ewɔa vovototo wɔnuku aɖe, ne èkpɔe le ahuhɔ̃e fesre si dzi vuvɔa katã le le gota, alo ne èlé ŋku ɖe eŋu tso fesre ma si me ka mele o, afisi vuvɔ le le axa eveawo, eye wight Ku koe nye glazier le.” Nyateƒee, mesusui, esi kpukpui sia va susu me nam—ŋɔŋlɔdzesi yibɔ xoxo, èbu tame nyuie. Ẽ, ŋku siawo nye fesrewo, eye nye ŋutilã siae nye aƒea. Nublanuinya kae nye si wònye be wometɔ te chinks kple crannies o ke hã, eye wotsɔ lint vi aɖe ƒu gbe ɖe afisia kple afima. Gake etsi megbe akpa be woawɔ ŋgɔyiyi aɖeke fifia. Xexeame katã wu enu; copestone la le edzi, eye wotsɔ tasiaɖam tsɔ chips la dzoe ƒe miliɔn ɖeka enye sia. Lazaro dahe si le afima, si le eƒe aɖuwo ƒom ɖe eƒe akɔtaɖonu ŋu, eye wònɔ eƒe ʋuʋudedi ʋuʋum kple eƒe ʋuʋudedi la, ate ŋu atsɔ avɔ abla to eveawo, eye wòatsɔ bli-ku ade nu me, eye ke hã ema maxe mɔ na... ahom sesẽ aɖe si woyɔna be Euroclydon. Euroclydon ƒe ŋkɔ! Dives tsitsi aɖe gblɔ, le eƒe seda ƒe agbalẽ xatsaxatsa dzĩ me—(ekpɔ esi biã wu le ema megbe) pooh, pooh! Zã nyui ka gbegbee nye si vuvɔ nɔ wɔwɔm; alesi Orion klẽnae; dziehe ƒe akaɖi kawo gbegbee nye si! Mina woaƒo nu tso woƒe ɣedzeƒetɔwo ƒe dzomeŋɔli ƒe yame ƒe nɔnɔme siwo nye nudzraɖoƒe siwo nɔa anyi ɖaa ŋu; na mɔnukpɔkpɔm be matsɔ nye ŋutɔ nye dzoka xɔxɔwo awɔ nye ŋutɔ nye dzomeŋɔli.

TA 3. Spouter-Inn ƒe dɔwɔƒea.

Esi nège ɖe Spouter-Inn ma si ƒe nuwuwu nye gable la, èkpɔ ɖokuiwò le mɔ si keke, si bɔbɔ, si me ʋuʋudedi le si me wainscot xoxowo le, si ɖo ŋku aɖaŋudɔ xoxo aɖewo siwo wobu fɔe ƒe mɔ̃ sesẽwo dzi na ame ɖeka. Wotsɔ amitata gã aɖe ŋutɔ si wotsɔ dzudzɔ ƒoe nyuie, eye wògblẽ le mɔ ɖesiaɖe nu ale gbegbe be le akaɖi siwo mesɔ o siwo dzi nèto kpɔe me la, nusɔsrɔ̃ veviedodotɔe kple sasrãkpɔ ɖe edzi ɖe ɖoɖo nu, kple aƒelikawo biabia nyuie me koe wònye, . be àte ŋu ase eƒe taɖodzinu gɔme le mɔ ɖesiaɖe nu. Vɔvɔli kple vɔvɔli gbogbo aɖewo siwo ŋu womebu akɔnta le o ale gbegbe be le gɔmedzedzea me la, èsusu kloe be ɖekakpui nutala aɖe si di vevie be yeaxɔ ŋkɔ, le New England ƒe hagwo ƒe ɣeyiɣia me, dze agbagba be yeaɖe zitɔtɔ ƒe nɔnɔmetata si wotsɔ afakaka wɔe. Gake to ŋugbledede geɖe kple veviedodo me, kple ŋugbledede enuenu zi geɖe, kple vevietɔ to fesre suea tsɔtsɔ ƒu gbe ɖe ʋɔtrua ƒe megbe gome la, èva ƒo nya ta mlɔeba be susu ma tɔgbe, aleke kee wòɖanye gbemelã o, ɖewohĩ masɔ kura o.

Gake nusi tɔtɔ wò wu eye wòtɔtɔ wò wue nye nane si le ʋuʋum le nɔnɔmetata la titina le fli blɔ etɔ̃ siwo me mekɔ o, siwo le tsiatrenu siwo le tsia dzi le amɔwɔʋãnu si ŋkɔ mele o me la ƒe ƒuƒoƒo didi aɖe si me mekɔ o, si me kɔ, si nye dzesi, si le yibɔ me. Nɔnɔmetata si me tsikpe le, si me tsi le, si me ʋuʋudedi le vavã, si sɔ gbɔ be wòana ŋutsu si tsi dzodzodzoe la ƒe susu nadzo. Ke hã ɖe kɔkɔƒe aɖe si seɖoƒe meli na o, si gbɔ wòtso afã kple afã, si womate ŋu asusu o nɔ eŋu si na wò tsikpe ɖe eŋu nyuie, vaseɖe esime nèka atam kple ɖokuiwò le lɔlɔ̃nu faa be yeanya nusi nutata wɔnuku ma fia. Ever and anon a bright, but, alas, deceptive idea would dart you through.—Enye Ƒuyibɔa le zãtitina ƒe ahomya me.—Enye aʋawɔwɔ si mele dzɔdzɔme nu o si nye nu vevi eneawo.—Enye dzoxɔxɔ si wogbã.—Enye Hyperborean dzomeŋɔli ƒe nukpɔkpɔ .—Enye Ɣeyiɣi ƒe tɔsisi si tsikpe ƒo xlãe ƒe gbagbã. Gake mlɔeba la, nususukpɔ siawo katã ɖe asi le nane ɖeka ma si nye dzesi si le nɔnɔmetata la titina la ŋu. Ema va nya zi ɖeka, eye susɔeawo katã dze ƒã. Gake dzudzɔ; ɖe meɖi tɔmelã gã aɖe vie oa? leviatan gã la ŋutɔ gɔ̃ hã?

Le nyateƒe me la, nutala la ƒe aɖaŋuwɔwɔ dze abe alea ene: nye ŋutɔ nye nufiafia mamlɛtɔ, si ƒe akpa aɖe nɔ te ɖe ame tsitsi geɖe siwo meɖo dze kplii tso nyatia ŋu ƒe nukpɔsusu siwo woƒo ƒu dzi. Nɔnɔmetata la tsi tre ɖi na Cape-Horner aɖe le ahom sesẽ aɖe me; meli si ƒe gɔmeɖoanyi afã le ʋuʋum le afima eye eƒe ʋuƒo etɔ̃ siwo wogbã ɖeɖeko dzena; eye tɔmelã aɖe si do dziku, si ɖoe be yeadzo le aɖaŋunu la dzi, le nuwɔna gã aɖe si nye be yeaklã ye ɖokui ɖe ati etɔ̃awo ƒe ta dzi.

Wotsɔ atikplɔ kple akplɔ dziŋɔwo ƒe hatsotso aɖe si le abe trɔ̃subɔlawo tɔ ene la ƒo xlã mɔ sia ƒe gli si tsi tre ɖe eŋu la katã. Wotsɔ aɖu siwo le keklẽm siwo ɖi nyitsu ƒe aŋetuwo ɖo wo dometɔ aɖewo ŋu wòbiã; bubuwo hã nye esiwo wotsɔ amegbetɔ ƒe taɖa ƒe ƒuƒoƒo ƒo ɖe wo nɔewo ŋu; eye ɖeka nye esi ƒe nɔnɔme le abe hɛ ene, eye eƒe asiléƒe gã aɖe le ʋuʋum godoo abe akpa si lãtsomɔ̃ si ƒe alɔ didi wɔ le gbe yeye si wotso me ene. Èʋuʋu esi nènɔ ŋku lém ɖe nu ŋu, eye nènɔ ɖokuiwò biam be ameɖula kple gbemelã vɔ̃ɖi kae ate ŋu atsɔ dɔwɔnu dziŋɔ sia si agbã nu dziŋɔ sia ayi kuŋeŋe me hã. Wotsaka kple esiawo nye tɔmelãwo léle ƒe akplɔ xoxo siwo xɔ dzo kple akplɔ siwo katã gbã eye woƒe nɔnɔme gblẽ. Wo dometɔ aɖewo nye aʋawɔnu siwo ŋu ŋutinya le. Nathan Swain tsɔ akplɔ sia si didi tsã, si ƒe abɔta le abe gbemelã ene fifia, wu tɔmelã wuiatɔ̃ le ɣedzeƒe kple ɣeɖoto dome ƒe blaatɔ̃ enye sia. Eye wotsɔ harpoon ma—si le abe korkscrew ene fifia—ƒu gbe ɖe Javan-ƒuwo me, eye ƒumelã aɖe si dzo kplii, si wowu le ƒe geɖe megbe le Cape of Blanco. Ga gbãtɔa ge ɖe asikea gbɔ, eye abe aŋe si meɖea dzi ɖi o si nɔa ŋutsu ƒe ŋutilã me ene la, ezɔ afɔ blaene bliboe, eye mlɔeba la, wokpɔe wòge ɖe akɔta la me.

Ne ètso mɔ sia si me do viviti le, eye nèyi edzi to yon mɔ si ƒe aɖakawo le bɔbɔe dzi—si wotso nusi anya nye titina dzodoƒe gã aɖe si me dzodoƒewo le ƒo xlãe le blema—la, ègena ɖe dutoƒoxɔa me. Teƒe si do viviti wue nye esia, eye atikpo siwo bɔbɔ ɖe anyi le etame, eye ʋuƒo xoxo siwo le fli me le ete ale gbegbe be àsusui kloe be yeatu afɔkpo aɖaŋudɔ xoxo aɖewo ƒe yameʋukuƒe, vevietɔ le zã si me wole ɣli dom alea gbegbe, esime aɖakaʋu xoxo sia si wotu ɖe dzogoe dzi la ʋuʋu dzikutɔe alea gbegbe . Kplɔ̃ didi aɖe si le bɔbɔe, si le abe agbalẽdzraɖoƒe ene, si dzi wotsɔ ahuhɔ̃e siwo me gbagbã le, si me nu siwo mebɔ o siwo me ke le siwo woƒo ƒu tso xexeame ƒe dzogoe gbadza sia si le didiƒe wu la yɔ fũ la nɔ tsitre ɖe akpa ɖeka. Do aɖe si dze abe ɖe wòdo viviti ene—si nye ahadzraƒea—si nye agbagbadzedze amemabumabutɔe be yeadze ɖusime tɔmelã aɖe ƒe ta ŋu la le dodom tso xɔa ƒe dzogoe si didi wu dzi. It was now about nine o'clock, and the room seeming almost supernaturally quiet after these orgies, I began to congratulate myself upon a little plan that had occurred to me just previous to the entrance of the seamen.

No man prefers to sleep two in a bed. In fact, you would a good deal rather not sleep with your own brother. I don't know how it is, but people like to be private when they are sleeping. And when it comes to sleeping with an unknown stranger, in a strange inn, in a strange town, and that stranger a harpooneer, then your objections indefinitely multiply. Nor was there any earthly reason why I as a sailor should sleep two in a bed, more than anybody else; for sailors no more sleep two in a bed at sea, than bachelor Kings do ashore. To be sure they all sleep together in one apartment, but you have your own hammock, and cover yourself with your own blanket, and sleep in your own skin.

The more I pondered over this harpooneer, the more I abominated the thought of sleeping with him. It was fair to presume that being a harpooneer, his linen or woollen, as the case might be, would not be of the tidiest, certainly none of the finest. I began to twitch all over. Besides, it was getting late, and my decent harpooneer ought to be home and going bedwards. Suppose now, he should tumble in upon me at midnight—how could I tell from what vile hole he had been coming?

“Landlord! I've changed my mind about that harpooneer.—I shan't sleep with him. I'll try the bench here.”

“Just as you please; I'm sorry I can't spare ye a tablecloth for a mattress, and it's a plaguy rough board here”—feeling of the knots and notches. “But wait a bit, Skrimshander; I've got a carpenter's plane there in the bar—wait, I say, and I'll make ye snug enough.” So saying he procured the plane; and with his old silk handkerchief first dusting the bench, vigorously set to planing away at my bed, the while grinning like an ape. The shavings flew right and left; till at last the plane-iron came bump against an indestructible knot. The landlord was near spraining his wrist, and I told him for heaven's sake to quit—the bed was soft enough to suit me, and I did not know how all the planing in the world could make eider down of a pine plank. So gathering up the shavings with another grin, and throwing them into the great stove in the middle of the room, he went about his business, and left me in a brown study.

I now took the measure of the bench, and found that it was a foot too short; but that could be mended with a chair. But it was a foot too narrow, and the other bench in the room was about four inches higher than the planed one—so there was no yoking them. I then placed the first bench lengthwise along the only clear space against the wall, leaving a little interval between, for my back to settle down in. But I soon found that there came such a draught of cold air over me from under the sill of the window, that this plan would never do at all, especially as another current from the rickety door met the one from the window, and both together formed a series of small whirlwinds in the immediate vicinity of the spot where I had thought to spend the night.

The devil fetch that harpooneer, thought I, but stop, couldn't I steal a march on him—bolt his door inside, and jump into his bed, not to be wakened by the most violent knockings? It seemed no bad idea; but upon second thoughts I dismissed it. For who could tell but what the next morning, so soon as I popped out of the room, the harpooneer might be standing in the entry, all ready to knock me down!

Still, looking round me again, and seeing no possible chance of spending a sufferable night unless in some other person's bed, I began to think that after all I might be cherishing unwarrantable prejudices against this unknown harpooneer. Thinks I, I'll wait awhile; he must be dropping in before long. I'll have a good look at him then, and perhaps we may become jolly good bedfellows after all—there's no telling.

But though the other boarders kept coming in by ones, twos, and threes, and going to bed, yet no sign of my harpooneer.

“Landlord!” said I, “what sort of a chap is he—does he always keep such late hours?” It was now hard upon twelve o'clock.

The landlord chuckled again with his lean chuckle, and seemed to be mightily tickled at something beyond my comprehension. “No,” he answered, “generally he's an early bird—airley to bed and airley to rise—yes, he's the bird what catches the worm. But to-night he went out a peddling, you see, and I don't see what on airth keeps him so late, unless, may be, he can't sell his head.”

“Can't sell his head?—What sort of a bamboozingly story is this you are telling me?” getting into a towering rage. “Do you pretend to say, landlord, that this harpooneer is actually engaged this blessed Saturday night, or rather Sunday morning, in peddling his head around this town?”

“That's precisely it,” said the landlord, “and I told him he couldn't sell it here, the market's overstocked.”

“With what?” shouted I.

“With heads to be sure; ain't there too many heads in the world?”

“I tell you what it is, landlord,” said I quite calmly, “you'd better stop spinning that yarn to me—I'm not green.”

“May be not,” taking out a stick and whittling a toothpick, “but I rayther guess you'll be done brown if that ere harpooneer hears you a slanderin' his head.”

“I'll break it for him,” said I, now flying into a passion again at this unaccountable farrago of the landlord's.

“It's broke a'ready,” said he.

“Sartain, and that's the very reason he can't sell it, I guess.”

I come to your house and want a bed; you tell me you can only give me half a one; that the other half belongs to a certain harpooneer. And about this harpooneer, whom I have not yet seen, you persist in telling me the most mystifying and exasperating stories tending to beget in me an uncomfortable feeling towards the man whom you design for my bedfellow—a sort of connexion, landlord, which is an intimate and confidential one in the highest degree. “Wall,” said the landlord, fetching a long breath, “that's a purty long sarmon for a chap that rips a little now and then. But be easy, be easy, this here harpooneer I have been tellin' you of has just arrived from the south seas, where he bought up a lot of 'balmed New Zealand heads (great curios, you know), and he's sold all on 'em but one, and that one he's trying to sell to-night, cause to-morrow's Sunday, and it would not do to be sellin' human heads about the streets when folks is goin' to churches. He wanted to, last Sunday, but I stopped him just as he was goin' out of the door with four heads strung on a string, for all the airth like a string of inions.”

This account cleared up the otherwise unaccountable mystery, and showed that the landlord, after all, had had no idea of fooling me—but at the same time what could I think of a harpooneer who stayed out of a Saturday night clean into the holy Sabbath, engaged in such a cannibal business as selling the heads of dead idolators?

“Depend upon it, landlord, that harpooneer is a dangerous man.”

“He pays reg'lar,” was the rejoinder. “But come, it's getting dreadful late, you had better be turning flukes—it's a nice bed; Sal and me slept in that ere bed the night we were spliced. There's plenty of room for two to kick about in that bed; it's an almighty big bed that. Why, afore we give it up, Sal used to put our Sam and little Johnny in the foot of it. But I got a dreaming and sprawling about one night, and somehow, Sam got pitched on the floor, and came near breaking his arm. Arter that, Sal said it wouldn't do. Come along here, I'll give ye a glim in a jiffy;” and so saying he lighted a candle and held it towards me, offering to lead the way. But I stood irresolute; when looking at a clock in the corner, he exclaimed “I vum it's Sunday—you won't see that harpooneer to-night; he's come to anchor somewhere—come along then; do come; won't ye come?”

I considered the matter a moment, and then up stairs we went, and I was ushered into a small room, cold as a clam, and furnished, sure enough, with a prodigious bed, almost big enough indeed for any four harpooneers to sleep abreast.

“There,” said the landlord, placing the candle on a crazy old sea chest that did double duty as a wash-stand and centre table; “there, make yourself comfortable now, and good night to ye.” I turned round from eyeing the bed, but he had disappeared.

Folding back the counterpane, I stooped over the bed. Though none of the most elegant, it yet stood the scrutiny tolerably well. I then glanced round the room; and besides the bedstead and centre table, could see no other furniture belonging to the place, but a rude shelf, the four walls, and a papered fireboard representing a man striking a whale. Of things not properly belonging to the room, there was a hammock lashed up, and thrown upon the floor in one corner; also a large seaman's bag, containing the harpooneer's wardrobe, no doubt in lieu of a land trunk. Likewise, there was a parcel of outlandish bone fish hooks on the shelf over the fire-place, and a tall harpoon standing at the head of the bed.

But what is this on the chest? I took it up, and held it close to the light, and felt it, and smelt it, and tried every way possible to arrive at some satisfactory conclusion concerning it. I can compare it to nothing but a large door mat, ornamented at the edges with little tinkling tags something like the stained porcupine quills round an Indian moccasin. There was a hole or slit in the middle of this mat, as you see the same in South American ponchos. But could it be possible that any sober harpooneer would get into a door mat, and parade the streets of any Christian town in that sort of guise? I put it on, to try it, and it weighed me down like a hamper, being uncommonly shaggy and thick, and I thought a little damp, as though this mysterious harpooneer had been wearing it of a rainy day. I went up in it to a bit of glass stuck against the wall, and I never saw such a sight in my life. I tore myself out of it in such a hurry that I gave myself a kink in the neck.

I sat down on the side of the bed, and commenced thinking about this head-peddling harpooneer, and his door mat. After thinking some time on the bed-side, I got up and took off my monkey jacket, and then stood in the middle of the room thinking. I then took off my coat, and thought a little more in my shirt sleeves. But beginning to feel very cold now, half undressed as I was, and remembering what the landlord said about the harpooneer's not coming home at all that night, it being so very late, I made no more ado, but jumped out of my pantaloons and boots, and then blowing out the light tumbled into bed, and commended myself to the care of heaven.

Whether that mattress was stuffed with corn-cobs or broken crockery, there is no telling, but I rolled about a good deal, and could not sleep for a long time. At last I slid off into a light doze, and had pretty nearly made a good offing towards the land of Nod, when I heard a heavy footfall in the passage, and saw a glimmer of light come into the room from under the door.

Lord save me, thinks I, that must be the harpooneer, the infernal head-peddler. But I lay perfectly still, and resolved not to say a word till spoken to. Holding a light in one hand, and that identical New Zealand head in the other, the stranger entered the room, and without looking towards the bed, placed his candle a good way off from me on the floor in one corner, and then began working away at the knotted cords of the large bag I before spoke of as being in the room. I was all eagerness to see his face, but he kept it averted for some time while employed in unlacing the bag's mouth. This accomplished, however, he turned round—when, good heavens! what a sight! Such a face! It was of a dark, purplish, yellow colour, here and there stuck over with large blackish looking squares. Yes, it's just as I thought, he's a terrible bedfellow; he's been in a fight, got dreadfully cut, and here he is, just from the surgeon. But at that moment he chanced to turn his face so towards the light, that I plainly saw they could not be sticking-plasters at all, those black squares on his cheeks. They were stains of some sort or other. At first I knew not what to make of this; but soon an inkling of the truth occurred to me. I remembered a story of a white man—a whaleman too—who, falling among the cannibals, had been tattooed by them. I concluded that this harpooneer, in the course of his distant voyages, must have met with a similar adventure. And what is it, thought I, after all! It's only his outside; a man can be honest in any sort of skin. But then, what to make of his unearthly complexion, that part of it, I mean, lying round about, and completely independent of the squares of tattooing. To be sure, it might be nothing but a good coat of tropical tanning; but I never heard of a hot sun's tanning a white man into a purplish yellow one. However, I had never been in the South Seas; and perhaps the sun there produced these extraordinary effects upon the skin. Now, while all these ideas were passing through me like lightning, this harpooneer never noticed me at all. But, after some difficulty having opened his bag, he commenced fumbling in it, and presently pulled out a sort of tomahawk, and a seal-skin wallet with the hair on. Placing these on the old chest in the middle of the room, he then took the New Zealand head—a ghastly thing enough—and crammed it down into the bag. He now took off his hat—a new beaver hat—when I came nigh singing out with fresh surprise. There was no hair on his head—none to speak of at least—nothing but a small scalp-knot twisted up on his forehead. His bald purplish head now looked for all the world like a mildewed skull. Had not the stranger stood between me and the door, I would have bolted out of it quicker than ever I bolted a dinner.

Even as it was, I thought something of slipping out of the window, but it was the second floor back. I am no coward, but what to make of this head-peddling purple rascal altogether passed my comprehension. Ignorance is the parent of fear, and being completely nonplussed and confounded about the stranger, I confess I was now as much afraid of him as if it was the devil himself who had thus broken into my room at the dead of night. In fact, I was so afraid of him that I was not game enough just then to address him, and demand a satisfactory answer concerning what seemed inexplicable in him.

Meanwhile, he continued the business of undressing, and at last showed his chest and arms. As I live, these covered parts of him were checkered with the same squares as his face; his back, too, was all over the same dark squares; he seemed to have been in a Thirty Years' War, and just escaped from it with a sticking-plaster shirt. Still more, his very legs were marked, as if a parcel of dark green frogs were running up the trunks of young palms. It was now quite plain that he must be some abominable savage or other shipped aboard of a whaleman in the South Seas, and so landed in this Christian country. I quaked to think of it. A peddler of heads too—perhaps the heads of his own brothers. He might take a fancy to mine—heavens! look at that tomahawk!

But there was no time for shuddering, for now the savage went about something that completely fascinated my attention, and convinced me that he must indeed be a heathen. Going to his heavy grego, or wrapall, or dreadnaught, which he had previously hung on a chair, he fumbled in the pockets, and produced at length a curious little deformed image with a hunch on its back, and exactly the colour of a three days' old Congo baby. Remembering the embalmed head, at first I almost thought that this black manikin was a real baby preserved in some similar manner. But seeing that it was not at all limber, and that it glistened a good deal like polished ebony, I concluded that it must be nothing but a wooden idol, which indeed it proved to be. For now the savage goes up to the empty fire-place, and removing the papered fire-board, sets up this little hunch-backed image, like a tenpin, between the andirons. The chimney jambs and all the bricks inside were very sooty, so that I thought this fire-place made a very appropriate little shrine or chapel for his Congo idol.

I now screwed my eyes hard towards the half hidden image, feeling but ill at ease meantime—to see what was next to follow. First he takes about a double handful of shavings out of his grego pocket, and places them carefully before the idol; then laying a bit of ship biscuit on top and applying the flame from the lamp, he kindled the shavings into a sacrificial blaze. Presently, after many hasty snatches into the fire, and still hastier withdrawals of his fingers (whereby he seemed to be scorching them badly), he at last succeeded in drawing out the biscuit; then blowing off the heat and ashes a little, he made a polite offer of it to the little negro. But the little devil did not seem to fancy such dry sort of fare at all; he never moved his lips. All these strange antics were accompanied by still stranger guttural noises from the devotee, who seemed to be praying in a sing-song or else singing some pagan psalmody or other, during which his face twitched about in the most unnatural manner. At last extinguishing the fire, he took the idol up very unceremoniously, and bagged it again in his grego pocket as carelessly as if he were a sportsman bagging a dead woodcock.

All these queer proceedings increased my uncomfortableness, and seeing him now exhibiting strong symptoms of concluding his business operations, and jumping into bed with me, I thought it was high time, now or never, before the light was put out, to break the spell in which I had so long been bound.

But the interval I spent in deliberating what to say, was a fatal one. Taking up his tomahawk from the table, he examined the head of it for an instant, and then holding it to the light, with his mouth at the handle, he puffed out great clouds of tobacco smoke. The next moment the light was extinguished, and this wild cannibal, tomahawk between his teeth, sprang into bed with me. I sang out, I could not help it now; and giving a sudden grunt of astonishment he began feeling me.

Stammering out something, I knew not what, I rolled away from him against the wall, and then conjured him, whoever or whatever he might be, to keep quiet, and let me get up and light the lamp again. But his guttural responses satisfied me at once that he but ill comprehended my meaning.

“Who-e debel you?”—he at last said—“you no speak-e, dam-me, I kill-e.” And so saying the lighted tomahawk began flourishing about me in the dark.

“Landlord, for God's sake, Peter Coffin!” shouted I. “Landlord! Watch! Coffin! Angels! save me!”

“Speak-e! tell-ee me who-ee be, or dam-me, I kill-e!” again growled the cannibal, while his horrid flourishings of the tomahawk scattered the hot tobacco ashes about me till I thought my linen would get on fire. But thank heaven, at that moment the landlord came into the room light in hand, and leaping from the bed I ran up to him.

“Don't be afraid now,” said he, grinning again, “Queequeg here wouldn't harm a hair of your head.”

“Stop your grinning,” shouted I, “and why didn't you tell me that that infernal harpooneer was a cannibal?”

“I thought ye know'd it;—didn't I tell ye, he was a peddlin' heads around town?—but turn flukes again and go to sleep. Queequeg, look here—you sabbee me, I sabbee—you this man sleepe you—you sabbee?”

“Me sabbee plenty”—grunted Queequeg, puffing away at his pipe and sitting up in bed.

“You gettee in,” he added, motioning to me with his tomahawk, and throwing the clothes to one side. He really did this in not only a civil but a really kind and charitable way. I stood looking at him a moment. For all his tattooings he was on the whole a clean, comely looking cannibal. What's all this fuss I have been making about, thought I to myself—the man's a human being just as I am: he has just as much reason to fear me, as I have to be afraid of him. Better sleep with a sober cannibal than a drunken Christian.

“Landlord,” said I, “tell him to stash his tomahawk there, or pipe, or whatever you call it; tell him to stop smoking, in short, and I will turn in with him. But I don't fancy having a man smoking in bed with me. It's dangerous. Besides, I ain't insured.”

This being told to Queequeg, he at once complied, and again politely motioned me to get into bed—rolling over to one side as much as to say—“I won't touch a leg of ye.”

“Good night, landlord,” said I, “you may go.”

I turned in, and never slept better in my life.
