Ge e ba go Starbuck ponagalo ya Squid e be e le selo sa dipontšho, go Queequeg e be e le selo se se fapanego kudu.

Gobane karolo ye ya Lewatle la India yeo ka nako yeo re bego re sepela ka yona ga se seo banna ba maruarua ba se bitšago lefelo le le phelago; ke gore, e nea dipono tše sego kae tša di- porpoise, di- dolphin, dihlapi tše di fofago le badudi ba bangwe ba mafolofolo ba meetse ao a šišinyago kudu, go feta bao ba lego ka ntle ga Rio de la Plata, goba mobu wa ka lebopong go tloga Peru.

E be e le sebaka sa ka sa go ema hlogong ya foreast; gomme ka magetla a-ka a ithekgile ka diširo tša bogoši tšeo di fokotšegilego, ke ile ka thekesela go ya le morago ka go se dire selo moyeng wo o bego o bonagala e le o loile. Ere go lebala ka mo go feletšego go ile gwa tla godimo ga ka, ke be ke lemogile gore basesiši ba dikepe kua main le mizzen-mast-heads ba be ba šetše ba otsela. Gomme bona! kgauswi ka fase ga lee ya rena, e sego difathome tše masomenne go tloga, Leruarua le legolo la Peu le be le rapaletše le kgokologa ka meetseng bjalo ka sebopego sa go menoga sa sekepe sa sekepe, mokokotlo wa gagwe wo o nabilego, wo o phadimago, wa mmala wa Ethiopia, o phadima mahlaseding a letšatši bjalo ka seipone. Eupša ka go tšwafa ka maphoto ka gare ga sebjana sa lewatle, gomme ka mehla le anon ka khutšo a tšholla jete ya gagwe ya mouwane, leruarua le be le bonagala bjalo ka burgher ya portly yeo e kgogago phaephe ya yona ya thapama ye borutho. Eupša phaephe yeo, leruarua la go hloka, e be e le la gago la mafelelo. Bjalo ka ge eka se bethilwe ke lepara le lengwe la moloi, sekepe seo se robetšego le yo mongwe le yo mongwe yo a robetšego ka go sona ka nako e tee ba ile ba thoma go phafoga; gomme mantšu a fetago masome a go tšwa dikarolong ka moka tša sekepe, ka nako e tee le dinoko tše tharo go tšwa godimo, a ile a goeletša sello se se tlwaetšego, ge hlapi ye kgolo e be e tšholla letswai le le phadimago moyeng ka go nanya le ka mehla.

“Hlwekiša dikepe! Luff!” gwa goeletša Ahaba. Gomme ka go kwa taelo ya gagwe ka noši, o ile a kitimiša lešoba la sekepe fase pele mosepediši a ka swara dipoko.

Go goeletša ga tšhoganetšo ga sehlopha sa basepediši go swanetše go ba go ile gwa tšhoša maruarua; gomme pele dikepe di be di le fase, di retologa ka bogolo, o ile a sesa a tloga go ya leeward, eupša ka khutšo ye e sa fetogego, gomme a dira maphoto a mmalwa kudu ge a sesa, mo e lego gore ka go nagana gore ka morago ga tšohle a ka no se hlwe a tšhoga, Ahaba o ile a fa taelo ya gore a se tšhoge go swanetše go dirišwa sekepe sa go sesa, gomme ga go motho yo a swanetšego go bolela ge e se ka go sebela. Ka gona re dutše bjalo ka ma-India a Ontario dithunya tša dikepe, re ile ra sesa ka lebelo eupša ka setu; go se amogele ka go iketla ga diseila tšeo di se nago lešata tšeo di bewago. Ga bjale, ge re thelela ka go realo re kitimiša, phoofolo ya go tšhoša e ile ya phaphasela mosela wa yona ka go otlologa moyeng, gomme ka morago ya nwelela ka ntle ga pono bjalo ka tora yeo e meditšwego.

“Go na le go ya difluke!” e be e le sello, tsebišo yeo e ilego ya latelwa gatee-tee ke go tšweletša ga Stubb motšhitšhi wa gagwe le go gotetša phaephe ya gagwe, ka gobane bjale go ile gwa newa nako ya go khutša. Go be go le molaleng, bjale, gore leruarua le be le šetše le lemogile bao ba le phegelelago ka nako ye telele. Ka gona go homola ka moka ga kelohloko go be go se sa šoma. Di- paddle di ile tša lahlwa, gomme di-oar di ile tša tsena ka go hlaboša. Gomme a sa dutše a budulela phaephe ya gagwe, Stubb o ile a hlaba mokgoši go sehlopha sa gagwe go ya tlhaselong.

Ee, phetogo e kgolo e be e tlile godimo ga dihlapi. Ka moka ba phela go ya kotsing ya gagwe, o be a eya “hlogo ka ntle”; karolo yeo e tšwelelago ka go sekama go tšwa komelong ya bohlanya yeo a e dirilego.*

Gaešita le ge go bonagala e le e kgolo kudu, go fihla ga bjale ke karolo e kgahlišago kudu ka yena. Ka ntle le moo, bophara bja karolo ya ka godimo ya karolo ya ka pele ya hlogo ya gagwe bo bjalo, le sebopego sa meetse a go segwa seo se tshesanego sa karolo ya ka fase, mo e lego gore ka go phagamiša hlogo ya gagwe ka go sekama, ka go realo go ka thwe o iphetoša go tšwa go bluff-bowed galliot e diegago ka gare ga seketswana sa go fofiša sefofane sa New York seo se nago le ntlha e bogale.

“Mo thomeng, mo thomeng, banna ba ka! Le se ke la itlhaganela; tšea nako e ntši—eupša mo thome; mo thome bjalo ka go opa magofsi ga diaduma, ke tšona fela,” gwa goeletša Stubb, a tšholla muši ge a bolela. “Mo thome, bjale; ba fe setorouku se setelele le se matla, Tashtego. Mo thome, Tash, mošemane wa ka—mo thome, ka moka; eupša dula o fodile, dula o fodile—dikomkomere ke lentšu—go bonolo, go bonolo—go mo thoma feela bjalo ka lehu le le nyamišago le diabolo tše di nyenyago, gomme o tsoše bahu bao ba epetšwego ba thekgile mabitleng a bona, bašemane—ke sona feela. Mo thome!”

“Woo-hoo! Wa-hee!” gwa goeletša Gay-Header ge a araba, a emiša di-whoop tše dingwe tša kgale tša ntwa magodimong; ge mosesi yo mongwe le yo mongwe ka seketswaneng seo se gateletšegilego a be a kgorometšwa pele ka go se rate ka seterouku se tee se segolo sa go eta pele seo Moindia yo a fišegelago a se filego.

Eupša go goeletša ga gagwe mo go hlagago go ile gwa arabja ke ba bangwe ba lešoka ka mo go swanago. “Kee-hee! Kee-hee!” gwa goeletša Daggoo, a itapiša pele le morago setulong sa gagwe, bjalo ka nkwe yeo e sepelago ka lesakaneng la gagwe.

“Ka-la! Koo-loo!” gwa bokolla Queequeg, bjalo ka ge eka o betha melomo ya gagwe godimo ga molomo wa nama ya Grenadier. Gomme ka go rialo ka di-oar le go goeletša di- keel di sega lewatle. Go sa le bjalo, Stubb a boloka lefelo la gagwe ka bene, o be a sa kgothaletša banna ba gagwe go thoma, nako ye ka moka a budulela muši molomong wa gagwe. Go swana le ba itlhobogilego ba ile ba goga gomme ba gateletšega, go fihlela mokgoši wa kamogelo o kwewa—“Ema, Tashtego!—mo nee wona!” Harpoon e ile ya lahlelwa. “Stern ka moka!” Bašomi ba dikepe ba ile ba thekga meetse; ka yona nako yeo selo se sengwe se ile sa fiša gomme sa šišinya go bapa le le lengwe le le lengwe la matsogo a bona. E be e le mola wa maleatlana. Ka ponyo ya leihlo pele ga moo, Stubb o be a swere ka lebelo mapheko a mabedi a tlaleletšo le yona go dikologa hlogo ya loggerhead, moo, ka lebaka la go oketšega ga yona ka go dikologa ka lebelo, muši wo mopududu wa hempen bjale o ile wa tšholla godimo gomme wa hlakana le muši wo o sa fetogego go tšwa phaepheng ya gagwe. Ge mola o feta go dikologa le go dikologa hlogo ya loggerhead; ka gona gape, pele ga ge e fihla ntlheng yeo, e ile ya feta ka makhopho ka gare le ka diatleng ka bobedi tša Stubb, tšeo go tšwa go tšona mašela a diatla, goba dikwere tša lešela la go quilted tšeo ka dinako tše dingwe di bego di aparwa ka dinako tše, di ilego tša wela fase ka phošo. Go be go swana le go swara tšhoša ye bogale ya lenaba ya magale a mabedi ka legare, gomme lenaba leo ka mehla le katanela go e ntšha ka gare ga seatleng sa gago.

“Kolobiša mothaladi! kolobiša mothaladi!” gwa goeletša Stubb go mosesiši wa bate (yo a bego a dutše kgauswi le bate) yo, a phamolago katiba ya gagwe, a ilego a kitimela meetse a lewatle ka gare ga yona.* Go ile gwa dirwa go retologa mo go oketšegilego, moo mothaladi o ilego wa thoma go swara lefelo la wona. *Ka karolo go bontšha go se nyakege ga tiro ye, mo go ka bolelwa, gore, ka go dihlapi tša kgale tša Dutch, mop e be e šomišwa go thula mothaladi wa go kitima ka meetse; dikepeng tše dingwe tše dintši, kolobe ya kota goba bailer e beetšwe ka thoko bakeng sa morero woo. Go tšwa mothalong wo o thothomelago wo o atološago botelele ka moka bja karolo ya ka godimo ya seketswana, le go tloga ge bjale e le yeo e tiilego kudu go feta thapo ya harepa, o be o tla nagana gore sekepe se be se na le di- keel tše pedi—e nngwe e aroganya meetse, e nngwe e le moya—bjalo ka sekepe churned on ka bobedi dielemente tše di ganetšanago ka nako e tee. Lephoto le le tšwelago pele le ile la bapala di- bora; eddy e sa kgaotšego e dikologago ka morago ga gagwe; gomme, ka tšhišinyego ye nnyane go tšwa ka gare, gaešita le ge e se ya monwana o monnyane, sekepe sa go thothomela, sa go phatloga se ile sa inama godimo ga sethunya sa sona sa go tšhošwa ka lewatleng. Ka tsela yeo ba ile ba kitimela; monna yo mongwe le yo mongwe ka maatla le main a kgomaretše setulong sa gagwe, go thibela go lahlelwa go mafofa; le sebopego se setelele sa Tashtego ka sekepeng sa go otlela se khuname mo e nyakilego go ba gabedi, e le gore a theogele fase bogare bja gagwe bja maatlakgogedi. Di-Atlantic le Pacific ka moka di ile tša bonagala di fetile ge di dutše di thuntšha tseleng ya tšona, go fihlela mafelelong leruarua le fokotša go fofa ga lona ka tsela e itšego.

“Gogela ka gare—go goga ka gare!” gwa goeletša Stubb go mošomi wa bora! gomme, ba lebeletše go dikologa go leba leruarua, diatla ka moka di ile tša thoma go gogela sekepe go yena, mola sekepe se be se sa gogwa. Go se go ye kae a phagama godimo ka lehlakore la gagwe, Stubb, a bjala lengwele la gagwe ka go tia ka gare ga cleat ya go se loke, o ile a kitimela ka mosebe ka morago ga mosebe ka gare ga dihlapi tše di fofago; ka lentšu la taelo, sekepe se be se fapantšha ka morago go tšwa tseleng ya go tšhoša ga leruarua, gomme ka morago sa rotogela godimo bakeng sa go lahlela gape.

Lephoto le lehubedu bjale le ile la tšhologa go tšwa mahlakoreng ka moka a phoofolo ya go tšhoša bjalo ka melapo go theoga mmotong. Mmele wa gagwe wo o tlaišwago ga se wa kgokologa ka gare ga letswai eupša ka mading, ao a bego a bubble le go tšhologa ka diferlong ka morago ga bona. Letšatši le le sekamego leo le bego le bapala godimo ga letamo le le khubedu ka lewatleng, le ile la romela morago ponagatšo ya lona sefahlegong se sengwe le se sengwe, gore ka moka ba phadima go yo mongwe le yo mongwe bjalo ka banna ba bahubedu. “Gogela godimo—gogela godimo!” bjale o ile a llela mohlabani wa bora, ge leruarua leo le bego le fokotšega le be le iketla ka pefelo ya lona. “Gogela godimo!—kgauswi le!” gomme seketswana se be se sepela go bapa le lehlakore la hlapi. Ge a fihlelela kgole godimo ga bora, Stubb o ile a šišinya lerumo la gagwe le letelele le bogale ka go nanya ka gare ga hlapi, gomme a le boloka fao, a šišinya le go šišinya ka kelohloko, bjalo ka ge eka ka kelohloko o nyaka go ikwa ka morago ga sešupanako se sengwe sa gauta seo leruarua le ka bago le se metše, le seo a bego a se boifa ya go thubega ere a ka e goketša ka ntle. Eupša sešupanako seo sa gauta seo a bego a se nyaka e be e le bophelo bja ka gare bja hlapi. Gomme bjale e otilwe; ka gobane, go thoma go tšwa go go tšhoga ga gagwe go ya go selo seo se sa bolelwago seo se bitšwago “go tšhologa” ga gagwe, phoofolo ya go tšhoša e ile ya thekesela mading a yona ka mo go šiišago, ya iphuthelela ka go gaša mo go sa tsenegego, ga bohlanya, mo go belago, mo e lego gore sekepe seo se bego se le kotsing, se wela ka morago gatee-tee, se ile sa ba le lešata le legolo ka bofofu gore se ka katana go tšwa go tšwa go leswiswi leo la phrensied go ya moyeng wo o sekilego wa letšatši.

Gomme bjale e fokotšega ka lephoto la gagwe, leruarua le ile la boela la kgokologela ka ntle gore le bonagale; go tšhologa go tloga ka lehlakoreng le lengwe go ya go le lengwe; spasmodically dilating le contracting hae spout-lesoba, le bohale, petsoha, agonized respirations. Mafelelong, go tšhologa ka morago ga go tšhologa ga gore e khubedu yeo e kgotletšwego, bjalo ka ge eka e be e le di- lees tše pherese tša beine e khubedu, di ile tša thuntšhwa moyeng wo o tšhogilego; gomme a wela morago gape, a kitima a rotha go theoga mahlakoreng a gagwe ao a sa šikinyegego ka lewatleng. Pelo ya gagwe e be e phatlogile!

“O hwile, Mna Stubb,” a realo Daggoo.

KGAOLO YA 62. The Dart.

Lentšu leo ​​le lego mabapi le tiragalo kgaolong ya mafelelo.

Go ya ka tšhomišo ye e sa fetogego ya lefelo la go rea dihlapi, seketswana sa maruarua se kgorometša go tloga sekepeng, ka hlogo goba mmolai wa maruarua e le mosepediši wa nakwana, gomme mohlabani wa harpooneer goba mofakiši wa maruarua a goga sekepe sa ka pele, seo se tsebjago e le harpooneer-oar . Bjale e nyaka letsogo le le tiilego le le tšhogilego gore e thule tšhipi ya mathomo ka gare ga hlapi; ka gobane gantši, go seo se bitšwago mosebe o motelele, sedirišwa se boima se swanetše go lahlelwa bokgoleng bja dimithara tše masomepedi goba tše masometharo. Eupša go sa šetšwe gore go kitimiša go tšea nako e telele le mo go lapišago gakaakang, go letetšwe gore modiri wa harpoone a goge sekepe sa gagwe mo nakong ye go fihla bokgoleng bjo bo feletšego; ka kgonthe, go letetšwe gore a bee mohlala wa modiro wo o phagametšego motho go ba bangwe ka moka, e sego feela ka go soka mo go makatšago, eupša ka go goeletša mo go hlabošago le mo go boeletšwago ka sebete; le seo e lego sona go dula o goeletša ka godimo ga khamphase ya motho, mola mešifa e mengwe ka moka e gateletšegile gomme e thomile seripa-gare—seo seo e lego sona ga go yo a se tsebago ge e se bao ba se lekilego. Go e nngwe, ga ke kgone go lla ka pelo kudu le go šoma ka go se šetše kudu ka nako e tee le e tee. Ka gona, boemong bjo bja go gateletšega le bja go lla, ka mokokotlo wa gagwe o lebeletše hlapi, ka nako e tee mošomi wa harpoone yo a lapilego o kwa mokgoši o kgahlišago—“Ema, o mo nee wona!” Bjale o swanetše go lahlela le go tiiša sekepe sa gagwe sa go sesa, a retologa bogareng bja gagwe seripagare sa tsela, a thope harpoon ya gagwe go tšwa crotch, gomme ka seo matla a mannyane a ka šalago, o essays go e lahlela ka tsela ye nngwe ka gare ga leruarua. Ga go makatše ge, go tšea sehlopha ka moka sa dikepe tša banna ba maruarua ka mmele, gore go tšwa go dibaka tše masomehlano tše di lokilego tša mosebe, ga se tše hlano tšeo di atlegago; ga go makatše ge ba-harpoone ba bantši gakaakaa bao ba se nago mahlatse ba rogwa ka tsela ya bohlanya le go nyamišwa; ga go makatše ge ba bangwe ba bona ba ile ba thunya methapo ya bona ya madi e le ka kgonthe ka seketswaneng; ga go makatše ge banna ba bangwe ba maruarua a peu ba se gona ka nywaga e mene ka megopo e mene; ga go makatše ge go beng ba dikepe ba bantši, go tsoma maruarua e fo ba go tshwenyega mo go lahlegelwago; ka gobane ke mohlabani wa harpoone yo a tšeago leeto, gomme ge o ka ntšha mohemo mmeleng wa gagwe o ka letela bjang go o hwetša moo mola bontši bo nyakega!

Le mo, ge e ba mosebe o atlega, gona ka motsotso wa bobedi o bohlokwa, ke gore ge leruarua le thoma go kitima, mo-boatheader le harpooneer ka mo go swanago ba thoma go kitima pele le ka morago, go ba kotsing yeo e lego kgaufsi ya bona le ba bangwe ka moka. Ke ka nako yeo ba fetolago mafelo; gomme molaodi wa sekepe, mohlankedi yo mogolo wa sekepe se senyenyane, o tšea seteišene sa gagwe se se swanetšego ka gare ga bora bja sekepe.

Bjale, ga ke na taba le gore ke mang yo a bolokago se se fapanego, eupša tše ka moka ke tša bošilo e bile ga di nyakege. Molaodi wa hlogo o swanetše go dula ka bora go tloga pele go fihla mafelelong; bobedi o swanetše go tšhoša harpoon le lerumo, gomme ga go na go soka le ge e le gofe mo go swanetšego go letelwa go yena, ka ntle le ka tlase ga maemo ao a lego molaleng go morei le ge e le ofe wa dihlapi. Ke a tseba gore se ka dinako tše dingwe se be se tla akaretša go lahlegelwa ganyenyane ga lebelo ge go kitimišwa; eupša phihlelo e telele go banna ba maruarua ba fapa-fapanego ba ditšhaba tše fetago e tee e nkgodišitše gore bontšing bjo bogolo bja go palelwa ga go rea dihlapi, ga se ya ba ka tsela le ge e le efe lebelo la maruarua kudu go etša go lapa mo go hlalositšwego pele ga harpooneer mo go bakilego bona.

Go inšorentšha bokgoni bjo bogolo kudu ka mosebe, ba-harpooneer ba lefase le ba swanetše go thoma go ema ka maoto go tšwa go go se dire selo, e sego go tšwa ka baka la go itapiša.





KGAOLO YA 63. Crotch.

Go tšwa kutu, makala a a gola; go tšwa go tšona, makala. Ka gona, dithutong tše di tšweletšago, godiša dikgaolo.

“‘Hind de hatchway, ka seketswana sa sekepe, goin’ ober de Roanoke.”

“O belegwe ka seketswana sa sekepe! Seo ke sa queer, le sona. Eupša ke nyaka go tseba gore o belegetšwe nageng efe, apea!”

“Na ga se ka re naga ya de Roanoke?” a lla ka bogale.

“Aowa, ga se wa dira bjalo, apea; eupša ke tla go botša gore ke tla go eng, apea. O swanetše go ya gae gomme o tswalwe lefsa; ga o tsebe go apea nama ya maruarua ga bjale.”

“Bress moya wa ka, ge nka apea noder one,” a rora, ka bogale, a retologa go tloga.

“Boela morago, apea;—mo, mphe di-tong tšeo;—bjale tšea karolwana yeo ya steak moo, gomme o mpotše ge e ba o nagana gore steak yeo e apeilwe ka fao e swanetšego go ba ka gona? E tšee, ke re”—a swere di- tong go yena—“e tšee, gomme o e latswe.”

Ka go fokola a betha melomo ya gagwe ye e omilego godimo ga yona ka motsotswana, mo-negro wa kgale o ile a ngunanguna, “Best cooked 'teak I eber taste; joosy, monokotšoai joosy.”

“Apea,” a realo Stubb, a ikwere gape; “na o wa kereke?”

“Ke fetile e tee gatee kua Cape-Down,” a realo mokgalabje ka go nyama.

“And you have once in your life passed a holy church in Cape-Town, where you doubtless overheard a holy parson addressing his hearers as his beloved fellow-creatures, have you, cook! And yet you come here, and tell me such a dreadful lie as you did just now, eh?” said Stubb. “Where do you expect to go to, cook?”

“Go to bed berry soon,” he mumbled, half-turning as he spoke.

“Avast! heave to! I mean when you die, cook. It's an awful question. Now what's your answer?”

“When dis old brack man dies,” said the negro slowly, changing his whole air and demeanor, “he hisself won't go nowhere; but some bressed angel will come and fetch him.”

“Fetch him? How? In a coach and four, as they fetched Elijah? And fetch him where?”

“Up dere,” said Fleece, holding his tongs straight over his head, and keeping it there very solemnly.

“So, then, you expect to go up into our main-top, do you, cook, when you are dead? But don't you know the higher you climb, the colder it gets? Main-top, eh?”

“Didn't say dat t'all,” said Fleece, again in the sulks.

“You said up there, didn't you? and now look yourself, and see where your tongs are pointing. But, perhaps you expect to get into heaven by crawling through the lubber's hole, cook; but, no, no, cook, you don't get there, except you go the regular way, round by the rigging. It's a ticklish business, but must be done, or else it's no go. But none of us are in heaven yet. Drop your tongs, cook, and hear my orders. Do ye hear? Hold your hat in one hand, and clap t'other a'top of your heart, when I'm giving my orders, cook. What! that your heart, there?—that's your gizzard! Aloft! aloft!—that's it—now you have it. Hold it there now, and pay attention.”

“All 'dention,” said the old black, with both hands placed as desired, vainly wriggling his grizzled head, as if to get both ears in front at one and the same time.

“Well then, cook, you see this whale-steak of yours was so very bad, that I have put it out of sight as soon as possible; you see that, don't you? Well, for the future, when you cook another whale-steak for my private table here, the capstan, I'll tell you what to do so as not to spoil it by overdoing. Hold the steak in one hand, and show a live coal to it with the other; that done, dish it; d'ye hear? And now to-morrow, cook, when we are cutting in the fish, be sure you stand by to get the tips of his fins; have them put in pickle. As for the ends of the flukes, have them soused, cook. There, now ye may go.”

But Fleece had hardly got three paces off, when he was recalled.

“Cook, give me cutlets for supper to-morrow night in the mid-watch. D'ye hear? away you sail, then.—Halloa! stop! make a bow before you go.—Avast heaving again! Whale-balls for breakfast—don't forget.”

“Wish, by gor! whale eat him, 'stead of him eat whale. I'm bressed if he ain't more of shark dan Massa Shark hisself,” muttered the old man, limping away; with which sage ejaculation he went to his hammock.





CHAPTER 65. The Whale as a Dish.

That mortal man should feed upon the creature that feeds his lamp, and, like Stubb, eat him by his own light, as you may say; this seems so outlandish a thing that one must needs go a little into the history and philosophy of it.

It is upon record, that three centuries ago the tongue of the Right Whale was esteemed a great delicacy in France, and commanded large prices there. Also, that in Henry VIIIth's time, a certain cook of the court obtained a handsome reward for inventing an admirable sauce to be eaten with barbacued porpoises, which, you remember, are a species of whale. Porpoises, indeed, are to this day considered fine eating. The meat is made into balls about the size of billiard balls, and being well seasoned and spiced might be taken for turtle-balls or veal balls. The old monks of Dunfermline were very fond of them. They had a great porpoise grant from the crown.

The fact is, that among his hunters at least, the whale would by all hands be considered a noble dish, were there not so much of him; but when you come to sit down before a meat-pie nearly one hundred feet long, it takes away your appetite. Only the most unprejudiced of men like Stubb, nowadays partake of cooked whales; but the Esquimaux are not so fastidious. We all know how they live upon whales, and have rare old vintages of prime old train oil. Zogranda, one of their most famous doctors, recommends strips of blubber for infants, as being exceedingly juicy and nourishing. And this reminds me that certain Englishmen, who long ago were accidentally left in Greenland by a whaling vessel—that these men actually lived for several months on the mouldy scraps of whales which had been left ashore after trying out the blubber. Among the Dutch whalemen these scraps are called “fritters”; which, indeed, they greatly resemble, being brown and crisp, and smelling something like old Amsterdam housewives' dough-nuts or oly-cooks, when fresh. They have such an eatable look that the most self-denying stranger can hardly keep his hands off.

But what further depreciates the whale as a civilized dish, is his exceeding richness. He is the great prize ox of the sea, too fat to be delicately good. Look at his hump, which would be as fine eating as the buffalo's (which is esteemed a rare dish), were it not such a solid pyramid of fat. But the spermaceti itself, how bland and creamy that is; like the transparent, half-jellied, white meat of a cocoanut in the third month of its growth, yet far too rich to supply a substitute for butter. Nevertheless, many whalemen have a method of absorbing it into some other substance, and then partaking of it. In the long try watches of the night it is a common thing for the seamen to dip their ship-biscuit into the huge oil-pots and let them fry there awhile. Many a good supper have I thus made.

In the case of a small Sperm Whale the brains are accounted a fine dish. The casket of the skull is broken into with an axe, and the two plump, whitish lobes being withdrawn (precisely resembling two large puddings), they are then mixed with flour, and cooked into a most delectable mess, in flavor somewhat resembling calves' head, which is quite a dish among some epicures; and every one knows that some young bucks among the epicures, by continually dining upon calves' brains, by and by get to have a little brains of their own, so as to be able to tell a calf's head from their own heads; which, indeed, requires uncommon discrimination. And that is the reason why a young buck with an intelligent looking calf's head before him, is somehow one of the saddest sights you can see. The head looks a sort of reproachfully at him, with an “Et tu Brute!” expression.

It is not, perhaps, entirely because the whale is so excessively unctuous that landsmen seem to regard the eating of him with abhorrence; that appears to result, in some way, from the consideration before mentioned: ie that a man should eat a newly murdered thing of the sea, and eat it too by its own light. But no doubt the first man that ever murdered an ox was regarded as a murderer; perhaps he was hung; and if he had been put on his trial by oxen, he certainly would have been; and he certainly deserved it if any murderer does. Go to the meat-market of a Saturday night and see the crowds of live bipeds staring up at the long rows of dead quadrupeds. Does not that sight take a tooth out of the cannibal's jaw? Cannibals? who is not a cannibal? I tell you it will be more tolerable for the Fejee that salted down a lean missionary in his cellar against a coming famine; it will be more tolerable for that provident Fejee, I say, in the day of judgment, than for thee, civilized and enlightened gourmand, who nailest geese to the ground and feastest on their bloated livers in thy paté-de-foie-gras.

But Stubb, he eats the whale by its own light, does he? and that is adding insult to injury, is it? Look at your knife-handle, there, my civilized and enlightened gourmand dining off that roast beef, what is that handle made of?—what but the bones of the brother of the very ox you are eating? And what do you pick your teeth with, after devouring that fat goose? With a feather of the same fowl. And with what quill did the Secretary of the Society for the Suppression of Cruelty to Ganders formally indite his circulars? It is only within the last month or two that that society passed a resolution to patronize nothing but steel pens.





CHAPTER 66. The Shark Massacre.

When in the Southern Fishery, a captured Sperm Whale, after long and weary toil, is brought alongside late at night, it is not, as a general thing at least, customary to proceed at once to the business of cutting him in. For that business is an exceedingly laborious one; is not very soon completed; and requires all hands to set about it. Therefore, the common usage is to take in all sail; lash the helm a'lee; and then send every one below to his hammock till daylight, with the reservation that, until that time, anchor-watches shall be kept; that is, two and two for an hour, each couple, the crew in rotation shall mount the deck to see that all goes well.

But sometimes, especially upon the Line in the Pacific, this plan will not answer at all; because such incalculable hosts of sharks gather round the moored carcase, that were he left so for six hours, say, on a stretch, little more than the skeleton would be visible by morning. In most other parts of the ocean, however, where these fish do not so largely abound, their wondrous voracity can be at times considerably diminished, by vigorously stirring them up with sharp whaling-spades, a procedure notwithstanding, which, in some instances, only seems to tickle them into still greater activity. But it was not thus in the present case with the Pequod's sharks; though, to be sure, any man unaccustomed to such sights, to have looked over her side that night, would have almost thought the whole round sea was one huge cheese, and those sharks the maggots in it.

Nevertheless, upon Stubb setting the anchor-watch after his supper was concluded; and when, accordingly, Queequeg and a forecastle seaman came on deck, no small excitement was created among the sharks; for immediately suspending the cutting stages over the side, and lowering three lanterns, so that they cast long gleams of light over the turbid sea, these two mariners, darting their long whaling-spades, kept up an incessant murdering of the sharks,* by striking the keen steel deep into their skulls, seemingly their only vital part. But in the foamy confusion of their mixed and struggling hosts, the marksmen could not always hit their mark; and this brought about new revelations of the incredible ferocity of the foe. They viciously snapped, not only at each other's disembowelments, but like flexible bows, bent round, and bit their own; till those entrails seemed swallowed over and over again by the same mouth, to be oppositely voided by the gaping wound. Nor was this all. It was unsafe to meddle with the corpses and ghosts of these creatures. A sort of generic or Pantheistic vitality seemed to lurk in their very joints and bones, after what might be called the individual life had departed. Killed and hoisted on deck for the sake of his skin, one of these sharks almost took poor Queequeg's hand off, when he tried to shut down the dead lid of his murderous jaw.

*The whaling-spade used for cutting-in is made of the very best steel; is about the bigness of a man's spread hand; and in general shape, corresponds to the garden implement after which it is named; only its sides are perfectly flat, and its upper end considerably narrower than the lower. This weapon is always kept as sharp as possible; and when being used is occasionally honed, just like a razor. In its socket, a stiff pole, from twenty to thirty feet long, is inserted for a handle.

“Queequeg no care what god made him shark,” said the savage, agonizingly lifting his hand up and down; “wedder Fejee god or Nantucket god; but de god wat made shark must be one dam Ingin.”
