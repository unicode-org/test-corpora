KGAOLO YA 4. Counterpane.

Ge ke tsoga mesong e latelago mabapi le seetša sa mosegare, ke ile ka hwetša letsogo la Queequeg le lahlilwe godimo ga-ka ka mokgwa o lerato kudu le o lerato kudu. O be o nyakile o nagana gore ke bile mosadi wa gagwe. Counterpane e be e le ya patchwork, e tletše ka dikwere tše nnyane tše di makatšago tša mebala ya parti le dikhutlotharo; gomme letsogo le la gagwe le be le tattoo gohle ka labyrinth e sa felego ya Kereta ya sebopego, yeo go se nago dikarolo tše pedi tša yona tšeo e bego e le tša moriti o tee o nepagetšego—ka baka la go boloka letsogo la gagwe lewatleng ka mo go sa swanelago letšatšing le moriting, matsogo a hempe ya gagwe a phuthetšwe ka mo go sa laolegego ka dinako tše di fapa-fapanego—letsogo lona le la gagwe, ke re, le ile la nyaka lefase ka moka bjalo ka lešela la yona quilt yeo ya go ba le dipatchwork. Ka nnete, karolo e robetše godimo ga yona bjalo ka ge letsogo le dirile ge ke be ke tsoga la mathomo, ke be ke sa kgone go e bona go tšwa go quilt, ba ile ba kopanya mebala ya bona mmogo kudu; gomme e be e le feela ka maikwelo a boima bja mmele le kgateletšo moo ke ilego ka kgona go bona gore Queequeg o be a nkgokarela.

Maikutlo a-ka a be a makatša. E re ke leke go di hlalosa. Ge ke be ke sa le ngwana, ke gopola gabotse boemo bjo bo swanago ka tsela e itšego bjo bo ilego bja ntiragalela; e ka ba e be e le selo sa kgonthe goba toro, le ka mohla ga se ka ka ka kgona go dula ka mo go feletšego. Boemo e be e le bjo. Ke be ke dutše ke sega caper ye nngwe goba ye nngwe—ke nagana gore e be e leka go gagabela godimo ga tshimini, bjalo ka ge ke bone go sweep go gonnyane go dira matšatši a mmalwa a go feta; le mma wa ka wa bobedi yo, ka tsela ye nngwe goba ye nngwe, a bego a nkhwetša ka mehla, goba a nthomela malaong ke sa je dijo tša mantšiboa,—mma o ile a nkgoga ka maoto ka ntle ga tshimini gomme a nphuthela go ya malaong, le ge e be e le iri ya bobedi fela thapama ya la 21st June, e lego letšatši le letelele kudu mo ngwageng karolong ya rena ya lefase. Ke ile ka ikwa ka tsela e boifišago. Eupša go be go se na thušo ya yona, ka fao godimo ga manamelo ke ile ka ya phapošing ya ka ye nnyane lebatong la boraro, ka itlhabola ka go nanya ka mo go kgonegago gore ke bolaye nako, gomme ka go hemela godimo mo go babago ka tsena magareng ga mašela.

Ke ile ka robala fao ka go nyamiša ke bala gore diiri tše lesometshela ka moka di swanetše go feta pele nka holofela tsogo. Diiri tše lesometshela malaong! ye nnyane ya mokokotlo wa ka e be e opa ge ke nagana ka yona. Gomme le yona e be e le bofefo kudu; letšatši le phadima ka lefasetereng, le go lla mo gogolo ga dikoloi ditarateng, le modumo wa mantšu a basodoma gohle ka ntlong. Ke ile ka ikwa ke mpefala le go feta—mafelelong ke ile ka tsoga, ka apara gomme ka theogela fase ka boleta ka maoto a-ka ao a nago le distoki, ka tsoma mma wa-ka wa bobedi gomme gatee-tee ka itahlela maotong a gagwe, ke mo kgopela e le mogau o kgethegilego gore a mphe go thelela mo gobotse bakeng sa-ka boitshwaro bjo bo gobogilego; selo le ge e le sefe e le ka kgonthe ge e se go nkgahlola go bolela maaka abed nako e telele gakaakaa yeo e sa kgotlelelegego. Eupša e be e le yo mokaone kudu le yo a nago le letswalo kudu go bomme ba bobedi, gomme morago ke ile ka swanelwa ke go ya phapošing ya ka. Ka diiri tše mmalwa ke ile ka robala moo ke phafogile kudu, ke ikwa ke le yo mobe kudu go feta kamoo ke kilego ka dira ka gona ga e sa le go tloga ka nako yeo, gaešita le ka baka la madimabe a magolo kudu ao a ilego a latela. Mafelelong ke swanetše go ba ke ile ka wela torong e tshwenyago ya go otsela; gomme ka go nanya ke tsoga go yona—ka seripa-gare ke nweletše ditoro—ke ile ka bula mahlo a-ka, gomme phapoši ya pele yeo e bonešitšwego ke letšatši bjale e be e phuthetšwe ke leswiswi la ka ntle. Gateetee ke ile ka kwa tšhogo e kitima foreime ya-ka ka moka; go be go se selo seo se bego se swanetše go bonwa, gomme go be go se na selo seo se bego se swanetše go kwewa; eupša seatla se se phagametšego tlhago se be se bonagala se beilwe ka go sa-ka. Letsogo la ka le be le fegilwe godimo ga counterpane, gomme sebopego se se se nago leina, seo se sa naganegego, se se homotšego goba phantom, yeo seatla e bego e le sa yona, se be se bonagala se dutše kgauswi ka thoko ga malao a ka. Gobane seo se bego se bonagala mengwaga e kgobokeditšwe ka mengwaga, ke ile ka robala fao, ke gatsetše ka dipoifo tše di šiišago kudu, ke sa iteta sebete sa go goga seatla sa ka; lega go le bjalo ke kile ka nagana gore ge nkabe nka e šišinya intšhi e tee, mantšu a go loya a šiišago a be a tla robega. Ke be ke sa tsebe kamoo temogo ye mafelelong e ilego ya thelela ka gona go tloga go nna; eupša ge ke tsoga mesong, ke ile ka e gopola ka moka ka go thothomela, gomme ka matšatši le dibeke le dikgwedi ka morago ga moo ke ile ka itahlela maitekong a gakantšhago a go hlalosa sephiri. Aowa, go fihla ka yona iri ye, gantši ke iphihla ka yona.

Bjale, tloša poifo ye e šiišago, gomme maikutlo a ka a go kwa seatla se se phagametšego tlhago ka go sa ka a be a swana kudu, ka go makatša ga ona, le ao ke itemogetšego ona ge ke tsoga le go bona letsogo la boheitene la Queequeg le lahlelwa go ntikologa. Eupša mafelelong ditiragalo ka moka tša bošegong bjo bo fetilego di ile tša ipoeletša ka go hlaphogelwa, ka e tee ka e tee, ka kgonthe e sa fetogego, gomme ka morago ke ile ka robala feela ke phela bothateng bjo bo segišago. Gobane gaešita le ge ke ile ka leka go šišinya letsogo la gagwe—go notlolla sefapano sa gagwe sa monyadi—le ge go le bjalo, a robetše bjalo ka ge a be a le bjalo, o be a sa dutše a nkgokarela ka go tia, bjalo ka ge eka ga go selo ge e se lehu seo se swanetšego go re aroganya. Ga bjale ke ile ka katanela go mo tsoša—“Queequeg!”—eupša karabo ya gagwe e nnoši e be e le go rora. Ke moka ke ile ka kgokologa, molala wa-ka o ikwa eka o ka gare ga molala wa pere; gomme gateetee a kwa a ngwatha go se nene. A lahlela ka thoko counterpane, fao go rapaletše tomahawk e robetše ka thoko ga sehlogo, bjalo ka ge eka ke lesea la sefahlego sa hatchet. Pickle ye botse, ka nnete, ke ile ka nagana; abed mo ka ntlong e sa tlwaelegago ka letšatši le le nabilego, ka motho yo a jago batho le tomahawk! “Queequeg!—ka leina la botse, Queequeg, tsoga!” Mafelelong, ka lebaka la go sothoga kudu, le ditlhalošo tše di hlabošago le tše di sa kgaotšego godimo ga go se swanelege ga go gokarela ga gagwe monna-gotee le yena ka mohuta woo wa setaele sa lenyalo, ke ile ka atlega go ntšha go ngunanguna; gomme mo nakong ye, o ile a gogela morago letsogo la gagwe, a itšhikinya gohle bjalo ka mpša ya Newfoundland go tšwa meetseng, gomme a dula godimo mpeteng, a thatafetše bjalo ka lepara la pike, a ntebelela, gomme a silila mahlo a gagwe bjalo ka ge eka ga se a gopola ka botlalo kamoo ke tlilego go ba moo, gaešita le ge temogo e fifetšego ya go tseba se sengwe ka nna e be e bonagala e mo hlaba ganyenyane-ganyenyane. Go sa le bjalo, ke ile ka robala ka setu ke mo tsepeletše, ke se na dipelaelo tše dikgolo bjale, gomme ka inama go lebelela ka mo go tshesane sebopiwa seo se ratago go tseba gakaakaa. Ge, mafelelong, monagano wa gagwe o be o bonagala o dirilwe go kgoma semelo sa modirišani wa gagwe wa malaong, gomme a ba, bjalo ka ge eka, a boelana le therešo; o ile a tlolela ka ntle fase, gomme ka maswao a itšego le medumo a mpha go kwešiša gore, ge e ba go be go nkgahla, o be a tla apara pele gomme ka morago a ntlogela gore ke apare ka morago ga moo, a tlogela folete ka moka go nna. Nagana nna, Queequeg, ka fase ga maemo, ye ke overture ye e hlabologilego kudu; eupša, therešo ke gore, diphoofolo tše tša sehlogo di na le maikutlo a tlhago a go ba bose, bolela seo o tlago go se tla; ke mo go makatšago kamoo ge e le gabotse ba nago le maitshwaro ka gona. Ke lefa theto ye e kgethegilego go Queequeg, ka gobane o ile a ntshwara ka mokgwa o mogolo kudu le go naganela, mola ke be ke le molato wa go hloka mekgwa mo gogolo; a mo tsepeletše a le malaong, gomme a lebelela ditšhišinyego tša gagwe ka moka tša ntlwana ya boithomelo; bakeng sa nako yeo go rata go tseba ga-ka go be go feta tswadišo ya-ka. Le ge go le bjalo, monna wa go swana le Queequeg ga o mmone letšatši le letšatši, yena le ditsela tša gagwe di be di swanetše go ba tše di sa tlwaelegago go di lebelela.

O ile a thoma go apara ka godimo ka go apara katiba ya gagwe ya beaver, e lego e telele kudu, ka thoko, ke moka—a sa dutše a tlošitšwe marokgo a gagwe—a tsoma dibutšwana tša gagwe. Seo ka fase ga magodimo a se diretšego sona, ga ke kgone go se bolela, eupša motšhene wa gagwe wo o latelago e be e le go ipiša—dibutšwana ka seatleng, gomme a apere katiba—ka fase ga mpete; ge, go tšwa go go hemela godimo ka bošoro mo go fapa-fapanego le go gateletšega, ke be ke phetha ka gore o be a šoma ka thata a itlhama; le ge e le gore ga go na molao wa tshwanelo wo ke kilego ka o kwa, monna le ge e le ofe o swanetše go ba wa sephiri ge a apara dibutšwana tša gagwe. Eupša Queequeg, na o a bona, e be e le sebopiwa seo se lego mogatong wa phetogo—e sego seboko goba serurubele. O be a fo ba a hlabologile ka mo go lekanego gore a ka bontšha go makatša ga gagwe ka mekgwa e makatšago kudu yeo e kgonegago. Thuto ya gagwe e be e se ya hlwa e phethwa. E be e le morutwana wa pele ga dithuto tša gagwe. Ge nkabe a se a hlabologa ka tekanyo e nyenyane, mohlomongwe kudu nkabe a se a itshwenya ka dibutšwana le gatee; eupša ka morago, ge nkabe a se a sa le sehlogo, o be a ka se tsoge a lorile ka go tsena ka fase ga mpete gore a di apare. Mafelelong, o ile a tšwelela ka katiba ya gagwe e kgopame kudu e bile e pšhatlagantšwe godimo ga mahlo a gagwe, gomme a thoma go lla le go kgopša ka phapošing, bjalo ka ge eka, ga se a tlwaela kudu dibutšwana, para ya gagwe ya letlalo la kgomo le le kolobilego, le le tšhogilego—mohlomongwe ga se a dirwa taelo le ge e le efe—go e na le moo e ile ya mo pitla le go mo tlaiša ge a thoma go tloga mesong e tonyago e šoro.

Ka go bona, bjale, gore go be go se na dikgaretene go ya lefasetereng, le gore mmila e be e le ye tshesane kudu, ntlo ye e lebanego e be e laela pono ye e kwagalago ka phapošing, gomme e lebelela kudu le go feta sebopego se se sa hlomphegego seo Queequeg a se dirilego, a tšhabela ka se sengwe se sennyane ge e se katiba ya gagwe le dibutšwana a apere; Ke ile ka mo kgopela gabotse ka mo nka kgonago, gore a akgofiše ntlwana ya gagwe ya boithomelo ka tsela e itšego, gomme kudu-kudu gore a tsene ka gare ga dipantaloon tša gagwe ka pela ka mo go kgonegago. O ile a dumelelana le seo, gomme ka morago a tšwela pele go hlapa. Ka nako yeo mesong Mokriste ofe goba ofe o be a tla hlapa sefahlego; eupša Queequeg, ka go makala ga-ka, o ile a kgotsofala ka go lekanyetša go hlapa ga gagwe sehubeng, matsogong le diatleng. Ke moka o ile a apara kobo ya gagwe ya letheka, gomme a tšea seripa sa sesepa se thata tafoleng ya bogareng ya go hlatswa, a se ina ka meetseng gomme a thoma go foka sefahlego sa gagwe. Ke be ke lebeletše go bona moo a bolokilego legare la gagwe, ge lo and behold, a tšea harpoon go tšwa sekhutlong sa mpete, a thelela ka ntle ga setoko se setelele sa kota, a lokolla hlogo, a e whets gannyane godimo ga boot ya gagwe, gomme a gata go fihla go bit ya seipone kgahlanong le lebota, o thoma go gohla ka matla, goba go e na le moo go harpooning marama a gagwe. Ke nagana gore nna, Queequeg, se ke go šomiša di-cutlery tše kaone tša Rogers ka tefetšo. Ka morago ga moo ke ile ka ipotšiša ka mo gonyenyane mošomong wo ge ke be ke tseba gore ke tšhipi efe e botse yeo hlogo ya harpoon e dirilwego, le kamoo mapheko a matelele a otlologilego a bolokwago a le bogale ka mo go feteletšego ka gona ka mehla.

Kapejana ntlwana ya gagwe ka moka e ile ya fihlelelwa, gomme ka boikgantšho a gwantela ka ntle ga phapoši, a phuthetšwe ka baki ya gagwe ye kgolo ya tšhwene ya mokgweetsi wa sefofane, gomme a bapala harpoon ya gagwe bjalo ka lepara la marshal.





KGAOLO YA 5. Dijo tša mesong.

Ke ile ka latela mohlala ka pela, gomme ge ke theogela ka phapošing ya bareng ka accosted mong wa ntlo yo a nyenyago ka mo go kgahlišago kudu. Ke be ke sa tšeele godimo lehufa go yena, le ge a be a dutše a skylarking le nna e sego go se nene tabeng ya modirišani wa ka wa malaong.

Le ge go le bjalo, go sega gabotse ke selo se sebotse se maatla, gomme go e na le moo ke selo se sebotse se se hlaelelago kudu; go feta moo go kwela bohloko. Ka fao, ge monna ofe goba ofe o tee, ka motho wa gagwe wa maleba, a kgona go reka dilo tša metlae ye mebotse go mang le mang, a a se ke a boela morago, eupša a a dumelele ka lethabo go diriša tšhelete le go dirišwa ka tsela yeo. Gomme monna yo a nago le selo se sengwe le se sengwe seo se segago ka mo go tletšego ka yena, kgonthiša gore go na le tše ntši ka go monna yoo go feta ka fao mohlomongwe o naganago ka gona.

Kamore ya bareng bjale e be e tletše ka ba-boarder bao ba bego ba dutše ba theoga bošegong bjo bo fetilego, le bao ke bego ke sešo ka ba lebelela gabotse. Mo e nyakilego go ba ka moka e be e le banna ba maruarua; balekane ba bagolo, le balekane ba bobedi, le balekane ba boraro, le babetli ba lewatle, le bašomi ba lewatle, le bašomi ba ditšhipi ba lewatle, le bašomi ba dithapo, le baleti ba dikepe; khamphani e sootho le brawny, e nang le ditedu tsa bosky; sete yeo e sa kgaolwago, ya go ba le mašoba, ka moka ba apere dijase tša ditšhwene bakeng sa digown tša mesong.

O be o ka kgona go bona gabotse gore e nngwe le e nngwe e be e le lebopong nako e kaakang. Lerameng le le phetšego gabotse la thaka ye le swana le pere ye e tositilwego ke letšatši ka mmala, gomme le be le tla bonagala le nkga mo e nyakilego go ba bjalo ka musky; a ka se be a ile a kotama matšatši a mararo go tšwa leetong la gagwe la India. Monna yola yo a lego kgauswi le yena o bonala a le bofefo ka meriti e sego kae; o ka re go kgoma kota ya satin go ka go yena. Ka letlalo la boraro sa lingers e tropic tawn, empa hanyenyane bleached withal; ka ntle le pelaelo o diegile dibeke ka moka lebopong. Eupša ke mang yo a bego a ka bontšha lerameng le le swanago le Queequeg? yeo, yeo e thibetšwego ka di- tint tše di fapa-fapanego, e bego e bonagala e le tshekamelo ya ka bodikela ya Andes, go bontšha ka lenaneo le tee, boemo bja leratadima bjo bo fapanego, lefelo ka lefelo.

“Grub, ho!” bjale gwa lla mong wa ntlo, a lahlela lebati, gomme ka gare ra ya go ja dijo tša mesong.

Ba re banna bao ba bonego lefase, ka go rialo ba ba bao ba iketlilego kudu ka mokgwa, ba tloga ba itšeela godimo ka khamphaning. Lega go le bjalo, ga se ka mehla: Ledyard, mosepedi yo mogolo wa New England, le Mungo Park, wa Scotch; go banna ka moka, ba be ba e-na le kgonthišetšo e nyenyane kudu ka phapošing ya go jela. Eupša mohlomongwe go fo tshela Siberia ka selei seo se gogwago ke dimpša bjalo ka ge Ledyard a dirile, goba go tšea leeto le letelele la go sepela o nnoši ka mpa e se nago selo, pelong ya batho ba baso ya Afrika, yeo e bego e le palomoka ya ditiragatšo tša Mungo yo a diilago—mohuta wo wa go sepela, . Ke re, e ka no se be mokgwa o mokaone kudu wa go fihlelela go pholisha ga leago mo go phagamego. Go sa dutše go le bjalo, karolong e kgolo, selo sa mohuta woo se swanetše go ba gona kae le kae.

Ditlhathollo tše feela mo di hlolwa ke maemo a gore ka morago ga ge ka moka re dutše tafoleng, gomme ke be ke itokišeletša go kwa dikanegelo tše dingwe tše botse ka ga go tsoma maruarua; go sa makatše ga-ka, mo e nyakilego go ba monna yo mongwe le yo mongwe o ile a homola ka mo go tseneletšego. Gomme e sego seo fela, eupša ba be ba bonala ba hlabja ke dihlong. Ee, mo go be go e-na le sehlopha sa dimpša tša lewatle, tšeo bontši bja tšona ka ntle le go hlabja ke dihlong le ganyenyane di bego di nametše maruarua a magolo mawatleng a magolo—e lego batho bao ba sa ba tsebego ka mo go feletšego go tšona—gomme di di duele di hwile ka ntle le go tšhoša mahlo; gomme le ge go le bjalo, mo ba ile ba dula tafoleng ya dijo tša mesong ya leago—ka moka e le ba pitšo e swanago, ka moka e le ba ditatso tše di swanago—ba lebelela go dikologa ka dihlong go yo mongwe le yo mongwe bjalo ka ge eka ga se ba ka ba ka ba ka ntle ga pono ya lešaka le lengwe la dinku gare ga Dithaba tše Tala. Pono ya go rata go tseba; dibere tše tša dihlong, banna ba ba bahlabani ba dihlong ba maruarua!

Eupša ge e le Queequeg—ka baka la’ng, Queequeg o ile a dula fao gare ga bona—hlogong ya tafola, le yona, go ile gwa direga ka kotsi kudu; e fodilego bjalo ka icicle. Go ba le bonnete nka se bolele mo gontši ka tswadišo ya gagwe. Morati wa gagwe yo mogolo o be a ka se lokafatše ka borutho go tliša ga gagwe harpoon ya gagwe ka dijong tša mesong le yena, le go e šomiša moo ntle le moletlo; a fihlelela godimo ga tafola ka yona, go ya kotsing ye e lego kgauswi ya dihlogo tše ntši, gomme a swaragana le dinama tša kgomo go leba go yena. Eupša seo ka nnete se dirilwe ka go tonya kudu ke yena, gomme yo mongwe le yo mongwe o a tseba gore go ya ka tekanyetšo ya batho ba bantši, go dira selo le ge e le sefe ka go tonya ke go se dira ka boleta.

Re ka se bolele ka dilo ka moka tše di kgethegilego tša Queequeg mo; kamoo a ilego a phema kofi le di-roll tše di fišago, gomme a diriša tlhokomelo ya gagwe yeo e sa aroganywago go di- beefsteak, tšeo di dirilwego ka sewelo. Go lekane, gore ge dijo tša mesong di fedile o ile a gogela morago bjalo ka ba bangwe ka phapošing ya setšhaba, a boneša phaephe ya gagwe ya tomahawk, gomme o be a dutše fao ka setu a šila le go kgoga a apere katiba ya gagwe yeo e sa aroganego, ge ke sallied ntle go sepelasepela.





KGAOLO YA 6. Setarata.

Ge nkabe ke ile ka makatšwa ke mathomong go bona motho yo a makatšago gakaakaa go swana le Queequeg yo a dikološwago gare ga setšhaba se se nago le maitshwaro sa toropo ye e hlabologilego, go makala moo go ile gwa tloga kapejana ge ke be ke tšea leeto la-ka la mathomo la mosegare ditarateng tša New Bedford.

Ditseleng tše di fetago kgaufsi le boema-kepe, boema-kepe le ge e le bofe bjo bo bonagalago gantši bo tla ithapela go lebelela dilo tše di sa hlalosegego tšeo di bonagalago e le tše di makatšago kudu go tšwa dikarolong tša dinaga dišele. Gaešita le ditarateng tša Broadway le Chestnut, basesiši ba dikepe ba Mediterranean ka dinako tše dingwe ba tla kgorometša mafumahadi ao a tšhogilego. Regent Street ga se yeo e sa tsebjego ke ma-Lascar le ma-Malay; gomme kua Bombay, kua Apollo Green, ma-Yanke a phelago gantši a tšhošitše ma-native. Eupša New Bedford e fenya Water Street le Wapping ka moka. Mafelong a a go tšhoša ao go boletšwego ka ona la mafelelo o bona feela basesiši ba dikepe; eupša kua New Bedford, batho ba kgonthe bao ba jago batho ba eme ba boledišana dikhutlong tša ditarateng; sehlogo ka go lebanya; bao ba bantši ba bona ba sa dutšego ba tšwetša pele marapo a bona nama e sa kgethwa. E dira gore motho yo a sa mo tsebego a lebelele ka mahlo a go tšhoša.

Eupša, ntle le Feegeeans, Tongatobooarrs, Erromanggoans, Pannangians, le Brighggians, le, ntle le dipeelano tša hlaga tša whaling-craft yeo e sa hlokomelwego reel ka ditarateng, o tla bona dipono tše dingwe tše di sa dutšego di nyaka go tseba kudu, ka nnete e le tše di segišago kudu. Go fihla beke le beke toropong ye masome a banna ba batala ba Vermont le New Hampshire, ka moka ba nyorilwe go hwetša poelo le letago ka go rea dihlapi. Bontši ke tše nnyane, tša diforeime tše di tiilego; ba bangwe bao ba remilego dithokgwa, gomme bjale ba nyaka go lahlela selepe le go phamola lerumo la maruarua. “Dikgopo le ditšhogo tšeo di lego ka gare ga leruarua, .
Arched godimo ga ka lefsifsi le le nyamišago, .
Ge maphoto ka moka a Modimo ao a bonešitšwego ke letšatši a be a kgokologa, .
Gomme o nkgogele ke tsenelela go fihla tshenyegong.

“Ke bone maw a go bula a dihele, .
Ka mahloko le manyami a sa felego moo;
Seo go se nago le o tee ge e se bao ba ikwago a ka se bolelago— .
Oo, ke be ke nwelela go itlhoboga.

“Ka tlalelo ya bathobaso, ke ile ka bitša Modimo wa ka, .
Ge ke be ke sa kgone go mo dumela wa ka, .
O ile a inamiša tsebe ya gagwe go dingongorego tša-ka— .
Ga go sa na leruarua leo le ilego la tswalela.

“Ka lebelo o ile a fofa go imolla, .
Bjalo ka ka dolphin e phadimago yeo e rwelego;
E šiišago, eupša e phadima, bjalo ka ge legadima le phadima
Sefahlego sa Mophološi wa ka Modimo.

“Pina ya ka go ya go ile e tla rekota
Iri yeo e šiišago, yeo ya lethabo;
Letago ke le neela Modimo wa ka, .
Kgaugelo ya gagwe ka moka le maatla.”

Mo e nyakilego go ba ka moka ba ile ba kopanela go opeleng koša ye, yeo e bego e rurugile godimo ka godimo ga go bokolla ga ledimo. Go ile gwa latela go khutša ka nako e kopana; moreri o ile a phetla matlakala a Beibele ganyenyane-ganyenyane, gomme mafelelong, a phutha seatla sa gagwe fase letlakaleng le le swanetšego, a re: “Batho bao ba rategago bao ba sepelago le sekepe, kgomaretšang temana ya mafelelo ya kgaolo ya pele ya Jona—‘Modimo o be a lokišeditše hlapi e kgolo go metša Jona.’”

“Shipmates, this book, containing only four chapters—four yarns—is one of the smallest strands in the mighty cable of the Scriptures. Yet what depths of the soul does Jonah's deep sealine sound! what a pregnant lesson to us is this prophet! What a noble thing is that canticle in the fish's belly! How billow-like and boisterously grand! We feel the floods surging over us; we sound with him to the kelpy bottom of the waters; sea-weed and all the slime of the sea is about us! But what is this lesson that the book of Jonah teaches? Shipmates, it is a two-stranded lesson; a lesson to us all as sinful men, and a lesson to me as a pilot of the living God. As sinful men, it is a lesson to us all, because it is a story of the sin, hard-heartedness, suddenly awakened fears, the swift punishment, repentance, prayers, and finally the deliverance and joy of Jonah. As with all sinners among men, the sin of this son of Amittai was in his wilful disobedience of the command of God—never mind now what that command was, or how conveyed—which he found a hard command. But all the things that God would have us do are hard for us to do—remember that—and hence, he oftener commands us than endeavors to persuade. And if we obey God, we must disobey ourselves; and it is in this disobeying ourselves, wherein the hardness of obeying God consists.

“With this sin of disobedience in him, Jonah still further flouts at God, by seeking to flee from Him. He thinks that a ship made by men will carry him into countries where God does not reign, but only the Captains of this earth. He skulks about the wharves of Joppa, and seeks a ship that's bound for Tarshish. There lurks, perhaps, a hitherto unheeded meaning here. By all accounts Tarshish could have been no other city than the modern Cadiz. That's the opinion of learned men. And where is Cadiz, shipmates? Cadiz is in Spain; as far by water, from Joppa, as Jonah could possibly have sailed in those ancient days, when the Atlantic was an almost unknown sea. Because Joppa, the modern Jaffa, shipmates, is on the most easterly coast of the Mediterranean, the Syrian; and Tarshish or Cadiz more than two thousand miles to the westward from that, just outside the Straits of Gibraltar. See ye not then, shipmates, that Jonah sought to flee world-wide from God? Miserable man! Oh! most contemptible and worthy of all scorn; with slouched hat and guilty eye, skulking from his God; prowling among the shipping like a vile burglar hastening to cross the seas. So disordered, self-condemning is his look, that had there been policemen in those days, Jonah, on the mere suspicion of something wrong, had been arrested ere he touched a deck. How plainly he's a fugitive! no baggage, not a hat-box, valise, or carpet-bag,—no friends accompany him to the wharf with their adieux. At last, after much dodging search, he finds the Tarshish ship receiving the last items of her cargo; and as he steps on board to see its Captain in the cabin, all the sailors for the moment desist from hoisting in the goods, to mark the stranger's evil eye. Jonah sees this; but in vain he tries to look all ease and confidence; in vain essays his wretched smile. Strong intuitions of the man assure the mariners he can be no innocent. In their gamesome but still serious way, one whispers to the other—“Jack, he's robbed a widow;” or, “Joe, do you mark him; he's a bigamist;” or, “Harry lad, I guess he's the adulterer that broke jail in old Gomorrah, or belike, one of the missing murderers from Sodom.” Another runs to read the bill that's stuck against the spile upon the wharf to which the ship is moored, offering five hundred gold coins for the apprehension of a parricide, and containing a description of his person. He reads, and looks from Jonah to the bill; while all his sympathetic shipmates now crowd round Jonah, prepared to lay their hands upon him. Frighted Jonah trembles, and summoning all his boldness to his face, only looks so much the more a coward. He will not confess himself suspected; but that itself is strong suspicion. So he makes the best of it; and when the sailors find him not to be the man that is advertised, they let him pass, and he descends into the cabin.

“'Who's there?' cries the Captain at his busy desk, hurriedly making out his papers for the Customs—'Who's there?' Oh! how that harmless question mangles Jonah! For the instant he almost turns to flee again. But he rallies. 'I seek a passage in this ship to Tarshish; how soon sail ye, sir?' Thus far the busy Captain had not looked up to Jonah, though the man now stands before him; but no sooner does he hear that hollow voice, than he darts a scrutinizing glance. 'We sail with the next coming tide,' at last he slowly answered, still intently eyeing him. 'No sooner, sir?'—'Soon enough for any honest man that goes a passenger.' Ha! Jonah, that's another stab. But he swiftly calls away the Captain from that scent. 'I'll sail with ye,'—he says,—'the passage money how much is that?—I'll pay now.' For it is particularly written, shipmates, as if it were a thing not to be overlooked in this history, 'that he paid the fare thereof' ere the craft did sail. And taken with the context, this is full of meaning.

“Now Jonah's Captain, shipmates, was one whose discernment detects crime in any, but whose cupidity exposes it only in the penniless. In this world, shipmates, sin that pays its way can travel freely, and without a passport; whereas Virtue, if a pauper, is stopped at all frontiers. So Jonah's Captain prepares to test the length of Jonah's purse, ere he judge him openly. He charges him thrice the usual sum; and it's assented to. Then the Captain knows that Jonah is a fugitive; but at the same time resolves to help a flight that paves its rear with gold. Yet when Jonah fairly takes out his purse, prudent suspicions still molest the Captain. He rings every coin to find a counterfeit. Not a forger, any way, he mutters; and Jonah is put down for his passage. 'Point out my state-room, Sir,' says Jonah now, 'I'm travel-weary; I need sleep.' 'Thou lookest like it,' says the Captain, 'there's thy room.' Jonah enters, and would lock the door, but the lock contains no key. Hearing him foolishly fumbling there, the Captain laughs lowly to himself, and mutters something about the doors of convicts' cells being never allowed to be locked within. All dressed and dusty as he is, Jonah throws himself into his berth, and finds the little state-room ceiling almost resting on his forehead. The air is close, and Jonah gasps. Then, in that contracted hole, sunk, too, beneath the ship's water-line, Jonah feels the heralding presentiment of that stifling hour, when the whale shall hold him in the smallest of his bowels' wards.

“Screwed at its axis against the side, a swinging lamp slightly oscillates in Jonah's room; and the ship, heeling over towards the wharf with the weight of the last bales received, the lamp, flame and all, though in slight motion, still maintains a permanent obliquity with reference to the room; though, in truth, infallibly straight itself, it but made obvious the false, lying levels among which it hung. The lamp alarms and frightens Jonah; as lying in his berth his tormented eyes roll round the place, and this thus far successful fugitive finds no refuge for his restless glance. But that contradiction in the lamp more and more appals him. The floor, the ceiling, and the side, are all awry. 'Oh! so my conscience hangs in me!' he groans, 'straight upwards, so it burns; but the chambers of my soul are all in crookedness!'

“Like one who after a night of drunken revelry hies to his bed, still reeling, but with conscience yet pricking him, as the plungings of the Roman race-horse but so much the more strike his steel tags into him; as one who in that miserable plight still turns and turns in giddy anguish, praying God for annihilation until the fit be passed; and at last amid the whirl of woe he feels, a deep stupor steals over him, as over the man who bleeds to death, for conscience is the wound, and there's naught to staunch it; so, after sore wrestlings in his berth, Jonah's prodigy of ponderous misery drags him drowning down to sleep.

“And now the time of tide has come; the ship casts off her cables; and from the deserted wharf the uncheered ship for Tarshish, all careening, glides to sea. That ship, my friends, was the first of recorded smugglers! the contraband was Jonah. But the sea rebels; he will not bear the wicked burden. A dreadful storm comes on, the ship is like to break. But now when the boatswain calls all hands to lighten her; when boxes, bales, and jars are clattering overboard; when the wind is shrieking, and the men are yelling, and every plank thunders with trampling feet right over Jonah's head; in all this raging tumult, Jonah sleeps his hideous sleep. He sees no black sky and raging sea, feels not the reeling timbers, and little hears he or heeds he the far rush of the mighty whale, which even now with open mouth is cleaving the seas after him. Aye, shipmates, Jonah was gone down into the sides of the ship—a berth in the cabin as I have taken it, and was fast asleep. But the frightened master comes to him, and shrieks in his dead ear, 'What meanest thou, O, sleeper! arise!' Startled from his lethargy by that direful cry, Jonah staggers to his feet, and stumbling to the deck, grasps a shroud, to look out upon the sea. But at that moment he is sprung upon by a panther billow leaping over the bulwarks. Wave after wave thus leaps into the ship, and finding no speedy vent runs roaring fore and aft, till the mariners come nigh to drowning while yet afloat. And ever, as the white moon shows her affrighted face from the steep gullies in the blackness overhead, aghast Jonah sees the rearing bowsprit pointing high upward, but soon beat downward again towards the tormented deep.

“Terrors upon terrors run shouting through his soul. In all his cringing attitudes, the God-fugitive is now too plainly known. The sailors mark him; more and more certain grow their suspicions of him, and at last, fully to test the truth, by referring the whole matter to high Heaven, they fall to casting lots, to see for whose cause this great tempest was upon them. The lot is Jonah's; that discovered, then how furiously they mob him with their questions. 'What is thine occupation? Whence comest thou? Thy country? What people? But mark now, my shipmates, the behavior of poor Jonah. The eager mariners but ask him who he is, and where from; whereas, they not only receive an answer to those questions, but likewise another answer to a question not put by them, but the unsolicited answer is forced from Jonah by the hard hand of God that is upon him.

“'I am a Hebrew,' he cries—and then—'I fear the Lord the God of Heaven who hath made the sea and the dry land!' Fear him, O Jonah? Aye, well mightest thou fear the Lord God then! Straightway, he now goes on to make a full confession; whereupon the mariners became more and more appalled, but still are pitiful. For when Jonah, not yet supplicating God for mercy, since he but too well knew the darkness of his deserts,—when wretched Jonah cries out to them to take him and cast him forth into the sea, for he knew that for his sake this great tempest was upon them; they mercifully turn from him, and seek by other means to save the ship. But all in vain; the indignant gale howls louder; then, with one hand raised invokingly to God, with the other they not unreluctantly lay hold of Jonah.

“And now behold Jonah taken up as an anchor and dropped into the sea; when instantly an oily calmness floats out from the east, and the sea is still, as Jonah carries down the gale with him, leaving smooth water behind. He goes down in the whirling heart of such a masterless commotion that he scarce heeds the moment when he drops seething into the yawning jaws awaiting him; and the whale shoots-to all his ivory teeth, like so many white bolts, upon his prison. Then Jonah prayed unto the Lord out of the fish's belly. But observe his prayer, and learn a weighty lesson. For sinful as he is, Jonah does not weep and wail for direct deliverance. He feels that his dreadful punishment is just. He leaves all his deliverance to God, contenting himself with this, that spite of all his pains and pangs, he will still look towards His holy temple. And here, shipmates, is true and faithful repentance; not clamorous for pardon, but grateful for punishment. And how pleasing to God was this conduct in Jonah, is shown in the eventual deliverance of him from the sea and the whale. Shipmates, I do not place Jonah before you to be copied for his sin but I do place him before you as a model for repentance. Sin not; but if you do, take heed to repent of it like Jonah.”

While he was speaking these words, the howling of the shrieking, slanting storm without seemed to add new power to the preacher, who, when describing Jonah's sea-storm, seemed tossed by a storm himself. His deep chest heaved as with a ground-swell; his tossed arms seemed the warring elements at work; and the thunders that rolled away from off his swarthy brow, and the light leaping from his eye, made all his simple hearers look on him with a quick fear that was strange to them.

There now came a lull in his look, as he silently turned over the leaves of the Book once more; and, at last, standing motionless, with closed eyes, for the moment, seemed communing with God and himself.

But again he leaned over towards the people, and bowing his head lowly, with an aspect of the deepest yet manliest humility, he spake these words:

“Shipmates, God has laid but one hand upon you; both his hands press upon me. I have read ye by what murky light may be mine the lesson that Jonah teaches to all sinners; and therefore to ye, and still more to me, for I am a greater sinner than ye. And now how gladly would I come down from this mast-head and sit on the hatches there where you sit, and listen as you listen, while some one of you reads me that other and more awful lesson which Jonah teaches to me , as a pilot of the living God. How being an anointed pilot-prophet, or speaker of true things, and bidden by the Lord to sound those unwelcome truths in the ears of a wicked Nineveh, Jonah, appalled at the hostility he should raise, fled from his mission, and sought to escape his duty and his God by taking ship at Joppa. But God is everywhere; Tarshish he never reached. As we have seen, God came upon him in the whale, and swallowed him down to living gulfs of doom, and with swift slantings tore him along 'into the midst of the seas,' where the eddying depths sucked him ten thousand fathoms down, and 'the weeds were wrapped about his head,' and all the watery world of woe bowled over him. Yet even then beyond the reach of any plummet—'out of the belly of hell'—when the whale grounded upon the ocean's utmost bones, even then, God heard the engulphed, repenting prophet when he cried. Then God spake unto the fish; and from the shuddering cold and blackness of the sea, the whale came breeching up towards the warm and pleasant sun, and all the delights of air and earth; and 'vomited out Jonah upon the dry land;' when the word of the Lord came a second time; and Jonah, bruised and beaten—his ears, like two sea-shells, still multitudinously murmuring of the ocean—Jonah did the Almighty's bidding. And what was that, shipmates? To preach the Truth to the face of Falsehood! That was it!

“This, shipmates, this is that other lesson; and woe to that pilot of the living God who slights it. Woe to him whom this world charms from Gospel duty! Woe to him who seeks to pour oil upon the waters when God has brewed them into a gale! Woe to him who seeks to please rather than to appal! Woe to him whose good name is more to him than goodness! Woe to him who, in this world, courts not dishonor! Woe to him who would not be true, even though to be false were salvation! Yea, woe to him who, as the great Pilot Paul has it, while preaching to others is himself a castaway!”

He dropped and fell away from himself for a moment; then lifting his face to them again, showed a deep joy in his eyes, as he cried out with a heavenly enthusiasm,—“But oh! shipmates! on the starboard hand of every woe, there is a sure delight; and higher the top of that delight, than the bottom of the woe is deep. Is not the main-truck higher than the kelson is low? Delight is to him—a far, far upward, and inward delight—who against the proud gods and commodores of this earth, ever stands forth his own inexorable self. Delight is to him whose strong arms yet support him, when the ship of this base treacherous world has gone down beneath him. Delight is to him, who gives no quarter in the truth, and kills, burns, and destroys all sin though he pluck it out from under the robes of Senators and Judges. Delight,—top-gallant delight is to him, who acknowledges no law or lord, but the Lord his God, and is only a patriot to heaven. Delight is to him, whom all the waves of the billows of the seas of the boisterous mob can never shake from this sure Keel of the Ages. And eternal delight and deliciousness will be his, who coming to lay him down, can say with his final breath—O Father!—chiefly known to me by Thy rod—mortal or immortal, here I die. I have striven to be Thine, more than to be this world's, or mine own. Yet this is nothing: I leave eternity to Thee; for what is man that he should live out the lifetime of his God?”

He said no more, but slowly waving a benediction, covered his face with his hands, and so remained kneeling, till all the people had departed, and he was left alone in the place.
