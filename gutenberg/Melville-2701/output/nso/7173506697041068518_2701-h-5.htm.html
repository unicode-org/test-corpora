Mafelelong, go ya mosegare wa sekgalela, ka go rakwa ga mafelelo ga bašomi ba sekepe ba go swara di- rigger, le ka morago ga ge Pequod e gogetšwe ka ntle go tšwa boema-kepeng, le ka morago ga ge Charity yeo e bego e dula e nagana e fologile ka seketswana sa maruarua, ka mpho ya gagwe ya mafelelo—bošego -cap bakeng sa Stubb, molekane wa bobedi, mogolo’agwe, le Beibele ya go se šomišwe bakeng sa molaki—ka morago ga tše ka moka, Bakapotene ba babedi, Peleg le Bildad, ba ile ba tšwa ka khabineng, gomme ba retologela go molekane yo mogolo, Peleg o boletše : 1 .

Eupša go be go se na sebaka se segolo sa go nagana ka taba ye, ka gobane Mokapotene Peleg bjale o be a phela ka moka. Go be go bonagala a dira bontši bja go bolela le go laela, e sego Bildada.

O ile a goeletša ka gore: “Ka morago ga mo, lena barwa ba bahlola-mpherefere,” ge basesiši ba be ba dutše ba diegile sekepeng se segolo. “Mong. Starbuck, o di otlele ka morago.”

“Otla tente moo!”—e bile taelo e latelago. Bjalo ka ge ke ile ka šišinya pele, marquee ye ya marapo a maruarua ga se ya ka ya lahlelwa ka ntle le boema-kepeng; gomme ka sekepeng sa Pequod, ka mengwaga ye masometharo, taelo ya go otla tente e be e tsebja gabotse bjalo ka selo se se latelago go emiša tshetledi.

“Monna wa capstan! Madi le diaduma!—tlola!”—e bile taelo e latelago, gomme sehlopha sa basepediši se ile sa tlolela bakeng sa ditlhale tša diatla.

Bjale ka go hwetša boima bja mmele, seteišene seo ka kakaretšo se dulwago ke mokgweetsi wa sekepe ke karolo ya ka pele ya sekepe. Gomme mo Bildad, yo, le Peleg, go ka tsebja, go tlaleletša go bahlankedi ba gagwe ba bangwe, e bego e le yo mongwe wa bakgweetsi ba difofane bao ba nago le mangwalo a tumelelo ya boemakepe—a go belaelwa gore o ile a itira mokgweetsi wa sefofane e le gore a boloke tefo ya mokgweetsi wa sefofane ya Nantucket go dikepe ka moka tšeo a bego a tshwenyegile ka tšona, ka gobane ga se a ke a fofiša sekepe se sengwe le se sengwe sa diatla—Bildad, ke re, bjale a ka bonwa a swaregile ka mafolofolo go lebelela godimo ga bora bakeng sa tshetledi yeo e batamelago, gomme ka dikgaotšo a opela seo se bego se bonagala e le lepara le le nyamišago la psalmode, go thabiša diatla go windlass, yo a ilego a rora pele mohuta wo mongwe wa khorase ka banenyana ba Booble Alley, ka thato ye botse ye e tšwago pelong. Lega go le bjalo, e sego matšatši a mararo pele ga moo, Bildad o be a ba boditše gore ga go na dikoša tše di gobogilego tšeo di bego di tla dumelelwa ka sekepeng sa Pequod, kudu-kudu tabeng ya go ba ka tlase ga boima bja mmele; gomme Charity, kgaetšedi ya gagwe, o be a beile kopi ye nnyane ya kgetho ya Watts ka lefelong la go emiša dikepe la mosesiši yo mongwe le yo mongwe.

Go sa le bjalo, ge a be a okametše karolo e nngwe ya sekepe, Mokapotene Peleg o ile a gagola gomme a ikana ka morago ka mokgwa o boifišago kudu. Ke ile ka nyakile ke nagana gore o tla nwelela sekepe pele tshetledi e ka tsoga; ka go se rate ke ile ka ema gannyane godimo ga handspike ya ka, gomme ka botša Queequeg gore a dire se se swanago, ke nagana ka dikotsi tšeo bobedi bja rena re ilego ra kitima, ka go thoma leeto le diabolo yo bjalo bakeng sa mokgweetsi wa sefofane. Ke be ke ikhomotša, le ge go le bjalo, ka kgopolo ya gore go Bildad wa borapedi go ka hwetšwa phološo ye nngwe, go sa šetšwe go ba ga gagwe ga makgolo a šupago le masomešupa-šupa a go se loke; ge ke ekwa go thula mo go bogale ga tšhoganetšo ka morago ga ka, gomme ke retologa, ke ile ka tšhošwa ke ponagalo ya Mokapotene Peleg ka tiro ya go gogela morago leoto la gagwe go tšwa tikologong ya ka ya kgauswi. Yeo e be e le go raga ga-ka ga mathomo.

“Na ke tsela yeo ba heave ka yona tirelong ya kgwebo?” a rora. “Selemo, wena hlogo ya nku; selemo, gomme o roba mokokotlo wa gago! Ke ka baka la’ng le sa hlome, ke re, ka moka ga lena—selemo! Quohog ya! selemo, wena chap ka maledu a mahwibidu; selemo moo, Scotch-cap; selemo, wena marokgo a matala. Selemo, ke re, lena ka moka, gomme le hlome mahlo a lena!” Gomme ge a bolela bjalo, o ile a sepela go bapa le windlass, mo le mo a šomiša leoto la gagwe ka bolokologi kudu, mola Bildad yo a sa tshwenyegego a be a dula a eta pele ka psalmode ya gagwe. Nagana nna, Mokapotene Peleg o swanetše go ba ke be ke enwa se sengwe lehono.

Mafelelong tshetledi e be e le godimo, diseila di be di beilwe gomme ra tloga re thelela. E be e le Keresemose ye kopana, ye e tonyago; gomme ge letšatši le lekopana la ka leboa le kopana le bošego, re ile ra ikhwetša re nyakile re nabile lewatleng la marega, leo go gaša ga lona mo go tonyago go bego go re tsenya ka gare ga leqhwa, bjalo ka ka ditlhamo tše di phadimago. Methaladi e metelele ya meno godimo ga dibo e be e phadima seetšeng sa ngwedi; gomme go swana le manaka a mašweu a manaka a tlou a tlou e nngwe e kgolo, di- icicle tše dikgolo tše di kobegilego di be di ithekgile ka bora.

Lank Bildad, bjalo ka mokgweetsi wa sefofane, o ile a etelela pele sešupanako sa mathomo, gomme ever and anon, ge sekepe sa kgale sa go sepela ka diatla se itahlela ka gare-gare mawatleng a matala, gomme sa romela tšhwaane ye e thothomelago gohle godimo ga gagwe, gomme diphefo di ile tša bokolla, gomme thapo ya lla, dinoutse tša gagwe tše di sa fetogego di be di le bjalo kwele,-

“Mašemo a bose ka mošola wa lefula le le rurugilego, .
Ema o apere ka botala bjo bo phelago.
Ka gona go Bajuda Kanana wa kgale a ema;
Ge Jorodane a dutše a kgokologa magareng.”

Le ka mohla mantšu ao a bose ga se a ka a kwagala ka bose go nna go feta ka nako yeo. Ba be ba tletše kholofelo le dienywa. Go sa šetšwe bošego bjo bjo bo tonyago bja marega ka Atlantic yeo e nago le lešata, go sa šetšwe maoto a-ka a kolobilego le baki e kolobilego, go be go sa dutše go e-na le, ka nako yeo go ile gwa bonagala go nna, e le mafelo a mantši a kgahlišago a go tšhabela ka lebenkeleng; le meads le glades ka mo go sa felego vernal, mo e lego gore bjang bo thuntšhitšwe godimo ke selemo, bo sa gatakelwa, bo sa pona, bo dula bogareng bja selemo.

Mafelelong re ile ra hwetša sebaka se segolo, moo e lego gore bakgweetsi ba babedi ba difofane ba be ba se sa nyakega. Sekepe se se tiilego sa seila seo se bego se re felegetša se ile sa thoma go sepela ka thoko.

E be e le go rata go tseba e bile e se mo go sa kgahlišego, kamoo Pelege le Bildad ba ilego ba kgongwa ka gona mo nakong ye, kudu-kudu Mokapotene Bildad. Gobane o hloile go tloga, le ge go le bjalo; go hloya kudu go tloga, ka mo go sa felego, sekepe seo se bego se lebile leetong le letelele gakaakaa le le kotsi—ka kua ga Dikapa ka bobedi tšeo di nago le ledimo; sekepe seo go sona go beeleditšwego diranta tše dingwe tše dikete tša ditolara tša gagwe tšeo a di šometšego ka thata; sekepe, seo go sona modirišani wa sekepe wa kgale a bego a sesa e le molaodi wa sekepe; monna yo a nyakilego go ba yo mogolo go swana le yena, gape a thoma go kopana le ditšhošetšo ka moka tša mohlagare wo o se nago kwelobohloko; go hloya go laelana le selo seo ka tsela e nngwe le e nngwe se bego se tletše kgahlego e nngwe le e nngwe go yena,—Bildad wa kgale wa modiidi o ile a diega nako e telele; paced mokatong wa sekepe ka dikgato tše di tshwenyegilego; a kitimela fase ka khabineng go bolela lentšu le lengwe la go laelana moo; gape a tla godimo ga mokatong wa sekepe, gomme a lebelela ka lehlakoreng la phefo; e ile ya lebelela go leba meetseng a sephara le ao a sa felego, ao a lekantšwego feela ke Dikontinente tša Bohlabela tšeo di sa bonwego kgole; a lebelela go leba nageng; a lebelela godimo; a lebelela ka go le letona le ka go le letshadi; o ile a lebelela gohle le go se be gona; gomme mafelelong, a kgokološa thapo ka motšhene godimo ga pini ya yona, ka go šikinyega a swara Peleg yo a tiilego ka seatla, gomme a emišetša lebone godimo, ka motsotswana a ema a lebeletše ka bogale sefahlegong sa gagwe, go fihla bokgoleng bja go bolela gore, “Le ge go le bjalo, mogwera Peleg, nka kgona e eme; ee, nka kgona.”

Ge e le Peleg ka boyena, o ile a e tšea kudu bjalo ka radifilosofi; eupša go filosofi ya gagwe ka moka, go be go na le megokgo yeo e bego e phadima ka leihlong la gagwe, ge lebone le be le batamela kudu. Gomme yena, le yena, ga se a kitima go se nene go tloga khabinete go ya mokatong—bjale lentšu ka fase, gomme bjale lentšu le Starbuck, molekane yo mogolo.

Eupša, mafelelong, o ile a retologela go modirišani wa gagwe, ka mohuta wa mafelelo wa go lebelela ka yena,—“Mokapotene Bildad—etla, modirišani wa sekepe wa kgale, re swanetše go ya. Morago ka jarata-kgolo moo! Sekepe ahoy! Ema ka thoko go batamela ka thoko, bjale! Hlokomela, šedi!—etla, Bildad, mošemane—bolela ya gago ya mafelelo. Mahlatse go lena, Starbuck—mahlatse go lena, Mna Stubb—mahlatse go lena, Mna Flask—laelana le mahlatse go lena ka moka—gomme letšatšing le mengwaga ye meraro ke tla ba le dijo tša mantšiboa tše fišago ke le kgoga ka Nantucket ya kgale . Potlaka o tloge!”

“Modimo a le šegofatše, gomme le be le lona ka gare ga tlhokomelo ya gagwe ye kgethwa, banna,” gwa ngunanguna Bildada wa kgale, mo e nyakilego go ba ka go se kwagale. “Ke holofela gore le tla ba le boemo bjo bobotse bja leratadima bjale, gore Mokapotene Ahaba a ka ba a sepela gare ga lena kgauswinyane—letšatši le le kgahlišago ke sohle seo a se hlokago, gomme le tla ba le tše ntši tša tšona leetong la tropike leo le yago go lona. Hlokomelang go tsomeng, lena balekane. Le se ke la thibela dikepe go sa nyakege, lena bašomi ba dithapo; lepolanka le lebotse le lešweu la mosedere le godišwa ka botlalo ka dipersente tše tharo. ka gare ga ngwaga. Le gona o se ke wa lebala dithapelo tša gago. Mna Starbuck, gopola gore cooper ga e senye dikota tša go se šomišwe. Ao! dinalete tša seila di ka gare ga locker ye tala! O se ke wa e whale kudu a' matšatši a Morena, banna; eupša le gona o se ke wa fetwa ke sebaka se se lokilego, seo ke go gana dimpho tše botse tša Legodimo. Eba le leihlo go tierce ya molasses, Mna Stubb; e be e dutla go se nene, ke ile ka nagana. Ge le kgoma dihlakahlakeng, Mna Flask, hlokomela bootswa. Laelana, laelana! O se ke wa boloka tšhese yeo nako ye telele kudu fase ka gare ga go swara, Mna Starbuck; e tla senya. Hlokomela botoro—disente tše masomepedi ka ponto yeo e bego e le yona, gomme o nagane, ge e ba—”

“Etla, tla, Mokapotene Bildada; tlogela go lla,—go tloga!” gomme ka seo, Pelege a mo kitimiša ka thoko, gomme bobedi bja bona ba wela ka seketswaneng.

Sekepe le sekepe di ile tša arogana; phefo e tonyago le e kolobilego ya bošego e ile ya foka magareng; gull yeo e goeletšago e ile ya fofa godimo ga hlogo; dikhutlo tše pedi tša dikepe di ile tša kgokologa ka lešoka; re ile ra fa dithabo tše tharo tša dipelo tše boima, gomme ka bofofu ra nwelela bjalo ka pheletšo ka gare ga Atlantic ye e lego noši.





KGAOLO YA 23. Lebopo la Lee.

Dikgaolo tše dingwe morago, Bulkington e tee e ile ya bolelwa ka yona, mosesiši yo motelele, wa go ba le naga ye mpsha, yo a kopanego le yena ka New Bedford ka ntlong ya baeti.

Ge bošegong bjoo bjo bo thothomelago bja marega, Pequod e ile ya kgoromeletša bora bja yona bja tefetšo maphotong a tonyago a kotsi, ke mang yo ke swanetšego go mmona a eme ka seatleng sa yona ge e se Bulkington! Ke ile ka lebelela ka poifo ya kwelobohloko le poifo go monna yo, yo bogareng bja marega a sa tšwago go kotama go tšwa leetong le kotsi la nywaga e mene, a bego a ka kgoromeletša kgole gape ka go se iketle gakaakaa bakeng sa nako e nngwe gape ya ledimo. Naga e be e bonagala e fiša maotong a gagwe. Dilo tše di makatšago kudu ke tšeo di sa bolelwego; dikgopotšo tše di tseneletšego ga di tšweletše di- epitaph; kgaolo ye ya disenthimithara tše tshela ke lebitla leo le se nago maswika la Bulkington. E re ke bolele feela gore e ile ya sepela le yena go swana le ka sekepe seo se akgethilwego ke ledimo, seo se otlelago ka manyami go bapa le naga ya leeward. Boemakepe bo be bo tla fain go fa thušo; boema-kepe bo a kwela bohloko; ka boema-kepeng ke polokego, boiketlo, leswika la go fiša, dijo tša mantšiboa, dikobo tše borutho, bagwera, ka moka tšeo di lego botho go mahu a rena. Eupša ledimo leo, boema-kepe, naga, ke kotsi e šoro kudu ya sekepe seo; o swanetše go fofiša kamogelo ka moka ya baeng; go kgoma ga tee ga naga, le ge e be e fo fula keel, go be go tla mo dira gore a thothomele ka gare le ka gare. Ka matla a gagwe ka moka mašaba ka moka a sesa go tloga lebopong; ka go dira bjalo, o lwa ‘kgahlanong le tšona diphefo tšeo fain e bego e tla mo fokela gae; o nyaka go hloka naga ka moka ga lewatle leo le hlabilwego gape; ka baka la botšhabelo go kitimela kotsing ka go hloka botho; mogwera wa gagwe a nnoši lenaba la gagwe le le babago kudu!

Na le a tseba bjale, Bulkington? Go bonagala le bona ka leihlo la kgopolo therešo yeo yeo e sa kgotlelelegego yeo e hwago; gore go nagana ka moka mo go tseneletšego le mo go tseneletšego e fo ba maiteko a sebete a moya a go boloka go ikemela mo go bulegilego ga lewatle la wona; mola diphefo tše di hlagago kudu tša legodimo le lefase di loga maanomabe a go mo lahlela lebopong la boradia le la bokgoba?

Eupša bjalo ka ge go hloka naga go nnoši go dula therešo e phagamego kudu, yeo e se nago lebopo, yeo e sa lekanywego bjalo ka Modimo—ka gona, go kaone go hwelela ka go se fele moo mo go bokolago, go e na le go thulwa ka mo go sa kgahlišego godimo ga lee, gaešita le ge seo e be e le polokego! Bakeng sa go swana le seboko, gona, oo! yo a bego a tla kganyoga go gagabela go fihla fase! Ditšhošetšo tša tše di šiišago! na bohloko bjo ka moka ke bja lefeela gakaakaa? Tšea pelo, e-ba pelo, O Bulkington! O kgotlelele ka go nyamiša, modimo wa seripagare! Godimo go tšwa go gašetšo ya gago ya lewatle-go fedišwa—go otlologa godimo, go tlola apotheosis ya gago!





KGAOLO YA 24. Mmuelli.

Bjalo ka ge nna le Queequeg bjale re thomile ka mo go lekanego kgwebong ye ya go tsoma maruarua; gomme ka ge kgwebo ye ya go tsoma maruarua ka tsela e itšego e tlile go lebelelwa gare ga bo-ra-naga e le go phegelela mo go sego ga sereto kudu le mo go se nago botumo; ka fao, ke tshwenyegile ka moka go le kgodiša, lena badudi ba naga, ka go hloka toka mo go dirilwego ka mo go rena batsomi ba maruarua.

Sa pele, go ka lebelelwa e le mo e nyakilego go ba mo go sa nyakegego go hloma therešo, ya gore gare ga batho ka kakaretšo, kgwebo ya go rema maruarua ga e balwe ka tekanyo le seo se bitšwago diprofešene tša tokologo. Ge motho yo a sa mo tsebego a be a ka tsenywa setšhabeng le ge e le sefe sa ditoropokgolo tše di fapa-fapanego, e be e tla tšwetša pele kgopolo ya kakaretšo ya bokgoni bja gagwe, ge nkabe a be a tšweletšwa khamphaning e le modiri wa di-harpoone, re re; gomme ge e ba ka go ekiša bahlankedi ba madira a ka lewatleng a swanetše go oketša ditlhaka tša mathomo tša SWF (Sperm Whale Fishery) karateng ya gagwe ya go etela, tshepedišo e bjalo e be e tla lebelelwa e le yeo e ikgogomošago kudu le yeo e segišago.

Ka ntle le pelaelo lebaka le lengwe le legolo leo ka lona lefase le ganago go hlompha rena banna ba maruarua, ke le: ba nagana gore, ge e le gabotse, pitšo ya rena e lekana le mohuta wa kgwebo ya go bolaya nama; le gore ge re swaregile ka mafolofolo go yona, re dikologilwe ke mehuta ka moka ya ditšhila. Re butchers re, seo ke nnete. Eupša bašomi ba nama, gape, le bašomi ba nama ba betšhe ya madi kudu e bile Balaodi ba Sešole ka moka bao lefase le thabelago go ba hlompha ka go se fetoge. Gomme ge e le taba ya go se hlweke mo go bolelwago ga kgwebo ya rena, kgauswinyane le tla thongwa ka dintlha tše itšego tšeo go fihla ga bjale di sa tsebjego kudu ka kakaretšo, le tšeo, ka kakaretšo, di tlago bjala ka phenyo sekepe sa maruarua a peu bonyenyane gare ga dilo tše di hlwekilego kudu tša thulaganyo ye lefase. Eupša gaešita le go dumelela molato wo go bolelwago ka wona gore e be therešo; ke di- deck dife tše di thelelago tše di sa rulaganywago tša sekepe sa maruarua tšeo di bapetšwago le carrion yeo e sa bolelegego ya mafelo ao a ntwa ao go tšwa go ona mašole a mantši gakaakaa a boelago go nwa ka ditheto ka moka tša mafumahadi? Le gona ge e ba kgopolo ya kotsi e godiša kudu boikgogomošo bjo bo tumilego bja profešene ya lešole; e re ke le kgonthišetše gore ba bantši ba mogale yo a ilego a gwantela ka bolokologi go fihla go betri, ba be ba tla boela morago ka pela ge go bonagala mosela o mogolo wa leruarua la peu, a tšholla moya godimo ga hlogo ya gagwe ka di- eddies. Gobane ke ditšhošetšo dife tše di kwešišegago tša motho ge di bapetšwa le ditšhošetšo le dimakatšo tša Modimo tšeo di kgokaganego!

Eupša, gaešita le ge lefase le re hlohlomiša batsomi ba maruarua, le ge go le bjalo le re hlompha ka mo go tseneletšego kudu ka go se lemoge; ee, borapedi bjo bo tletšego ka moka! ka gobane mo e nyakilego go ba di- taper ka moka, mabone le dikerese tšeo di tukago go dikologa lefase, di tuka, go swana le pele ga mafelo a makgethwa a mantši gakaakaa, bakeng sa letago la rena!

Eupša lebelela taba ye ka mabone a mangwe; e lekanye ka dikala tša mehuta ka moka; bona seo rena banna ba maruarua re lego sona, gomme re bile sona.

Ge e ba, ka gona, go basesiši ba dikepe ba go se be le mohola kudu, le bahlanogi le ba lahlilwego, ka morago ke tla abela dika tše di phagamego, gaešita le ge e le tše lefsifsi; loga go ba dikologa megau ya masetla-pelo; ge e ba gaešita le yo a llago kudu, mohlomongwe yo a nyenyefaditšwego kudu, gare ga bona ka moka, ka dinako tše dingwe a tla iphagamišetša dithabeng tše di phagamego; ge nka kgoma letsogo la mošomi yoo ka seetša se sengwe sa ethereal; ge nka gaša molalatladi godimo ga go sobela ga gagwe ga letšatši mo go kotsi; ke moka kgahlanong le basekaseki ka moka ba ba hwago nkgotlelele ka go yona, wena feela Moya wa Tekatekano, wo o phatlaladitšego kobo e tee ya bogoši ya botho godimo ga mohuta wa ka ka moka! KGAOLO YA 27. Knights le Squires.

Stubb e be e le molekane wa bobedi. E be e le setlogolwana sa Cape Cod; gomme ka fao, go ya ka tšhomišo ya lefelong leo, e be e bitšwa Cape-Cod-man. A thabile-go-mahlatse; ga se yo a kganyogago goba yo a nago le sebete; go tšea dikotsi ge di be di etla ka moya wo o sa kgomegego; gomme ge a dutše a swaregile ka mathata ao a lego kgauswi kudu a go kitimiša, a šoma ka thata, a iketlile e bile a kgobokeditšwe bjalo ka mohlakanelwa wa mosepedi yo a swaregilego ka ngwaga. Ka ge a be a metlae gabotse, a le bonolo e bile a sa šetše, o ile a okamela seketswana sa gagwe sa maruarua bjalo ka ge eka go kopana mo go bolayago kudu e be e fo ba dijo tša mantšiboa, gomme sehlopha sa gagwe ka moka se ile sa laletša baeng. O be a le yo a kgethegilego ka thulaganyo ya boiketlo ya karolo ya gagwe ya sekepe, go etša ge mootledi wa kgale wa sefala a bolela ka go snugness ga lepokisi la gagwe. Ge a be a le kgaufsi le leruarua, ka gare ga lona la lehu la ntwa, o ile a swara lerumo la gagwe leo le se nago kwelobohloko ka go tonya le ka go se kgahliše, ge motho yo a letša molodi a tšhoša hamore ya gagwe. O be a tla duma godimo ga melodi ya gagwe ya kgale ya rigadig mola a le flank le flank le phoofolo ya go tšhoša yeo e galefilego kudu. Tšhomišo ye telele e be e, go Stubb yo, e fetotše mehlagare ya lehu go ba setulo se bonolo. Seo a bego a se nagana ka lehu ka bolona, ​​ga go na seo se bolelwago. Gore o kile a nagana ka yona le gatee, e ka ba potšišo; but, if he ever did chance to cast his mind that way after a comfortable dinner, no doubt, like a good sailor, he took it to be a sort of call of the watch to tumble aloft, and bestir themselves there, about something which he would find out when he obeyed the order, and not sooner.

What, perhaps, with other things, made Stubb such an easy-going, unfearing man, so cheerily trudging off with the burden of life in a world full of grave pedlars, all bowed to the ground with their packs; what helped to bring about that almost impious good-humor of his; that thing must have been his pipe. For, like his nose, his short, black little pipe was one of the regular features of his face. You would almost as soon have expected him to turn out of his bunk without his nose as without his pipe. He kept a whole row of pipes there ready loaded, stuck in a rack, within easy reach of his hand; and, whenever he turned in, he smoked them all out in succession, lighting one from the other to the end of the chapter; then loading them again to be in readiness anew. For, when Stubb dressed, instead of first putting his legs into his trowsers, he put his pipe into his mouth.

I say this continual smoking must have been one cause, at least, of his peculiar disposition; for every one knows that this earthly air, whether ashore or afloat, is terribly infected with the nameless miseries of the numberless mortals who have died exhaling it; and as in time of the cholera, some people go about with a camphorated handkerchief to their mouths; so, likewise, against all mortal tribulations, Stubb's tobacco smoke might have operated as a sort of disinfecting agent.

The third mate was Flask, a native of Tisbury, in Martha's Vineyard. A short, stout, ruddy young fellow, very pugnacious concerning whales, who somehow seemed to think that the great leviathans had personally and hereditarily affronted him; and therefore it was a sort of point of honor with him, to destroy them whenever encountered. So utterly lost was he to all sense of reverence for the many marvels of their majestic bulk and mystic ways; and so dead to anything like an apprehension of any possible danger from encountering them; that in his poor opinion, the wondrous whale was but a species of magnified mouse, or at least water-rat, requiring only a little circumvention and some small application of time and trouble in order to kill and boil. This ignorant, unconscious fearlessness of his made him a little waggish in the matter of whales; he followed these fish for the fun of it; and a three years' voyage round Cape Horn was only a jolly joke that lasted that length of time. As a carpenter's nails are divided into wrought nails and cut nails; so mankind may be similarly divided. Little Flask was one of the wrought ones; made to clinch tight and last long. They called him King-Post on board of the Pequod; because, in form, he could be well likened to the short, square timber known by that name in Arctic whalers; and which by the means of many radiating side timbers inserted into it, serves to brace the ship against the icy concussions of those battering seas.

Now these three mates—Starbuck, Stubb, and Flask, were momentous men. They it was who by universal prescription commanded three of the Pequod's boats as headsmen. In that grand order of battle in which Captain Ahab would probably marshal his forces to descend on the whales, these three headsmen were as captains of companies. Or, being armed with their long keen whaling spears, they were as a picked trio of lancers; even as the harpooneers were flingers of javelins.

And since in this famous fishery, each mate or headsman, like a Gothic Knight of old, is always accompanied by his boat-steerer or harpooneer, who in certain conjunctures provides him with a fresh lance, when the former one has been badly twisted, or elbowed in the assault; and moreover, as there generally subsists between the two, a close intimacy and friendliness; it is therefore but meet, that in this place we set down who the Pequod's harpooneers were, and to what headsman each of them belonged.

First of all was Queequeg, whom Starbuck, the chief mate, had selected for his squire. But Queequeg is already known.

Next was Tashtego, an unmixed Indian from Gay Head, the most westerly promontory of Martha's Vineyard, where there still exists the last remnant of a village of red men, which has long supplied the neighboring island of Nantucket with many of her most daring harpooneers. In the fishery, they usually go by the generic name of Gay-Headers. Tashtego's long, lean, sable hair, his high cheek bones, and black rounding eyes—for an Indian, Oriental in their largeness, but Antarctic in their glittering expression—all this sufficiently proclaimed him an inheritor of the unvitiated blood of those proud warrior hunters, who, in quest of the great New England moose, had scoured, bow in hand, the aboriginal forests of the main. But no longer snuffing in the trail of the wild beasts of the woodland, Tashtego now hunted in the wake of the great whales of the sea; the unerring harpoon of the son fitly replacing the infallible arrow of the sires. To look at the tawny brawn of his lithe snaky limbs, you would almost have credited the superstitions of some of the earlier Puritans, and half-believed this wild Indian to be a son of the Prince of the Powers of the Air. Tashtego was Stubb the second mate's squire.

Third among the harpooneers was Daggoo, a gigantic, coal-black negro-savage, with a lion-like tread—an Ahasuerus to behold. Suspended from his ears were two golden hoops, so large that the sailors called them ring-bolts, and would talk of securing the top-sail halyards to them. In his youth Daggoo had voluntarily shipped on board of a whaler, lying in a lonely bay on his native coast. And never having been anywhere in the world but in Africa, Nantucket, and the pagan harbors most frequented by whalemen; and having now led for many years the bold life of the fishery in the ships of owners uncommonly heedful of what manner of men they shipped; Daggoo retained all his barbaric virtues, and erect as a giraffe, moved about the decks in all the pomp of six feet five in his socks. There was a corporeal humility in looking up at him; and a white man standing before him seemed a white flag come to beg truce of a fortress. Curious to tell, this imperial negro, Ahasuerus Daggoo, was the Squire of little Flask, who looked like a chess-man beside him. As for the residue of the Pequod's company, be it said, that at the present day not one in two of the many thousand men before the mast employed in the American whale fishery, are Americans born, though pretty nearly all the officers are. Herein it is the same with the American whale fishery as with the American army and military and merchant navies, and the engineering forces employed in the construction of the American Canals and Railroads. The same, I say, because in all these cases the native American liberally provides the brains, the rest of the world as generously supplying the muscles. No small number of these whaling seamen belong to the Azores, where the outward bound Nantucket whalers frequently touch to augment their crews from the hardy peasants of those rocky shores. In like manner, the Greenland whalers sailing out of Hull or London, put in at the Shetland Islands, to receive the full complement of their crew. Upon the passage homewards, they drop them there again. How it is, there is no telling, but Islanders seem to make the best whalemen. They were nearly all Islanders in the Pequod, Isolatoes too, I call such, not acknowledging the common continent of men, but each Isolato living on a separate continent of his own. Yet now, federated along one keel, what a set these Isolatoes were! An Anacharsis Clootz deputation from all the isles of the sea, and all the ends of the earth, accompanying Old Ahab in the Pequod to lay the world's grievances before that bar from which not very many of them ever come back. Black Little Pip—he never did—oh, no! he went before. Poor Alabama boy! On the grim Pequod's forecastle, ye shall ere long see him, beating his tambourine; prelusive of the eternal time, when sent for, to the great quarter-deck on high, he was bid strike in with angels, and beat his tambourine in glory; called a coward here, hailed a hero there!





CHAPTER 28. Ahab.

For several days after leaving Nantucket, nothing above hatches was seen of Captain Ahab. The mates regularly relieved each other at the watches, and for aught that could be seen to the contrary, they seemed to be the only commanders of the ship; only they sometimes issued from the cabin with orders so sudden and peremptory, that after all it was plain they but commanded vicariously. Yes, their supreme lord and dictator was there, though hitherto unseen by any eyes not permitted to penetrate into the now sacred retreat of the cabin.

Every time I ascended to the deck from my watches below, I instantly gazed aft to mark if any strange face were visible; for my first vague disquietude touching the unknown captain, now in the seclusion of the sea, became almost a perturbation. This was strangely heightened at times by the ragged Elijah's diabolical incoherences uninvitedly recurring to me, with a subtle energy I could not have before conceived of. But poorly could I withstand them, much as in other moods I was almost ready to smile at the solemn whimsicalities of that outlandish prophet of the wharves. But whatever it was of apprehensiveness or uneasiness—to call it so—which I felt, yet whenever I came to look about me in the ship, it seemed against all warrantry to cherish such emotions. For though the harpooneers, with the great body of the crew, were a far more barbaric, heathenish, and motley set than any of the tame merchant-ship companies which my previous experiences had made me acquainted with, still I ascribed this—and rightly ascribed it—to the fierce uniqueness of the very nature of that wild Scandinavian vocation in which I had so abandonedly embarked. But it was especially the aspect of the three chief officers of the ship, the mates, which was most forcibly calculated to allay these colourless misgivings, and induce confidence and cheerfulness in every presentment of the voyage. Three better, more likely sea-officers and men, each in his own different way, could not readily be found, and they were every one of them Americans; a Nantucketer, a Vineyarder, a Cape man. Now, it being Christmas when the ship shot from out her harbor, for a space we had biting Polar weather, though all the time running away from it to the southward; and by every degree and minute of latitude which we sailed, gradually leaving that merciless winter, and all its intolerable weather behind us. It was one of those less lowering, but still grey and gloomy enough mornings of the transition, when with a fair wind the ship was rushing through the water with a vindictive sort of leaping and melancholy rapidity, that as I mounted to the deck at the call of the forenoon watch, so soon as I levelled my glance towards the taffrail, foreboding shivers ran over me. Reality outran apprehension; Captain Ahab stood upon his quarter-deck.

There seemed no sign of common bodily illness about him, nor of the recovery from any. He looked like a man cut away from the stake, when the fire has overrunningly wasted all the limbs without consuming them, or taking away one particle from their compacted aged robustness. His whole high, broad form, seemed made of solid bronze, and shaped in an unalterable mould, like Cellini's cast Perseus. Threading its way out from among his grey hairs, and continuing right down one side of his tawny scorched face and neck, till it disappeared in his clothing, you saw a slender rod-like mark, lividly whitish. It resembled that perpendicular seam sometimes made in the straight, lofty trunk of a great tree, when the upper lightning tearingly darts down it, and without wrenching a single twig, peels and grooves out the bark from top to bottom, ere running off into the soil, leaving the tree still greenly alive, but branded. Whether that mark was born with him, or whether it was the scar left by some desperate wound, no one could certainly say. By some tacit consent, throughout the voyage little or no allusion was made to it, especially by the mates. But once Tashtego's senior, an old Gay-Head Indian among the crew, superstitiously asserted that not till he was full forty years old did Ahab become that way branded, and then it came upon him, not in the fury of any mortal fray, but in an elemental strife at sea. Yet, this wild hint seemed inferentially negatived, by what a grey Manxman insinuated, an old sepulchral man, who, having never before sailed out of Nantucket, had never ere this laid eye upon wild Ahab. Nevertheless, the old sea-traditions, the immemorial credulities, popularly invested this old Manxman with preternatural powers of discernment. So that no white sailor seriously contradicted him when he said that if ever Captain Ahab should be tranquilly laid out—which might hardly come to pass, so he muttered—then, whoever should do that last office for the dead, would find a birth-mark on him from crown to sole.

So powerfully did the whole grim aspect of Ahab affect me, and the livid brand which streaked it, that for the first few moments I hardly noted that not a little of this overbearing grimness was owing to the barbaric white leg upon which he partly stood. It had previously come to me that this ivory leg had at sea been fashioned from the polished bone of the sperm whale's jaw. “Aye, he was dismasted off Japan,” said the old Gay-Head Indian once; “but like his dismasted craft, he shipped another mast without coming home for it. He has a quiver of 'em.”

I was struck with the singular posture he maintained. Upon each side of the Pequod's quarter deck, and pretty close to the mizzen shrouds, there was an auger hole, bored about half an inch or so, into the plank. His bone leg steadied in that hole; one arm elevated, and holding by a shroud; Captain Ahab stood erect, looking straight out beyond the ship's ever-pitching prow. There was an infinity of firmest fortitude, a determinate, unsurrenderable wilfulness, in the fixed and fearless, forward dedication of that glance. Not a word he spoke; nor did his officers say aught to him; though by all their minutest gestures and expressions, they plainly showed the uneasy, if not painful, consciousness of being under a troubled master-eye. And not only that, but moody stricken Ahab stood before them with a crucifixion in his face; in all the nameless regal overbearing dignity of some mighty woe.

Ere long, from his first visit in the air, he withdrew into his cabin. But after that morning, he was every day visible to the crew; either standing in his pivot-hole, or seated upon an ivory stool he had; or heavily walking the deck. As the sky grew less gloomy; indeed, began to grow a little genial, he became still less and less a recluse; as if, when the ship had sailed from home, nothing but the dead wintry bleakness of the sea had then kept him so secluded. And, by and by, it came to pass, that he was almost continually in the air; but, as yet, for all that he said, or perceptibly did, on the at last sunny deck, he seemed as unnecessary there as another mast. But the Pequod was only making a passage now; not regularly cruising; nearly all whaling preparatives needing supervision the mates were fully competent to, so that there was little or nothing, out of himself, to employ or excite Ahab, now; and thus chase away, for that one interval, the clouds that layer upon layer were piled upon his brow, as ever all clouds choose the loftiest peaks to pile themselves upon.

Nevertheless, ere long, the warm, warbling persuasiveness of the pleasant, holiday weather we came to, seemed gradually to charm him from his mood. For, as when the red-cheeked, dancing girls, April and May, trip home to the wintry, misanthropic woods; even the barest, ruggedest, most thunder-cloven old oak will at least send forth some few green sprouts, to welcome such glad-hearted visitants; so Ahab did, in the end, a little respond to the playful allurings of that girlish air. More than once did he put forth the faint blossom of a look, which, in any other man, would have soon flowered out in a smile.
