POGLAVJE 48. Prvo znižanje.

Fantomi, kajti tako se je takrat zdelo, so švigali na drugi strani palube in z neslišno hitrostjo sproščali pribor in pasove čolna, ki je tam zanihal. Ta čoln je vedno veljal za enega izmed rezervnih čolnov, čeprav se je tehnično imenoval kapitanov, ker je visel z desne strani. Podoba, ki je zdaj stala ob svojih lokih, je bila visoka in črna, z enim belim zobom, ki je zlobno štrlel iz jeklenih ustnic. Pogrebno ga je oblekel zmečkan kitajski suknjič iz črnega bombaža s širokimi črnimi hlačami iz iste temne tkanine. Toda nenavadno je kronal to ebenovino bleščeče bel prepleten turban, živi lasje pa so bili spleteni in zviti okrog in okrog na njegovi glavi. Manj črni po videzu so bili spremljevalci te figure tiste žive, tigrasto rumene polti, ki je bila značilna za nekatere domorodne staroselce v Manillah; rasa, ki je bila razvpita po določenem diabolizmu subtilnosti in nekateri pošteni beli mornarji naj bi bili plačani vohuni in tajni zaupni agenti na vodi hudiča, njihovega gospodarja, čigar preštevalnica naj bi bila drugje.

Medtem ko je začudena ladijska družba še strmela v te tujce, je Ahab zavpil starcu z belim turbanom na njihovem čelu: "Si pripravljen, Fedallah?"

"Pripravljen," se je glasil napol sikajoč odgovor.

»Nižje torej; slišiš?" kričanje po palubi. "Tam nižje, pravim."

Njegov glas je bil tako grmeč, da so moški kljub začudenju skočili čez ograjo; snopi so se vrteli v blokih; z valovanjem so trije čolni padli v morje; medtem ko so mornarji s spretno, neprimerno drznostjo, ki je ni poznana v nobenem drugem poklicu, kot koze skakali po valovitem ladijskem boku v premetavane čolne spodaj.

Komaj so se potegnili izpod ladijskega zavetrja, se je četrta kobilica, ki je prihajala z vetrne strani, potegnila pod krmo in pokazala petim tujcem, kako veslajo Ahaba, ki je, stoječ pokončen na krmi, glasno klical Starbucka, Stubba, in Flask, da se razširijo na široko, tako da pokrijejo veliko vodno površino. Toda z vsemi svojimi očmi, ki so bile spet prikovane na črnega Fedallaha in njegovo posadko, jetniki drugih čolnov niso ubogali ukaza.

"Kapitan Ahab? -" je rekel Starbuck.

»Razprostrite se,« je zaklical Ahab; »Umaknite se, vsi štirje čolni. Ti, Flask, potegnite bolj v zavetrje!«

"Da, da, gospod," je veselo zavpil mali King-Post in pometal okrog svojega velikega krmilnega vesla. "Ulezi se!" nagovarja svojo posadko. »Tam! — tam! — spet tam! Tam piha naravnost naprej, fantje! - Ulezite se!"

"Nikoli ne upoštevaj tistih rumenih fantov, Archy."

"Oh, ne moti jih, gospod," je rekel Archy; »Vse sem vedel že prej. Jih nisem slišal v prtljažniku? In ali nisem o tem povedal Cabacu tukaj? Kaj praviš, Cabaco? So slepi potniki, gospod Flask.

»Potegnite, potegnite, moja lepa srca-živa; povlecite, otroci moji; potegni, moji mali,« je vlečeče in pomirjujoče zavzdihnil Stubb svoji ekipi, od katere so nekateri še vedno kazali znake nelagodja. »Zakaj si ne zlomite hrbtenice, moji fantje? V kaj buljiš? Tisti fantje v tistem čolnu? Tut! Samo še pet rok nam pride na pomoč – ne glede na to, od kod – več jih je, tem bolje. Potegni, torej, povleci; ne pozabite na žveplo - hudiči so dovolj dobri ljudje. Tako tako; tam si zdaj; to je udarec za tisoč funtov; to je poteza za pometanje vložkov! Hura za zlato skodelico spermičnega olja, moji heroji! Trikrat na zdravje, moški - vsi živi! Enostavno, enostavno; ne mudi se — ne mudi se. Zakaj ne zaskočite z vesli, nevaljalci? Nekaj ​​ugriznite, psi! Tako, tako, tako, torej: — tiho, tiho! To je to — to je to! dolg in močan. Popustite tam, popustite! Hudič vas prinese, vi ragamuffin rapscallions; vsi spite. Nehajte smrčati, zaspanci, in potegnite. Potegni, boš? potegni, ne moreš? potegni, kajne? Zakaj v imenu gudgeons in ingverjevih tort ne potegnete? - potegnite in nekaj zlomite! potegni in začni oči! Tukaj!" izvlekel oster nož iz svojega pasu; »Vsak materin sin potegne svoj nož in vleče z rezilom med zobmi. To je to - to je to. Zdaj naredite nekaj; tako izgleda, moji jekleni koščki. Zaženite jo - zaženite jo, moje srebrne žličke! Začni jo, marling-spikes!«

Stubbov exordium do svoje posadke je tukaj podan na splošno, ker je imel precej svojevrsten način pogovarjanja z njimi na splošno in še posebej pri vcepljanju religije veslanja. Toda iz tega vzorca njegovih pridig ne smete domnevati, da je kdaj zašel v poštene strasti s svojo kongregacijo. Sploh ne; in v tem je bila njegova glavna posebnost. Svoji posadki je govoril najbolj grozljive stvari, v tonu, ki je bil tako nenavadno sestavljen iz zabave in besa, in bes se je zdel tako preračunan zgolj kot popestritev zabave, da noben veslač ni mogel slišati takšnih čudnih klicev, ne da bi ga potegnil za drago, in vendar vleče za samo šalo. Poleg tega je bil sam ves čas videti tako lahkoten in brezbrižen, tako ležerno je upravljal svoje krmilno veslo in tako široko razprt – včasih z odprtimi usti – da je sam pogled na tako zehajočega poveljnika, s čisto silo kontrasta, deloval kot čar nad posadko. Še enkrat, Stubb je bil eden tistih nenavadnih humoristov, katerih veselje je včasih tako nenavadno dvoumno, da spravi vse podrejene na prežo, da bi jih ubogali.

Po Ahabovem znaku je Starbuck zdaj poševno vlekel čez Stubbov lok; in ko sta bila čolna za kakšno minuto precej blizu drug drugemu, je Stubb zaklical sopotniku.

"Gospod. Starbuck! zadnji čoln tam, ajoj! beseda z vami, gospod, če dovolite!«

"Halo!" se je vrnil Starbuck, ki se ni obrnil niti za en centimeter, ko je govoril; še vedno resno, a šepetaje priganja svojo posadko; njegov obraz je bil kot kremen iz Stubbovega.

"Kaj si mislite o teh rumenih fantih, gospod!"

»Nekako pretihotapljena na krovu, preden je ladja odplula. (Močno, močno, fantje!)« šepeta svoji ekipi, nato pa spet na glas: »Žalostna stvar, g. Stubb! (kipi jo, kipi, moji fantje!) ampak nič hudega, gospod Stubb, vse najboljše. Naj vsa vaša posadka močno potegne, pa bo, kar bo. (Pomlad, moji možje, pomlad!) Pred vami je kopica sperme, gospod Stubb, in to je tisto, po kar ste prišli. (Potegnite, moji fantje!) Sperma, sperma je igra! To je vsaj dolžnost; dolžnost in dobiček z roko v roki.«

»Ja, ja, tako sem mislil,« je rekel Stubb, ko sta se čolna razšla, »takoj ko sem ju pogledal, sem tako mislil. Da, in to je tisto, zaradi česar je šel v zadrževanje, tako pogosto, kot je Dough-Boy dolgo sumil. Bili so skriti tam spodaj. Beli kit je na dnu. No, no, pa naj bo! Ne morem si pomagati! V redu! Popustite, moški! Danes ni Beli kit! Popustite!"

Prihod teh nenavadnih tujcev v tako kritičnem trenutku, kot je spuščanje čolnov s palube, ni nerazumno prebudil nekakšnega vraževernega začudenja v nekaterih ladijskih družbah; toda Archyjevo domišljijsko odkritje, ki je nekaj časa pred tem prišlo mednje v tujino, čeprav takrat res ni bilo priznano, jih je to v neki manjši meri pripravilo na dogodek. To je vzelo skrajni rob njihovega čudenja; in kaj torej z vsem tem in Stubbovim samozavestnim načinom pojasnjevanja njihovega videza, so bili za nekaj časa osvobojeni vraževernih domnev; čeprav je afera še vedno puščala obilo prostora za vse vrste divjih domnev glede temne Ahabove natančne vloge v zadevi od začetka. Zame sem se na tihem spomnil skrivnostnih senc, ki sem jih videl, kako se plazijo na krovu Pequoda med mračno zarjo na Nantucketu, pa tudi zagonetnih namigov o nerazložljivem Elijahu.

Medtem je bil Ahab, tako da ga njegovi častniki niso slišali, ko je zašel najbolj proti vetru, še vedno v razponu pred drugimi čolni; okoliščina, ki je govorila o tem, kako močna posadka ga je vlekla. Ta njegova tigrasto rumena bitja so bila videti vsa jeklena in kitova kost; kot pet udarnih kladiv so se dvigali in spuščali z rednimi močnimi udarci, ki so občasno pognali čoln po vodi kot vodoravni razpočeni kotel iz parnika iz Mississippija. Kar zadeva Fedallaha, ki so ga videli, kako vleče harpunarsko veslo, je odvrgel svoj črni jopič in pokazal svoje gole prsi s celim delom telesa nad ograjo, jasno razrezano proti izmeničnim vdolbinam vodnega obzorja; medtem ko je na drugem koncu čolna Ahab z eno roko, kot pri sabljaču, vržen napol nazaj v zrak, kot da bi uravnotežil kakršno koli težnjo po spotikanju; Ahaba je bilo videti, kako vztrajno upravlja svoje veslo kot v tisočih spustih čolna, preden ga je Beli kit strgal. Kar naenkrat je iztegnjena roka naredila nenavaden gib in nato ostala pritrjena, medtem ko je bilo pet vesla čolna videti istočasno pokončanih. Čoln in posadka so nepremično obstali na morju. V trenutku so se trije razprti čolni zadaj ustavili na poti. Kiti so se neenakomerno umestili v modrino, tako da na daleč niso dajali opaznega znaka gibanja, čeprav ga je Ahab opazoval iz svoje bližine.

"Vsak človek pazi na svoja vesla!" je zavpil Starbuck. "Ti, Queequeg, vstani!"

Divjak se je okretno dvignil na trikotno dvignjeno škatlo v premcu in tam stal pokončno ter z močno željnimi očmi strmel proti mestu, kjer je bil nazadnje opisan lov. Podobno na skrajnem krmnem delu čolna, kjer je bil prav tako trikotno postavljen v ravnini z ograjo, je bil viden sam Starbuck, ki je hladnokrvno in spretno uravnotežil med sunkovitimi meti svojega čipa plovila in nemo zrl v ogromno modro oko morja.

Tudi nedaleč oddaljeni Flaškin čoln je brez sape ležal; njegov poveljnik nepremišljeno stoji na vrhu ladje, močne vrste droga, zakoreninjenega v kobilico, in se dviga približno dva metra nad nivojem krmne ploščadi. Uporablja se za lovljenje zavojev s kitovo vrvico. Njen vrh ni prostornejši od človekove dlani in ko je stala na takem podstavku, se je zdelo, da Flask stoji na jamboru neke ladje, ki je potonila vsem, razen njenim tovornjakom. Toda mali King-Post je bil majhen in nizek, hkrati pa je bil mali King-Post poln velike in visoke ambicije, tako da to njegovo neskladno stališče King-Posta nikakor ni zadovoljilo.

»Tri morja ne vidim; tam nam nagni veslo in pusti me k temu.”

Po tem je Daggoo, z obema rokama na ograji, da bi ohranil svojo pot, hitro zdrsnil na krmo in se nato pokončno dvignil, ponudil svoja vzvišena ramena za podstavek.

»Dober jambor, gospod. Boste montirali?"

»Tako bom, in najlepša hvala, moj dobri kolega; samo želim si, da bi bil petdeset čevljev višji.”

Nato se je velikanski črnec, ki se je z nogami trdno naslonil na dve nasprotni deski čolna, malce sklonil, svojo ploščato dlan položil na Flaškovo stopalo, nato pa Flaškovo roko položil na njegovo s pogrebnim vozom pernato glavo in mu ukazal, da poskoči, kot bi moral sam vreči, z enim spretnim zamahom je malega človeka visoko in suho spustil na njegova ramena. In tukaj je bil Flask, ki je zdaj stal, Daggoo z eno dvignjeno roko pa mu je priskrbel prsni trak, na katerega se je naslonil in se držal.

Tiroju je kadar koli nenavaden pogled, ko vidi, s kakšno čudovito navado nezavedne spretnosti bo kitolovec obdržal pokončno držo v svojem čolnu, tudi ko ga razburja najbolj nemirno perverzno in prečno morje. Še bolj nenavadno pa ga je videti vrtoglavo sedečega na samem kladu v takih okoliščinah. Toda pogled na majhno Flaško, nameščeno na velikanskega Daggooja, je bil še bolj radoveden; ker se je vzdrževal s hladno, brezbrižno, lahkotno, nepremišljeno, barbarsko veličastnostjo, je plemeniti črnec na vsak val morja harmonično valjal svojo fino obliko. Na njegovem širokem hrbtu je bila Flaška z lanenimi lasmi videti kot snežinka. Nosilec je bil videti plemenitejši od jezdeca. Čeprav resnično živahen, buren, razmetljiv mali Flask bi tu in tam zatiskal od nestrpnosti; vendar s tem ni dal niti enega dodatnega dviga v črnsko gosposko skrinjo. Tako sem videl, kako strast in nečimrnost žigosata živo velikodušno zemljo, vendar zemlja zaradi tega ni spremenila svojih plimovanja in letnih časov.

Medtem Stubb, tretji častnik, ni izdal takšnih daljnogledih skrbi. Kiti so morda izvedli eno od svojih običajnih sondiranj, ne pa začasni potop iz zgolj strahu; in če bi bilo temu tako, je bil Stubb, kot se zdi v navadi v takšnih primerih, odločen, da bo s svojo pipo tolažil omedlevi interval. Umaknil ga je s traku za klobuk, kjer ga je vedno nosil poševno kot pero. Naložil ga je in ga s palcem udaril domov; a komaj je prižgal vžigalico na grobem brusnem papirju v svoji roki, je Tashtego, njegov harpunar, čigar oči so bile usmerjene proti vetru kot dve zvezdi stalnici, nenadoma kot svetloba padel s svojega pokončnega položaja na svoj sedež in hitro zavpil blaznost naglice, "Dol, dol vsi in popustite! - tam so!"

Kopenarju v tistem trenutku ne bi bilo videti nobenega kita ali kakršnega koli znaka sleda; nič drugega kot nemiren košček zelenkasto bele vode in tanki razpršeni oblački hlapov, ki lebdijo nad njo in soffusingly pihajo proti zavetrju, kot zmeden scud iz belih valovitih valov. Zrak naokoli je nenadoma zavibriral in zaskelel, tako rekoč, kot zrak nad močno razgretimi železnimi ploščami. Pod tem atmosferskim valovanjem in zvijanjem ter delno tudi pod tanko plastjo vode so plavali kiti. Gledano vnaprej glede na vse druge znake, so bili oblački hlapov, ki so jih bruhali, videti kot njihovi predhodni kurirji in ločeni leteči kolesarji.

Vsi štirje čolni so zdaj močno zasledovali to edino točko nemirne vode in zraka. Vendar jih je bilo pošteno prehiteti; letelo je naprej in naprej, kot množica mešanih mehurčkov, ki jih je hiter tok nosil s hribov.

"Vleci, povleci, moji dobri fantje," je dejal Starbuck z najtišjim možnim, a najmočnejšim koncentriranim šepetom svojim možem; medtem ko je oster nepremični pogled iz njegovih oči švignil naravnost pred premec, se je skoraj zdel kot dve vidni igli v dveh nezmotljivih šestilih. Svoji posadki pa ni veliko povedal, niti posadka njemu ni rekla ničesar. Samo tišino čolna je občasno osupljivo prerezal eden od njegovih nenavadnih šepetov, zdaj oster z ukazom, zdaj mehak s prošnjo.

Kako drugačen je glasen mali King-Post. »Zapojte in povejte nekaj, srčki moji. Rohnite in vlecite, moji streli! Naplavite me, naplavite me na njihove črne hrbte, fantje; storite to samo zame, in podpisal vam bom svojo plantažo Martha's Vineyard, fantje; vključno z ženo in otroki, fantje. Lezi me - lezi me! O Gospod, Gospod! ampak jaz bom oster, strmeč nor! Glej! glej to belo vodo!" In tako kričeč je potegnil klobuk z glave in tolkel gor in dol po njem; potem pa ga je dvignil, spogledal daleč na morje; in končno padel, da bi se dvignil in padel v krmo čolna kot ponoreli žrebiček iz prerije.

»Poglej zdaj tega fanta,« je filozofsko zavlekel Stubb, ki je s svojo neprižgano kratko pipo, mehansko zadržano med zobmi, na kratki razdalji sledil za – »Ima krče, tale Flask. Ustreza? da, daj mu fits – to je prava beseda – pitch se jim prilega. Veselo, veselo, srca živa. Puding za večerjo, saj veste; veselo je beseda. Vleci, dojenčki — vleci, dojenčki — vleci, vsi. Ampak kaj hudiča se ti mudi? Nežno, nežno in vztrajno, moji moški. Samo vlecite in vlecite naprej; nič več. Počite si vse hrbtenice in pregriznite nože na dvoje — to je vse. Počasi — zakaj ne pomiriš, pravim, in si poči vsa jetra in pljuča!«

Toda kaj je tisti nedoumljivi Ahab rekel tisti svoji tigrasto rumeni ekipi – te besede je tukaj najbolje izpustiti; saj živiš pod blagoslovljeno lučjo evangeličanske dežele. Samo neverni morski psi v drznih morjih lahko prisluhnejo takšnim besedam, ko je Ahab s tornado obrvjo in rdečimi morilskimi očmi ter s peno zlepljenimi ustnicami skočil za svojim plenom.

Medtem so se vsi čolni utrgali. Ponavljajoča se specifična namigovanja Flaska na »tega kita«, kot je imenoval izmišljeno pošast, za katero je izjavil, da s svojim repom nenehno draži premec njegovega čolna – te njegove aluzije so bile včasih tako žive in resnične, da bi povzročile eden ali dva od njegovih mož, da bi ugrabil prestrašen pogled čez ramo. Toda to je bilo proti vsem pravilom; kajti veslači morajo iztakniti oči in si zabiti nabodalo skozi vrat; uporaba, ki pravi, da v teh kritičnih trenutkih ne smejo imeti organov, razen ušes, in udov, razen rok.

Bil je prizor, poln hitrega čudenja in strahospoštovanja! Široki valovi vsemogočnega morja; naraščajoče, votlo rjovenje, ki so ga izdali, ko so se kotalili po osmih robovih, kot velikanske sklede v brezmejni zelenici za kegljanje; kratka suspendirana agonija čolna, ko bi se za trenutek nagnila na nožu podoben rob ostrejših valov, ki je skoraj grozil, da ga bo prerezal na dvoje; nenaden globok padec v vodne kotanje in kotanje; močni vzgibi in vzgibi, da bi dosegli vrh nasprotnega hriba; vratolomen, sani podoben drsnik po njegovi drugi strani; - vse to, z vzkliki glavarjev in harpunarjev, in drhtečim sopihanjem veslačev, s čudovitim pogledom na slonokoščenega Pequoda, ki se spušča na svoje čolne z razpetimi jadri, kakor divja kokoš po svojem kričečem zarodu; — vse to je bilo vznemirljivo.

Ne surovi novinec, ki koraka iz naročja svoje žene v vročino svoje prve bitke; ne mrtvečev duh, ki se sreča s prvim neznanim fantomom na drugem svetu; nobeden od teh ne more občutiti bolj nenavadnih in močnejših čustev kot tisti človek, ki se prvič znajde v očaranem, vznemirjenem krogu lovljenega kita sesalca.

Plesoča bela voda, ki jo je povzročil lov, je zdaj postajala čedalje bolj vidna zaradi čedalje temnejših temnih oblačnih senc, ki so jih metale na morje. Curki pare se niso več mešali, temveč so se nagibali povsod na desno in levo; zdelo se je, da kiti ločujejo svoje sledi. Čolni so bili bolj narazen; Starbuck lovi tri kite, ki mrtvi bežijo v zavetrje. Naše jadro je bilo zdaj razpeto in s še naraščajočim vetrom smo hiteli naprej; čoln je s tako norostjo šel skozi vodo, da je bilo zavetrno veslo komaj mogoče delati dovolj hitro, da bi se izognili iztrganju iz vrstnih ključavnic.

Kmalu smo tekli skozi napihljivo široko tančico megle; ne ladje ne čolna ni videti.

»Umaknite se, možje,« je zašepetal Starbuck in še bolj zategnil škoto svojega jadra; »Je še čas za ubijanje rib, preden pride nevihta. Spet je bela voda!—Blizu! Pomlad!"

Kmalu zatem sta dva vzklika v hitrem zaporedju na vsaki naši strani pokazala, da so drugi čolni pohiteli; a komaj so jih preslišali, ko je Starbuck z bliskovitim drhtečim šepetom rekel: "Vstani!" in Queequeg je s harpuno v roki skočil na noge.

Čeprav se nobeden od veslačev takrat ni soočal z življenjsko in smrtno nevarnostjo tako blizu njih spredaj, pa so z očmi, uprtimi v intenziven obraz sopotnika na krmi čolna, vedeli, da je prišel neizbežen trenutek; slišali so tudi ogromen valoviti zvok, kot bi se petdeset slonov mešalo v svojem leglu. Medtem je čoln še vedno brnel skozi meglo, valovi pa so se zvijali in sikali okoli nas kot pokončni grebeni razjarjenih kač.

»To je njegova grba. Tja , tam , daj mu!« je zašepetal Starbuck.

Iz čolna je skočil kratek hiteč zvok; bilo je puščeno železo iz Queequega. Toda skoraj vsi so domnevali, da mora biti ta posebna pripravljalna previdnost pri Ahabu le z namenom končnega zasledovanja Mobyja Dicka; saj je že razkril svojo namero, da bo osebno lovil tisto smrtno pošast. But such a supposition did by no means involve the remotest suspicion as to any boat's crew being assigned to that boat.

Now, with the subordinate phantoms, what wonder remained soon waned away; for in a whaler wonders soon wane. Besides, now and then such unaccountable odds and ends of strange nations come up from the unknown nooks and ash-holes of the earth to man these floating outlaws of whalers; and the ships themselves often pick up such queer castaway creatures found tossing about the open sea on planks, bits of wreck, oars, whaleboats, canoes, blown-off Japanese junks, and what not; that Beelzebub himself might climb up the side and step down into the cabin to chat with the captain, and it would not create any unsubduable excitement in the forecastle.

But be all this as it may, certain it is that while the subordinate phantoms soon found their place among the crew, though still as it were somehow distinct from them, yet that hair-turbaned Fedallah remained a muffled mystery to the last. Whence he came in a mannerly world like this, by what sort of unaccountable tie he soon evinced himself to be linked with Ahab's peculiar fortunes; nay, so far as to have some sort of a half-hinted influence; Heaven knows, but it might have been even authority over him; all this none knew. But one cannot sustain an indifferent air concerning Fedallah. He was such a creature as civilized, domestic people in the temperate zone only see in their dreams, and that but dimly; but the like of whom now and then glide among the unchanging Asiatic communities, especially the Oriental isles to the east of the continent—those insulated, immemorial, unalterable countries, which even in these modern days still preserve much of the ghostly aboriginalness of earth's primal generations, when the memory of the first man was a distinct recollection, and all men his descendants, unknowing whence he came, eyed each other as real phantoms, and asked of the sun and the moon why they were created and to what end; when though, according to Genesis, the angels indeed consorted with the daughters of men, the devils also, add the uncanonical Rabbins, indulged in mundane amours.





CHAPTER 51. The Spirit-Spout.

Days, weeks passed, and under easy sail, the ivory Pequod had slowly swept across four several cruising-grounds; that off the Azores; off the Cape de Verdes; on the Plate (so called), being off the mouth of the Rio de la Plata; and the Carrol Ground, an unstaked, watery locality, southerly from St. Helena.

It was while gliding through these latter waters that one serene and moonlight night, when all the waves rolled by like scrolls of silver; and, by their soft, suffusing seethings, made what seemed a silvery silence, not a solitude; on such a silent night a silvery jet was seen far in advance of the white bubbles at the bow. Lit up by the moon, it looked celestial; seemed some plumed and glittering god uprising from the sea. Fedallah first descried this jet. For of these moonlight nights, it was his wont to mount to the main-mast head, and stand a look-out there, with the same precision as if it had been day. And yet, though herds of whales were seen by night, not one whaleman in a hundred would venture a lowering for them. You may think with what emotions, then, the seamen beheld this old Oriental perched aloft at such unusual hours; his turban and the moon, companions in one sky. But when, after spending his uniform interval there for several successive nights without uttering a single sound; when, after all this silence, his unearthly voice was heard announcing that silvery, moon-lit jet, every reclining mariner started to his feet as if some winged spirit had lighted in the rigging, and hailed the mortal crew. “There she blows!” Had the trump of judgment blown, they could not have quivered more; yet still they felt no terror; rather pleasure. For though it was a most unwonted hour, yet so impressive was the cry, and so deliriously exciting, that almost every soul on board instinctively desired a lowering.

Walking the deck with quick, side-lunging strides, Ahab commanded the t'gallant sails and royals to be set, and every stunsail spread. The best man in the ship must take the helm. Then, with every mast-head manned, the piled-up craft rolled down before the wind. The strange, upheaving, lifting tendency of the taffrail breeze filling the hollows of so many sails, made the buoyant, hovering deck to feel like air beneath the feet; while still she rushed along, as if two antagonistic influences were struggling in her—one to mount direct to heaven, the other to drive yawingly to some horizontal goal. And had you watched Ahab's face that night, you would have thought that in him also two different things were warring. While his one live leg made lively echoes along the deck, every stroke of his dead limb sounded like a coffin-tap. On life and death this old man walked. But though the ship so swiftly sped, and though from every eye, like arrows, the eager glances shot, yet the silvery jet was no more seen that night. Every sailor swore he saw it once, but not a second time.

This midnight-spout had almost grown a forgotten thing, when, some days after, lo! at the same silent hour, it was again announced: again it was descried by all; but upon making sail to overtake it, once more it disappeared as if it had never been. And so it served us night after night, till no one heeded it but to wonder at it. Mysteriously jetted into the clear moonlight, or starlight, as the case might be; disappearing again for one whole day, or two days, or three; and somehow seeming at every distinct repetition to be advancing still further and further in our van, this solitary jet seemed for ever alluring us on.

Nor with the immemorial superstition of their race, and in accordance with the preternaturalness, as it seemed, which in many things invested the Pequod, were there wanting some of the seamen who swore that whenever and wherever descried; at however remote times, or in however far apart latitudes and longitudes, that unnearable spout was cast by one self-same whale; and that whale, Moby Dick. For a time, there reigned, too, a sense of peculiar dread at this flitting apparition, as if it were treacherously beckoning us on and on, in order that the monster might turn round upon us, and rend us at last in the remotest and most savage seas.

These temporary apprehensions, so vague but so awful, derived a wondrous potency from the contrasting serenity of the weather, in which, beneath all its blue blandness, some thought there lurked a devilish charm, as for days and days we voyaged along, through seas so wearily, lonesomely mild, that all space, in repugnance to our vengeful errand, seemed vacating itself of life before our urn-like prow.

But, at last, when turning to the eastward, the Cape winds began howling around us, and we rose and fell upon the long, troubled seas that are there; when the ivory-tusked Pequod sharply bowed to the blast, and gored the dark waves in her madness, till, like showers of silver chips, the foam-flakes flew over her bulwarks; then all this desolate vacuity of life went away, but gave place to sights more dismal than before.

Close to our bows, strange forms in the water darted hither and thither before us; while thick in our rear flew the inscrutable sea-ravens. And every morning, perched on our stays, rows of these birds were seen; and spite of our hootings, for a long time obstinately clung to the hemp, as though they deemed our ship some drifting, uninhabited craft; a thing appointed to desolation, and therefore fit roosting-place for their homeless selves. And heaved and heaved, still unrestingly heaved the black sea, as if its vast tides were a conscience; and the great mundane soul were in anguish and remorse for the long sin and suffering it had bred.

Cape of Good Hope, do they call ye? Rather Cape Tormentoso, as called of yore; for long allured by the perfidious silences that before had attended us, we found ourselves launched into this tormented sea, where guilty beings transformed into those fowls and these fish, seemed condemned to swim on everlastingly without any haven in store, or beat that black air without any horizon. But calm, snow-white, and unvarying; still directing its fountain of feathers to the sky; still beckoning us on from before, the solitary jet would at times be descried.

During all this blackness of the elements, Ahab, though assuming for the time the almost continual command of the drenched and dangerous deck, manifested the gloomiest reserve; and more seldom than ever addressed his mates. In tempestuous times like these, after everything above and aloft has been secured, nothing more can be done but passively to await the issue of the gale. Then Captain and crew become practical fatalists. So, with his ivory leg inserted into its accustomed hole, and with one hand firmly grasping a shroud, Ahab for hours and hours would stand gazing dead to windward, while an occasional squall of sleet or snow would all but congeal his very eyelashes together. Meantime, the crew driven from the forward part of the ship by the perilous seas that burstingly broke over its bows, stood in a line along the bulwarks in the waist; and the better to guard against the leaping waves, each man had slipped himself into a sort of bowline secured to the rail, in which he swung as in a loosened belt. Few or no words were spoken; and the silent ship, as if manned by painted sailors in wax, day after day tore on through all the swift madness and gladness of the demoniac waves. By night the same muteness of humanity before the shrieks of the ocean prevailed; still in silence the men swung in the bowlines; still wordless Ahab stood up to the blast. Even when wearied nature seemed demanding repose he would not seek that repose in his hammock. Never could Starbuck forget the old man's aspect, when one night going down into the cabin to mark how the barometer stood, he saw him with closed eyes sitting straight in his floor-screwed chair; the rain and half-melted sleet of the storm from which he had some time before emerged, still slowly dripping from the unremoved hat and coat. On the table beside him lay unrolled one of those charts of tides and currents which have previously been spoken of. His lantern swung from his tightly clenched hand. Though the body was erect, the head was thrown back so that the closed eyes were pointed towards the needle of the tell-tale that swung from a beam in the ceiling.*

*The cabin-compass is called the tell-tale, because without going to the compass at the helm, the Captain, while below, can inform himself of the course of the ship.

Terrible old man! thought Starbuck with a shudder, sleeping in this gale, still thou steadfastly eyest thy purpose.





CHAPTER 52. The Albatross.

South-eastward from the Cape, off the distant Crozetts, a good cruising ground for Right Whalemen, a sail loomed ahead, the Goney (Albatross) by name. As she slowly drew nigh, from my lofty perch at the fore-mast-head, I had a good view of that sight so remarkable to a tyro in the far ocean fisheries—a whaler at sea, and long absent from home.

As if the waves had been fullers, this craft was bleached like the skeleton of a stranded walrus. All down her sides, this spectral appearance was traced with long channels of reddened rust, while all her spars and her rigging were like the thick branches of trees furred over with hoar-frost. Only her lower sails were set. A wild sight it was to see her long-bearded look-outs at those three mast-heads. They seemed clad in the skins of beasts, so torn and bepatched the raiment that had survived nearly four years of cruising. Standing in iron hoops nailed to the mast, they swayed and swung over a fathomless sea; and though, when the ship slowly glided close under our stern, we six men in the air came so nigh to each other that we might almost have leaped from the mast-heads of one ship to those of the other; yet, those forlorn-looking fishermen, mildly eyeing us as they passed, said not one word to our own look-outs, while the quarter-deck hail was being heard from below.

“Ship ahoy! Have ye seen the White Whale?”

But as the strange captain, leaning over the pallid bulwarks, was in the act of putting his trumpet to his mouth, it somehow fell from his hand into the sea; and the wind now rising amain, he in vain strove to make himself heard without it. Meantime his ship was still increasing the distance between. While in various silent ways the seamen of the Pequod were evincing their observance of this ominous incident at the first mere mention of the White Whale's name to another ship, Ahab for a moment paused; it almost seemed as though he would have lowered a boat to board the stranger, had not the threatening wind forbade. But taking advantage of his windward position, he again seized his trumpet, and knowing by her aspect that the stranger vessel was a Nantucketer and shortly bound home, he loudly hailed—“Ahoy there! This is the Pequod, bound round the world! Tell them to address all future letters to the Pacific ocean! and this time three years, if I am not at home, tell them to address them to ——”

At that moment the two wakes were fairly crossed, and instantly, then, in accordance with their singular ways, shoals of small harmless fish, that for some days before had been placidly swimming by our side, darted away with what seemed shuddering fins, and ranged themselves fore and aft with the stranger's flanks. Though in the course of his continual voyagings Ahab must often before have noticed a similar sight, yet, to any monomaniac man, the veriest trifles capriciously carry meanings.

“Swim away from me, do ye?” murmured Ahab, gazing over into the water. There seemed but little in the words, but the tone conveyed more of deep helpless sadness than the insane old man had ever before evinced. But turning to the steersman, who thus far had been holding the ship in the wind to diminish her headway, he cried out in his old lion voice,—“Up helm! Keep her off round the world!”

Round the world! There is much in that sound to inspire proud feelings; but whereto does all that circumnavigation conduct? Only through numberless perils to the very point whence we started, where those that we left behind secure, were all the time before us.

Were this world an endless plain, and by sailing eastward we could for ever reach new distances, and discover sights more sweet and strange than any Cyclades or Islands of King Solomon, then there were promise in the voyage. But in pursuit of those far mysteries we dream of, or in tormented chase of that demon phantom that, some time or other, swims before all human hearts; while chasing such over this round globe, they either lead us on in barren mazes or midway leave us whelmed.
