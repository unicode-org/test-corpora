POGLAVJE 89. Hitra in ohlapna riba.

Aluzija na waif in waif-pole v predzadnjem poglavju zahteva nekaj poročila o zakonih in predpisih kitovskega ribolova, pri katerih se waif lahko šteje za veliki simbol in značko.

Pogosto se zgodi, da ko pluje več ladij v družbi, kita lahko zadene ena ladja, nato pobegne in ga nazadnje ubije in ujame drugo ladjo; in tukaj so posredno vključeni številni manjši nepredvideni dogodki, ki so vsi deležni te ene velike značilnosti. Na primer - po napornem in nevarnem lovu in ujetju kita se lahko truplo zaradi močne nevihte osvobodi z ladje; in odtava daleč stran v zavetrje, ga ponovno ujame drugi kitolov, ki ga v mirnem, tesnem položaju vleče ob bok, brez tveganja za življenje ali vrvico. Tako bi med ribiči pogosto prihajalo do najbolj nadležnih in nasilnih sporov, če ne bi obstajalo neko pisano ali nenapisano, univerzalno, nesporno pravo, ki bi veljalo za vse primere.

Morda je bil edini formalni kitolovski kodeks, odobren z zakonodajo, nizozemski. Leta 1695. leta n. št. so ga odredili generalni zvezni državi. Toda čeprav nobena druga država ni imela pisnega zakona o kitolovu, so bili ameriški ribiči v tej zadevi sami sebi zakonodajalci in odvetniki. Zagotovili so sistem, ki po jedrnati izčrpnosti presega Justinijanove pandekte in statut kitajske družbe za zatiranje vmešavanja v posle drugih ljudi. Da; te zakone bi lahko vgravirali na faring kraljice Ane ali na bodico harpune in jih nosili okoli vratu, tako majhni so.

I. Fast-Fish pripada skupini, ki je hitra.

II. Loose-Fish je poštena igra za vsakogar, ki jo lahko čim prej ulovi.

Toda tisto, kar je s tem mojstrskim kodeksom v škodo, je njegova občudovanja vredna kratkost, ki zahteva ogromno komentarjev, da bi ga razložili.

Prvič: Kaj je Fast-Fish? Živa ali mrtva riba je tehnično hitra, ko je povezana z zasedeno ladjo ali čolnom s kakršnim koli medijem, ki ga lahko nadzoruje potnik ali potniki – jambor, veslo, 9-palčni kabel, telegrafska žica ali pramen pajčevine, vse je isto. Podobno je riba tehnično hitra, ko nosi waif ali kateri koli drug priznan simbol posesti; dokler stranka, ki se ji odpoveduje, jasno dokaže svojo sposobnost, da jo kadar koli vzame zraven, pa tudi svoj namen, da to stori.

To so znanstveni komentarji; toda komentarji samih kitolovcev so včasih sestavljeni iz težkih besed in močnejših udarcev - Coca-Cola-upon-Littleton iz pesti. Res je, da se med bolj poštenimi in častnimi kitolovci vedno upoštevajo posebni primeri, ko bi bila nezaslišana moralna krivica, če bi ena stran zahtevala lastništvo kita, ki ga je prej preganjala ali ubila druga stran. Toda drugi nikakor niso tako skrbni.

Pred kakšnimi petdesetimi leti je bil v Angliji nenavaden primer kitopotovalca, v katerem so tožniki trdili, da so po hudem lovu na kita v severnih morjih; in kdaj jim je (tožnikom) res uspelo s harpuno uloviti ribo; končno so bili zaradi nevarnosti svojega življenja prisiljeni zapustiti ne le svoje vrvi, ampak tudi svoj čoln. Nazadnje so tožene stranke (posadka druge ladje) prišle do kita, ga udarile, ubile, zasegle in si ga nazadnje prisvojile pred očmi tožnikov. In ko so tem obtožencem očitali, je njihov kapitan tožnikom tlesknil s prsti v zobe in jim zagotovil, da bo v skladu z doksologijo dejanja, ki ga je storil, zdaj obdržal njihovo vrvico, harpune in čoln, ki sta ostala pritrjena kitu ob zasegu. Zato so tožniki zdaj zahtevali izterjavo vrednosti svojega kita, vrvi, harpun in čolna.

G. Erskine je bil zagovornik obtoženih; Lord Ellenborough je bil sodnik. Med zagovorom je duhoviti Erskine svoje stališče ponazoril z namigom na nedavni zločin. con. primer, v katerem je gospod, potem ko je zaman poskušal brzdati ženino zlobnost, jo končno zapustil na morju življenja; toda čez leta je, ker se je tega koraka pokesal, vložil tožbo, da bi jo povrnil v posest. Erskine je bil na drugi strani; in nato je to podprl z besedami, da čeprav je gospod prvotno harpuniral gospo in jo je nekoč postil, jo je končno zapustil le zaradi velikega stresa njene potapljajoče hudobije; vendar jo je zapustil, tako da je postala spuščena riba; in zato, ko jo je kasnejši gospod ponovno harpuniral, je gospa postala last tega kasnejšega gospoda, skupaj s kakršno koli harpuno, ki je bila morda ugotovljena, da se je zataknila vanjo.

V tem primeru je Erskine trdil, da sta primera kita in dame recipročno ponazarjala drug drugega.

Te vloge in nasprotne vloge, ki so bile pravilno zaslišane, je zelo učeni sodnik pod določenimi pogoji odločil, da je, da je čoln dodelil tožnikom, ker so ga le zapustili, da bi rešili svoja življenja; toda kar zadeva spornega kita, harpune in vrvico, so pripadali tožencem; kita, ker je bil v času končnega ujetja neodvisna riba; in harpune in vrvico, ker ko je riba pobegnila z njimi, je (riba) pridobila lastnino v teh predmetih; in zato je imel vsakdo, ki je ribe pozneje vzel, pravico do njih. Zdaj so obtoženci nato vzeli ribo; torej so bili prej omenjeni členi njihovi.

Običajni človek, ki gleda na to odločitev zelo učenega sodnika, bi ji lahko ugovarjal. Toda preorano do primarne kamnine zadeve, dve veliki načeli, določeni v prej citiranih zakonih o dvojnem kitolovu, ki jih je uporabil in razjasnil Lord Ellenborough v zgoraj citiranem primeru; pravim, da bosta ta dva zakona, ki se dotikata hitrih in ohlapnih rib, po premisleku ugotovila kot temelj vse človeške sodne prakse; kajti ne glede na svojo zapleteno kiparsko risbo ima tempelj zakona, tako kot tempelj Filistejcev, le dva opornika, na katerih stoji.

Ali ni rek v vseh ustih: Posest je polovica zakona: torej ne glede na to, kako je stvar prišla v posest? Toda pogosto je posest ves zakon. Kaj so žile in duše ruskih podložnikov in republikanskih sužnjev kot Fast-Fish, katerih posest je cel zakon? Kaj je za grabežljivega veleposestnika vdova zadnja lepša kot Hitra riba? Kaj je tam neodkriti zlobnežev marmorni dvorec z vratno ploščo za klošarja; kaj je to kot Fast-Fish? Kakšen je uničujoč popust, ki ga Mordecai, posrednik, dobi od ubogega Woebegoneja, bankrota, na posojilo, da bi Woebegonejeva družina obvarovala lakote; kaj je ta uničujoč popust kot Fast-Fish? Kakšen je dohodek nadškofa Savesoula v višini 100.000 GBP, zaseženih od pičlega kruha in sira na stotine tisočev delavcev z zlomljenim hrbtom (vsi so prepričani v nebesa brez Savesoulove pomoči), kaj je teh okroglih 100.000 GBP kot hitra riba? Kaj so dedna mesta in zaselki vojvode Dunderskega kot Fast-Fish? Kaj je za tistega dvomljivega harpunarja, Johna Bulla, uboga Irska kot Hitra riba? Kaj je za tistega apostolskega lovca, brata Jonathana, Teksas kot hitra riba? In glede vsega tega, ali ni posest ves zakon?

Toda če je doktrina hitre ribe uporabna precej splošno, je sorodna doktrina ohlapne ribe še vedno bolj uporabna. To je mednarodno in univerzalno uporabno.

Kaj je bila Amerika leta 1492 kot nespametna riba, v kateri je Kolumb udaril po španskem standardu tako, da se ga je odrekel svojemu kraljevemu gospodarju in ljubici? Kaj je bila Poljska za carja? Kakšna Grčija Turku? Kakšna Indija do Anglije? Kaj bo končno Mehika za Združene države? Vse Loose-Fish.

Kaj so človekove pravice in svoboščine sveta razen lovskih rib? Kakšne vse moške misli in mnenja razen Loose-Fish? Kaj je pri njih načelo religioznega verovanja kot nerazgibana riba? Kaj so za bahave tihotapske verbaliste misli mislecev kot Loose-Fish? Kaj je sam veliki globus kot le riba? In kaj si ti, bralec, če ne ohlapna in hitra riba?

»Oh, nič! To je voščen nos; Moram ga zadržati. Lep dan, kajne? Rekel bi, da je zrak precej vrtnarski; vrzi nam šop posijev, boš, Bouton-de-Rose?«

"Kaj za vraga hočeš tukaj?" je zarjovel Guernseyman, ki ga je nenadoma prevzela strast.

»Oh! ohlajati se — ohladiti? ja, to je ta beseda! zakaj teh kitov ne zapakiraš v led, medtem ko jih delaš? A vendarle šalo na stran; ali veš, Rose-bud, da je vse nesmiselno poskušati dobiti olje iz takih kitov? Kar se tiče tistega posušenega, tam, v celem trupu nima škrg.”

»To dovolj dobro vem; ampak, vidiš, kapitan tega ne bo verjel; to je njegovo prvo potovanje; prej je bil kölnski proizvajalec. Toda vkrcaj se in morda ti bo verjel, če meni ne bo; in tako se bom rešil iz tega umazanega škrapa.”

"Vse, da bi te zadovoljil, moj sladki in prijetni kolega," se je pridružil Stubbu in s tem se je kmalu povzpel na krov. Tam se je prikazala queer scena. Mornarji, v kapah z resicami iz rdečega kamgana, so dobivali težke pripomočke v pripravljenosti za kite. Vendar sta delala precej počasi in se pogovarjala zelo hitro ter bila videti vse prej kot dobro razpoložena. Vsi njihovi nosovi so štrleli navzgor iz njihovih obrazov kot številni jib-boomi. Tu in tam so pari pustili svoje delo in tekli do vrha jambora na svež zrak. Nekateri, ki so mislili, da se bodo okužili s kugo, so hrast pomočili v premogov katran in ga občasno prinesli k svojim nosnicam. Drugi, ki so skoraj do konca odlomili peclje svojih pip pri skledi, so močno pihali tobačni dim, tako da je ves čas polnil njihove vonjave.

Stubba je zadela ploha vpitja in anatem, ki je prihajala iz kapitanove okrogle hiše na krmi; in pogledal v to smer, je videl ognjevit obraz, ki se je sunil izza vrat, ki so bila od znotraj priprta. To je bil trpinčeni kirurg, ki se je po zaman ugovarjanju proti takratnim postopkom odpravil v kapitanovo okroglo hišo ( kabinet , ki jo je imenoval), da bi se izognil škodljivcu; a kljub temu si včasih ni mogel pomagati, da bi vpil svoje roteče in ogorčene.

Ko je vse to označil, se je Stubb dobro zagovarjal v prid svojemu načrtu in se obrnil k možakarju z Guernseyja z njim malo poklepetal, med katerim je neznanec izrazil sovraštvo do svojega kapitana kot domišljavega nevedneža, ki jih je vse spravil v tako neokusno in nedonosna kisla kumarica. Stubb ga je skrbno preizkušal in ugotovil, da mož z Guernseyja nima niti najmanjšega suma glede ambre. Zato je o tej glavi molčal, sicer pa je bil z njim precej odkrit in zaupen, tako da sta hitro skovala majhen načrt, kako obiti kapitana in ga osramotiti, ne da bi se mu sploh sanjalo, da bi dvomil o njuni iskrenosti. V skladu s tem njihovim majhnim načrtom naj bi Guernsey-man pod krinko tolmačeve pisarne povedal kapitanu, kar hoče, vendar kot da prihaja od Stubba; in kar zadeva Stubba, je moral izreči vsako neumnost, ki bi mu med intervjujem padla na glavo.

V tem času se je njihova usojena žrtev pojavila iz njegove kabine. Bil je majhen in temen, vendar precej občutljiv moški za morskega kapitana, vendar z velikimi brki in brki; in je nosil rdeč bombažno žametni telovnik s pečati za uro ob strani. Temu gospodu je Stubba sedaj vljudno predstavil mož z Guernseyja, ki je takoj razmetljivo dal vidik tolmačenja med njima.

"Kaj naj mu najprej rečem?" je rekel.

"Zakaj," je rekel Stubb, opazujoč žametni jopič, uro in pečate, "lahko začneš tako, da mu poveš, da se mi zdi nekako bebav, čeprav se ne pretvarjam, da sem sodnik."

»Pravi, gospod,« je rekel guernseyjanec v francoščini in se obrnil k svojemu kapitanu, »da je njegova ladja šele včeraj govorila o ladji, katere kapitan in prvi častnik s šestimi mornarji sta umrla zaradi vročine razstreljenega kita, ki so ga prinesli zraven.«

Na to je kapitan začel in si je vneto želel izvedeti več.

"Kaj zdaj?" je rekel igralec z Guernseyja Stubbu.

»Zakaj, ko to sprejema tako lahkotno, mu povej, da zdaj, ko sem ga natančno opazoval, sem povsem prepričan, da ni nič bolj primeren za poveljevanje kitove kot opica St. Jago. Pravzaprav mu povej od mene, da je pavijan.

»Priseže in izjavi, gospod, da je drugi kit, tisti posušeni, veliko bolj smrtonosen od razstreljenega; v redu, gospod, on nam pričara, ker cenimo svoja življenja, da se odrežemo tem ribam.«

Kapitan je takoj stekel naprej in z glasnim glasom zapovedal svoji posadki, naj ne dvignejo rezil in nemudoma odvržejo kable in verige, ki so kite omejevale na ladjo.

"Kaj zdaj?" je rekel mož z Guernseyja, ko se je kapitan vrnil k njim.

»Zakaj, da vidim; da, prav tako mu lahko zdaj poveš, da – to – pravzaprav, povej mu, da sem ga zavajal, in (razen njega samega) morda še komu.«

"Pravi, gospod, da je zelo vesel, da nam je kakor koli pomagal."

Ko je to slišal, je kapitan obljubil, da sta hvaležni stranki (kar pomeni on in kolega), in zaključil s povabilom Stubba v svojo kabino, da bi spil steklenico Bordeauxa.

»Želi, da s seboj vzameš kozarec vina,« je rekel tolmač.

»Hvala mu srčno; ampak povej mu, da je proti mojim načelom, da pijem s človekom, ki sem ga omagal. Pravzaprav mu povej, da moram iti.

»Pravi, gospod, da njegova načela ne priznavajo, da pije; če pa hoče gospod preživeti še en dan za pijačo, potem je najbolje, da spusti vse štiri čolne in umakne ladjo stran od teh kitov, saj je tako mirna, da ne bodo odplavali.«

V tem času je bil Stubb že čez bok in vstopil v svoj čoln, pozdravil človeka z Guernseyja v tem smislu, da ima dolgo vlečno vrv v svojem čolnu in bo naredil vse, kar je v njegovi moči, da bi jim pomagal, tako da bi izvlekel lažji kit od obeh z ladijskega boka. Medtem ko so Francozovi čolni nato vlekli ladjo v eno smer, je Stubb dobrodušno odvlekel svojega kita v drugo smer, pri čemer je navidezno popustil zelo nenavadno dolgo vlečno vrv.

Zdaj je zapihal vetrič; Stubb se je pretvarjal, da je odvrgel kita; dvignil svoje čolne, je Francoz kmalu povečal razdaljo, medtem ko je Pequod zdrsnil med njega in Stubbovega kita. Nato je Stubb hitro potegnil do lebdečega telesa in klical Pequod, da ga obvesti o njegovih namerah, takoj pa je začel žeti sadove svoje nepravične zvitosti. Zgrabil je svojo ostro lopato za čoln in začel izkopavati telo, malo za stransko plavutjo. Skoraj bi bil mislil, da koplje tam v morju klet; in ko je njegova lopata končno udarila ob mršava rebra, je bilo, kot da bi dvignil stare rimske ploščice in lončenino, zakopano v debeli angleški ilovici. Posadka njegovega čolna je bila vsa navdušena, vneto je pomagala svojemu poglavarju in bila videti zaskrbljena kot lovci na zlato.

In ves čas je nešteto kokoši potapljalo, se sklanjalo, kričalo, vpilo in se borilo okoli njih. Stubb je bil videti razočaran, še posebej, ko se je grozljivo brstenje povečalo, ko se je nenadoma iz samega srca te kuge ukradel šibek curek parfuma, ki je tekel skozi plimo neprijetnih vonjav, ne da bi ga absorbiral, kot ena reka. bo stekel v in nato skupaj z drugim, ne da bi se za nekaj časa sploh zlil z njim.

»Imam jo, imam jo,« je veselo zaklical Stubb in udarjal po nečem v podzemlju, »torbico! torbica!"

Odložil je lopato, potisnil obe roki in izvlekel prgišča nečesa, kar je bilo videti kot zrelo milo Windsor ali bogat pegast stari sir; zelo mastno in okusno. Z lahkoto ga lahko udrnete s palcem; je v odtenku med rumeno in pepelnato barvo. In to, dobri prijatelji, je ambra, vredna zlata gvineja za unčo za vsakega lekarnarja. Dobilo se je kakih šest prgišč; toda več se je neizogibno izgubilo v morju in še več bi morda bilo zavarovanega, če ne bi nestrpni Ahab glasno ukazal Stubbu, naj preneha in se vkrca, sicer bi se ladja poslovila od njih.





POGLAVJE 92. Ambra.

Ta ambra je zelo nenavadna snov in tako pomembna kot komercialni artikel, da so leta 1791 v baru angleškega spodnjega doma na to temo pregledali nekega kapetana Coffina, rojenega v Nantucketu. Kajti v tistem času in res do sorazmerno poznega dne je natančen izvor ambre ostal, tako kot jantar sam, problem za učence. Čeprav je beseda ambra le francoska spojina za sivi jantar, sta obe snovi precej različni. Kajti jantar, čeprav ga včasih najdemo na morski obali, kopljejo tudi v nekaterih tleh daleč v notranjosti, medtem ko ambro ne najdemo nikoli razen na morju. Poleg tega je jantar trda, prozorna, krhka snov brez vonja, ki se uporablja za ustnike do pip, za kroglice in okraske; vendar je ambra mehka, voskasta in tako zelo dišeča in začinjena, da se v veliki meri uporablja v parfumeriji, v pastilah, dragocenih svečah, prahu za lase in pomatumu. Turki ga uporabljajo v kulinariki in ga nosijo tudi v Meko, za isti namen, kot kadilo nosijo k sv. Petru v Rim. Nekateri trgovci z vinom spustijo nekaj zrn v klaret, da ga aromatizirajo.

Kdo bi si torej mislil, da bi se tako fine dame in gospodje lahko posladkali z esenco, najdeno v neslavnem črevesju bolnega kita! Pa vendar je tako. Po nekaterih naj bi bila ambra vzrok, po drugih posledica dispepsije pri kitu. Težko je bilo reči, kako ozdraviti takšno dispepsijo, razen s tremi ali štirimi tovori čolnov Brandrethovih tablet, nato pa zmanjkalo nevarnosti, kot delavci delajo pri razstreljevanju kamnov.

Pozabil sem povedati, da so v tej ambri našli določene trde, okrogle, koščene plošče, za katere je Stubb sprva mislil, da bi lahko bili gumbi mornarskih hlač; vendar se je kasneje izkazalo, da niso nič drugega kot koščki majhnih lignjevih kosti, balzamiranih na ta način.

Zdaj, ko bi morala biti v srcu takšnega razpada najdena netrohljivost te najbolj dišeče ambre; je to nič? Pomislite na izrek svetega Pavla v Korinčanom o pokvarjenosti in nepokvarjenosti; kako smo posejani v nečasti, vstali pa v slavi. In prav tako se spomnite Paracelzusovega izreka o tem, kaj je tisto, kar naredi najboljši mošus. Ne pozabite tudi na nenavadno dejstvo, da je od vseh stvari slabega okusa kolonjska voda v svojih osnovnih proizvodnih fazah najslabša.

I should like to conclude the chapter with the above appeal, but cannot, owing to my anxiety to repel a charge often made against whalemen, and which, in the estimation of some already biased minds, might be considered as indirectly substantiated by what has been said of the Frenchman's two whales. Elsewhere in this volume the slanderous aspersion has been disproved, that the vocation of whaling is throughout a slatternly, untidy business. But there is another thing to rebut. They hint that all whales always smell bad. Now how did this odious stigma originate?

I opine, that it is plainly traceable to the first arrival of the Greenland whaling ships in London, more than two centuries ago. Because those whalemen did not then, and do not now, try out their oil at sea as the Southern ships have always done; but cutting up the fresh blubber in small bits, thrust it through the bung holes of large casks, and carry it home in that manner; the shortness of the season in those Icy Seas, and the sudden and violent storms to which they are exposed, forbidding any other course. The consequence is, that upon breaking into the hold, and unloading one of these whale cemeteries, in the Greenland dock, a savor is given forth somewhat similar to that arising from excavating an old city grave-yard, for the foundations of a Lying-in Hospital.

I partly surmise also, that this wicked charge against whalers may be likewise imputed to the existence on the coast of Greenland, in former times, of a Dutch village called Schmerenburgh or Smeerenberg, which latter name is the one used by the learned Fogo Von Slack, in his great work on Smells, a text-book on that subject. As its name imports (smeer, fat; berg, to put up), this village was founded in order to afford a place for the blubber of the Dutch whale fleet to be tried out, without being taken home to Holland for that purpose. It was a collection of furnaces, fat-kettles, and oil sheds; and when the works were in full operation certainly gave forth no very pleasant savor. But all this is quite different with a South Sea Sperm Whaler; which in a voyage of four years perhaps, after completely filling her hold with oil, does not, perhaps, consume fifty days in the business of boiling out; and in the state that it is casked, the oil is nearly scentless. The truth is, that living or dead, if but decently treated, whales as a species are by no means creatures of ill odor; nor can whalemen be recognised, as the people of the middle ages affected to detect a Jew in the company, by the nose. Nor indeed can the whale possibly be otherwise than fragrant, when, as a general thing, he enjoys such high health; taking abundance of exercise; always out of doors; though, it is true, seldom in the open air. I say, that the motion of a Sperm Whale's flukes above water dispenses a perfume, as when a musk-scented lady rustles her dress in a warm parlor. What then shall I liken the Sperm Whale to for fragrance, considering his magnitude? Must it not be to that famous elephant, with jewelled tusks, and redolent with myrrh, which was led out of an Indian town to do honor to Alexander the Great?





CHAPTER 93. The Castaway.

It was but some few days after encountering the Frenchman, that a most significant event befell the most insignificant of the Pequod's crew; an event most lamentable; and which ended in providing the sometimes madly merry and predestinated craft with a living and ever accompanying prophecy of whatever shattered sequel might prove her own.

Now, in the whale ship, it is not every one that goes in the boats. Some few hands are reserved called ship-keepers, whose province it is to work the vessel while the boats are pursuing the whale. As a general thing, these ship-keepers are as hardy fellows as the men comprising the boats' crews. But if there happen to be an unduly slender, clumsy, or timorous wight in the ship, that wight is certain to be made a ship-keeper. It was so in the Pequod with the little negro Pippin by nick-name, Pip by abbreviation. Poor Pip! ye have heard of him before; ye must remember his tambourine on that dramatic midnight, so gloomy-jolly.

In outer aspect, Pip and Dough-Boy made a match, like a black pony and a white one, of equal developments, though of dissimilar colour, driven in one eccentric span. But while hapless Dough-Boy was by nature dull and torpid in his intellects, Pip, though over tender-hearted, was at bottom very bright, with that pleasant, genial, jolly brightness peculiar to his tribe; a tribe, which ever enjoy all holidays and festivities with finer, freer relish than any other race. For blacks, the year's calendar should show naught but three hundred and sixty-five Fourth of Julys and New Year's Days. Nor smile so, while I write that this little black was brilliant, for even blackness has its brilliancy; behold yon lustrous ebony, panelled in king's cabinets. But Pip loved life, and all life's peaceable securities; so that the panic-striking business in which he had somehow unaccountably become entrapped, had most sadly blurred his brightness; though, as ere long will be seen, what was thus temporarily subdued in him, in the end was destined to be luridly illumined by strange wild fires, that fictitiously showed him off to ten times the natural lustre with which in his native Tolland County in Connecticut, he had once enlivened many a fiddler's frolic on the green; and at melodious even-tide, with his gay ha-ha! had turned the round horizon into one star-belled tambourine. So, though in the clear air of day, suspended against a blue-veined neck, the pure-watered diamond drop will healthful glow; yet, when the cunning jeweller would show you the diamond in its most impressive lustre, he lays it against a gloomy ground, and then lights it up, not by the sun, but by some unnatural gases. Then come out those fiery effulgences, infernally superb; then the evil-blazing diamond, once the divinest symbol of the crystal skies, looks like some crown-jewel stolen from the King of Hell. But let us to the story.

It came to pass, that in the ambergris affair Stubb's after-oarsman chanced so to sprain his hand, as for a time to become quite maimed; and, temporarily, Pip was put into his place.

The first time Stubb lowered with him, Pip evinced much nervousness; but happily, for that time, escaped close contact with the whale; and therefore came off not altogether discreditably; though Stubb observing him, took care, afterwards, to exhort him to cherish his courageousness to the utmost, for he might often find it needful.

Now upon the second lowering, the boat paddled upon the whale; and as the fish received the darted iron, it gave its customary rap, which happened, in this instance, to be right under poor Pip's seat. The involuntary consternation of the moment caused him to leap, paddle in hand, out of the boat; and in such a way, that part of the slack whale line coming against his chest, he breasted it overboard with him, so as to become entangled in it, when at last plumping into the water. That instant the stricken whale started on a fierce run, the line swiftly straightened; and presto! poor Pip came all foaming up to the chocks of the boat, remorselessly dragged there by the line, which had taken several turns around his chest and neck.

Tashtego stood in the bows. He was full of the fire of the hunt. He hated Pip for a poltroon. Snatching the boat-knife from its sheath, he suspended its sharp edge over the line, and turning towards Stubb, exclaimed interrogatively, “Cut?” Meantime Pip's blue, choked face plainly looked, Do, for God's sake! All passed in a flash. In less than half a minute, this entire thing happened.

“Damn him, cut!” roared Stubb; and so the whale was lost and Pip was saved.

So soon as he recovered himself, the poor little negro was assailed by yells and execrations from the crew. Tranquilly permitting these irregular cursings to evaporate, Stubb then in a plain, business-like, but still half humorous manner, cursed Pip officially; and that done, unofficially gave him much wholesome advice. The substance was, Never jump from a boat, Pip, except—but all the rest was indefinite, as the soundest advice ever is. Now, in general, Stick to the boat , is your true motto in whaling; but cases will sometimes happen when Leap from the boat , is still better. Moreover, as if perceiving at last that if he should give undiluted conscientious advice to Pip, he would be leaving him too wide a margin to jump in for the future; Stubb suddenly dropped all advice, and concluded with a peremptory command, “Stick to the boat, Pip, or by the Lord, I won't pick you up if you jump; mind that. We can't afford to lose whales by the likes of you; a whale would sell for thirty times what you would, Pip, in Alabama. Bear that in mind, and don't jump any more.” Hereby perhaps Stubb indirectly hinted, that though man loved his fellow, yet man is a money-making animal, which propensity too often interferes with his benevolence.

But we are all in the hands of the Gods; and Pip jumped again. It was under very similar circumstances to the first performance; but this time he did not breast out the line; and hence, when the whale started to run, Pip was left behind on the sea, like a hurried traveller's trunk. Alas! Stubb was but too true to his word. It was a beautiful, bounteous, blue day; the spangled sea calm and cool, and flatly stretching away, all round, to the horizon, like gold-beater's skin hammered out to the extremest. Bobbing up and down in that sea, Pip's ebon head showed like a head of cloves. No boat-knife was lifted when he fell so rapidly astern. Stubb's inexorable back was turned upon him; and the whale was winged. In three minutes, a whole mile of shoreless ocean was between Pip and Stubb. Out from the centre of the sea, poor Pip turned his crisp, curling, black head to the sun, another lonely castaway, though the loftiest and the brightest.

Now, in calm weather, to swim in the open ocean is as easy to the practised swimmer as to ride in a spring-carriage ashore. But the awful lonesomeness is intolerable. The intense concentration of self in the middle of such a heartless immensity, my God! who can tell it? Mark, how when sailors in a dead calm bathe in the open sea—mark how closely they hug their ship and only coast along her sides.

But had Stubb really abandoned the poor little negro to his fate? No; he did not mean to, at least. Because there were two boats in his wake, and he supposed, no doubt, that they would of course come up to Pip very quickly, and pick him up; though, indeed, such considerations towards oarsmen jeopardized through their own timidity, is not always manifested by the hunters in all similar instances; and such instances not unfrequently occur; almost invariably in the fishery, a coward, so called, is marked with the same ruthless detestation peculiar to military navies and armies.

But it so happened, that those boats, without seeing Pip, suddenly spying whales close to them on one side, turned, and gave chase; and Stubb's boat was now so far away, and he and all his crew so intent upon his fish, that Pip's ringed horizon began to expand around him miserably. By the merest chance the ship itself at last rescued him; but from that hour the little negro went about the deck an idiot; such, at least, they said he was. The sea had jeeringly kept his finite body up, but drowned the infinite of his soul. Not drowned entirely, though. Rather carried down alive to wondrous depths, where strange shapes of the unwarped primal world glided to and fro before his passive eyes; and the miser-merman, Wisdom, revealed his hoarded heaps; and among the joyous, heartless, ever-juvenile eternities, Pip saw the multitudinous, God-omnipresent, coral insects, that out of the firmament of waters heaved the colossal orbs. He saw God's foot upon the treadle of the loom, and spoke it; and therefore his shipmates called him mad. So man's insanity is heaven's sense; and wandering from all mortal reason, man comes at last to that celestial thought, which, to reason, is absurd and frantic; and weal or woe, feels then uncompromised, indifferent as his God.

For the rest, blame not Stubb too hardly. The thing is common in that fishery; and in the sequel of the narrative, it will then be seen what like abandonment befell myself.
