POGLAVJE 53. Gam.

Domnevni razlog, zakaj Ahab ni šel na krov kitolovca, o katerem smo govorili, je bil ta: veter in morje sta napovedovala nevihte. A tudi če temu ne bi bilo tako, se morda kljub vsemu ne bi vkrcal nanjo – sodeč po njegovem poznejšem vedenju ob podobnih priložnostih – če bi bilo tako, da je s postopkom klicanja dobil negativen odgovor vprašanje, ki ga je postavil. Kajti, kot se je sčasoma izkazalo, mu ni bilo mar, da bi se družil, niti za pet minut, s katerim koli neznanim kapitanom, razen če bi lahko prispeval nekaj informacij, ki jih je tako zavzeto iskal. Toda vse to bi lahko ostalo neustrezno ocenjeno, če tukaj ne bi bilo omenjeno nekaj posebnega načina uporabe kitolovskih ladij, ko se srečujejo v tujih morjih, zlasti na skupnem križarjenju.

Če dva tujca prečkata Pine Barrens v zvezni državi New York ali enako pusto Salisbury Plain v Angliji; če se naključno srečata v tako negostoljubnih divjinah, se ta dva za življenje ne moreta izogniti medsebojnemu pozdravu; in se za trenutek ustavimo, da izmenjamo novice; in morda za nekaj časa sedeti in skupaj počivati: potem je koliko bolj naravno, da se na brezmejnih morskih ravninah Pine Barrens in Salisbury Plains dve kitolovski ladji opazujeta na koncu sveta – pred osamljenim Fanningovim otokom. , ali oddaljeni Kraljevi mlini; koliko bolj naravno, pravim, da bi te ladje v takšnih okoliščinah ne le izmenjale toče, ampak stopile v še tesnejši, bolj prijateljski in družaben stik. Zdi se, da je to še posebej samoumevno v primeru plovil, ki so v lasti enega morskega pristanišča in katerih kapitani, častniki in nemalo moških se med seboj osebno poznajo; in posledično imajo vse vrste dragih domačih stvari za pogovor.

Za dolgo odsotno ladjo ima zunanja meja morda črke na krovu; v vsakem primeru ji bo zagotovo dala nekaj dokumentov z datumom leto ali dve pozneje kot zadnji v njenih zamegljenih in od palca obrabljenih datotekah. In v zameno za to vljudnost bi ladja, namenjena v tujino, prejela najnovejše podatke o kitolovu s kraja križarjenja, kamor je morda namenjena, kar je zanjo izjemno pomembno. In v veliki meri bo vse to veljalo za kitolovce, ki si križajo pot na samem križarjenju, čeprav so enako dolgo odsotne od doma. Kajti eden od njih je morda prejel prenos pisem s kakšnega tretjega in zdaj daleč oddaljenega plovila; in nekatera od teh pisem so morda za ljudi na ladji, ki jih zdaj sreča. Poleg tega bi izmenjali novice o kitolovu in prijetno poklepetali. Kajti ne le, da bi se srečali z vsemi simpatijami mornarjev, temveč tudi z vsemi posebnimi sorodnostmi, ki izhajajo iz skupnega prizadevanja in medsebojno skupnih primanjkljajev in nevarnosti.

Tudi razlika v državi ne bi pomenila bistvene razlike; to je, dokler obe strani govorita en jezik, kot je to v primeru Američanov in Angležev. Res je, da se zaradi majhnega števila angleških kitolov takšna srečanja ne zgodijo pogosto, in ko se zgodijo, je preveč primerno, da bi med njimi obstajala nekakšna sramežljivost; kajti vaš Anglež je precej zadržan, vaš Yankee pa ne mara takih stvari pri nikomer razen pri sebi. Poleg tega imajo angleški kitolovci včasih nekakšno metropolitansko premoč nad ameriškimi kitolovci; o dolgem, suhem Nantucketerju, z njegovimi neopisnimi provincializmi, kot o nekakšnem morskem kmetu. Toda kje je ta premoč angleških kitolovcev v resnici, bi bilo težko reči, saj Jenkiji v enem dnevu skupaj pobijejo več kitov kot vsi Angleži skupaj v desetih letih. Toda to je neškodljiva majhna pomanjkljivost angleških lovcev na kite, ki si je Nantucketer ne jemlje preveč k srcu; verjetno zato, ker ve, da ima tudi sam nekaj pomanjkljivosti.

Torej vidimo, da imajo kitolovci od vseh ladij, ki ločeno plujejo po morju, največ razlogov, da so družabni – in taki tudi so. Ker bodo nekatere trgovske ladje, ki prečkajo brazde druga druge v srednjem Atlantiku, pogosto šle naprej brez ene same besede prepoznavnosti in se medsebojno presekale na odprtem morju, kot par dandijev na Broadwayu; in ves čas se morda prepuščata fini kritiki drug drugega. Kar se tiče vojnih mož, ko se srečajo na morju, gredo najprej skozi tako vrsto neumnih priklonov in strganja, tako umikanja praporščakov, da se zdi, da ni veliko srčne dobre volje. in bratska ljubezen o tem sploh. Kot ganljivo srečanje suženjskih ladij, zakaj se jim tako neznansko mudi, čim prej pobegnejo drug od drugega. In kar se tiče piratov, ko drug drugemu prekrižajo kosti, je prva toča — »Koliko lobanj?« — na enak način kot kitolovci kličejo — »Koliko sodov?« In ko dobiš odgovor na to vprašanje, se pirati takoj ločijo, saj so peklenski zlobneži na obeh straneh in ne marajo videti preveč zlobnih podobnosti drug drugega.

Toda poglejte bogaboječega, poštenega, neopaznega, gostoljubnega, družabnega, svobodnega in lahkega kitolovca! Kaj naredi kitolovka, ko sreča drugega kitolovca v kakšnem primernem vremenu? Ima " Gam ", stvar, ki je vsem drugim ladjam tako popolnoma neznana, da sploh niso slišale za ime; in če slučajno slišijo za to, se temu samo nasmehnejo in ponavljajo smešne stvari o "izlivalcih" in "kuhačih" in podobnih lepih vzklikih. Zakaj vsi trgovski pomorščaki, pa tudi vsi pirati in možje vojnega človeka ter mornarji suženjskih ladij gojijo tako zaničljiv občutek do kitovcev; to je vprašanje, na katerega bi težko odgovorili. Ker bi recimo v primeru piratov rad vedel, ali ima ta njihov poklic kakšno posebno slavo. Včasih se res konča v neobičajni višini; ampak samo na vislicah. In poleg tega, ko je človek povišan na tako nenavaden način, nima prave podlage za svojo superiorno višino. Zato sklepam, da pirat v tem, ko se hvali, da je visoko dvignjen nad kitolovca, v tej trditvi nima trdne podlage.

Toda kaj je Gam? Morda boste izrabili svoj kazalec, ko bo tekal gor in dol po stolpcih slovarjev, in nikoli ne boste našli besede. Dr. Johnson ni nikoli dosegel te erudicije; Noah Websterjeva barka tega ne zdrži. Kljub temu je ta ista izrazna beseda zdaj že vrsto let v stalni uporabi med približno petnajst tisoč pravimi rojenimi Jenkiji. Vsekakor potrebuje definicijo in jo je treba vključiti v leksikon. S tem pogledom naj ga poučeno opredelim.

GAM. SAMOSTALNIK— Družabno srečanje dveh ( ali več ) kitolov, običajno na križarjenju; ko si po izmenjavi pozdravov izmenjajo obiske posadk čolnov: dva kapitana ostaneta za nekaj časa na krovu ene ladje, dva glavna čolnov pa na drugi.

Obstaja še ena malenkost o igrah na srečo, ki je tukaj ne smete pozabiti. Vsi poklici imajo svoje majhne posebnosti podrobnosti; enako velja za ribolov kitov. Na gusarski, vojaški ladji ali suženjski ladji, ko kapitan vesla kjer koli v svojem čolnu, vedno sedi na krmnih ponjavah na udobnem, včasih oblazinjenem sedežu, in pogosto sam krmari s čudovito majhno modrsko ročico. okrašena z gejevskimi vrvicami in trakovi. Toda kitolov nima sedeža za krmo, nobene kavče te vrste in sploh nima krmila. Zares visoki časi, če bi se kapitani kitolov vozili po vodi na koleščkih kot protinski stari svetniki na lakiranih stolih. In kar se tiče krmila, kitolovka nikoli ne priznava takšne ženstvenosti; in ker mora pri igranju na srečo celotna posadka čolna zapustiti ladjo, in ker je krmar čolna ali harpunar med njimi, je ta podrejeni ob tej priložnosti krmar, kapitan pa se umakne, ker nima prostora za sedenje. na njegov obisk ves stoječ kot bor. In pogosto boste opazili, da ob zavedanju oči celotnega vidnega sveta, uprtih vanj s strani obeh ladij, ta stoječi kapitan popolnoma razume pomen ohranjanja svojega dostojanstva tako, da ohranja svoje noge. Tudi to ni zelo lahka zadeva; kajti zadaj je neizmerno štrleče krmilno veslo, ki ga tu in tam udari v križ, zadnje veslo pa mu udarja po kolenih spredaj. Tako je popolnoma zagozden spredaj in zadaj, vstran pa se lahko razširi le tako, da se usede na stegnjene noge; toda nenaden, silovit nagib čolna bo pogosto daleč, da ga prevrne, ker dolžina temelja ni nič brez ustrezne širine. Samo naredite kot razprostiranja dveh drogov in ju ne morete postaviti pokonci. Potem pa, spet, nikoli ne bi bilo primerno na očeh prikovanih oči sveta, nikoli ne bi bilo primerno, pravim, da bi tega razkosnega kapitana videli, kako se umiri za najmanjši delček tako, da z rokami ujame karkoli; v resnici, kot znak njegovega popolnega, živahnega samoobvladovanja, običajno nosi roke v žepih hlač; morda pa ker so na splošno zelo velike, težke roke, jih nosi tja kot balast. Kljub temu so se zgodili primeri, tudi dobro potrjeni, ko je bil kapitan znan v neobičajno kritičnem trenutku ali dveh, v nenadnem neurju, recimo – da se je prijel za lase najbližjega veslača in tam vztrajal kot huda smrt.





POGLAVJE 54. Zgodba Town-Hoja.

( Kot so povedali v Golden Inn. )

Rt dobrega upanja in vse vodnato območje okoli njega je podobno nekaterim znanim štirim vogalom velike avtoceste, kjer srečaš več popotnikov kot kjerkoli drugje.

Kmalu po tem, ko je spregovoril Goney, so naleteli na drugega kitolovca, ki se je vračal domov, Town-Ho*. Skoraj v celoti so jo upravljali Polinezijci. V kratki igri, ki je sledila, nam je sporočila močne novice o Moby Dicku. Nekaterim se je splošno zanimanje za belega kita zdaj divje povečalo zaradi okoliščine zgodbe Town-Hoja, za katero se je zdelo nejasno, da s kitom vključuje nekakšen čudovit, obrnjen obisk ene od tistih tako imenovanih božjih sodb, ki so včasih rekel, da prehiti nekaj moških. Ta zadnja okoliščina s svojimi posebnimi spremljevalci, ki tvorijo tisto, čemur bi lahko rekli skrivni del tragedije, o kateri bomo pripovedovali, nikoli ni prišla do ušes kapitana Ahaba ali njegovih tovarišev. Kajti ta skrivni del zgodbe ni bil znan samemu kapitanu Town-Hoja. Bila je zasebna last treh konfederacijskih belih mornarjev te ladje, eden izmed njih pa jo je, kot kaže, sporočil Tashtegu z rimskimi prepovedmi tajnosti, toda naslednjo noč je Tashtego blodil v spanju in na ta način razkril toliko tega. , da ko so ga zbudili, ni mogel dobro zadržati počitka. Kljub temu pa je ta stvar imela tako močan vpliv na tiste mornarje v Pequodu, ki so to v celoti spoznali, in s tako čudno delikatnostjo, da jo tako imenujemo, so bili v tej zadevi vodeni, da so skrivnost hranili med sami, tako da se nikoli ni izkazalo za Pequodovim glavnim jamborom. S prepletanjem te temnejše niti na njenem pravem mestu z zgodbo, kot je bila javno pripovedana na ladji, nadaljujem s tem, da celotno to nenavadno zadevo zapisujem v trajen zapis.

*Starodavni kitov krik ob prvem opazovanju kita z vrha jambora, ki ga še vedno uporabljajo kitolovci pri lovu na znamenito galipaško kornjačo.

Zaradi humorja bom ohranil slog, v katerem sem to nekoč pripovedoval v Limi, krogu svojih španskih prijateljev, nekega svetega večera, ko se je kadilo na trgu z debelimi pozlačenimi ploščicami Golden Inn. Od teh finih kavalirjev sta bila mlada Donsa, Pedro in Sebastian, v tesnejših odnosih z mano; in od tod tudi vmesna vprašanja, ki jih občasno postavijo in na katera takrat dobijo ustrezne odgovore.

»Približno dve leti preden sem prvič izvedel za dogodke, ki vam jih želim ponoviti, gospodje, je Town-Ho, kitolov semenčic iz Nantucketa, križaril v vašem Pacifiku tukaj, ne prav veliko dni plovbe proti vzhodu od strehe tega dobra Golden Inn. Bila je nekje severno od črte. Nekega jutra ob ravnanju s črpalkami so glede na dnevno uporabo opazili, da je v svojem skladišču naredila več vode kot običajno. Domnevali so, da jo je zabodla mečarica, gospodje. Toda kapitan je imel nenavaden razlog za prepričanje, da ga na teh zemljepisnih širinah čaka redka sreča; in ker je bila zato zelo nenaklonjena temu, da bi jih opustili, puščanje pa takrat ni veljalo za nič nevarnega, čeprav ga dejansko niso mogli najti, potem ko so preiskali skladišče tako nizko, kot je bilo mogoče v razmeroma težkem vremenu, je ladja vseeno nadaljevala svoje križarjenje mornarji, ki delajo na črpalkah v širokih in lahkih intervalih; a sreče ni bilo; več dni je minilo in ne samo, da puščanje še ni bilo odkrito, ampak se je občutno povečalo. Tako zelo, da se je kapitan zdaj nekoliko preplašil in se je na vsa jadra odpravil proti najbližjemu pristanišču med otoki, da bi mu trup izpraznili in ga popravili.

»Čeprav pred njo ni bil majhen prehod, pa se, če je bila naklonjena najobičajnejša priložnost, sploh ni bal, da bi se njegova ladja mimogrede nasedla, ker so bile njegove črpalke najboljše in so občasno razbremenile teh šest- in trideset njegovih mož bi zlahka ohranilo ladjo prosto; ni pomembno, če bi se puščanje podvojilo na njej. V resnici je skoraj ves ta prehod spremljal zelo uspešen vetrič, Town-Ho pa je povsem varno prispela v svoje pristanišče, ne da bi prišlo do najmanjše smrtne žrtve, če ne bi bilo brutalne prepotentnosti Radneyja. , partnerja, vinogradnika, in bridko izzvanega maščevanja Steelkilta, Jezernika in obupanega iz Buffala.

»Lakeman!—Buffalo! Kaj je Lakeman in kje je Buffalo?« je rekel don Sebastian in vstal na svoji travnati blazini.

»Na vzhodni obali našega jezera Erie, Don; toda — hrepenim po vaši vljudnosti — morda boste kmalu slišali še več o vsem tem. Zdaj, gospodje, v ladjah s kvadratnimi jadri in trijambornih ladjah, skoraj tako velikih in krepkih kot katera koli, ki je kdaj plula iz vašega starega Callaa v daljno Manillo; ta Lakeman, v osrčju naše Amerike brez izhoda na kopno, je bil še hranjen z vsemi tistimi agrarnimi prostimi vtisi, ki so bili priljubljeni povezani z odprtim oceanom. Kajti v svojem medsebojnem prelivanju imajo ta naša veličastna sladkovodna morja, Erie, Ontario, Huron, Superior in Michigan, oceansko razsežnost, z mnogimi najplemenitejšimi lastnostmi oceana; s številnimi obrobljenimi različicami ras in podnebij. Vsebujejo okrogle arhipelage romantičnih otokov, tako kot polinezijske vode; v veliki meri jih obdajata dva velika nasprotujoča si naroda, kot je Atlantik; zagotavljajo dolge pomorske pristope do naših številnih teritorialnih kolonij z vzhoda, posejanih po vseh njihovih bregovih; tu in tam se namrščijo nad baterijami in kozličastimi strmimi puškami vzvišenega Mackinawa; slišali so grmenje flote pomorskih zmag; v presledkih predajo svoje plaže divjim barbarom, katerih rdeče pobarvani obrazi blestijo iz njihovih mehkih wigwamov; kajti lige in lige so obdane s starodavnimi in nevstopenimi gozdovi, kjer suhljati borovci stojijo kot zaporedne vrste kraljev v gotskih rodoslovjih; tisti isti gozdovi, ki dajejo zatočišče divjim afriškim zveri roparicam in svilenim bitjem, katerih izvožena krzna dajejo oblačila tatarskim cesarjem; odsevajo tlakovane prestolnice Buffalo in Cleveland ter vasi Winnebago; lebdijo enako kot polno opremljena trgovska ladja, oborožena državna križarka, parnik in bukov kanu; preplavijo jih borejski in osupljivi sunki, tako strašni kot vsi, ki bičajo slani val; vedo, kaj so brodolomi, kajti izven pogleda kopnega, pa čeprav v notranjosti, so potopili marsikatero polnočno ladjo z vso njeno kričečo posadko. Tako, gospodje, Steelkilt je bil, čeprav domačin, rojen v divjem oceanu in negovan v divjem oceanu; prav tako drzen mornar kot kateri koli drugi. In za Radneyja, čeprav ga je morda v povojih položil na samotno plažo Nantucket, da bi dojil svoje materinsko morje; čeprav je v posmrtnem življenju dolgo sledil našemu strogemu Atlantiku in vašemu kontemplativnemu Pacifiku; vendar je bil prav tako maščevalen in poln družbenih prepirov kot gozdni mornar, ki je bil pravkar iz zemljepisnih širin nožev Bowie z ročajem iz buckhorna. Vendar je bil ta Nantucketer človek z nekaj dobrosrčnimi lastnostmi; in ta Lakeman, pomorščak, ki, čeprav je res neke vrste hudič, morda vendarle zaradi neprilagodljive trdnosti, le umirjen s tisto običajno spodobnostjo človeškega priznanja, ki je najhujša pravica sužnja; tako obdelan je ta Steelkilt dolgo ostal neškodljiv in ubogljiv. Vsekakor je to doslej dokazal; toda Radney je bil obsojen na propad in je bil obnoren, Steelkilt pa - toda, gospodje, slišali boste.

»Ni minilo več kot dan ali dva največ po tem, ko je svoj premec usmerila proti svojemu otoškemu zatočišču, ko se je zdelo, da je puščanje Town-Ho spet naraščalo, a le tako, da je vsak dan potrebovala uro ali več na črpalkah. Vedeti morate, da v urejenem in civiliziranem oceanu, kot je na primer naš Atlantik, nekateri kapitani ne razmišljajo o tem, da bi prečrpali celotno pot čez njega; kljub mirni, zaspani noči, če bi častnik krova slučajno pozabil na svojo dolžnost v zvezi s tem, bi bila verjetnost, da se on in njegovi tovariši tega nikoli več ne bi spomnili, ker se vse roke nežno popuščajo k dnu. Niti v samotnih in divjih morjih, ki so daleč od vas proti zahodu, gospodje, ni povsem nenavadno, da ladje kar naprej v polnem zboru žvenketajo na svojih ročajih črpalke, tudi na precej dolgem potovanju; to je, če ležijo ob znosno dostopni obali ali če jim je omogočen kak drug razumen umik. Šele ko je plovilo, ki pušča, v zelo oddaljenem delu teh voda, na neki res brezopetni zemljepisni širini, se njen kapitan začne počutiti nekoliko zaskrbljeno.

»Približno tako je bilo z Town-Ho; tako da, ko je bilo ugotovljeno, da je njeno puščanje še enkrat pridobilo, je bilo v resnici nekaj malega zaskrbljenosti, ki jo je pokazalo več članov njene družbe; predvsem Radney the mate. Ukazal je, naj se zgornja jadra dobro dvignejo, na novo pokrijejo s ponjavo in na vse strani razširijo do vetra. Zdaj je bil ta Radney, domnevam, tako majhen strahopetec in tako malo nagnjen k kakršni koli živčni prestrašenosti, ki bi se dotaknila lastne osebe, kot katero koli neustrašno, nepremišljeno bitje na kopnem ali na morju, ki si ga lahko udobno predstavljate, gospodje. Zato so nekateri mornarji, ko je izdal to skrb za varnost ladje, izjavili, da je to samo zato, ker je njen delni lastnik. Torej, ko so tisti večer delali na črpalkah, se med njimi ni dogajala nobena majhna igrivost, saj so stali z nogami, ki jih je nenehno preplavljala valovita bistra voda; jasno kot vsak gorski izvir, gospodje - to brbotanje iz črpalk je teklo po krovu in se izlivalo v enakomernih izlivih v zavetrnih luknjah.

»Kot dobro veste, ni tako redko v tem našem običajnem svetu – vodnem ali drugačnem; da ko oseba, ki ima poveljstvo nad svojimi soljudmi, ugotovi, da je eden od njih zelo boljši od njega v splošnem ponosu moškosti, do tega človeka takoj začne nepremagljivo odpor in zagrenjenost; in če bo imel priložnost, bo podrl in zdrobil v prah tisti podrejeni stolp in iz njega naredil majhen kup prahu. Naj bo ta moja domišljavost kakorkoli že, gospodje, v vsakem primeru je bil Steelkilt visoka in plemenita žival z glavo kot Rimljan in razvejano zlato brado kot ohišja z resicami smrkajočega polnilnika vašega zadnjega podkralja; in možgani, in srce, in duša v njem, gospodje, zaradi česar je Steelkilt postal Karel Veliki, če bi bil rojen sin očetu Karla Velikega. Toda Radney, tovariš, je bil grd kot mula; vendar tako trdoživ, tako trmast, kot zloben. Steelkilta ni maral in Steelkilt je to vedel.

»Ko je opazoval kolega, ki se je približeval, medtem ko je delal na črpalki z ostalimi, je Jezernik prizadel, da ga ni opazil, vendar je brez strahu nadaljeval s svojim gejevskim norčevanjem.

»Da, da, moji veseli fantje, to je živahno puščanje; drži konzervo, eden od vas, in poskusimo. Pri Gospodu, vredno je ustekleničiti! Povem vam kaj, moški, naložba starega Rada mora biti vredna! najbolje, da je odrezal svoj del trupa in ga odvlekel domov. Dejstvo je, fantje, da je mečarica šele začela delo; spet se je vrnil s tolpo ladijskih tesarjev, rib žag in rib rib in še kaj; in cela četa jih zdaj pridno reže in reže po dnu; delati izboljšave, predvidevam. Če bi bil stari Rad zdaj tukaj, bi mu rekel, naj skoči čez krov in jih raztrese. Lahko mu povem, da se igrajo hudiča z njegovim posestvom. Toda on je preprosta stara duša, Rad, in tudi lepotec. Fantje, pravijo, da je preostanek njegovega premoženja vložen v ogledala. Sprašujem se, ali bi ubogemu hudiču, kot sem jaz, dal model svojega nosu.«

»Prekleto tvoje oči! zakaj se ta črpalka ustavi?' je zarjovel Radney in se pretvarjal, da ni slišal pogovora mornarjev. 'Grom stran od tega!'

»'Da, da, gospod,' je rekel Steelkilt, vesel kot čriček. 'Živahno, fantje, zdaj živahno!' In s tem je črpalka zažvenketala kot petdeset gasilskih avtomobilov; možje so mu vrgli klobuke dol in kmalu se je zaslišalo tisto nenavadno sopenje pljuč, ki označuje največjo napetost skrajne življenjske energije.

»Ko je Lakeman končno zapustil črpalko, je s preostalimi člani svoje skupine šel naprej ves sopihoč in se usedel na vitel; njegov obraz ognjeno rdeč, oči krvave in si briše obilen pot s čela. Ne vem, kateri hudič je bil, gospodje, da je obsedel Radneyja, da se je vpletel v takega človeka v tem telesno razburjenem stanju; ampak tako se je zgodilo. Ko je neznosno korakal po palubi, mu je sopotnik ukazal, naj vzame metlo in pomete deske, pa tudi lopato in odstrani nekaj žaljivih stvari, ki so posledica tega, ko je pustil prašiču bežati na prostosti.

»Gospodje, pometanje ladijskega krova na morju je gospodinjsko delo, ki se ga v vseh časih, razen v divjanju neurja, redno ukvarja vsak večer; znano je, da se to izvaja v primeru ladij, ki se takrat dejansko potopijo. Takšna je, gospodje, neprilagodljivost pomorskih navad in instinktivna ljubezen do urejenosti mornarjev; nekateri med njimi se ne bi rade volje utopili, ne da bi si prej umili obraz. Toda na vseh plovilih je ta posel z metlami predpisana naloga fantov, če so fantje na krovu. Poleg tega so bili močnejši možje v Town-Hoju razdeljeni v tolpe, ki so se menjavale na črpalkah; in ker je bil Steelkilt najbolj atletski mornar od vseh, je bil redno imenovan za kapitana ene od tolp; posledično bi moral biti osvobojen vsakršnih nepomembnih poslov, ki niso bili povezani z resnično pomorskimi dolžnostmi, kar velja za njegove tovariše. Vse te podrobnosti omenjam zato, da boste natančno razumeli, kako je ta afera stala med moškima.

»Vendar je bilo več kot le to: ukaz o lopati je bil skoraj tako očitno mišljen, da bodi in žali Steelkilta, kot da bi mu Radney pljunil v obraz. Vsak človek, ki je postal mornar na kitolovki, bo to razumel; in vse to in nedvomno še veliko več je Jezernik popolnoma razumel, ko je sopotnik izrekel svoj ukaz. Toda ko je za trenutek sedel pri miru in ko je neomajno pogledal v zlobno oko svojega kolega in zaznal kupe sodov smodnika, nakopičenih v njem, in počasne vžigalice, ki so tiho gorele proti njim; ko je nagonsko videl vse to, tisto nenavadno potrpežljivost in nepripravljenost razvneti globljo strast v katerem koli že tako grozljivem bitju – gnus, ki ga najbolj čutijo, ko sploh čutijo, resnično pogumni moški, tudi ko so prizadeti – ta brezimni fantomski občutek, gospodje, je ukradel čez Steelkilt.

»Zato mu je s svojim običajnim tonom, le nekoliko zlomljen zaradi telesne izčrpanosti, v kateri je bil začasno, odgovoril, da pometanje palube ni njegova stvar in da tega ne bo počel. In potem je, ne da bi namigoval na lopato, pokazal na tri fante kot običajne pometače; ki, ker ni bil nameščen na črpalkah, je ves dan naredil malo ali nič. Na to je Radney odgovoril s prisego, na nadvladujoč in nezaslišan način brezpogojno ponovil svoj ukaz; medtem pa je napredoval proti še vedno sedečemu Jezerniku z dvignjenim cooperjevim kladivom, ki ga je pograbil iz soda v bližini.

»Kot je bil razgret in razdražen zaradi njegovega krčevitega truda na črpalkah, je kljub svojemu prvemu brezimnemu občutku potrpežljivosti prepoteni Steelkilt le slabo prenesel to držo v kolega; vendar je nekako še vedno dušil požar v sebi, ne da bi spregovoril, je ostal vztrajno zakoreninjen na svojem sedežu, dokler končno razjarjeni Radney ni stresel kladiva v nekaj centimetrih od njegovega obraza in mu besno ukazal, naj izpolni svoje ukaze.

»Steelkilt je vstal in se počasi umikal okoli vitla, vztrajno mu je sledil sopotnik s svojim grozečim kladivom, namerno ponovil svojo namero, da ne bo ubogal. Ker pa je videl, da njegova potrpežljivost nima niti najmanjšega učinka, je z grozljivim in neizrekljivim namigovanjem s svojo zvito roko posvaril nespametnega in zaljubljenca; vendar je bilo zaman. In na ta način sta šla enkrat počasi okoli vitla; ko je Jezernik končno odločen, da se ne bo več umaknil, saj je mislil, da se je zdaj odpovedal temu, kar je v skladu s svojim humorjem, obstal na loputah in tako spregovoril častniku:

"'Gospod. Radney, ne bom te ubogal. Odnesi to kladivo stran, ali pa se oziraj nase.' Toda vnaprej določeni partner, ki se mu je še bolj približal, kjer je Jezernik stal pritrjen, je zdaj stresel težko kladivo na centimeter od svojih zob; medtem pa ponavlja niz nevzdržnih prekletstev. Umik ne za tisočinko palca; Steelkilt ga je zabodel v oko z neomahljivim poniardom svojega pogleda, stisnil desno roko za seboj in jo plazeče potegnil nazaj, svojemu preganjalcu rekel, da če ga bo kladivo le oplazilo po licu, ga bo (Steelkilt) umoril. Toda, gospodje, norca so bogovi označili za zakol. Takoj se je kladivo dotaknilo lica; v naslednjem trenutku je bila spodnja čeljust partnerja v njegovi glavi; padel je na loputo in brizgal kri kot kit.

»Preden je krik lahko zadonel, je Steelkilt zamajal enega od zadnjih opornikov, ki so vodili daleč visoko do mesta, kjer sta dva njegova tovariša stala z jamboroma. Oba sta bila Canallerja.

»'Canallers!' je zavpil don Pedro. »V naših pristaniščih smo videli veliko kitolovcev, a še nikoli nismo slišali za vaše Canallerje. Oprostite: kdo in kaj so?'

»Canallerji, Don, so čolnarji, ki pripadajo našemu velikemu prekopu Erie. Gotovo ste slišali za to.'

»Ne, gospod; nekje v tej dolgočasni, topli, najbolj lenobni in dedni deželi le malo poznamo vaš živahni sever.'

"'Ja? No, Don, napolni mi skodelico. Tvoja chicha je zelo dobra; in preden nadaljujem, vam bom povedal, kaj so naši Canallerji; kajti takšne informacije lahko vržejo stransko luč na mojo zgodbo.«

»Za tristo šestdeset milj, gospodje, po vsej širini zvezne države New York; skozi številna naseljena mesta in najbolj cvetoče vasi; skozi dolga, mračna, nenaseljena močvirja in bogata, obdelana polja, ki jim rodovitnost ni para; z biljardnico in barom; skozi svetišča velikih gozdov; na rimskih obokih nad indijskimi rekami; skozi sonce in senco; srečnih ali zlomljenih src; skozi vso široko kontrastno pokrajino teh plemiških okrožij Mohawk; predvsem pa ob vrstah snežno belih kapelic, katerih vrhovi stojijo skoraj kot miljniki, teče en nenehen tok beneško pokvarjenega in pogosto brezpravnega življenja. Tukaj je vaš pravi Ashantee, gospodje; tam tulijo tvoji pogani; kjer jih kdaj najdeš, pri tebi; pod dolgotrajno senco in prijetnim pokroviteljskim zavetrjem cerkva. Kajti zaradi neke nenavadne usodnosti, kot je pogosto opaženo o vaših metropolitanskih svobodnjakih, da se vedno utaborijo okoli sodnih dvoran, je grešnikov, gospodje, največ v najsvetejših okolicah.

"'Je to pater mimo?" je dejal Don Pedro in s šaljivo zaskrbljenostjo pogledal navzdol v nabito poln trg.

»No, za našega severnega prijatelja, inkvizicija dame Isabelle v Limi pojenja,« se je zasmejal don Sebastian. 'Nadaljujte, gospod.'

"'Moment! Oprostite!' je zavpil drugi iz družbe. »V imenu vseh nas, Limejcev, vam želim povedati, gospod mornar, da nikakor nismo spregledali vaše rahločutnosti, ko v vaši pokvarjeni primerjavi sedanje Lime niste nadomestili z daljnimi Benetkami. Oh! ne klanjajte se in ne gledajte presenečeno; poznate pregovor po vsej tej obali - "Pokvarjen kot Lima." Vendar potrjuje tudi vaše besede; cerkve, ki jih je več kot biljardnih miz in za vedno odprte – in »pokvarjene kot Lima«. Tako tudi Benetke; Bil sem tam; sveto mesto blaženega evangelista, svetega Marka! — sv. Dominic, očisti ga! Tvoja skodelica! Hvala: tukaj napolnim; zdaj pa spet izliješ.'

»Če bi bil svobodno upodobljen v svojem poklicu, gospodje, bi bil Canaller odličen dramski junak, tako obilno in slikovito hudoben je. Kakor Mark Antonij dneve in dneve po svojem zelenem, cvetličnem Nilu brezbrižno lebdi in se odkrito poigrava s svojo rdečelično Kleopatro, ki zori svoje marelično stegno na sončni palubi. Toda na kopnem je vsa ta ženstvenost razbita. Razbojniški videz, ki ga Canaller tako ponosno nosi; njegov spuščen in z veselimi trakovi okrašen klobuk nakazuje njegove velike poteze. Groza nasmejani nedolžnosti vasi, skozi katere plava; njegovega modrega obraza in drznega bahanja se v mestih ne izogibajo. Nekoč potepuh na lastnem kanalu me je eden od teh Canallerjev dobro obrnil; Srčno se mu zahvaljujem; ne bi bil nehvaležen; vendar je pogosto ena od glavnih odrešilnih lastnosti vašega nasilnega moža, da ima včasih tako trdo roko, da podpre ubogega tujca v stiski, kot da pleni bogatega. Skratka, gospodje, kakšna divjina je tega kanalskega življenja, je jasno razvidno iz tega; da naš divji kitolov obsega toliko najbolj končanih diplomantov in da komaj kateri koli rasi človeštva, razen moških v Sydneyju, naši kitolovski kapitani tako zelo ne zaupajo. Prav nič ne zmanjša zanimivosti te zadeve, da mnogim tisočem naših podeželskih fantov in mladeničev, rojenih vzdolž te linije, poskusno življenje Velikega kanala predstavlja edini prehod med tihim žetjem na krščanskem žitnem polju in nepremišljeno orje vode najbolj barbarskih morij.

"'Vidim! Vidim!' je vneto vzkliknil Don Pedro in razlil čičo po svojih srebrnastih volančkih. 'Ni treba potovati! Edina Lima na svetu. Zdaj sem mislil, da so bile generacije na vašem zmernem severu hladne in svete kot hribi. — Toda zgodba.«

»Gospodje, končal sem tam, kjer je Jezernik stresel hrbtni drog. Komaj je to storil, so ga obkrožili trije mlajši sopotniki in štirje harpunarji, ki so ga vsi nagnetli na krovu. But sliding down the ropes like baleful comets, the two Canallers rushed into the uproar, and sought to drag their man out of it towards the forecastle. Others of the sailors joined with them in this attempt, and a twisted turmoil ensued; while standing out of harm's way, the valiant captain danced up and down with a whale-pike, calling upon his officers to manhandle that atrocious scoundrel, and smoke him along to the quarter-deck. At intervals, he ran close up to the revolving border of the confusion, and prying into the heart of it with his pike, sought to prick out the object of his resentment. But Steelkilt and his desperadoes were too much for them all; they succeeded in gaining the forecastle deck, where, hastily slewing about three or four large casks in a line with the windlass, these sea-Parisians entrenched themselves behind the barricade.

“'Come out of that, ye pirates!' roared the captain, now menacing them with a pistol in each hand, just brought to him by the steward. 'Come out of that, ye cut-throats!'

“Steelkilt leaped on the barricade, and striding up and down there, defied the worst the pistols could do; but gave the captain to understand distinctly, that his (Steelkilt's) death would be the signal for a murderous mutiny on the part of all hands. Fearing in his heart lest this might prove but too true, the captain a little desisted, but still commanded the insurgents instantly to return to their duty.

“'Will you promise not to touch us, if we do?' demanded their ringleader.

“'Turn to! turn to!—I make no promise;—to your duty! Do you want to sink the ship, by knocking off at a time like this? Turn to!' and he once more raised a pistol.

“'Sink the ship?' cried Steelkilt. 'Aye, let her sink. Not a man of us turns to, unless you swear not to raise a rope-yarn against us. What say ye, men?' turning to his comrades. A fierce cheer was their response.

“The Lakeman now patrolled the barricade, all the while keeping his eye on the Captain, and jerking out such sentences as these:—'It's not our fault; we didn't want it; I told him to take his hammer away; it was boy's business; he might have known me before this; I told him not to prick the buffalo; I believe I have broken a finger here against his cursed jaw; ain't those mincing knives down in the forecastle there, men? look to those handspikes, my hearties. Captain, by God, look to yourself; say the word; don't be a fool; forget it all; we are ready to turn to; treat us decently, and we're your men; but we won't be flogged.'

“'Turn to! I make no promises, turn to, I say!'

“'Look ye, now,' cried the Lakeman, flinging out his arm towards him, 'there are a few of us here (and I am one of them) who have shipped for the cruise, d'ye see; now as you well know, sir, we can claim our discharge as soon as the anchor is down; so we don't want a row; it's not our interest; we want to be peaceable; we are ready to work, but we won't be flogged.'

“'Turn to!' roared the Captain.

“Steelkilt glanced round him a moment, and then said:—'I tell you what it is now, Captain, rather than kill ye, and be hung for such a shabby rascal, we won't lift a hand against ye unless ye attack us; but till you say the word about not flogging us, we don't do a hand's turn.'

“'Down into the forecastle then, down with ye, I'll keep ye there till ye're sick of it. Down ye go.'

“'Shall we?' cried the ringleader to his men. Most of them were against it; but at length, in obedience to Steelkilt, they preceded him down into their dark den, growlingly disappearing, like bears into a cave.

“As the Lakeman's bare head was just level with the planks, the Captain and his posse leaped the barricade, and rapidly drawing over the slide of the scuttle, planted their group of hands upon it, and loudly called for the steward to bring the heavy brass padlock belonging to the companionway. Then opening the slide a little, the Captain whispered something down the crack, closed it, and turned the key upon them—ten in number—leaving on deck some twenty or more, who thus far had remained neutral.

“All night a wide-awake watch was kept by all the officers, forward and aft, especially about the forecastle scuttle and fore hatchway; at which last place it was feared the insurgents might emerge, after breaking through the bulkhead below. But the hours of darkness passed in peace; the men who still remained at their duty toiling hard at the pumps, whose clinking and clanking at intervals through the dreary night dismally resounded through the ship.

“At sunrise the Captain went forward, and knocking on the deck, summoned the prisoners to work; but with a yell they refused. Water was then lowered down to them, and a couple of handfuls of biscuit were tossed after it; when again turning the key upon them and pocketing it, the Captain returned to the quarter-deck. Twice every day for three days this was repeated; but on the fourth morning a confused wrangling, and then a scuffling was heard, as the customary summons was delivered; and suddenly four men burst up from the forecastle, saying they were ready to turn to. The fetid closeness of the air, and a famishing diet, united perhaps to some fears of ultimate retribution, had constrained them to surrender at discretion. Emboldened by this, the Captain reiterated his demand to the rest, but Steelkilt shouted up to him a terrific hint to stop his babbling and betake himself where he belonged. On the fifth morning three others of the mutineers bolted up into the air from the desperate arms below that sought to restrain them. Only three were left.

“'Better turn to, now?' said the Captain with a heartless jeer.

“'Shut us up again, will ye!' cried Steelkilt.

“'Oh certainly,' said the Captain, and the key clicked.

“It was at this point, gentlemen, that enraged by the defection of seven of his former associates, and stung by the mocking voice that had last hailed him, and maddened by his long entombment in a place as black as the bowels of despair; it was then that Steelkilt proposed to the two Canallers, thus far apparently of one mind with him, to burst out of their hole at the next summoning of the garrison; and armed with their keen mincing knives (long, crescentic, heavy implements with a handle at each end) run amuck from the bowsprit to the taffrail; and if by any devilishness of desperation possible, seize the ship. For himself, he would do this, he said, whether they joined him or not. That was the last night he should spend in that den. But the scheme met with no opposition on the part of the other two; they swore they were ready for that, or for any other mad thing, for anything in short but a surrender. And what was more, they each insisted upon being the first man on deck, when the time to make the rush should come. But to this their leader as fiercely objected, reserving that priority for himself; particularly as his two comrades would not yield, the one to the other, in the matter; and both of them could not be first, for the ladder would but admit one man at a time. And here, gentlemen, the foul play of these miscreants must come out.

“Upon hearing the frantic project of their leader, each in his own separate soul had suddenly lighted, it would seem, upon the same piece of treachery, namely: to be foremost in breaking out, in order to be the first of the three, though the last of the ten, to surrender; and thereby secure whatever small chance of pardon such conduct might merit. But when Steelkilt made known his determination still to lead them to the last, they in some way, by some subtle chemistry of villany, mixed their before secret treacheries together; and when their leader fell into a doze, verbally opened their souls to each other in three sentences; and bound the sleeper with cords, and gagged him with cords; and shrieked out for the Captain at midnight.

“Thinking murder at hand, and smelling in the dark for the blood, he and all his armed mates and harpooneers rushed for the forecastle. In a few minutes the scuttle was opened, and, bound hand and foot, the still struggling ringleader was shoved up into the air by his perfidious allies, who at once claimed the honor of securing a man who had been fully ripe for murder. But all these were collared, and dragged along the deck like dead cattle; and, side by side, were seized up into the mizzen rigging, like three quarters of meat, and there they hung till morning. 'Damn ye,' cried the Captain, pacing to and fro before them, 'the vultures would not touch ye, ye villains!'

“At sunrise he summoned all hands; and separating those who had rebelled from those who had taken no part in the mutiny, he told the former that he had a good mind to flog them all round—thought, upon the whole, he would do so—he ought to—justice demanded it; but for the present, considering their timely surrender, he would let them go with a reprimand, which he accordingly administered in the vernacular.

“'But as for you, ye carrion rogues,' turning to the three men in the rigging—'for you, I mean to mince ye up for the try-pots;' and, seizing a rope, he applied it with all his might to the backs of the two traitors, till they yelled no more, but lifelessly hung their heads sideways, as the two crucified thieves are drawn.

“'My wrist is sprained with ye!' he cried, at last; 'but there is still rope enough left for you, my fine bantam, that wouldn't give up. Take that gag from his mouth, and let us hear what he can say for himself.'

“For a moment the exhausted mutineer made a tremulous motion of his cramped jaws, and then painfully twisting round his head, said in a sort of hiss, 'What I say is this—and mind it well—if you flog me, I murder you!'

“'Say ye so? then see how ye frighten me'—and the Captain drew off with the rope to strike.

“'Best not,' hissed the Lakeman.

“'But I must,'—and the rope was once more drawn back for the stroke.

“Steelkilt here hissed out something, inaudible to all but the Captain; who, to the amazement of all hands, started back, paced the deck rapidly two or three times, and then suddenly throwing down his rope, said, 'I won't do it—let him go—cut him down: d'ye hear?'

“But as the junior mates were hurrying to execute the order, a pale man, with a bandaged head, arrested them—Radney the chief mate. Ever since the blow, he had lain in his berth; but that morning, hearing the tumult on the deck, he had crept out, and thus far had watched the whole scene. Such was the state of his mouth, that he could hardly speak; but mumbling something about his being willing and able to do what the captain dared not attempt, he snatched the rope and advanced to his pinioned foe.

“'You are a coward!' hissed the Lakeman.

“'So I am, but take that.' The mate was in the very act of striking, when another hiss stayed his uplifted arm. He paused: and then pausing no more, made good his word, spite of Steelkilt's threat, whatever that might have been. The three men were then cut down, all hands were turned to, and, sullenly worked by the moody seamen, the iron pumps clanged as before.

“Just after dark that day, when one watch had retired below, a clamor was heard in the forecastle; and the two trembling traitors running up, besieged the cabin door, saying they durst not consort with the crew. Entreaties, cuffs, and kicks could not drive them back, so at their own instance they were put down in the ship's run for salvation. Still, no sign of mutiny reappeared among the rest. On the contrary, it seemed, that mainly at Steelkilt's instigation, they had resolved to maintain the strictest peacefulness, obey all orders to the last, and, when the ship reached port, desert her in a body. But in order to insure the speediest end to the voyage, they all agreed to another thing—namely, not to sing out for whales, in case any should be discovered. For, spite of her leak, and spite of all her other perils, the Town-Ho still maintained her mast-heads, and her captain was just as willing to lower for a fish that moment, as on the day his craft first struck the cruising ground; and Radney the mate was quite as ready to change his berth for a boat, and with his bandaged mouth seek to gag in death the vital jaw of the whale.

“But though the Lakeman had induced the seamen to adopt this sort of passiveness in their conduct, he kept his own counsel (at least till all was over) concerning his own proper and private revenge upon the man who had stung him in the ventricles of his heart. He was in Radney the chief mate's watch; and as if the infatuated man sought to run more than half way to meet his doom, after the scene at the rigging, he insisted, against the express counsel of the captain, upon resuming the head of his watch at night. Upon this, and one or two other circumstances, Steelkilt systematically built the plan of his revenge.

“During the night, Radney had an unseamanlike way of sitting on the bulwarks of the quarter-deck, and leaning his arm upon the gunwale of the boat which was hoisted up there, a little above the ship's side. In this attitude, it was well known, he sometimes dozed. There was a considerable vacancy between the boat and the ship, and down between this was the sea. Steelkilt calculated his time, and found that his next trick at the helm would come round at two o'clock, in the morning of the third day from that in which he had been betrayed. At his leisure, he employed the interval in braiding something very carefully in his watches below.

“'What are you making there?' said a shipmate.

“'What do you think? what does it look like?'

“'Like a lanyard for your bag; but it's an odd one, seems to me.'

“'Yes, rather oddish,' said the Lakeman, holding it at arm's length before him; 'but I think it will answer. Shipmate, I haven't enough twine,—have you any?'

“But there was none in the forecastle.

“'Then I must get some from old Rad;' and he rose to go aft.

“'You don't mean to go a begging to him! ' said a sailor.

“'Why not? Do you think he won't do me a turn, when it's to help himself in the end, shipmate?' and going to the mate, he looked at him quietly, and asked him for some twine to mend his hammock. It was given him—neither twine nor lanyard were seen again; but the next night an iron ball, closely netted, partly rolled from the pocket of the Lakeman's monkey jacket, as he was tucking the coat into his hammock for a pillow. Twenty-four hours after, his trick at the silent helm—nigh to the man who was apt to doze over the grave always ready dug to the seaman's hand—that fatal hour was then to come; and in the fore-ordaining soul of Steelkilt, the mate was already stark and stretched as a corpse, with his forehead crushed in.

“But, gentlemen, a fool saved the would-be murderer from the bloody deed he had planned. Yet complete revenge he had, and without being the avenger. For by a mysterious fatality, Heaven itself seemed to step in to take out of his hands into its own the damning thing he would have done.

“It was just between daybreak and sunrise of the morning of the second day, when they were washing down the decks, that a stupid Teneriffe man, drawing water in the main-chains, all at once shouted out, 'There she rolls! there she rolls!' Jesu, what a whale! It was Moby Dick.

“'Moby Dick!' cried Don Sebastian; 'St. Dominic! Sir sailor, but do whales have christenings? Whom call you Moby Dick?'

“'A very white, and famous, and most deadly immortal monster, Don;—but that would be too long a story.'

“'How? how?' cried all the young Spaniards, crowding.

“'Nay, Dons, Dons—nay, nay! I cannot rehearse that now. Let me get more into the air, Sirs.'

“'The chicha! the chicha!' cried Don Pedro; 'our vigorous friend looks faint;—fill up his empty glass!'

“No need, gentlemen; one moment, and I proceed.—Now, gentlemen, so suddenly perceiving the snowy whale within fifty yards of the ship—forgetful of the compact among the crew—in the excitement of the moment, the Teneriffe man had instinctively and involuntarily lifted his voice for the monster, though for some little time past it had been plainly beheld from the three sullen mast-heads. All was now a phrensy. 'The White Whale—the White Whale!' was the cry from captain, mates, and harpooneers, who, undeterred by fearful rumours, were all anxious to capture so famous and precious a fish; while the dogged crew eyed askance, and with curses, the appalling beauty of the vast milky mass, that lit up by a horizontal spangling sun, shifted and glistened like a living opal in the blue morning sea. Gentlemen, a strange fatality pervades the whole career of these events, as if verily mapped out before the world itself was charted. The mutineer was the bowsman of the mate, and when fast to a fish, it was his duty to sit next him, while Radney stood up with his lance in the prow, and haul in or slacken the line, at the word of command. Moreover, when the four boats were lowered, the mate's got the start; and none howled more fiercely with delight than did Steelkilt, as he strained at his oar. After a stiff pull, their harpooneer got fast, and, spear in hand, Radney sprang to the bow. He was always a furious man, it seems, in a boat. And now his bandaged cry was, to beach him on the whale's topmost back. Nothing loath, his bowsman hauled him up and up, through a blinding foam that blent two whitenesses together; till of a sudden the boat struck as against a sunken ledge, and keeling over, spilled out the standing mate. That instant, as he fell on the whale's slippery back, the boat righted, and was dashed aside by the swell, while Radney was tossed over into the sea, on the other flank of the whale. He struck out through the spray, and, for an instant, was dimly seen through that veil, wildly seeking to remove himself from the eye of Moby Dick. But the whale rushed round in a sudden maelstrom; seized the swimmer between his jaws; and rearing high up with him, plunged headlong again, and went down.

“Meantime, at the first tap of the boat's bottom, the Lakeman had slackened the line, so as to drop astern from the whirlpool; calmly looking on, he thought his own thoughts. But a sudden, terrific, downward jerking of the boat, quickly brought his knife to the line. He cut it; and the whale was free. But, at some distance, Moby Dick rose again, with some tatters of Radney's red woollen shirt, caught in the teeth that had destroyed him. All four boats gave chase again; but the whale eluded them, and finally wholly disappeared.

“In good time, the Town-Ho reached her port—a savage, solitary place—where no civilized creature resided. There, headed by the Lakeman, all but five or six of the foremastmen deliberately deserted among the palms; eventually, as it turned out, seizing a large double war-canoe of the savages, and setting sail for some other harbor.

“The ship's company being reduced to but a handful, the captain called upon the Islanders to assist him in the laborious business of heaving down the ship to stop the leak. But to such unresting vigilance over their dangerous allies was this small band of whites necessitated, both by night and by day, and so extreme was the hard work they underwent, that upon the vessel being ready again for sea, they were in such a weakened condition that the captain durst not put off with them in so heavy a vessel. After taking counsel with his officers, he anchored the ship as far off shore as possible; loaded and ran out his two cannon from the bows; stacked his muskets on the poop; and warning the Islanders not to approach the ship at their peril, took one man with him, and setting the sail of his best whale-boat, steered straight before the wind for Tahiti, five hundred miles distant, to procure a reinforcement to his crew.

“On the fourth day of the sail, a large canoe was descried, which seemed to have touched at a low isle of corals. He steered away from it; but the savage craft bore down on him; and soon the voice of Steelkilt hailed him to heave to, or he would run him under water. The captain presented a pistol. With one foot on each prow of the yoked war-canoes, the Lakeman laughed him to scorn; assuring him that if the pistol so much as clicked in the lock, he would bury him in bubbles and foam.

“'What do you want of me?' cried the captain.

“'Where are you bound? and for what are you bound?' demanded Steelkilt; 'no lies.'

“'I am bound to Tahiti for more men.'

“'Very good. Let me board you a moment—I come in peace.' With that he leaped from the canoe, swam to the boat; and climbing the gunwale, stood face to face with the captain.

“'Cross your arms, sir; throw back your head. Now, repeat after me. As soon as Steelkilt leaves me, I swear to beach this boat on yonder island, and remain there six days. If I do not, may lightnings strike me!'

“'A pretty scholar,' laughed the Lakeman. 'Adios, Senor!' and leaping into the sea, he swam back to his comrades.

“Watching the boat till it was fairly beached, and drawn up to the roots of the cocoa-nut trees, Steelkilt made sail again, and in due time arrived at Tahiti, his own place of destination. There, luck befriended him; two ships were about to sail for France, and were providentially in want of precisely that number of men which the sailor headed. They embarked; and so for ever got the start of their former captain, had he been at all minded to work them legal retribution.

“Some ten days after the French ships sailed, the whale-boat arrived, and the captain was forced to enlist some of the more civilized Tahitians, who had been somewhat used to the sea. Chartering a small native schooner, he returned with them to his vessel; and finding all right there, again resumed his cruisings.

“Where Steelkilt now is, gentlemen, none know; but upon the island of Nantucket, the widow of Radney still turns to the sea which refuses to give up its dead; still in dreams sees the awful white whale that destroyed him. * * * *

“'Are you through?' said Don Sebastian, quietly.

“'I am, Don.'

“'Then I entreat you, tell me if to the best of your own convictions, this your story is in substance really true? It is so passing wonderful! Did you get it from an unquestionable source? Bear with me if I seem to press.'

“'Also bear with all of us, sir sailor; for we all join in Don Sebastian's suit,' cried the company, with exceeding interest.

“'Is there a copy of the Holy Evangelists in the Golden Inn, gentlemen?'

“'Nay,' said Don Sebastian; 'but I know a worthy priest near by, who will quickly procure one for me. I go for it; but are you well advised? this may grow too serious.'

“'Will you be so good as to bring the priest also, Don?'

“'Though there are no Auto-da-Fés in Lima now,' said one of the company to another; 'I fear our sailor friend runs risk of the archiepiscopacy. Let us withdraw more out of the moonlight. I see no need of this.'

“'Excuse me for running after you, Don Sebastian; but may I also beg that you will be particular in procuring the largest sized Evangelists you can.'

* * * * * *

“'This is the priest, he brings you the Evangelists,' said Don Sebastian, gravely, returning with a tall and solemn figure.

“'Let me remove my hat. Now, venerable priest, further into the light, and hold the Holy Book before me that I may touch it.

“'So help me Heaven, and on my honor the story I have told ye, gentlemen, is in substance and its great items, true. I know it to be true; it happened on this ball; I trod the ship; I knew the crew; I have seen and talked with Steelkilt since the death of Radney.'”
