POGLAVJE 43. Čuj!

»ZGODOVINA! Si slišal ta hrup, Cabaco?«

Bila je srednja ura: lepa mesečina; mornarji so stali v kordonu, ki se je raztezal od ene od sladkovodnih opornikov v pasu do oklepa blizu ograje. Na ta način so si podajali vedra, da so napolnili žleb. Večinoma so stali na posvečenih območjih četrtdeka in pazili, da niso govorili ali zašumeli z nogami. Iz rok v roke so vedra prehajala v najgloblji tišini, ki sta jo prekinila le občasno zavijanje jadra in enakomerno brnenje nenehno napredujoče kobilice.

Sredi tega počitka je Archy, eden od kordona, katerega postojanka je bila blizu zapornih loput, svojemu sosedu, Cholu, zašepetal zgornje besede.

»Zgodovina! si slišal ta hrup, Cabaco?«

»Vzemi vedro, boš, Archy? kakšen hrup misliš?"

"Tam je spet - pod loputami - ali ne slišite - kašelj - slišati je bilo kot kašelj."

»Preklet naj bo kašelj! Podaj to povratno vedro."

"Tam spet - tam je! - sliši se, kot da bi se dva ali trije spalci prevrnili, zdaj!"

»Karamba! opravil, ladijski kolega, boš? To so trije namočeni piškoti, ki jih jeste za večerjo, ki jih obrnete v sebi – nič drugega. Poglej v vedro!"

»Povejte, kar hočete, ladijski kolega; Imam ostra ušesa.”

»Da, ti si tisti fant, kajne, ki je slišal brnenje pletilnih igel stare Quakeress petdeset milj na morju od Nantucketa; ti si pravi."

»Nasmehni se; bomo videli kaj bo. Čuj, Cabaco, spodaj v zadrževalnem prostoru je nekdo, ki ga še niso videli na krovu; in sumim, da tudi naš stari Mogul ve nekaj o tem. Slišal sem, da je Stubb na neki jutranji straži rekel Flasku, da je nekaj takega v vetru.«

»Tish! vedro!"





POGLAVJE 44. Graf.

Če bi sledili kapitanu Ahabu dol v njegovo kabino po neurju, ki se je pojavilo tisto noč po tistem divjem potrditvi njegovega namena s svojo posadko, bi ga videli, kako je šel do omarice na krmi in iz nje prinesel velik naguban zvitek rumenkaste morske karte, jih razprostre pred njim na njegovi priviti mizi. Ko bi potem sedel pred njim, bi ga videli, kako pozorno preučuje različne črte in senčenja, ki so se tam srečala z njegovim očesom; in s počasnim, a vztrajnim svinčnikom sledite dodatnim potekom čez prostore, ki so bili prej prazni. Občasno se je skliceval na kupe starih ladijskih dnevnikov poleg sebe, v katerih so bili zapisani letni časi in kraji, kjer so bili na različnih nekdanjih potovanjih različnih ladij ujeti ali videni kiti sperme.

Medtem ko je bila tako uporabljena, se je težka kositrna svetilka, ki je bila v verigah obešena nad njegovo glavo, nenehno zibala z gibanjem ladje in za vedno metala premikajoče se leske in sence črt na njegovo nagubano čelo, dokler se skoraj ni zdelo, da medtem ko je sam označeval črte in tečaje na nagubanih kartah, je neki nevidni svinčnik risal črte in tečaje tudi na globoko zaznamovani karti njegovega čela.

Toda Ahab ni ravno to noč v samoti svoje koče razmišljal o svojih kartah. Skoraj vsako noč so jih pripeljali ven; skoraj vsako noč so bile nekatere oznake svinčnika izbrisane, druge pa zamenjane. Kajti s kartami vseh štirih oceanov pred seboj je Ahab prepletal labirint tokov in vrtincev z namenom, da bi bolj zanesljivo uresničil to monomansko misel svoje duše.

Vsakemu, ki ni popolnoma seznanjen z načini levijatanov, se lahko zdi nesmiselno brezupna naloga, da bi tako iskali eno samotno bitje v neokrnjenih oceanih tega planeta. A Ahabu, ki je poznal sklope vseh plim in tokov, se ni zdelo tako; in s tem izračunavanje odnašanja hrane kitovega kita; in tudi spominjanje na redne, ugotovljene sezone za lov nanj v določenih zemljepisnih širinah; bi lahko prišel do razumnih domnev, ki bi se skoraj približale gotovostim, glede najbolj primernega dneva, ko bi bil na tem ali onem terenu v iskanju svojega plena.

Dejansko je dejstvo o periodičnosti zatekanja kitov semenalcev v določene vode res tako zanesljivo, da mnogi lovci verjamejo, da bi ga lahko pozorno opazovali in preučevali po vsem svetu; če bi skrbno primerjali dnevnike za eno potovanje celotne kitove flote, bi se ugotovilo, da selitve kitov sperme v nespremenljivosti ustrezajo migracijam jat sleda ali letom lastovk. Na podlagi tega namiga so bili narejeni poskusi sestaviti podrobne selitvene karte kitov senatov.*

Poleg tega pri prehodu od enega hranilišča do drugega kiti sperme, ki jih vodi nek nezmotljiv instinkt – recimo skrivna inteligenca božanstva – večinoma plavajo po žilah , kot se imenujejo; nadaljujejo svojo pot vzdolž dane oceanske črte s tako nepogrešljivo natančnostjo, da nobena ladja ni nikoli plula svoje smeri, po kateri koli karti, z eno desetino tako čudovite natančnosti. Čeprav je v teh primerih smer, ki jo ubere kateri koli kit, ravna kot geodetska vzporednica in čeprav je linija napredovanja strogo omejena na njegovo lastno neizogibno naravnost, pa naj bi bila samovoljna žila , v kateri naj bi v teh trenutkih plavati, na splošno zajema nekaj milj v širino (bolj ali manj, saj se domneva, da se vena razširi ali skrči); vendar nikoli ne preseže vizualnega zamaha z vrhov jamborov kitolovke, ko preudarno drsi po tem čarobnem območju. Skratka, v določenih letnih časih znotraj te širine in vzdolž te poti je mogoče z velikim zaupanjem iskati selitvene kite.

In zato je Ahab lahko upal, da bo naletel na svoj plen, ne samo ob utemeljenih časih, na dobro znanih ločenih krmiščih; toda pri prečkanju najširših vodnih prostranstev med temi podlagami se je lahko s svojo umetnostjo tako umestil in časovno umestil na svojo pot, da tudi takrat ne bi bil povsem brez možnosti srečanja.

Obstajala je okoliščina, za katero se je na prvi pogled zdelo, da zapleta njegov deliričen, a še vedno metodičen načrt. A v resnici morda ni tako. Čeprav imajo družabni kiti sperme svoje redne sezone za določene razloge, vendar na splošno ne morete sklepati, da se bodo črede, ki so letos strašile na taki in taki zemljepisni širini ali dolžini, recimo, izkazale za popolnoma enake tistim, ki so bile tam najdene. prejšnja sezona; čeprav obstajajo posebni in nedvomni primeri, kjer se je izkazalo nasprotno. Na splošno enaka pripomba, le v manj široki meji, velja za samotarje in puščavnike med zrelimi, ostarelimi kiti semeni. Torej, čeprav je bil Moby Dick v prejšnjem letu viden na primer na tako imenovanem Sejšelskem ozemlju v Indijskem oceanu ali Volcano Bayu na japonski obali; vendar ni sledilo, da če bi Pequod obiskal katero od teh točk v kateri koli naslednji ustrezni sezoni, bi ga tam nezmotljivo srečala. Tako tudi z nekaterimi drugimi krmišči, kjer se je včasih razgalil. Toda vse to se je zdelo le njegova priložnostna postajališča in oceanske gostilne, tako rekoč, ne pa kraji njegovega daljšega bivanja. In tam, kjer se je doslej govorilo o Ahabovih možnostih za uresničitev njegovega cilja, je bila omenjena le aluzija na kakršno koli stransko stran, predhodne, dodatne možnosti, ki so bile njegove, preden bi bil dosežen določen čas ali kraj, ko bi vse možnosti postale verjetnosti, in , kot je rad mislil Ahab, je vsaka možnost naslednja stvar gotovosti. Ta določeni čas in kraj sta bila združena v eni tehnični frazi – Sezona na spletu. Kajti tam in takrat je bil nekaj zaporednih let občasno opisan Moby Dick, ki se je nekaj časa zadrževal v teh vodah, ko se sonce v svojem letnem krogu lebdi za predviden interval v katerem koli znamenju zodiaka. Tam se je zgodila tudi večina smrtonosnih srečanj z belim kitom; tam so valovi pisali o njegovih dejanjih; tam je bilo tudi tisto tragično mesto, kjer je monomanski starec našel grozen motiv za svoje maščevanje. Toda v previdni vsestranskosti in nepozabni pazljivosti, s katero je Ahab vrgel svojo zamišljeno dušo v ta neomajni lov, si ni dovolil, da bi vse svoje upe naslonil na eno zgoraj omenjeno kronsko dejstvo, pa naj je še tako laskavo tem upanjem; niti v nespečnosti svoje zaobljube ni mogel tako pomiriti svojega nemirnega srca, da bi odložil vsa vmesna prizadevanja.

Sedaj je Pequod odplul iz Nantucketa na samem začetku sezone na liniji. Nobeno prizadevanje potem njenemu poveljniku ne bi moglo omogočiti velikega prehoda proti jugu, dvojnega rta Horn in nato s tekom po šestdesetih stopinjah zemljepisne širine prispeti v ekvatorialni Pacifik pravočasno za križarjenje tam. Zato mora počakati na naslednjo sezono. Vendar je Ahab morda pravilno izbral prezgodnjo uro Pequodove plovbe glede na to zapletenost stvari. Kajti pred njim je bil interval tristo petinšestdeset dni in noči; interval, ki bi ga namesto nestrpnega prestajanja na kopnem preživel v raznovrstnem lovu; če slučajno beli kit, ki preživlja svoje počitnice v morjih, ki so daleč stran od njegovih občasnih prehranjevalnih območij, dvigne svoje nagubano čelo ob Perzijskem zalivu, v Bengalskem zalivu ali v kitajskem morju ali v kateri koli drugi vodi, kjer straši njegov dirka. Tako da Monsuni, Pampas, Nor'-Westers, Harmattans, Trades; vsak veter, razen Levanterja in Simoona, bi lahko odpihnil Mobyja Dicka v zvit cik-cak svetovni krog Pequodovega krožnega sledu.

Toda podelitev vsega tega; vendar, če ga obravnavamo diskretno in hladnokrvno, se zdi, da to ni le nora ideja; da je v širnem brezmejnem oceanu en samotni kit, tudi če ga srečamo, sposoben osebnega prepoznavanja svojega lovca, tudi kot belobradi mufti na natrpanih ulicah Konstantinopla? ja Kajti nenavadno snežno belo obrvi Mobyja Dicka in njegova snežno bela grba sta bili nezgrešljivi. In ali nisem preštel kita, si je mrmral Ahab, medtem ko se je potem, ko je dolgo po polnoči brskal po svojih kartah, vrgel nazaj v sanjarjenje – preštel ga je in bo pobegnil? Njegove široke plavuti so zdolgočasene in nazobčane kot izgubljeno ovčje uho! In tukaj bi njegov nori um tekel naprej v dirki brez sape; dokler ga ni prevzela utrujenost in omedlevica razmišljanja; in na prostem na palubi si je skušal povrniti moč. Ah, bog! kakšne transe muk prenaša tisti človek, ki ga razjeda ena neizpolnjena maščevalna želja. Spi s stisnjenimi rokami; in se zbudi z lastnimi okrvavljenimi nohti v dlaneh.

Pogosto, ko so ga silile iz viseče mreže naporne in nevzdržno žive nočne sanje, ki so, ko so čez dan nadaljevale njegove lastne intenzivne misli, jih prenašale naprej med trkom frendov in jih vrtele krog in krog in krog v njegovih gorečih možganih, dokler ni utripanje njegovega življenjskega mesta postalo nevzdržna bolečina; in ko so, kot se je včasih zgodilo, te duhovne stiske v njem dvignile njegovo bitje iz temelja in se je zdelo, da se v njem odpira prepad, iz katerega so se razcepili plameni in strele, in prekleti hudiči so ga vabili, naj skoči mednje; ko bi ta pekel v njem samem zehal pod njim, divji krik bi se zaslišal po ladji; in z žarečimi očmi je Ahab planil iz svoje dnevne sobe, kot bi pobegnil iz postelje, ki je gorela. Vendar so bili ti morda, namesto da bi bili nezadržni simptomi neke latentne šibkosti ali strahu pred njegovo lastno odločnostjo, le najjasnejši znaki njene intenzivnosti. Kajti v takšnih časih nori Ahab, spletkarski, nepomirjeno vztrajni lovec na belega kita; ta Ahab, ki je šel v svojo visečo mrežo, ni bil povzročitelj, zaradi katerega je znova planil iz nje v grozi. Slednja je bila večno, živo načelo ali duša v njem; in v spanju, ko je bil za nekaj časa ločen od karakterizirajočega uma, ki ga je v drugih časih uporabljal za svojega zunanjega nosilca ali agenta, je spontano iskal pobeg iz žgočega sosedstva podivjane stvari, od katere za tisti čas ni bil daljši integral. Ker pa um ne obstaja, če ni povezan z dušo, je moralo biti tako v Ahabovem primeru, da je vse svoje misli in domišljije prepustil svojemu edinemu najvišjemu namenu; ta namen se je s svojo lastno samozavestnostjo volje prisilil proti bogovom in hudičem v nekakšno samoprevzeto, lastno neodvisno bitje. Ne, lahko bi mračno živel in gorel, medtem ko je skupna vitalnost, s katero je bila povezana, z grozo bežala pred nepoklicanim in neočetovskim rojstvom. Zato je bil trpeči duh, ki je žarel iz telesnih oči, ko je nekaj, kar se je zdelo, Ahab, planil iz njegove sobe, za nekaj časa le izpraznjena stvar, brezoblično somnambulno bitje, žarek žive svetlobe, seveda, vendar brez predmeta, da bi barva in zato praznina sama po sebi. Bog ti pomagaj, stari, tvoje misli so ustvarile bitje v tebi; in tisti, čigar intenzivno razmišljanje ga tako naredi za Prometeja; jastreb se večno hrani s tem srcem; da je jastreb prav bitje, ki ga ustvari.





POGLAVJE 45. Zaprisežena izjava.

Kar se tiče pripovedi v tej knjigi; in res, ker se posredno dotika ene ali dveh zelo zanimivih in radovednih podrobnosti v navadah kitov sperme, je prejšnje poglavje v svojem prejšnjem delu enako pomembno, kot ga bomo našli v tem zvezku; toda glavno zadevo tega je treba še bolj in bolj domače razširiti, da bi jo ustrezno razumeli in poleg tega odstranili vsako nezaupljivost, ki bi jo globoko nepoznavanje celotne teme lahko povzročilo v nekaterih glavah glede naravne resnice. glavnih točk te zadeve.

Ne skrbim, da bi ta del svoje naloge opravljal metodično; vendar se bom zadovoljil z ustvarjanjem želenega vtisa z ločenimi navedbami elementov, ki so mi kot kitolovcu praktično ali zanesljivo poznani; in sklepam, da bo iz teh citatov ciljni sklep sledil sam od sebe.

Prvič: osebno sem poznal tri primere, ko je kit, potem ko je prejel harpuno, popolnoma pobegnil; in po presledku (v enem primeru treh let) je bil ponovno udarjen z isto roko in ubit; ko sta bila oba železa, oba označena z isto zasebno šifro, vzeta iz telesa. V primeru, ko je med vrženjem dveh harpun minilo tri leta; in mislim, da je bilo morda nekaj več kot to; človek, ki jih je poslal, se je v tem presledku zgodil, da je šel s trgovsko ladjo na potovanje v Afriko, se tam izkrcal, se pridružil skupini odkritij in prodrl daleč v notranjost, kjer je potoval skoraj dve leti, pogosto ogrožajo kače, divjaki, tigri, strupene miazme, z vsemi drugimi običajnimi nevarnostmi, ki jih spremlja tavanje v srcu neznanih območij. Medtem je moral tudi kit, ki ga je udaril, potovati; nedvomno je trikrat obkrožil svet in s svojimi boki dotaknil vse obale Afrike; vendar brez namena. Ta človek in ta kit sta se spet združila in eden je premagal drugega. Pravim, da sem sam poznal tri podobne primere; v dveh od njih sem videl zadete kite; in ob drugem napadu je zagledal dva železa z ustreznimi oznakami, ki sta bila nato vzeta iz mrtvih rib. V triletnem primeru je tako padlo, da sem bil v čolnu obakrat, prvič in zadnjič, in zadnjič razločno prepoznal nekakšno nekakšno ogromno krtino pod kitovim očesom, ki sem jo tam opazoval pred tremi leti. Rekel sem tri leta, vendar sem skoraj prepričan, da je bilo več kot to. Tukaj so torej trije primeri, za katere osebno vem resnice; vendar sem slišal za številne druge primere od oseb, katerih resničnost v tej zadevi ni utemeljena za obtožbo.

Drugič: dobro znano je v ribolovu na kite šamponate, ne glede na to, kako obalni svet tega ne pozna, je bilo več nepozabnih zgodovinskih primerov, ko je bil določen kit v oceanu v oddaljenih časih in na splošno znanih mestih. Zakaj je tak kit postal tako označen, ni bilo povsem in prvotno zaradi njegovih telesnih posebnosti, ki se razlikujejo od drugih kitov; kajti ne glede na to, da je naključni kit nenavaden v tem pogledu, kmalu odpravijo njegove posebnosti tako, da ga ubijejo in skuhajo v posebno dragoceno olje. Ne: razlog je bil tale: da je zaradi usodnih ribiških izkušenj takšen kit, kot je bil Rinaldo Rinaldini, imel strašen ugled nevarnosti, tako da se je večina ribičev zadovoljila s tem, da ga je prepoznala tako, da se je zgolj dotaknila njihove ponjave, ko bi najdejo jih med poležavanjem na morju, ne da bi si prizadevali za intimnejše poznanstvo. Kot nekateri ubogi hudiči na kopnem, ki po naključju poznajo razdražljivega velikega moža, ga na ulici nevsiljivo pozdravljajo od daleč, da ne bi, če bi še naprej sledili znancu, zaradi svoje prevzetnosti prejeli kratek udarec.

Toda ne samo, da je vsak od teh slavnih kitov užival veliko posamično slavo – ne, temu lahko rečete sloves po vsem oceanu; ne samo, da je bil slaven v življenju in je zdaj nesmrten v zgodbah o nogah po smrti, ampak so mu bile priznane vse pravice, privilegiji in razlike imena; Imel je tako ime kot Cambyses ali Cæsar. Ali ni bilo tako, o Timor Tom! ti slavni leviatan, brazgotinjen kot ledena gora, ki tako dolgo nisi prežil v orientalski ožini s tem imenom, katerega izliv je bil pogosto viden s palmove plaže Ombay? Ali ni bilo tako, o novozelandski Jack! ti groza vseh križark, ki so prečkale njihove brazde v bližini Tattoo Landa? Ali ni bilo tako, o Morquan! Japonski kralj, čigar vzvišeni curek je, kot pravijo, včasih prevzel podobo snežno belega križa proti nebu? Ali ni bilo tako, o Don Miguel! ti čilski kit, označen kot stara želva z mističnimi hieroglifi na hrbtu! V preprosti prozi so tukaj štirje kiti, tako dobro znani študentom zgodovine kitov in delfinov kot Marius ali Sylla klasičnim učenjakom.

Vendar to ni vse. Nova Zelandija Tom in Don Miguel, potem ko sta v različnih obdobjih povzročila veliko opustošenje med čolni različnih plovil, sta se končno odpravila na iskanje, sistematično lovila, zasledovala in ubila s strani pogumnih kitolovskih kapitanov, ki so dvignili svoja sidra s tem ekspresnim predmetom, kot veliko v mislih, tako kot na poti skozi gozd Narragansett, je stotnik Butler nekoč imel v mislih, da ujame tistega razvpitega morilskega divjaka Annawona, najmočnejšega bojevnika indijskega kralja Filipa.

Ne vem, kje najdem boljše mesto kot samo tukaj, da omenim še eno ali dve stvari, ki se mi zdita pomembni, saj v tiskani obliki v vseh pogledih ugotavljata smiselnost celotne zgodbe o Belem kitu, predvsem pa katastrofa. Kajti to je eden tistih žalostnih primerov, ko resnica zahteva popolno podporo kot zmota. Večina kopenarjev je tako nevednih o nekaterih najbolj preprostih in najbolj otipljivih čudes sveta, da bi brez namigov, ki se dotikajo preprostih dejstev, zgodovinskih in drugih, o ribištvu, morda iskali Moby Dicka kot pošastno bajko ali še huje in bolj gnusna, ostudna in nedopustna alegorija.

Prvič: Čeprav ima večina ljudi nekaj nejasnih begajočih idej o splošnih nevarnostih velikega ribolova, pa nimajo ničesar podobnega fiksnemu, živemu pojmovanju teh nevarnosti in pogostosti, s katero se ponavljajo. Eden od razlogov je morda ta, da niti ena od petdesetih dejanskih nesreč in smrti zaradi žrtev v ribištvu nikoli ne najde javne evidence doma, pa naj bo ta evidenca prehodna in takoj pozabljena. In the sixth Christian century lived Procopius, a Christian magistrate of Constantinople, in the days when Justinian was Emperor and Belisarius general. As many know, he wrote the history of his own times, a work every way of uncommon value. By the best authorities, he has always been considered a most trustworthy and unexaggerating historian, except in some one or two particulars, not at all affecting the matter presently to be mentioned.

Now, in this history of his, Procopius mentions that, during the term of his prefecture at Constantinople, a great sea-monster was captured in the neighboring Propontis, or Sea of Marmora, after having destroyed vessels at intervals in those waters for a period of more than fifty years. A fact thus set down in substantial history cannot easily be gainsaid. Nor is there any reason it should be. Of what precise species this sea-monster was, is not mentioned. But as he destroyed ships, as well as for other reasons, he must have been a whale; and I am strongly inclined to think a sperm whale. And I will tell you why. For a long time I fancied that the sperm whale had been always unknown in the Mediterranean and the deep waters connecting with it. Even now I am certain that those seas are not, and perhaps never can be, in the present constitution of things, a place for his habitual gregarious resort. But further investigations have recently proved to me, that in modern times there have been isolated instances of the presence of the sperm whale in the Mediterranean. I am told, on good authority, that on the Barbary coast, a Commodore Davis of the British navy found the skeleton of a sperm whale. Now, as a vessel of war readily passes through the Dardanelles, hence a sperm whale could, by the same route, pass out of the Mediterranean into the Propontis.

In the Propontis, as far as I can learn, none of that peculiar substance called brit is to be found, the aliment of the right whale. But I have every reason to believe that the food of the sperm whale—squid or cuttle-fish—lurks at the bottom of that sea, because large creatures, but by no means the largest of that sort, have been found at its surface. If, then, you properly put these statements together, and reason upon them a bit, you will clearly perceive that, according to all human reasoning, Procopius's sea-monster, that for half a century stove the ships of a Roman Emperor, must in all probability have been a sperm whale.





CHAPTER 46. Surmises.

Though, consumed with the hot fire of his purpose, Ahab in all his thoughts and actions ever had in view the ultimate capture of Moby Dick; though he seemed ready to sacrifice all mortal interests to that one passion; nevertheless it may have been that he was by nature and long habituation far too wedded to a fiery whaleman's ways, altogether to abandon the collateral prosecution of the voyage. Or at least if this were otherwise, there were not wanting other motives much more influential with him. It would be refining too much, perhaps, even considering his monomania, to hint that his vindictiveness towards the White Whale might have possibly extended itself in some degree to all sperm whales, and that the more monsters he slew by so much the more he multiplied the chances that each subsequently encountered whale would prove to be the hated one he hunted. But if such an hypothesis be indeed exceptionable, there were still additional considerations which, though not so strictly according with the wildness of his ruling passion, yet were by no means incapable of swaying him.

To accomplish his object Ahab must use tools; and of all tools used in the shadow of the moon, men are most apt to get out of order. He knew, for example, that however magnetic his ascendency in some respects was over Starbuck, yet that ascendency did not cover the complete spiritual man any more than mere corporeal superiority involves intellectual mastership; for to the purely spiritual, the intellectual but stand in a sort of corporeal relation. Starbuck's body and Starbuck's coerced will were Ahab's, so long as Ahab kept his magnet at Starbuck's brain; still he knew that for all this the chief mate, in his soul, abhorred his captain's quest, and could he, would joyfully disintegrate himself from it, or even frustrate it. It might be that a long interval would elapse ere the White Whale was seen. During that long interval Starbuck would ever be apt to fall into open relapses of rebellion against his captain's leadership, unless some ordinary, prudential, circumstantial influences were brought to bear upon him. Not only that, but the subtle insanity of Ahab respecting Moby Dick was noways more significantly manifested than in his superlative sense and shrewdness in foreseeing that, for the present, the hunt should in some way be stripped of that strange imaginative impiousness which naturally invested it; that the full terror of the voyage must be kept withdrawn into the obscure background (for few men's courage is proof against protracted meditation unrelieved by action); that when they stood their long night watches, his officers and men must have some nearer things to think of than Moby Dick. For however eagerly and impetuously the savage crew had hailed the announcement of his quest; yet all sailors of all sorts are more or less capricious and unreliable—they live in the varying outer weather, and they inhale its fickleness—and when retained for any object remote and blank in the pursuit, however promissory of life and passion in the end, it is above all things requisite that temporary interests and employments should intervene and hold them healthily suspended for the final dash.

Nor was Ahab unmindful of another thing. In times of strong emotion mankind disdain all base considerations; but such times are evanescent. The permanent constitutional condition of the manufactured man, thought Ahab, is sordidness. Granting that the White Whale fully incites the hearts of this my savage crew, and playing round their savageness even breeds a certain generous knight-errantism in them, still, while for the love of it they give chase to Moby Dick, they must also have food for their more common, daily appetites. For even the high lifted and chivalric Crusaders of old times were not content to traverse two thousand miles of land to fight for their holy sepulchre, without committing burglaries, picking pockets, and gaining other pious perquisites by the way. Had they been strictly held to their one final and romantic object—that final and romantic object, too many would have turned from in disgust. I will not strip these men, thought Ahab, of all hopes of cash—aye, cash. They may scorn cash now; but let some months go by, and no perspective promise of it to them, and then this same quiescent cash all at once mutinying in them, this same cash would soon cashier Ahab.

Nor was there wanting still another precautionary motive more related to Ahab personally. Having impulsively, it is probable, and perhaps somewhat prematurely revealed the prime but private purpose of the Pequod's voyage, Ahab was now entirely conscious that, in so doing, he had indirectly laid himself open to the unanswerable charge of usurpation; and with perfect impunity, both moral and legal, his crew if so disposed, and to that end competent, could refuse all further obedience to him, and even violently wrest from him the command. From even the barely hinted imputation of usurpation, and the possible consequences of such a suppressed impression gaining ground, Ahab must of course have been most anxious to protect himself. That protection could only consist in his own predominating brain and heart and hand, backed by a heedful, closely calculating attention to every minute atmospheric influence which it was possible for his crew to be subjected to.

For all these reasons then, and others perhaps too analytic to be verbally developed here, Ahab plainly saw that he must still in a good degree continue true to the natural, nominal purpose of the Pequod's voyage; observe all customary usages; and not only that, but force himself to evince all his well known passionate interest in the general pursuit of his profession.

Be all this as it may, his voice was now often heard hailing the three mast-heads and admonishing them to keep a bright look-out, and not omit reporting even a porpoise. This vigilance was not long without reward.





CHAPTER 47. The Mat-Maker.

It was a cloudy, sultry afternoon; the seamen were lazily lounging about the decks, or vacantly gazing over into the lead-coloured waters. Queequeg and I were mildly employed weaving what is called a sword-mat, for an additional lashing to our boat. So still and subdued and yet somehow preluding was all the scene, and such an incantation of reverie lurked in the air, that each silent sailor seemed resolved into his own invisible self.

I was the attendant or page of Queequeg, while busy at the mat. As I kept passing and repassing the filling or woof of marline between the long yarns of the warp, using my own hand for the shuttle, and as Queequeg, standing sideways, ever and anon slid his heavy oaken sword between the threads, and idly looking off upon the water, carelessly and unthinkingly drove home every yarn: I say so strange a dreaminess did there then reign all over the ship and all over the sea, only broken by the intermitting dull sound of the sword, that it seemed as if this were the Loom of Time, and I myself were a shuttle mechanically weaving and weaving away at the Fates. There lay the fixed threads of the warp subject to but one single, ever returning, unchanging vibration, and that vibration merely enough to admit of the crosswise interblending of other threads with its own. This warp seemed necessity; and here, thought I, with my own hand I ply my own shuttle and weave my own destiny into these unalterable threads. Meantime, Queequeg's impulsive, indifferent sword, sometimes hitting the woof slantingly, or crookedly, or strongly, or weakly, as the case might be; and by this difference in the concluding blow producing a corresponding contrast in the final aspect of the completed fabric; this savage's sword, thought I, which thus finally shapes and fashions both warp and woof; this easy, indifferent sword must be chance—aye, chance, free will, and necessity—nowise incompatible—all interweavingly working together. The straight warp of necessity, not to be swerved from its ultimate course—its every alternating vibration, indeed, only tending to that; free will still free to ply her shuttle between given threads; and chance, though restrained in its play within the right lines of necessity, and sideways in its motions directed by free will, though thus prescribed to by both, chance by turns rules either, and has the last featuring blow at events.

Thus we were weaving and weaving away when I started at a sound so strange, long drawn, and musically wild and unearthly, that the ball of free will dropped from my hand, and I stood gazing up at the clouds whence that voice dropped like a wing. High aloft in the cross-trees was that mad Gay-Header, Tashtego. His body was reaching eagerly forward, his hand stretched out like a wand, and at brief sudden intervals he continued his cries. To be sure the same sound was that very moment perhaps being heard all over the seas, from hundreds of whalemen's look-outs perched as high in the air; but from few of those lungs could that accustomed old cry have derived such a marvellous cadence as from Tashtego the Indian's.

As he stood hovering over you half suspended in air, so wildly and eagerly peering towards the horizon, you would have thought him some prophet or seer beholding the shadows of Fate, and by those wild cries announcing their coming.

“There she blows! there! there! there! she blows! she blows!”

“Where-away?”

“On the lee-beam, about two miles off! a school of them!”

Instantly all was commotion.

The Sperm Whale blows as a clock ticks, with the same undeviating and reliable uniformity. And thereby whalemen distinguish this fish from other tribes of his genus.

“There go flukes!” was now the cry from Tashtego; and the whales disappeared.

“Quick, steward!” cried Ahab. “Time! time!”

Dough-Boy hurried below, glanced at the watch, and reported the exact minute to Ahab.

The ship was now kept away from the wind, and she went gently rolling before it. Tashtego reporting that the whales had gone down heading to leeward, we confidently looked to see them again directly in advance of our bows. For that singular craft at times evinced by the Sperm Whale when, sounding with his head in one direction, he nevertheless, while concealed beneath the surface, mills round, and swiftly swims off in the opposite quarter—this deceitfulness of his could not now be in action; for there was no reason to suppose that the fish seen by Tashtego had been in any way alarmed, or indeed knew at all of our vicinity. One of the men selected for shipkeepers—that is, those not appointed to the boats, by this time relieved the Indian at the main-mast head. The sailors at the fore and mizzen had come down; the line tubs were fixed in their places; the cranes were thrust out; the mainyard was backed, and the three boats swung over the sea like three samphire baskets over high cliffs. Outside of the bulwarks their eager crews with one hand clung to the rail, while one foot was expectantly poised on the gunwale. So look the long line of man-of-war's men about to throw themselves on board an enemy's ship.

But at this critical instant a sudden exclamation was heard that took every eye from the whale. With a start all glared at dark Ahab, who was surrounded by five dusky phantoms that seemed fresh formed out of air.
