CAIBIDIL 55. De na Pictiúir monstrous na Míolta Móra.

Déanfaidh mé péint fhada chugat chomh maith le canbhas gan chanbhás, rud éigin cosúil le fíorfhoirm na míolta móra mar a fheictear do shúil an mhíol mhóir é nuair a bhíonn an míol mór feistithe ina chorp féin taobh leis an long míol mór ionas go mbeidh is féidir é a sheasamh go cothrom ansin. B’fhéidir gurbh fhiú, mar sin, a fhógairt roimhe seo do na portráidí samhailteacha aisteacha sin de a thugann dúshlán muiníneach do chreideamh an fhearainn go dtí an lá inniu. Tá sé in am an domhan a shocrú i gceart san ábhar seo, trí phictiúir den sórt sin a chruthú den mhíol mór mícheart.

B’fhéidir go bhfaighfear bunfhoinse na seachmaill pictiúrtha sin go léir i measc na ndeilbh Hindoo, Éigipteach agus Gréigise is sine. Go deo ó shin i leith na n-amanna nuálacha ach neamhscrupallacha sin nuair a bhí an deilf tarraingthe i scálaí armúr slabhra cosúil le Saladin, agus ceann clogadáilte ar na painéil mharmar de theampall, ar pheileadóirí na dealbha, agus ar sciatha, medallions, cupáin agus boinn. Naomh Seoirse; ó shin i leith a bhí rud éigin den chineál céanna ceadúnais i réim, ní hamháin sna pictiúir is coitianta den mhíol mór, ach ina lán cur i láthair eolaíoch air.

Anois, ní haon chor ar bith é, tá an phortráid is ársa ar marthain ar aon nós a airbheartaíonn gurbh í an mhíol mór í, le fáil i bpluais clúiteach Elephanta, san India. Deir na Brahmins go raibh na ceirdeanna agus na tóraíochtaí go léir, gach glór a d’fhéadfaí a shamhlú de dhéantús an duine, sna deilbh nach mór gan deireadh den pagoda cuimhneacháin sin, aoiseanna réamhdhéanta sular tháinig aon cheann acu ar an saol. Ní h-iongnadh mar sin go mbéadh ár ngairm uasal míol mór uirri i n-aon chor ar scáth a chéile. An míol mór Hindoo dá dtagraítear, a tharlaíonn i roinn ar leith den bhalla, a thaispeánann an incarnation de Vishnu i bhfoirm leviathan, learnedly ar a dtugtar an Avatar Matse. Ach cé gur leath-dhuine agus leath-mhíol-mhara an dealbh so, ionnas go dtugaidís eireaball an deiridh sin, gidheadh ​​tá an chuid bhig sin uile mícheart. Breathnaíonn sé níos cosúla le heireaball barrchaolaithe anaconda, ná le bosa leathana fíor-phlucanna móra na míolta móra.

Ach imigh leat go dtí na sean-Ghailearaithe, agus féach anois ar phortráid mhór phéintéir Chríostaí den iasc seo; óir ní fearr a n-éiríonn leis ná an Hindoo antilúiveach. Is é pictiúr Guido é de Perseus ag tarrtháil Andraiméide ón ollphéist farraige nó ón míol mór. Cá bhfuair Guido samhail de chréatúr aisteach mar sin? Ní dhéanann Hogarth ach an oiread, agus an radharc céanna á phéinteáil aige ina “Perseus Descending” féin, rud níos fearr a bhaint amach. Téann corpulence ollmhór an ollphéist Hogarthian sin ar an dromchla, agus is gann go dtarraingíonn sé orlach uisce. Tá saghas howdah ar a dhroim, agus d’fhéadfaí a bhéal tuscáilte a bhfuil na bioráin ag rolladh isteach ann, a thabhairt do Gheata na bhFealltóirí a théann ón Thames le huisce isteach sa Túr. Ansin, tá na míolta móra Prodromus ón sean-Albanach Sibbald, agus míol mór Ióná, mar a léirítear i bpriontaí na Sean-Bhíobla agus i gearrthacha na sean-primers. Cad a déarfar díobh seo? Maidir le míol mór an ceanglóra leabhar ag dul i léig ar nós gas fíniúna thart ar stoc ancaire íslitheach—mar stampáilte agus óraithe ar dhroim agus leathanaigh teidil go leor leabhar, idir shean agus nua—créatúr an-pictiúrtha ach fíor-mhaisiúil é sin, aithris, glacaim é, ó na figiúirí cosúla ar vásaí seandachtaí. Cé gur deilf atá ainmnithe go huilíoch, mar sin féin tugaim iasc an cheangail leabhar seo mar iarracht ar mhíol mór; toisc go raibh sé beartaithe amhlaidh nuair a tugadh isteach an gléas ar dtús. Thug seanfhoilsitheoir Iodálach isteach é áit éigin thart ar an 15ú haois, le linn Athbheochan na Foghlama; agus sna laethanta sin, agus fiú síos go dtí tréimhse measartha déanach, bhí deilfeanna ceaptha go coitianta a bheith ina speiceas de na Leviathan.

Sna vignettes agus embellishments eile de roinnt leabhar ársa a bheidh tú ag bualadh uaireanta le baint an-aisteach ag an míol mór, áit a bhfuil gach modh spouts, jets d'eau, te Springs agus fuar, Saratoga agus Baden-Baden, teacht bubbling suas as a chuid. inchinn unexhausted. Ar an leathanach teidil den eagrán bunaidh den “Chun Chun Cinn na Foghlama” gheobhaidh tú míolta móra aisteacha.

Ach éirí as na hiarrachtaí neamhghairmiúla seo go léir, déanaimis sracfhéachaint ar na pictiúir sin de Leviathan a airbheartaíonn a bheith sober, sainmhínithe eolaíochta, ag daoine a bhfuil aithne acu. I seanchnuasach turais Harris tá roinnt plátaí míolta móra a bhaintear as leabhar turais Ollannach, AD 1671, dar teideal “A Whaling Voyage to Spitzbergen in the ship Jonas in the Whale, Peter Peterson of Friesland, master.” I gceann de na plátaí sin tá na míolta móra, cosúil le raftaí móra lomán, ina luí i measc na n-oileán oighir, agus béir bhána ag rith thar a ndroim bheo. I bpláta eile, déantar an botún ollmhór chun an míol mór a léiriú le flukes ingearacha.

Ansin arís, tá ceathrú iontach, scríofa ag Captaen Colnett amháin, Captaen Poist i gcabhlach Shasana, dar teideal “A Voyage round Cape Horn into the South Seas, ar mhaithe le hIascach Míolta Móra Spermaceti a leathnú.” Sa leabhar seo tá imlíne a airbheartaíonn a bheith ina “Pictiúr de mhíol mór Fístéar nó Spermaceti, arna tharraingt de réir scála ó dhuine a maraíodh ar chósta Mheicsiceo, Lúnasa, 1793, agus a ardaíodh ar an deic.” Níl amhras orm gur tógadh an pictiúr fíor seo ag an gcaptaen ar mhaithe lena mhuirí. Gan ach rud amháin a lua faoi, lig dom a rá go bhfuil súil aige a chuirfeadh, de réir an scála a ghabhann leis, le míol mór speirme lánfhásta, a dhéanfadh súl na míolta móra sin ina bogha-fhuinneog cúig troigh ar fad. Ah, a chaptaen cróga, cén fáth nár thug tú Ióná dúinn ag breathnú amach as an tsúil sin!

Níl na cnuasaigh is coinsiasach de Stair an Dúlra ach an oiread, chun leasa na n-óg agus na tairisceana, saor ón heinousness céanna botún. Féach ar an saothar móréilimh sin "Goldsmith's Animated Nature." San eagrán giorraithe de Londain de 1807, tá plátaí de “mhíol mór” líomhnaithe agus “narwhale”. Ní mian liom a bheith neamhleithscéalach, ach tá cuma an-chosúil ar an míol mór gránna seo le cránach teasctha; agus, maidir leis an narwhale, is leor radharc amháin a dhéanamh air chun iontas a dhéanamh, go bhféadfaí sa naoú haois déag a leithéid de dobhareach a chur ar fáil go fírinneach ar aon phobal cliste de bhuachaillí scoile.

Ansin, arís, i 1825, d'fhoilsigh Bernard Germain, Count de Lacépède, nádúraí mór, leabhar míol mór córasach eolaíoch, ina bhfuil roinnt pictiúr de na speicis éagsúla de na Leviathan. Ní hamháin go bhfuil siad seo go léir mícheart, ach dearbhaíonn an pictiúr den mhíol mór Mysticetus nó Ghraonlainn (is é sin le rá an míol mór Ceart), fiú Scoresby, fear a bhfuil taithí fhada aige ar an speiceas sin, nach bhfuil a mhacasamhail sa nádúr.

Ach is don duine eolaíoch Frederick Cuvier, deartháir an Bharúin iomráiteach, a cuireadh an caipín-sguab leis an ngnó mallaithe seo ar fad. Sa bhliain 1836, d'fhoilsigh sé Natural History of Whales, ina dtugann sé pictiúr den Mhíol Mór Sperm mar a thugann sé air. Sula dtaispeánfá an pictiúr sin d’aon Nantucket, b’fhearr duit do chúlú achomair ó Nantucket a sholáthar. I bhfocal, ní míol mór Sperm é Míol Mór Frederick Cuvier, ach scuais. Ar ndóigh, níor bhain sé leas riamh as aistear míol mór (is annamh a bhíonn a leithéid ag fir), ach cén áit ar bhain sé an pictiúr sin amach, cé is féidir a insint? B’fhéidir go bhfuair sé é mar fuair a réamhtheachtaí eolaíochta sa réimse céanna, Desmarest, ceann dá ghinmhilleadh barántúla; is é sin, ó líníocht Síneach. Agus cén sórt lads bríomhar a bhfuil an peann luaidhe ag na Sínigh orthu, cuireann go leor cupáin agus saucer níos ciúine in iúl dúinn.

Maidir le míolta móra na bpéindéirí comharthaí a fheictear ar na sráideanna ar crochadh os cionn siopaí na ndéileálaithe ola, cad a déarfar fúthu? Is iad Risteard III de ghnáth. míolta móra, le cromáin dromedary, agus an-fianna; bricfeasta a dhéanamh ar thrí nó ceithre toirtín mairnéalach, is é sin báid míol mór lán le mairnéalach: a n-éagmais ag titim i bhfarraigí fola agus péint ghorm.

Ach ní haon ionadh iad na botúin iomadúla seo agus an míol mór á léiriú. Mheas! Tógadh an chuid is mó de na líníochtaí eolaíocha ó na héisc shnáithe; agus tá siad seo thart ar chomh ceart le líníocht de long wrecked, le ais briste, a bheadh ​​ionadaíocht i gceart an t-ainmhí uasal féin ina bród undashed de chabhail agus spars. Cé gur sheas elephants dá lánfhad, níor shnámh an Leviathan beo riamh go cothrom dá phortráid. Níl an míol mór beo, ina mhórgacht agus ina shuntas iomlán, le feiceáil ar an bhfarraige ach in uiscí neamhshaolta; agus ar snámh tá an chuid is mó de dó as radharc, cosúil le seoladh líne-de-cath long; agus as an eilimint sin tá sé dodhéanta go síoraí ag an duine bás a chorp a ardú go haer san aer, ionas go gcaomhnófar a swells agus a dhroimneachtaí móra. Agus, gan trácht ar an mór-dhifríocht contúirte idir míol mór diúil óg agus Leviathan Platonach lánfhásta; fós, fiú amháin i gcás ceann de na míolta móra diúil óga sin a ardaíodh chuig deic loinge, is é sin an cruth neamhghnách, eascanna, limbered, éagsúil air, nach bhféadfadh a léiriú beacht an diabhal é féin a ghabháil.

Ach d’fhéadfadh sé a bheith feiliúnach, ó chnámharlach nocht an mhíol mhara shnáithe, go bhféadfaí leideanna cruinne a fháil ag baint lena fhíorfhoirm. Níl ar chor ar bith. Toisc go bhfuil sé ar cheann de na rudaí is aisteach mar gheall ar an Leviathan, nach dtugann a chnámharlach tuairim ar a chruth ginearálta. Cé go dtugann creatlach Jeremy Bentham, atá ar crochadh le haghaidh candelabra i leabharlann duine dá sheiceadóirí, an smaoineamh i gceart maidir le seanfhear uasal utilitarian donn, le tréithe pearsanta ceannródaíocha eile Jeremy; ach ní fhéadfaí aon rud den chineál seo a bhaint as cnámha altacha Leviathan. Déanta na fírinne, mar a deir an Hunter iontach, tá an gaol céanna ag creatlach an mhíol mhóir leis an ainmhí atá lán-infheistithe agus stuáilte agus a dhéanann an fheithidí don chrysalis a chlúdaíonn go cruinn é. Tá an tsaint seo le sonrú go mór sa chloigeann, mar a thaispeánfar go teagmhasach i gcuid éigin den leabhar seo. Tá sé ar taispeáint go han-aisteach freisin san eite taobh, agus freagraíonn na cnámha beagnach go díreach do chnámha lámh an duine, lúide an ordóg amháin. Tá ceithre mhéar chnámh rialta ag an eite seo, an t-innéacs, an lár, an fáinne agus an mhéar bheag. Ach déantar iad seo go léir a thaisceadh go buan ina gcumhdach méithe, mar mhéara an duine i gcumhdach saorga. “Mar sin féin, go meargánta féadann an míol mór freastal orainn uaireanta,” a dúirt Stubb greannmhar lá amháin, “ní féidir a rá go fírinneach go láimhseálann sé sinn gan mittens.”

Ar na cúiseanna seo go léir, mar sin, ar bhealach ar bith is féidir leat breathnú air, ní mór duit a thabhairt i gcrích gurb é an Leviathan mór an créatúr amháin ar domhan nach mór fanacht gan phéinteáil go dtí an ceann deireanach. Is fíor gur féidir go mbuailfeadh portráid amháin an marc i bhfad níos gaire ná ceann eile, ach ní féidir le haon phortráid í a bhualadh go mór cruinn. Mar sin níl aon bhealach domhanda ann le fáil amach go beacht cad is cuma ar an míol mór. Agus is é an t-aon mhodh amháin inar féidir leat fiú smaoineamh inghlactha a fháil ar a chomhrian maireachtála, ná dul ag iascaireacht iascaireachta duit féin; ach trína dhéanamh sin, ní bhíonn baol ar bith agat go mbeidh tú sorn go síoraí agus go rachadh sé faoi. Ar an ábhar sin, feictear dom gurbh fhearr gan a bheith ró-ghéar i do fhiosracht ag baint leis an Leviathan seo.





CAIBIDIL 56. De na Pictiúir is Lú Earráideach de Mhíolta Móra, agus na Fíor-Phictiúir de Láithreacha Míolta Móra.

I gcónasc le pictiúir ionraice na míolta móra, is mór an cathú orm anseo a chur isteach ar na scéalta is iontaí fós fúthu atá le fáil i leabhair áirithe, idir shean agus nua-aimseartha, go háirithe i bPliny, Purchas, Hackluyt, Harris, Cuvier, srl. Ach cuirim an t-ábhar sin ar aghaidh.

Níl ach ceithre imlíne foilsithe agam ar an Míol Mór Sperm; Colnett's, Huggins's, Frederick Cuvier's, agus Beale's. Sa chaibidil roimhe seo tá tagairt déanta do Colnett agus Cuvier. Tá Huggins i bhfad níos fearr ná a gceann féin; ach, is mór an seans, is é Beale's an ceann is fearr. Tá gach líníocht de chuid Beale den míol mór seo go maith, ach amháin an figiúr lár sa phictiúr de thrí míolta móra i ndearcadh éagsúla, ag cur teorainn lena dara caibidil. Tá a chuid tosaigh, báid ag ionsaí Míolta Móra Sperm, cé nach bhfuil aon amhras orthu go spreagfadh sé amhras sibhialta roinnt fir parlús, tá sé iontach ceart agus cosúil leis an saol ina éifeacht ghinearálta. Tá comhrian ceart go leor ag cuid de na léaráidí de Mhíolta Móra Sperm in J. Ross Browne; ach tá siad greanta go wretchedly. Ní hé sin an locht áfach.

As an Míol Mór ceart, tá na pictiúir imlíne is fearr i Scoresby; ach tarraingítear iad ar scála ró-bheag chun tuiscint inmhianaithe a chur in iúl. Níl ach pictiúr amháin aige de radharcanna míolta móra, agus is easnamh brónach é seo, mar is ó phictiúir den sórt sin amháin, nuair a dhéantar iad go maith ar chor ar bith, is féidir leat teacht ar aon rud cosúil le smaoineamh fírinneach ar an míol mór beo mar a fheiceann a shealgairí beo é.

Ach, ar an iomlán, ar na cinn is fearr i bhfad, cé nach iad na míolta móra agus na radharcanna míolta móra atá le fáil in áit ar bith, iad na cinn is fearr i bhfad, agus iad tógtha as pictiúir le Garnery amháin. Tá cuid de phreab an bháid gan bhriseadh, agus tarraingítear é díreach ag cothromú ar dhrom an ollphéist; agus ag seasamh sa phreab sin, óir an t-aon splanc amháin sin do-áirighthe aimsire, do fhéachas ar reamhar, leath faoi chuimne ag gob fiuchta borb na míolta móra, agus ag léimneach amhail ó thuisle. Tá gníomh an rud ar fad iontach maith agus fíor. Snámhann an líne-tub leathfholamh ar an bhfarraige bhánaithe; cuaillí adhmaid na gcruitíní doirte go sileacain ann; tá cinnirí na foirne snámha scaipthe faoin míol mór agus iad i gcodarsnacht lena chéile; agus i gcéin dubh stoirme tá an long ag dul i bhfeidhm ar an ardán. B'fhéidir go bhfaighfí locht tromchúiseach ar shonraí anatamaíocha an mhíol mhóir seo, ach lig dó; óir, ar feadh mo shaoil, ní fhéadfainn ceann chomh maith sin a tharraingt.

Sa dara greanadh, tá an bád ag tarraingt taobh le cliathán giolcach an Mhíol Mhíle Dheis mhóir, a rollaíonn a chuid mór dubh fiailí san fharraige mar a bheadh ​​carraig-sleamhnán caonach ó aillte na bPatagánach. Tá a scairdeanna in airde, lán, agus dubh cosúil le súiche; ionnas go mbéadh an oiread sin deatach sa simléar, cheapfá go gcaithfeá suipéar cróga a bheith ag cócaireacht sna putóga móra thíos. Thus glistening for a moment, as slowly it subsided, and sank. Then once more arose, and silently gleamed. It seemed not a whale; and yet is this Moby Dick? thought Daggoo. Again the phantom went down, but on re-appearing once more, with a stiletto-like cry that startled every man from his nod, the negro yelled out—“There! there again! there she breaches! right ahead! The White Whale, the White Whale!”

Upon this, the seamen rushed to the yard-arms, as in swarming-time the bees rush to the boughs. Bare-headed in the sultry sun, Ahab stood on the bowsprit, and with one hand pushed far behind in readiness to wave his orders to the helmsman, cast his eager glance in the direction indicated aloft by the outstretched motionless arm of Daggoo.

Whether the flitting attendance of the one still and solitary jet had gradually worked upon Ahab, so that he was now prepared to connect the ideas of mildness and repose with the first sight of the particular whale he pursued; however this was, or whether his eagerness betrayed him; whichever way it might have been, no sooner did he distinctly perceive the white mass, than with a quick intensity he instantly gave orders for lowering.

The four boats were soon on the water; Ahab's in advance, and all swiftly pulling towards their prey. Soon it went down, and while, with oars suspended, we were awaiting its reappearance, lo! in the same spot where it sank, once more it slowly rose. Almost forgetting for the moment all thoughts of Moby Dick, we now gazed at the most wondrous phenomenon which the secret seas have hitherto revealed to mankind. A vast pulpy mass, furlongs in length and breadth, of a glancing cream-colour, lay floating on the water, innumerable long arms radiating from its centre, and curling and twisting like a nest of anacondas, as if blindly to clutch at any hapless object within reach. No perceptible face or front did it have; no conceivable token of either sensation or instinct; but undulated there on the billows, an unearthly, formless, chance-like apparition of life.

As with a low sucking sound it slowly disappeared again, Starbuck still gazing at the agitated waters where it had sunk, with a wild voice exclaimed—“Almost rather had I seen Moby Dick and fought him, than to have seen thee, thou white ghost!”

“What was it, Sir?” said Flask.

“The great live squid, which, they say, few whale-ships ever beheld, and returned to their ports to tell of it.”

But Ahab said nothing; turning his boat, he sailed back to the vessel; the rest as silently following.

Whatever superstitions the sperm whalemen in general have connected with the sight of this object, certain it is, that a glimpse of it being so very unusual, that circumstance has gone far to invest it with portentousness. So rarely is it beheld, that though one and all of them declare it to be the largest animated thing in the ocean, yet very few of them have any but the most vague ideas concerning its true nature and form; notwithstanding, they believe it to furnish to the sperm whale his only food. For though other species of whales find their food above water, and may be seen by man in the act of feeding, the spermaceti whale obtains his whole food in unknown zones below the surface; and only by inference is it that any one can tell of what, precisely, that food consists. At times, when closely pursued, he will disgorge what are supposed to be the detached arms of the squid; some of them thus exhibited exceeding twenty and thirty feet in length. They fancy that the monster to which these arms belonged ordinarily clings by them to the bed of the ocean; and that the sperm whale, unlike other species, is supplied with teeth in order to attack and tear it.

There seems some ground to imagine that the great Kraken of Bishop Pontoppodan may ultimately resolve itself into Squid. The manner in which the Bishop describes it, as alternately rising and sinking, with some other particulars he narrates, in all this the two correspond. But much abatement is necessary with respect to the incredible bulk he assigns it.

By some naturalists who have vaguely heard rumors of the mysterious creature, here spoken of, it is included among the class of cuttle-fish, to which, indeed, in certain external respects it would seem to belong, but only as the Anak of the tribe.





CHAPTER 60. The Line.

With reference to the whaling scene shortly to be described, as well as for the better understanding of all similar scenes elsewhere presented, I have here to speak of the magical, sometimes horrible whale-line.

The line originally used in the fishery was of the best hemp, slightly vapored with tar, not impregnated with it, as in the case of ordinary ropes; for while tar, as ordinarily used, makes the hemp more pliable to the rope-maker, and also renders the rope itself more convenient to the sailor for common ship use; yet, not only would the ordinary quantity too much stiffen the whale-line for the close coiling to which it must be subjected; but as most seamen are beginning to learn, tar in general by no means adds to the rope's durability or strength, however much it may give it compactness and gloss.

Of late years the Manilla rope has in the American fishery almost entirely superseded hemp as a material for whale-lines; for, though not so durable as hemp, it is stronger, and far more soft and elastic; and I will add (since there is an æsthetics in all things), is much more handsome and becoming to the boat, than hemp. Hemp is a dusky, dark fellow, a sort of Indian; but Manilla is as a golden-haired Circassian to behold.

The whale-line is only two-thirds of an inch in thickness. At first sight, you would not think it so strong as it really is. By experiment its one and fifty yarns will each suspend a weight of one hundred and twenty pounds; so that the whole rope will bear a strain nearly equal to three tons. In length, the common sperm whale-line measures something over two hundred fathoms. Towards the stern of the boat it is spirally coiled away in the tub, not like the worm-pipe of a still though, but so as to form one round, cheese-shaped mass of densely bedded “sheaves,” or layers of concentric spiralizations, without any hollow but the “heart,” or minute vertical tube formed at the axis of the cheese. As the least tangle or kink in the coiling would, in running out, infallibly take somebody's arm, leg, or entire body off, the utmost precaution is used in stowing the line in its tub. Some harpooneers will consume almost an entire morning in this business, carrying the line high aloft and then reeving it downwards through a block towards the tub, so as in the act of coiling to free it from all possible wrinkles and twists.

In the English boats two tubs are used instead of one; the same line being continuously coiled in both tubs. There is some advantage in this; because these twin-tubs being so small they fit more readily into the boat, and do not strain it so much; whereas, the American tub, nearly three feet in diameter and of proportionate depth, makes a rather bulky freight for a craft whose planks are but one half-inch in thickness; for the bottom of the whale-boat is like critical ice, which will bear up a considerable distributed weight, but not very much of a concentrated one. When the painted canvas cover is clapped on the American line-tub, the boat looks as if it were pulling off with a prodigious great wedding-cake to present to the whales.

Both ends of the line are exposed; the lower end terminating in an eye-splice or loop coming up from the bottom against the side of the tub, and hanging over its edge completely disengaged from everything. This arrangement of the lower end is necessary on two accounts. First: In order to facilitate the fastening to it of an additional line from a neighboring boat, in case the stricken whale should sound so deep as to threaten to carry off the entire line originally attached to the harpoon. In these instances, the whale of course is shifted like a mug of ale, as it were, from the one boat to the other; though the first boat always hovers at hand to assist its consort. Second: This arrangement is indispensable for common safety's sake; for were the lower end of the line in any way attached to the boat, and were the whale then to run the line out to the end almost in a single, smoking minute as he sometimes does, he would not stop there, for the doomed boat would infallibly be dragged down after him into the profundity of the sea; and in that case no town-crier would ever find her again.

Before lowering the boat for the chase, the upper end of the line is taken aft from the tub, and passing round the loggerhead there, is again carried forward the entire length of the boat, resting crosswise upon the loom or handle of every man's oar, so that it jogs against his wrist in rowing; and also passing between the men, as they alternately sit at the opposite gunwales, to the leaded chocks or grooves in the extreme pointed prow of the boat, where a wooden pin or skewer the size of a common quill, prevents it from slipping out. From the chocks it hangs in a slight festoon over the bows, and is then passed inside the boat again; and some ten or twenty fathoms (called box-line) being coiled upon the box in the bows, it continues its way to the gunwale still a little further aft, and is then attached to the short-warp—the rope which is immediately connected with the harpoon; but previous to that connexion, the short-warp goes through sundry mystifications too tedious to detail.

Thus the whale-line folds the whole boat in its complicated coils, twisting and writhing around it in almost every direction. All the oarsmen are involved in its perilous contortions; so that to the timid eye of the landsman, they seem as Indian jugglers, with the deadliest snakes sportively festooning their limbs. Nor can any son of mortal woman, for the first time, seat himself amid those hempen intricacies, and while straining his utmost at the oar, bethink him that at any unknown instant the harpoon may be darted, and all these horrible contortions be put in play like ringed lightnings; he cannot be thus circumstanced without a shudder that makes the very marrow in his bones to quiver in him like a shaken jelly. Yet habit—strange thing! what cannot habit accomplish?—Gayer sallies, more merry mirth, better jokes, and brighter repartees, you never heard over your mahogany, than you will hear over the half-inch white cedar of the whale-boat, when thus hung in hangman's nooses; and, like the six burghers of Calais before King Edward, the six men composing the crew pull into the jaws of death, with a halter around every neck, as you may say.

Perhaps a very little thought will now enable you to account for those repeated whaling disasters—some few of which are casually chronicled—of this man or that man being taken out of the boat by the line, and lost. For, when the line is darting out, to be seated then in the boat, is like being seated in the midst of the manifold whizzings of a steam-engine in full play, when every flying beam, and shaft, and wheel, is grazing you. It is worse; for you cannot sit motionless in the heart of these perils, because the boat is rocking like a cradle, and you are pitched one way and the other, without the slightest warning; and only by a certain self-adjusting buoyancy and simultaneousness of volition and action, can you escape being made a Mazeppa of, and run away with where the all-seeing sun himself could never pierce you out.

Again: as the profound calm which only apparently precedes and prophesies of the storm, is perhaps more awful than the storm itself; for, indeed, the calm is but the wrapper and envelope of the storm; and contains it in itself, as the seemingly harmless rifle holds the fatal powder, and the ball, and the explosion; so the graceful repose of the line, as it silently serpentines about the oarsmen before being brought into actual play—this is a thing which carries more of true terror than any other aspect of this dangerous affair. But why say more? All men live enveloped in whale-lines. All are born with halters round their necks; but it is only when caught in the swift, sudden turn of death, that mortals realize the silent, subtle, ever-present perils of life. And if you be a philosopher, though seated in the whale-boat, you would not at heart feel one whit more of terror, than though seated before your evening fire with a poker, and not a harpoon, by your side.
