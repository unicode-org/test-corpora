Mahse, chu thil chu Cape Horn tuipui kama a chunga thil thleng chungchangah chuan engmah a awm lo, hun rei tak kal ta, ni thum leh zan thum chhung thi ang maia a mut lai khan; Santa-a maicham hmaa Spaniard nena an skrimmage thihna thlen thei chungchang engmah a awm lo?—chu chu engmah i hre lo, eh? A chil chhak chhunga tangkarua calabash chungchang engmah a awm lo em ni? Tin, a ke a hloh chungchang pawh engmah a awm lo, hrilhlawkna angin. An chungchang leh thil dang chungchang thu pakhat mah in hre lo em ni, eh? Ni lo, in tih ka ring lo; engtin nge in theih ang? Tuin nge hria ang? Nantucket zawng zawng ni lo, ka ring. Mahse, hows'ever, mayhap, ke chungchang sawi in hre tawh a, a hloh dan pawh; aye, chu chu in hre tawh a ni, ka ti ngam. Oh yes, that everyone knows a’most—ka sawi tum chu ke pakhat chauh a ni tih an hria a; tin, parmacetti pakhatin a dang chu a la chhuak tih pawh a sawi bawk.”

“Ka thianpa,” ka ti a, “he thu i thusawi zawng zawng hi eng nge a nih, ka hre lo va, ka ngaihtuah vak lo bawk; ka ngaihdan chuan i lu chu tlem a chhiat phah ngei ang. Mahse, Captain Ahaba, chuta chu lawng, Pequod chungchang i sawi a nih chuan, ka hrilh ang che, a ke hloh chungchang hi ka hre vek a ni.”

Pequod-a lam hawia kut zungtang nghat leh mitmeng nghing chungin, diltu ang maia mi hriat loh chu rei vak lo a ding a, mumang buai tak nei ang maiin a ding a; tichuan tlem a intan a, a hawi let a:—“In thawn tawh a ni lawm ni? Paper-ah hming tarlan a ni em? Awle, a tha e, eng nge sign chu, sign a ni; leh eng nge ni tur, a ni ang; tin, chutah pawh chuan, a ni lo mai thei, a tawpah chuan. Eng pawh nise, a fix vek a, a ruahman fel vek tawh a'ready; tin, lawng khalhtu ṭhenkhat emaw, mi dang emaw pawh a zui ve ngei tur a ni, ka ring; chutiang bawkin hengte hi mipa dang ang bawkin, Pathianin khawngaih rawh u! Zing chu nangni lawngpuite u, zing; sawi theih loh vante chuan malsawm rawh u; Ka titawp che hi pawi ka ti hle mai.”

“Hetah hian en teh, thianpa,” ka ti a, “eng thil pawimawh min hrilh tur i neih chuan, chhuak rawh; mahse, min bamboozle mai mai a nih chuan i game-ah i tisual a ni; chu chauh chu ka sawi tur a ni.”

“Tin, a sawi tha hle bawk a, chutianga chap pakhatin a sawi chhuah ka hriat hi ka duh ber a; a tâna mipa mai i ni—nangni angte hi. Zing chu nangni lawngpuite u, zing! Awh! chuta in thlen hunah chuan pakhat mah siam lo turin ka thutlukna ka siam tawh tih hrilh rawh.”

“Ah, ka thian duh tak, chutiang chuan min bum thei lo—min bum thei lo. Mipa tan chuan thuruk ropui tak nei ang maia inlan hi khawvela thil awlsam ber a ni.”

“Zing lamah nangni lawngpuite u, zing.”

“Zing a ni tawh,” ka ti a, “Lo kal rawh, Queequeg, he mi â hi i kalsan ang u. Mahse, chawlhsan rawh, i hming min hrilh rawh, i ti ang em?”

“Elia.”

Elija! ka ngaihtuah a, kan kal ta a, kan pahnih chuan comment in, kan fashion angin, he lawng khalhtu upa hring hring chungah hian; tin, humbug tih loh chu engmah a ni lo tih a pawm a, bugbear nih a tum bawk. Mahse, yard za aia tam pawh kan kal lo mai thei, corner pakhat hawi tura chance kan neih lai khan, chutianga ka tih lai chuan hnunglam ka hawi a, hla takah ni mah se, Elija tih loh chu tu nge min zui tih hmuh tur awm ang. Engtin emaw tak chuan a hmuh chuan min tithinur hle a, Queequeg hnenah chuan a hnung lama a awm thu engmah ka sawi lo va, ka comrade nen chuan ka kal ta a, chu mi hriat loh chuan kan tih ang chiah kha a rawn hawi leh dawn em tih ngaihtuah chungin ka kal ta a ni. A ti a; tin, chutah chuan min dogging niin ka hria a, mahse eng thil tum nen nge ka nunna atan ka ngaihtuah theih loh. He dinhmun hi a thusawi chiang lo, half-hinting, half-revealing, shrouded sort of te nen chuan tunah chuan ka chhungah thilmak leh half-aprehension chi hrang hrang a lo piang ta a, Pequod nen pawh a inzawm vek a leh Captain Ahaba te; tin, a ke a hloh tawh chu; leh Cape Horn fit te pawh; tin, tangkarua kawr chu; tin, Captain Peleg-a’n a chunga a sawi, a hma ni lawka lawng ka chhuahsan lai khan; leh squaw Tistig-a hrilhlawkna te; tin, lawnga kal tûrin kan inbuatsaih tawhna chu; tin, thil dang za chuang zet pawh a awm bawk.

He ragged Elijah hian min dogging tak tak em tih chu ka lungawi tir ka tum a, chutiang tumna nen chuan Queequeg nen chuan kawng kan zawh a, chu lam hawi chuan kan ke pen chu ka zawh leh ta a ni. Mahse Elija chuan min hre lo ang maiin a kal pel ta a. Hei hian min ti hahdam a; tin, vawi khat chu, a tawpah chuan ka ngaihdan angin ka thinlungah chuan humbug tiin ka sawi chhuak leh ta a.





BUNG 20. All Astir.

Ni khat emaw ni hnih emaw a liam a, Pequod-ah chuan thiltih ropui tak tak a awm a. Sail hlui te chu siamthat a ni mai ni lovin, lawng thar a rawn lut a, canvas bolt te, rigging coil te pawh a lo thleng bawk a; a tawi zawngin, engkim hian lawng inbuatsaihna chu a tawp nghal vat tih a tilang a ni. Captain Peleg-a chu tuipuiah a kal ngai lo va, a kal ngai lo va, mahse a wigwam-ah chuan kut chu ngun takin a thlir reng a: Bildad-a chuan dawrah thil lei leh pek zawng zawng a ti vek a tin, hold leh rigging-a thawk mipa te chu zan rei tak hnuah pawh an thawk a.

Queequeg-a’n article-te a sign hnu ni khatah chuan lawng company dinna inn zawng zawngah thu an sawi a, an rilru chu zan hmain lawngah an chuang vek tur a ni tih thu an sawi a, chu chu lawng chu eng chen nge a kal thuai theih ang tih sawi theih a ni lo. Chuvangin Queequeg nen chuan kan thangah kan chhuk thla a, mahse, a tawp ber thlenga tuipui kamah mut kan tum ta a ni. Mahse heng thil thlengah hian hun rei tak chhung hriattirna an pe fo niin a lang a, lawng hi ni engemaw zat chu a kal lo. Mahse, thil mak a ni lo; tih tur tha tak a awm a, thil engzat nge ngaihtuah tur tih pawh sawi theih a ni lo, Pequod chu a thuam kim hma chuan.

Thil tam tak—khum te, sauce-pan te, chemte leh fork te, shovel leh tong te, napkin te, nut-cracker te, leh a dangte chu in enkawlna sumdawnna atan chuan a pawimawh hle tih chu mi zawng zawngin kan hre vek a ni. Chutiang bawkin whale man pawh a ni a, chu chuan tuifinriat zau takah kum thum chhung in enkawl a ngai a, chu chu thil zuartu, costermonger, doctor, chhang siamtu, leh banker zawng zawng atanga hla tak a ni. Tin, hei hi sumdawng lawngah pawh a dik a, mahse engti kawng mahin whaleman-te ang kha a ni lo. Whale man hun rei tak a nih bakah, sangha mantute thubuai siamsakna atana thil danglam tak tam tak te, leh lawng chawlhna hmun hla tak tak, an tlawh fo thinna hmuna thlak theih lohna bakah hian lawng zawng zawng zingah hian whale mantu lawngte hi an langsar ber tih hriat reng a ngai a ni accident chi hrang hrangah te, a bik takin lawnga zin hlawhtlinna innghahna ber thilte tihchhiat leh hlohnaah te. Chuvangin, lawng spare te, spare spars te, leh spare line leh harpoon te, leh spare everythings te, almost, mahse spare Captain leh duplicate ship te.

Island kan thlen hun lai khan Pequod dahkhawmna rit ber chu zawhfel a ni tawh a; a bawngsa, chhang, tui, fuel, leh thir hrui leh tiangte awmna. Mahse, a hmaa kan sawi tawh ang khan, hun engemaw chen chu thil lian leh te, divers odds leh ends te chu fetching and carrying on board-ah a awm chhunzawm zel a.

Hetiang fetching leh carrying titu zinga lian ber chu Captain Bildad-a farnu a ni a, chu chu nula upa thau tak, rilru tumruh tak leh chau lo tak, mahse withal very kindhearted, a tumruh ang maia lang, a puih theih chuan Pequod-ah hian engmah tlakchhamna hmuh tur a awm lo , vawi khat fair taka tuipuiah a thlen hnuah. Hun khat chu steward-a pantry atan pickles jar kengin lawngah a rawn chuang thin a; vawi khat chu chief mate-a dawhkan atan quill bunch khat a keng a, chutah chuan a log a dah a; a third time with a roll of flannel for the small of some one's rheumatic back. Never did any woman better deserve her name, which was Charity—Aunt Charity, as everybody called her. And like a sister of charity did this charitable Aunt Charity bustle about hither and thither, ready to turn her hand and heart to anything that promised to yield safety, comfort, and consolation to all on board a ship in which her beloved brother Bildad was concerned, and in which she herself owned a score or two of well-saved dollars.

But it was startling to see this excellent hearted Quakeress coming on board, as she did the last day, with a long oil-ladle in one hand, and a still longer whaling lance in the other. Nor was Bildad himself nor Captain Peleg at all backward. As for Bildad, he carried about with him a long list of the articles needed, and at every fresh arrival, down went his mark opposite that article upon the paper. Every once in a while Peleg came hobbling out of his whalebone den, roaring at the men down the hatchways, roaring up to the riggers at the mast-head, and then concluded by roaring back into his wigwam.

During these days of preparation, Queequeg and I often visited the craft, and as often I asked about Captain Ahab, and how he was, and when he was going to come on board his ship. To these questions they would answer, that he was getting better and better, and was expected aboard every day; meantime, the two captains, Peleg and Bildad, could attend to everything necessary to fit the vessel for the voyage. If I had been downright honest with myself, I would have seen very plainly in my heart that I did but half fancy being committed this way to so long a voyage, without once laying my eyes on the man who was to be the absolute dictator of it, so soon as the ship sailed out upon the open sea. But when a man suspects any wrong, it sometimes happens that if he be already involved in the matter, he insensibly strives to cover up his suspicions even from himself. And much this way it was with me. I said nothing, and tried to think nothing.

At last it was given out that some time next day the ship would certainly sail. So next morning, Queequeg and I took a very early start.





CHAPTER 21. Going Aboard.

It was nearly six o'clock, but only grey imperfect misty dawn, when we drew nigh the wharf.

“There are some sailors running ahead there, if I see right,” said I to Queequeg, “it can't be shadows; she's off by sunrise, I guess; come on!”

“Avast!” cried a voice, whose owner at the same time coming close behind us, laid a hand upon both our shoulders, and then insinuating himself between us, stood stooping forward a little, in the uncertain twilight, strangely peering from Queequeg to me. It was Elijah.

“Going aboard?”

“Hands off, will you,” said I.

“Lookee here,” said Queequeg, shaking himself, “go 'way!”

“Ain't going aboard, then?”

“Yes, we are,” said I, “but what business is that of yours? Do you know, Mr. Elijah, that I consider you a little impertinent?”

“No, no, no; I wasn't aware of that,” said Elijah, slowly and wonderingly looking from me to Queequeg, with the most unaccountable glances.

“Elijah,” said I, “you will oblige my friend and me by withdrawing. We are going to the Indian and Pacific Oceans, and would prefer not to be detained.”

“Ye be, be ye? Coming back afore breakfast?”

“He's cracked, Queequeg,” said I, “come on.”

“Holloa!” cried stationary Elijah, hailing us when we had removed a few paces.

“Never mind him,” said I, “Queequeg, come on.”

But he stole up to us again, and suddenly clapping his hand on my shoulder, said—“Did ye see anything looking like men going towards that ship a while ago?”

Struck by this plain matter-of-fact question, I answered, saying, “Yes, I thought I did see four or five men; but it was too dim to be sure.”

“Very dim, very dim,” said Elijah. “Morning to ye.”

Once more we quitted him; but once more he came softly after us; and touching my shoulder again, said, “See if you can find 'em now, will ye?

“Find who?”

“Morning to ye! morning to ye!” he rejoined, again moving off. “Oh! I was going to warn ye against—but never mind, never mind—it's all one, all in the family too;—sharp frost this morning, ain't it? Good-bye to ye. Shan't see ye again very soon, I guess; unless it's before the Grand Jury.” And with these cracked words he finally departed, leaving me, for the moment, in no small wonderment at his frantic impudence.

At last, stepping on board the Pequod, we found everything in profound quiet, not a soul moving. The cabin entrance was locked within; the hatches were all on, and lumbered with coils of rigging. Going forward to the forecastle, we found the slide of the scuttle open. Seeing a light, we went down, and found only an old rigger there, wrapped in a tattered pea-jacket. He was thrown at whole length upon two chests, his face downwards and inclosed in his folded arms. The profoundest slumber slept upon him.

“Those sailors we saw, Queequeg, where can they have gone to?” said I, looking dubiously at the sleeper. But it seemed that, when on the wharf, Queequeg had not at all noticed what I now alluded to; hence I would have thought myself to have been optically deceived in that matter, were it not for Elijah's otherwise inexplicable question. But I beat the thing down; and again marking the sleeper, jocularly hinted to Queequeg that perhaps we had best sit up with the body; telling him to establish himself accordingly. He put his hand upon the sleeper's rear, as though feeling if it was soft enough; and then, without more ado, sat quietly down there.

“Gracious! Queequeg, don't sit there,” said I.

“Oh! perry dood seat,” said Queequeg, “my country way; won't hurt him face.”

“Face!” said I, “call that his face? very benevolent countenance then; but how hard he breathes, he's heaving himself; get off, Queequeg, you are heavy, it's grinding the face of the poor. Get off, Queequeg! Look, he'll twitch you off soon. I wonder he don't wake.”

Queequeg removed himself to just beyond the head of the sleeper, and lighted his tomahawk pipe. I sat at the feet. We kept the pipe passing over the sleeper, from one to the other. Meanwhile, upon questioning him in his broken fashion, Queequeg gave me to understand that, in his land, owing to the absence of settees and sofas of all sorts, the king, chiefs, and great people generally, were in the custom of fattening some of the lower orders for ottomans; and to furnish a house comfortably in that respect, you had only to buy up eight or ten lazy fellows, and lay them round in the piers and alcoves. Besides, it was very convenient on an excursion; much better than those garden-chairs which are convertible into walking-sticks; upon occasion, a chief calling his attendant, and desiring him to make a settee of himself under a spreading tree, perhaps in some damp marshy place.

While narrating these things, every time Queequeg received the tomahawk from me, he flourished the hatchet-side of it over the sleeper's head.

“What's that for, Queequeg?”

“Perry easy, kill-e; oh! perry easy!”

He was going on with some wild reminiscences about his tomahawk-pipe, which, it seemed, had in its two uses both brained his foes and soothed his soul, when we were directly attracted to the sleeping rigger. The strong vapor now completely filling the contracted hole, it began to tell upon him. He breathed with a sort of muffledness; then seemed troubled in the nose; then revolved over once or twice; then sat up and rubbed his eyes.

“Holloa!” he breathed at last, “who be ye smokers?”

“Shipped men,” answered I, “when does she sail?”

“Aye, aye, ye are going in her, be ye? She sails to-day. The Captain came aboard last night.”

“What Captain?—Ahab?”

“Who but him indeed?”

I was going to ask him some further questions concerning Ahab, when we heard a noise on deck.

“Holloa! Starbuck's astir,” said the rigger. “He's a lively chief mate, that; good man, and a pious; but all alive now, I must turn to.” And so saying he went on deck, and we followed.

It was now clear sunrise. Soon the crew came on board in twos and threes; the riggers bestirred themselves; the mates were actively engaged; and several of the shore people were busy in bringing various last things on board. Meanwhile Captain Ahab remained invisibly enshrined within his cabin.
