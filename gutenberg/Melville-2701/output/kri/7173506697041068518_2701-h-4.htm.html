As Queequeg in Ramadan, ɔ Fasting and Humiliation, bin fɔ kɔntinyu ɔl di de, a nɔ bin disayd fɔ distɔb am te towards nait-foll; bikɔs a valyu di big big rɛspɛkt fɔ ɔlman in rilijɔn ɔbligayshɔn, a nɔ ɛva tink bɔt aw i kɔmik, ɛn a nɔ bin ebul fɔ si am na mi at fɔ ɔndavalyu ivin wan kɔngrigeshɔn we gɛt ant dɛn we de wɔship tod-stɔl; ɔ dɛn ɔda tin dɛn we de na sɔm pat dɛn na wi wɔl, we wit wan digri fɔ futman we nɔ ɛva bi na ɔda planɛt dɛn, de butu bifo di bɔdi fɔ wan pɔsin we dɔn day we gɛt land jɔs bikɔs ɔf di bɔku bɔku prɔpati dɛn we dɛn stil gɛt ɛn rɛnt insay in nem.

A go mek di bed afta a dɔn it brɛf, ɛn dɛn lɔk di domɔt; ɛn nɔto maws fɔ yɛri; ɛn i jɔs dɔn so kwayɛt frɔm da tɛm de. “Wud-os!” a kray se, “us we fɔ go de? Rɔn fɔ Gɔd in sek, ɛn kam wit sɔntin fɔ pul di domɔt—di aks!—di aks! i dɔn gɛt strok; dipen pan am!”—ɛn so a bin de se a bin de rɔsh ɔp stej bak ɛmti an we nɔ gɛt wan we, we Misis Husi put di mɔstad-pɔt ɛn vinega-kru, ɛn di wan ol kastɔ na in fes.

PAPA GƆD sɔri fɔ in gɔst! Wetin na da nɔys de we de de? Yu, yɔŋ man, avast de!”

Ɛn we i rɔn go afta mi, i kech mi as a de tray bak fɔ fos fɔ opin di domɔt.

“A nɔ de alaw am; A nɔ go mek mi ples pwɛl. Go fɔ di lɔksmit, wan de we de lɛk wan mayl frɔm ya. Bɔt avast!” put in an insay in sayd-pɔkit, “na wan ki de we go fit, a tink se; lɛ wi si.” Ɛn wit dat, i tɔn am na di lɔk; bɔt, alas! Queequeg in supliment bolt bin stil de we dɛn nɔ pul am insay.

Naw, as a bin don hint bifo, a no get eni objekshon to eni pesin rilijon, i bi wetin e bi, so long as dat pesin no kil o insul eni oda pesin, bikos dat oda pesin no biliv am tu. CHAPTER 18. In Mak.

As wi bin de waka dɔŋ di ɛnd pan di wɔf go na di ship, Kwik de kɛr in harpun, Kpɛntin Pɛlɛg wit in grɔf vɔys bin de prez wi lawd wan frɔm in wigwam, se i nɔ bin sɔprayz se mi padi na pɔsin we de it pipul dɛn, ɛn mɔ i anɔys se i lɛf nɔ pipul dɛn we de it pipul dɛn na da kraft de, pas nɔmɔ dɛn bin dɔn mek dɛn pepa dɛn bifo tɛm.

Pɛlɛg se: “Nɔ, ɛn i nɔ baptayz rayt bak, ɔ i bin fɔ dɔn was sɔm pan da dɛbul in blu kɔmɔt na in fes.”

Bildad kray se: “Naw, yu tink se dis Filistin na mɛmba ɔltɛm na Dikon Ditarɔnɔmi in mitin? A nɔ ɛva si am de go de, ɛn a kin pas am ɛvri Masta in de.”

A se: “A nɔ no natin bɔt Dikon Ditarɔnɔmi ɔ in mitin; “ɔl wetin a no na, se Kwik we de ya na mɛmba we dɛn bɔn na di Fɔs Kɔngrigeshɔn Chɔch. Insɛf na dikon, Kwik na dikon.”

Bildad bin tɔk tranga wan se: “Yɔŋ man, yu de skaylark wit mi—ɛksplen yusɛf, yu yɔŋ Itayt. Us chɔch yu min? ansa mi.”

We a si se dɛn dɔn push mi so tranga wan, a ansa. “A min, sa, di sem ol Katolik Chɔch we mi ɛn yu, ɛn Kpɛntin Pɛlɛg de, ɛn Kwik ya, ɛn wi ɔl, ɛn ɛvri mama in pikin ɛn sol we wi de pan; di big ɛn sote go Fɔs Kɔngrigeshɔn fɔ dis wan ol wɔl we de wɔship; wi ɔl de pan dat; na sɔm pan wi nɔmɔ de cherish sɔm queer crotchets no ways fɔ tɔch di grand biliv; we wi ɔl jɔyn an.”

Pɛlɛg kray, we i kam nia am, se: “Splais, yu min se yu splay an.” “Yɔŋ man, i bɛtɛ fɔ mek yu ship fɔ mishɔnari, instead fɔ yuz an fɔ di fɔs-mast; A nɔ ɛva yɛri sɛmin we bɛtɛ pas dis. Dikon Ditaronomi—wetin mek Fada Mapple insɛf nɔ bin ebul fɔ win am, ɛn i dɔn kɔl sɔntin. Kam insay di bot, kam insay di bot; nɔ ɛva tink bɔt di pepa dɛn. A se, tɛl Quohog de—wetin na dat yu kɔl am? tɛl Quohog fɔ step along. Bay di big ankɔ, wetin na harpun i dɔn gɛt de! luk lɛk gud tin dɛn we; ɛn i de handle am lɛk aw i rayt. A se, Quohog, ɔ ɛnitin we yu nem, yu ɛva tinap na di ed fɔ wɛl-bɔt? yu bin ɛva bit fish?”

Witout tok eni wod, Queequeg, in in wild sort of way, jomp pan di bulwarks, frɔm de insay di bow fɔ wan pan di wɛl-bot dɛn we hang na di sayd; ɛn afta dat, i brayt in lɛft ni, ɛn put in harpun, i ala sɔm kayn we lɛk dis:—

“Cap’ain, yu si am smɔl drop tar pan wata dere? Yu si am? wɛl, spɔz am wan wɛl yay, wɛl, den!” ɛn i tek shap shap aim pan am, i dart di ayɛn rayt oba di ol Bildad in brayt brim, klin akɔdin to di ship in dɛk, ɛn nak di tar spat we de shayn usay pipul dɛn nɔ go si am.

“Naw,” Kwik se, we i de pul di layn kwayɛt wan, “spos-ee am wɛl-e ay; wetin mek, papa wɛl dɔn day.”

Pɛlɛg, we na in patna, we bin sɔprayz fɔ si di say we di hapun we de flay bin de nia, bin dɔn rɔnawe go na di kabin gangway, bin tɔk se: “Kwik, Bildad.” “Kwik, a se, yu Bildad, ɛn tek di ship in pepa dɛn. Wi fɔ gɛt Ɛdjɔg de, a min Quohog, insay wan pan wi bot dɛn. Luk yu, Quohog, wi go gi yu di naynti lay, ɛn dat pas ɛni ɔda tɛm we dɛn gi yu wan harpooneer yet aut na Nantucket.”

So dɔŋ wi go insay di kebin, ɛn a rili gladi fɔ no se i nɔ tu te, dɛn rayt Kwik in nem pan di sem ship in kɔmni we misɛf bin de pan.

We ɔl di prɛliminari dɛn dɔn ɛn Pɛlɛg dɔn rɛdi ɔltin fɔ sayn, i tɔn to mi ɛn se, “A guess, Quohog de nɔ sabi rayt, nɔto so? A se, Quohog, blast yu! yu de sayn yu nem ɔ yu de mek yu mak?”

Bɔt we dɛn aks dis kwɛstyɔn, Kwik, we bin dɔn tek pat tu ɔ tri tɛm bifo dis kayn sɛrimɔni, nɔ bin luk lɛk se i shem; bɔt i tek di pen we dɛn bin de gi, we dɛn kɔpi pan di pepa, na di rayt ples, we na wan ɛksaktɔ kɔmpin fɔ wan kwayɛt rawnd figa we dɛn tatu pan in an; so dat tru Kpɛntin Pɛlɛg in traŋa mistek we i tɔch in apil, i tinap sɔntin lɛk dis:—

Quohog we dɛn kɔl Quohog. in X mak.

Insay da tɛm de, Kpɛntin Bildad bin sidɔm wit ɔl in at ɛn tinap tranga wan de luk Kwik, ɛn las las i grap wit ɔl in at ɛn fumbl insay di big big poket dɛn na in brayt-skata drab kot, i pul wan bɔndɛl trakt dɛn, ɛn pik wan we gɛt di taytul “Di Lata De we De Kam; ɔ No Taym fɔ Lɔs,” put am na Kwik in an, dɔn i ol dɛn ɛn di buk wit ɔl tu in yon, luk in yay wit ɔl in at, ɛn se, “Daknɛs pikin, a fɔ du mi wok bay yu; Mi na pat ɔna fɔ dis ship, ɛn a fil se a bisin bɔt ɔl di wan dɛn we de wok na in sol; if yu stil de klin yu Pegan we, we a de fred bad bad wan, a de beg yu, nɔ fɔ bi Belial slevman fɔ aye. Spurn di aydɔl Bɛl, ɛn di hideous dragon; tɔn frɔm di wamat we gɛt fɔ kam; tink yu yay, a de se; O! gudnɛs we gɛt sɔri-at! stɛp kɔmɔt na di ol we gɛt faya!”

Sɔntin na di sɔl si yet bin de insay ol Bildad in langwej, we dɛn miks difrɛn we wit Skripchɔ ɛn domestik wɔd dɛn.

Pɛlɛg kray se: “Avast de, avast de, Bildad, avast naw de pwɛl wi harpuoneer.” “Di wan dɛn we de mek harpoon we de wɔship Gɔd nɔ kin ɛva mek gud pipul dɛn we de travul—i kin pul di shak kɔmɔt pan dɛn; no harpooneer na wert wan straw we aint priti sharkish. Na bin yɔŋ Nat Swaine, we bin de bifo pan di bot-hɛda we bin gɛt maynd pas ɔl di Nantucket ɛn di Vineyard; i bin jɔyn di mitin, ɛn i nɔ ɛva kam fɔ bi gud. I bin so fred bɔt in plaguy sol, dat i bin shrink ɛn shere away frɔm wɛl, bikɔs i bin de fred fɔ afta-klap, if i gɛt stov ɛn go to Davy Jones.”

“Pɛlɛg fɔ tɔk! Pɛlɛg!” Bildad se, as i es in yay ɛn an, “yu sɛf, lɛk misɛf, yu dɔn si bɔku tɛm we denja; yu no, Pɛlɛg, wetin i min fɔ fred fɔ day; aw, so, yu go ebul fɔ prat insay dis kayn we we nɔ de mek Gɔd fred. Yu de biliv yu yon at, Pɛlɛg. Tɛl mi, we dis sem Pɛkud we de ya bin gɛt in tri mast dɛn ovabɔd insay da tayfun de na Jepan, da sem voyage de we yu bin go mate wit Kpɛntin Eab, yu nɔ bin tink bɔt Day ɛn di Jɔjmɛnt da tɛm de?”

Pɛlɛg bin ala se: “Una yɛri am, yɛri am naw,” ɛn i bin de mach krɔs di kebin, ɛn put in an dɛn fa fawe dɔŋ insay in poket,—“una ɔl yɛri am. Tink bɔt dat! We ɛvri mɔnt wi bin de tink se di ship go sink! Day ɛn di Jɔjmɛnt da tɛm de? Wetin? Wit ɔl di tri mast dɛn we de mek dis kayn tɛnda we go de sote go agens di sayd; ɛn ɔl di si we de brok oba wi, bifo ɛn biɛn. Yu tink bɔt Day ɛn di Jɔjmɛnt da tɛm de? Nɔ! no tɛm fɔ tink bɔt Day da tɛm de. Laif na bin wetin mi ɛn Kpɛntin Eab bin de tink bɔt; ɛn aw fɔ sev ɔl an—aw fɔ rig juri-mas—aw fɔ go insay di pɔt we de nia yu; na dat a bin de tink bɔt.”

Bildad nɔ se igen, bɔt i bɔtin in kot, i stalk na di dɛk, usay wi fala am. Na de i tinap, i bin rili kwayɛt wan luk sɔm pipul dɛn we de mek sel we bin de mek wan top-sel na di wes. Naw ɛn naw i kin butu fɔ pik wan pat, ɔ sev wan ɛnd pan tar we gɛt tar, we if nɔto dat, i go dɔn west.





CHAPTER 19. Di Prɔfɛt.

“Una we de wok wit una ship, una dɔn ship insay da ship de?”

Mi ɛn Kwik bin jɔs kɔmɔt na di Pɛkud, ɛn wi bin de rɔn go fa frɔm di wata, fɔ di tɛm we ɛni wan pan dɛn bin de ɔkup wit in yon tink, we wan strenja bin put di wɔd dɛn we de ɔp to wi, we, we i stɔp bifo wi, lɛvɛl in big big fɔs finga pan di vessel we dɛn de tɔk bɔt. I bin jɔs wɛr shabbily klos insay jakit we dɔn fayn ɛn trawza we gɛt pat; wan rag we gɛt blak hankɛf we de invɛst in nɛk. Wan smɛl-poks we bin de rɔsh bin dɔn flɔ ɔlsay na in fes, ɛn lɛf am lɛk di kɔmplikɛt rib bed we wan big big briz de mek, we di wata we de rɔsh dɔn dray.

“Una dɔn ship insay am?” i bin ripit.

“Yu min di ship Pequod, a tink se,” a se, as a de tray fɔ gɛt smɔl mɔ tɛm fɔ luk am we nɔ go ambɔg am.

“Aye, di Pequod—dat ship de,” i se, i pul in wan ol an bak, dɔn i push am kwik kwik wan kɔmɔt stret frɔm am, wit di fiks bayonet we in pɔynt finga de dart ful wan pan di tin.

A se: “Yɛs, wi jɔs dɔn sayn di atikul dɛn.”

“Ɛnitin dɔŋ de bɔt una sol dɛn?”

“Bɔt wetin?”

“O, sɔntɛm yu nɔ gɛt ɛni wan,” i se kwik kwik wan. “No mata though, a sabi plenti chaps we hav’n’t get eni,—gud lak to 'em; ɛn dɛn ɔl de bɛtɛ fɔ am. Sɔl na wan kayn fayv wil to wagon.”

“Wetin yu de jab, shipmet?” na in a se.

“ I dɔn gɛt inof, bɔt, fɔ mek ɔl di dɛfisiɛns dɛn we tan lɛk dat na ɔda chaps,” di strenja bin tɔk wantɛm wantɛm, ɛn put wan nervɔs ɛmpɛshmɛnt pan di wɔd i .

A se: “Kwik, lɛ wi go; dis felo dɔn brok loose frɔm sɔmsay; i de tɔk bɔt sɔntin ɛn pɔsin we wi nɔ no.”

"Lɛf!" di strenja kray. “Yu se tru—yu nɔ si Ol Tɛnda yet, nɔto so?”

“Udat na Ol Tɛnda?” a se, i rivet bak wit di krayse earnestness of in manner.

“Kaptɛn Eab.”

"Wetin! di kapten fɔ wi ship, di Pequod?”

“Aye, bitwin sɔm pan wi ol selɔ chaps, i de go wit da nem de. Una nɔ si am yet, nɔto so?”

“Nɔ, wi nɔ dɔn du am. Dɛn se i sik, bɔt i de wɛl, ɛn i go fayn bak i nɔ go te igen.”

“Ol rayt bak bifo tɛm!” di strenja laf, wit wan solemnly derisive sort of laf. “Una luk; we Kpɛntin Eab go fayn, na da tɛm de dis lɛft an we a gɛt go fayn; nɔto bifo.”

“Wetin yu no bɔt am?”

“Wetin dɛn tɛl yu bɔt am? Se dat!”

“Dɛn nɔ bin tɛl bɔku tin bɔt am; na in nɔmɔ a dɔn yɛri se in na gud pɔsin we de fɛn wɛl, ɛn na gud kapten to in kru.”

“Dat na tru, dat na tru—yes, ɔl tu na tru inof. Bɔt yu fɔ jomp we i gi ɔda lɔ. Step ɛn grɔmbul; grɔmbul ɛn go—na dat na di wɔd wit Kpɛntin Eab. Bɔt natin bɔt da tin de we apin to am nia Kep Hɔn, lɔng tɛm bifo, we i bin ledɔm lɛk pɔsin we dɔn day fɔ tri dez ɛn nɛt; natin bɔt da deadly skrimmage wit di Spanish man bifo di ɔlta na Santa?—yu nɔ yɛri natin bɔt dat, eh? Natin nɔ de bɔt di silva kala we i spit insay? Ɛn natin nɔ de bɔt aw i lɔs in leg las trip, akɔdin to di prɔfɛsi. Yu no yehri wan wod boht dem mata en sohmtin mo, eh? Nɔ, a nɔ tink se una bin du am; aw una go ebul fɔ du dat? Udat no am? Nɔto ɔl Nantucket, a guess. Bɔt aw, mayhap, una dɔn yɛri tɛl bɔt di leg, ɛn aw i lɔs am; aye, una don yehri boht dat, a dea se. Oh yes, se ɔlman no a’most—a min se dɛn no se na wan leg nɔmɔ i bi; ɛn se wan parmacetti pul di ɔda wan.”

A se: “Mi padi, wetin ɔl dis gibberish we yu de tɔk de tɔk bɔt, a nɔ no, ɛn a nɔ bisin tumɔs; bikɔs i tan lɛk to mi se yu fɔ dɔn pwɛl smɔl na yu ed. Bɔt if yu de tɔk bɔt Kpɛntin Eab, bɔt da ship de de, di Pɛkud, den lɛ a tɛl yu, se a no ɔltin bɔt di lɔs we in leg lɔs.”

“ Ɔltin bɔt am, eh—shɔ se yu du am?—ɔltin?”

“I rili shɔ.”

Wit finga we dɛn pɔynt ɛn in yay lɛvɛl pan di Pɛkud, di strenja we tan lɛk begman tinap fɔ smɔl tɛm, lɛk se i de tink bɔt trɔbul; dɔn i bigin smɔl, tɔn ɛn se:—“Yu dɔn ship, nɔto so? Nem dɛn we de dɔŋ na di pepa dɛn? Wɛl, wɛl, wetin dɛn sayn, na in dɛn sayn; ɛn wetin fɔ bi, go bi; ɛn afta dat bak, sɔntɛm i nɔ go bi, afta ɔl. Enihow, e ol fix en arenj a'redi; ɛn sɔm pipul dɛn we de wok na di bot ɔ ɔda wan dɛn fɔ go wit am, a tink se; as well dis as eni oda man, God pity 'em! Mɔnin to una, una we de wok wit di ship, mɔnin; di ɛvin we wi nɔ ebul fɔ tɔk bɔt de blɛs una; A sɔri we a stɔp yu.”

A se, “Luk ya, padi, if yu gɛt ɛnitin we impɔtant fɔ tɛl wi, aut wit am; bɔt if yu jɔs de tray fɔ bamboozle wi, yu mistek na yu gem; na dat nɔmɔ a gɛt fɔ tɔk.”

“Ɛn dɛn tɔk am fayn fayn wan, ɛn a lɛk fɔ yɛri wan chap de tɔk ɔp da we de; una na jɔs di man fɔ am—di wan dɛn we tan lɛk una. Mɔnin to una, shipmet dɛn, mɔnin! O! we una rich de, tɛl dɛn se a dɔn disayd se a nɔ fɔ mek wan pan dɛn.”

“Ah, mi dia kɔmpin, yu nɔ go ebul fɔ ful wi da we de—yu nɔ go ebul fɔ ful wi. Na di tin we izi pas ɔl na di wɔl fɔ mek man luk lɛk se i gɛt big sikrit insay am.”

“Mɔnin to una, una we de wok wit di ship, mɔnin.”

“Mɔnin na,” a se, “Kam, Kwik, lɛ wi lɛf dis krayzi man. Bɔt stɔp, tɛl mi yu nem, yu go du am?”

“Ilayja.”

Ilayja! a bin tink, ɛn wi waka go, wi ɔl tu de kɔmɛnt, afta wisɛf in fashɔn, pan dis ragged ol selɔ; ɛn gri se i nɔto natin pas humbug, we de tray fɔ bi bugbear. Bɔt wi nɔ bin dɔn go sɔntɛm pas wan ɔndrɛd yad, we wi chans fɔ tɔn wan kɔna, ɛn luk bak as a de du dat, udat fɔ si pas Ilayja de fala wi, pan ɔl we i de fa. Somhow, di sight of him struck mi so, dat a no tok enitin to Queequeg se im de bihain, bot a pas on wit mi comrade, an wori fo si if di strenja go ton di sem kona we wi du. I bin du am; ɛn afta dat i bin tan lɛk to mi se i de dɔg wi, bɔt wit wetin a nɔ bin ebul fɔ imajin fɔ di layf we a gɛt. Dis sikɔstɛms, kɔpl wit in ambiguous, haf-hinting, haf-rivɛl, shrouded sɔm kayn tɔk, naw bigin insay mi ɔl kayn vayg wɔnda ɛn af-aprɛhɛnshɔn, ɛn ɔl kɔnɛkt wit di Pequod; ɛn Kpɛntin Eab; ɛn di leg we i bin dɔn lɔs; ɛn di Kep Hɔn fit; ɛn di silva kalabash; ɛn wetin Kpɛntin Pɛlɛg bin tɔk bɔt am, we a kɔmɔt na di ship di de bifo; ɛn di prɛdikshɔn fɔ di skwa Tistig; ɛn di voyage we wi bin dɔn tay wisɛf fɔ sel; ɛn wan ɔndrɛd ɔda tin dɛn we gɛt shado.

A bin dɔn mekɔp mi maynd fɔ satisfay misɛf if dis ragged Ilayja bin rili de dɔg wi ɔ nɔ de dɔg wi, ɛn wit da intenshɔn de a krɔs di rod wit Kwik, ɛn na da say de a tray bak fɔ tray wi stɛp dɛn. Bɔt Ilayja bin pas, i nɔ bin tan lɛk se i notis wi. Dis bin mek a fil fri; ɛn wan tɛm mɔ, ɛn las las as i bin tan lɛk to mi, a kɔl am na mi at, as humbug.





CHAPTER 20. Ɔl di Astir.

Wan ɔ tu dez pas, ɛn bɔku tin dɛn bin de apin na di Pequod. Nɔto di ol sel dɛn nɔmɔ dɛn bin de mek, bɔt nyu sel dɛn bin de kam insay di bot, ɛn bolt dɛn we dɛn mek wit kanvas, ɛn kɔyl dɛn we dɛn mek wit rig; in shot, ɔltin bin sho se di ship in pripia dɛn de rɔsh fɔ dɔn. Kpɛntin Pɛlɛg nɔ bin de go na di si ɔ i nɔ bin de ɛva go na di si, bɔt i bin de sidɔm na in wigwam de kip shap luk-ɔut pan di an dɛn: Bildad bin de du ɔl di tin dɛn we i bin de bay ɛn gi na di stɔ dɛn; ɛn di man dɛn we dɛn bin de wok fɔ na di hold ɛn na di rig bin de wok te lɔng afta nɛt dɔn kam.

Di de afta we Kwik bin sayn di atikul dɛn, dɛn bin tɔk na ɔl di os dɛn usay di ship in kɔmni bin de stɔp, se dɛn chɛst fɔ dɔn de insay di ship bifo nɛt, bikɔs dɛn nɔ bin ebul fɔ no aw kwik di ship go dɔn go. So mi ɛn Kwik bin go dɔŋ wi trap dɛn, bɔt wi bin disayd fɔ slip na di si te di las tɛm. Bɔt i tan lɛk se dɛn kin gi rili lɔng notis ɔltɛm pan dɛn kes dɛn ya, ɛn di ship nɔ bin de go fɔ sɔm dez. Bɔt wi nɔ wɔnda; gud tin bin de fɔ du, ɛn notin nɔ de fɔ tɛl ɔmɔs tin dɛn fɔ tink bɔt, bifo di Pequod bin ful-ɔp wit ɔl di tin dɛn we dɛn nid.

Ɔlman no wetin bɔku bɔku tin dɛn—bed, sos-pan, naif ɛn fɔk, shovel ɛn tong, napkin, nat-krak, ɛn wetin nɔto, impɔtant fɔ di biznɛs fɔ kia fɔ di os. Jɔs so wit wɛl, we nid fɔ mek dɛn de kia fɔ di os fɔ tri ia na di big si, we de fa frɔm ɔl di wan dɛn we de sɛl tin fɔ it, di wan dɛn we de sɛl tin fɔ it, di dɔktɔ dɛn, di wan dɛn we de mek bred, ɛn di wan dɛn we de wok na bank. Ɛn pan ɔl we dis kin apin bak to biznɛs bot dɛn, bɔt nɔto di sem we lɛk aw i kin bi wit wɛlman dɛn. Bikɔs apat frɔm di lɔng we di ship dɛn kin tek fɔ kech wɛl, di bɔku bɔku atikul dɛn we spɛshal fɔ di we aw dɛn kin prosɛs di fishaman dɛn, ɛn di we aw dɛn nɔ kin ebul fɔ chenj dɛn na di rimot haba dɛn we pipul dɛn kin go bɔku tɛm, wi fɔ mɛmba se pan ɔl di ship dɛn, na di bot dɛn we dɛn kin yuz fɔ kech wɛl na dɛn kin de na do pas ɔl to ɔlkayn aksidɛnt, ɛn mɔ fɔ pwɛl ɛn lɔs di sem tin dɛn we di sakrifays fɔ di voyage de dipen mɔ pan. So, di spay bot, spay spa, ɛn spay layn ɛn harpun, ɛn spay ɔltin, ɔlmost, bɔt wan spay Kaptɛn ɛn dupliket ship.

Di tɛm we wi rich na di Ayland, i bin dɔn lɛf smɔl fɔ lɛ dɛn dɔn fɔ kip di Pɛkɔd we bin ebi pas ɔl; we gɛt in bif, bred, wata, fiul, ɛn ayɛn hup ɛn stik dɛn. Bɔt, lɛk aw wi bin dɔn hint bifo tɛm, fɔ sɔm tɛm, dɛn bin de kɔntinyu fɔ fetch ɛn kɛr di wan dɛn we de dayvɔs ɔds ɛn ɛnd pan tin dɛn, big ɛn smɔl.

Chif pan di wan dɛn we du dis fetch ɛn kɛr na Kpɛntin Bildad in sista, wan slim ol uman we gɛt spirit we dɔn mekɔp in maynd ɛn we nɔ de taya, bɔt wital rili gud at, we i bin tan lɛk se i dɔn disayd se, if i ebul fɔ ɛp am, dɛn nɔ fɔ fɛn natin we nɔ gɛt na di Pequod , afta wan tɛm i fayn fɔ rich na di si. Wan tɛm, i bin de kam insay di bot wit wan jɔg we gɛt pikɛl fɔ di stiwɔd in pantri; wan ɔda tɛm wit wan bɔnch kwil fɔ di chif mate in tebul, usay i kin kip in tik; a third time with a roll of flannel for the small of some one's rheumatic back. Never did any woman better deserve her name, which was Charity—Aunt Charity, as everybody called her. And like a sister of charity did this charitable Aunt Charity bustle about hither and thither, ready to turn her hand and heart to anything that promised to yield safety, comfort, and consolation to all on board a ship in which her beloved brother Bildad was concerned, and in which she herself owned a score or two of well-saved dollars.

But it was startling to see this excellent hearted Quakeress coming on board, as she did the last day, with a long oil-ladle in one hand, and a still longer whaling lance in the other. Nor was Bildad himself nor Captain Peleg at all backward. As for Bildad, he carried about with him a long list of the articles needed, and at every fresh arrival, down went his mark opposite that article upon the paper. Every once in a while Peleg came hobbling out of his whalebone den, roaring at the men down the hatchways, roaring up to the riggers at the mast-head, and then concluded by roaring back into his wigwam.

During these days of preparation, Queequeg and I often visited the craft, and as often I asked about Captain Ahab, and how he was, and when he was going to come on board his ship. To these questions they would answer, that he was getting better and better, and was expected aboard every day; meantime, the two captains, Peleg and Bildad, could attend to everything necessary to fit the vessel for the voyage. If I had been downright honest with myself, I would have seen very plainly in my heart that I did but half fancy being committed this way to so long a voyage, without once laying my eyes on the man who was to be the absolute dictator of it, so soon as the ship sailed out upon the open sea. But when a man suspects any wrong, it sometimes happens that if he be already involved in the matter, he insensibly strives to cover up his suspicions even from himself. And much this way it was with me. I said nothing, and tried to think nothing.

At last it was given out that some time next day the ship would certainly sail. So next morning, Queequeg and I took a very early start.





CHAPTER 21. Going Aboard.

It was nearly six o'clock, but only grey imperfect misty dawn, when we drew nigh the wharf.

“There are some sailors running ahead there, if I see right,” said I to Queequeg, “it can't be shadows; she's off by sunrise, I guess; come on!”

“Avast!” cried a voice, whose owner at the same time coming close behind us, laid a hand upon both our shoulders, and then insinuating himself between us, stood stooping forward a little, in the uncertain twilight, strangely peering from Queequeg to me. It was Elijah.

“Going aboard?”

“Hands off, will you,” said I.

“Lookee here,” said Queequeg, shaking himself, “go 'way!”

“Ain't going aboard, then?”

“Yes, we are,” said I, “but what business is that of yours? Do you know, Mr. Elijah, that I consider you a little impertinent?”

“No, no, no; I wasn't aware of that,” said Elijah, slowly and wonderingly looking from me to Queequeg, with the most unaccountable glances.

“Elijah,” said I, “you will oblige my friend and me by withdrawing. We are going to the Indian and Pacific Oceans, and would prefer not to be detained.”

“Ye be, be ye? Coming back afore breakfast?”

“He's cracked, Queequeg,” said I, “come on.”

“Holloa!” cried stationary Elijah, hailing us when we had removed a few paces.

“Never mind him,” said I, “Queequeg, come on.”

But he stole up to us again, and suddenly clapping his hand on my shoulder, said—“Did ye see anything looking like men going towards that ship a while ago?”

Struck by this plain matter-of-fact question, I answered, saying, “Yes, I thought I did see four or five men; but it was too dim to be sure.”

“Very dim, very dim,” said Elijah. “Morning to ye.”

Once more we quitted him; but once more he came softly after us; and touching my shoulder again, said, “See if you can find 'em now, will ye?

“Find who?”

“Morning to ye! morning to ye!” he rejoined, again moving off. “Oh! I was going to warn ye against—but never mind, never mind—it's all one, all in the family too;—sharp frost this morning, ain't it? Good-bye to ye. Shan't see ye again very soon, I guess; unless it's before the Grand Jury.” And with these cracked words he finally departed, leaving me, for the moment, in no small wonderment at his frantic impudence.

At last, stepping on board the Pequod, we found everything in profound quiet, not a soul moving. The cabin entrance was locked within; the hatches were all on, and lumbered with coils of rigging. Going forward to the forecastle, we found the slide of the scuttle open. Seeing a light, we went down, and found only an old rigger there, wrapped in a tattered pea-jacket. He was thrown at whole length upon two chests, his face downwards and inclosed in his folded arms. The profoundest slumber slept upon him.

“Those sailors we saw, Queequeg, where can they have gone to?” said I, looking dubiously at the sleeper. But it seemed that, when on the wharf, Queequeg had not at all noticed what I now alluded to; hence I would have thought myself to have been optically deceived in that matter, were it not for Elijah's otherwise inexplicable question. But I beat the thing down; and again marking the sleeper, jocularly hinted to Queequeg that perhaps we had best sit up with the body; telling him to establish himself accordingly. He put his hand upon the sleeper's rear, as though feeling if it was soft enough; and then, without more ado, sat quietly down there.

“Gracious! Queequeg, don't sit there,” said I.

“Oh! perry dood seat,” said Queequeg, “my country way; won't hurt him face.”

“Face!” said I, “call that his face? very benevolent countenance then; but how hard he breathes, he's heaving himself; get off, Queequeg, you are heavy, it's grinding the face of the poor. Get off, Queequeg! Look, he'll twitch you off soon. I wonder he don't wake.”

Queequeg removed himself to just beyond the head of the sleeper, and lighted his tomahawk pipe. I sat at the feet. We kept the pipe passing over the sleeper, from one to the other. Meanwhile, upon questioning him in his broken fashion, Queequeg gave me to understand that, in his land, owing to the absence of settees and sofas of all sorts, the king, chiefs, and great people generally, were in the custom of fattening some of the lower orders for ottomans; and to furnish a house comfortably in that respect, you had only to buy up eight or ten lazy fellows, and lay them round in the piers and alcoves. Besides, it was very convenient on an excursion; much better than those garden-chairs which are convertible into walking-sticks; upon occasion, a chief calling his attendant, and desiring him to make a settee of himself under a spreading tree, perhaps in some damp marshy place.

While narrating these things, every time Queequeg received the tomahawk from me, he flourished the hatchet-side of it over the sleeper's head.

“What's that for, Queequeg?”

“Perry easy, kill-e; oh! perry easy!”

He was going on with some wild reminiscences about his tomahawk-pipe, which, it seemed, had in its two uses both brained his foes and soothed his soul, when we were directly attracted to the sleeping rigger. The strong vapor now completely filling the contracted hole, it began to tell upon him. He breathed with a sort of muffledness; then seemed troubled in the nose; then revolved over once or twice; then sat up and rubbed his eyes.

“Holloa!” he breathed at last, “who be ye smokers?”

“Shipped men,” answered I, “when does she sail?”

“Aye, aye, ye are going in her, be ye? She sails to-day. The Captain came aboard last night.”

“What Captain?—Ahab?”

“Who but him indeed?”

I was going to ask him some further questions concerning Ahab, when we heard a noise on deck.

“Holloa! Starbuck's astir,” said the rigger. “He's a lively chief mate, that; good man, and a pious; but all alive now, I must turn to.” And so saying he went on deck, and we followed.

It was now clear sunrise. Soon the crew came on board in twos and threes; the riggers bestirred themselves; the mates were actively engaged; and several of the shore people were busy in bringing various last things on board. Meanwhile Captain Ahab remained invisibly enshrined within his cabin.
