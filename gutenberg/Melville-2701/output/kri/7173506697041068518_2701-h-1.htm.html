CHAPTER 1. Di tin dɛn we de apin.

Una kɔl mi Ismaɛl. Sɔm ia bifo naw—nɔ ɛva tink bɔt aw lɔng prɛsishɔn—bikɔs a nɔ bin gɛt bɛtɛ mɔni ɔ a nɔ bin gɛt ɛni mɔni na mi pɔs, ɛn a nɔ bin gɛt ɛni patikyula tin fɔ intres mi na di sho, a bin tink se a go sel lɛk smɔl ɛn si di wata pat na di wɔl. Na we we a gɛt fɔ drɛb kɔmɔt na di splin ɛn fɔ mek di blɔd go ɔlsay. Ɛnitɛm we a si se a de gro grim bɔt di mɔt; ɛnitɛm we na damp, drizzly Novɛmba na mi sol; ɛnitɛm we a si se a de stɔp bifo kɔfin we dɛn de kip tin dɛn we a nɔ want, ɛn briŋ di riya fɔ ɛni bɛrin we a mit; ɛn mɔ ɛnitɛm we mi hypos gɛt so ɔpa han pan mi, dat i nid fɔ gɛt strɔng moral prinsipul fɔ mek a nɔ step insay di strit bay wilful, ɛn fɔ mek a knock pipul dɛn hat di we aw dɛn de du am—dɛn, a de akɔntayn am se na ay tɛm fɔ rich na si as soon as a ebul. Dis na mi substitute fɔ pistol ɛn bɔl. Wit wan filɔsofik flourish Keto trowe insɛf pan in sɔd; A kwayɛt wan tek go na di ship. Natin nɔ de we de mek wi sɔprayz pan dis. If dɛn bɔt bin no am, ɔlmost ɔl man dɛn we de na dɛn digri, sɔm tɛm ɔ ɔda tɛm, kin cherish rili klos to di sem filin dɛn to di si wit mi.

Naw, yu insular siti we nem Manhattoes de, we dɛn dɔn bɛlt rawnd di watawɛl dɛn lɛk di Indian ayland dɛn wit kɔral rif dɛn—kɔmishɔn de rawnd am wit in sɔf. Rayt ɛn lɛft, di strit dɛn de kɛr yu go na wata. I ekstrim daun tawn na di batri, usay da nobul mol de was wev, ɛn kol wit briz, we sɔm awa bifo dat nɔ bin de na land. Luk di krawd we de wach wata de.

Sakumbul di siti fɔ wan drim Sabat aftanun. Go frɔm Corlears Hook to Coenties Slip, ɛn frɔm de, bay Whitehall, go na di nɔt. Wetin yu si?—Dɛn post dɛn lɛk sɛntinɛl dɛn we nɔ de tɔk ɔlsay na di tɔŋ, dɛn tinap bɔku bɔku pipul dɛn we de day ɛn dɛn tinap tranga wan we dɛn de tink bɔt di si. Sɔm dɛn de ledɔm pan di spayl dɛn; sɔm sidɔm na di pier-heads; sɔm de luk oba di bulwark dɛn fɔ ship dɛn we kɔmɔt na Chaena; sɔm ay ay ay wan na di rig, lɛk se dɛn de tray fɔ gɛt wan stil bɛtɛ peep we de go na di si. Bɔt dɛn ɔl na landman dɛn; fɔ wik de dɛn we dɛn kin kip insay lat ɛn plasta—dɛn tay dɛn na kɔnta dɛn, dɛn nel dɛn na bɛnch dɛn, dɛn kin tay dɛn na dɛsk dɛn. So aw dis kin bi? Yu tink se di grɔn fil dɛn nɔ de igen? Wetin dɛn de du na ya?

Bɔt luk! na ya mɔ krawd de kam, we de waka stret fɔ di wata, ɛn i tan lɛk se dɛn de go dayv. Strenj! Natin nɔ go satisfay dɛn pas di limit we pas ɔl na di land; loitering anda di shady lee of yonder warehouses nɔ go du fɔ yu. Nɔ, dɛn fɔ kam nia di wata jɔs lɛk aw dɛn go ebul fɔ kam nia di wata we dɛn nɔ fɔdɔm insay.Ɛn na de dɛn tinap—mayl dɛn frɔm dɛn—lig dɛn. Inlanders ɔl, dɛn kɔmɔt frɔm len ɛn ali, strit ɛn avɛnyu—nɔt, ist, sawt, ɛn wɛst. Bɔt stil, na ya dɛn ɔl gɛt wanwɔd. Tɛl mi, yu tink se di magnɛtik gud we di nidul dɛn we de na di kɔmpas dɛn na ɔl dɛn ship dɛn de, kin mek dɛn kam de?

Wan tɛm bak. Se yu de na di kɔntri; na sɔm ay ay land dɛn we gɛt lek dɛn. Tek klos to ɛni rod we yu want, ɛn tɛn to wan i de kɛr yu go dɔŋ na wan dal, ɛn lɛf yu de nia wan pul na di strim. Majik de insay de. Mek di man dɛn we nɔ de tink gud wan, go insay in dip dip tin dɛn we i de tink bɔt—stand da man de na in leg, put in fut a-go, ɛn i go lid yu to wata we nɔ go mek mistek, if wata de na ɔl da rijyɔn de. If yu fɔ ɛva tɔsti na di big Amɛrikan dɛzat, tray dis ɛkspiriɛns, if yu karavan apin fɔ saplae wit mɛtafizik prɔfɛsɔ. Yɛs, as ɔlman no, fɔ tink gud wan ɛn wata kin mared sote go.

Bɔt na wan atis de ya. I want fɔ peint yu di drimest, shadiest, kwayɛt, mɔs ɛnchantin bit fɔ romantik land skay na ɔl di vali na di Sako. Wetin na di men tin we i de yuz? Na de in tik dɛn tinap, ɛn ɛni wan pan dɛn gɛt wan ol tik, lɛk se wan ɛmit ɛn wan krɔs de insay; ɛn na ya in mɛdɔ de slip, ɛn na de in kaw dɛn de slip; ɛn ɔp frɔm de kɔtij, wan smok we de slip de go. Dip insay fa fa wudlan dɛn, briz kin blo wan mazi we, ɛn i kin rich to di mawnten dɛn we de ɔvalap we dɛn was wit dɛn blu we de nia di il. Bɔt pan ɔl we di pikchɔ de so tran, ɛn pan ɔl we dis pain tik de shek in sigrɛt lɛk lif na dis shɛpad in ed, ɔltin na fɔ natin, pas nɔmɔ di shɛpad in yay bin de pan di majik strim we bin de bifo am. Go visit di Prairies insay Jun, we fɔ bɔku bɔku mayl dɛn yu kin waka dip yu ni-dip bitwin Tayga-lili dɛn—wetin na di wan charm we yu want?—Wata—nɔto wan drop wata de de! Yu tink se Niagara na bin katarakt we gɛt san, yu go travul fɔ yu tawzin mayl fɔ si am? Wetin mek di po pɔyt na Tɛnisi, we i gɛt tu anful silva wantɛm wantɛm, bin tink if i fɔ bay kot fɔ am, we i sɔri fɔ no se i nid, ɔ fɔ put in mɔni fɔ travul fɔ waka na Rɔkway Bich? Wetin mek ɔlmost ɛvri strɔng wɛlbɔdi bɔbɔ wit strɔng wɛlbɔdi sol insay am, na sɔm tɛm ɔ ɔda krayzi fɔ go na si? Wetin mek we yu bin de travul fɔ di fɔstɛm as pasenja, yusɛf bin fil dis kayn mistik shek, we dɛn bin tɛl yu fɔ di fɔstɛm se yu ɛn yu ship nɔ de si yu ɛn yu ship naw? Wetin mek di ol pipul dɛn na Pashia bin de kip di si oli? Wetin mek di Grik dɛn gi am wan sɛpret gɔd, ɛn in yon brɔda fɔ Jove? Fɔ tru, ɔl dis nɔto tin we nɔ gɛt wan minin. Ɛn stil dip di minin fɔ da stori de bɔt Nasis, we bikɔs i nɔ bin ebul fɔ ɔndastand di imej we bin de mek i sɔfa ɛn we nɔ gɛt wan bɔt we i bin si na di watawɛl, i bin go insay de ɛn drawn am. Bɔt da sem imej de, wisɛf de si na ɔl di riva ɛn si. Na di imej fɔ di phantom fɔ layf we pɔsin nɔ ebul fɔ ɔndastand; ɛn dis na di ki fɔ ɔl dis.

Naw, we a se a gɛt di abit fɔ go na si ɛnitɛm we a bigin fɔ gro hazy bɔt di yay, ɛn bigin fɔ ɔva kɔnshɛns pan mi lɔng, a nɔ min fɔ mek dɛn tink se a kin ɛva go na si as wan pasenja. Fɔ mek yu go as pasenja yu nid fɔ gɛt pɔs, ɛn pɔs na jɔs rag pas yu gɛt sɔntin insay. Apat frɔm dat, pasenja dɛn kin sik na di si—dɛn kin gro fɔ fɛt—nɔ kin slip na nɛt—nɔ kin ɛnjɔy dɛnsɛf bɔku, as jɔnaral tin;—nɔ, a nɔ de ɛva go as pasenja; ɛn, pan ɔl we a na sɔntin we gɛt sɔl, a nɔ kin ɛva go na si as Kɔmwɔd, ɔ Kpɛntin, ɔ Kuk. A de lɛf di glori ɛn difrɛns we dɛn kayn ɔfis dɛn de gɛt to di wan dɛn we lɛk dɛn. Fɔ mi pat, a et ɔl di ɔnaful rɛspɛktful wok, prɔblɛm dɛn, ɛn ɔlkayn trɔbul dɛn. I rili tan lɛk aw a kin du fɔ kia fɔ misɛf, we a nɔ tek kia ɔf ship, bark, brig, skun, ɛn wetin nɔ de. Ɛn as fɔ go as kuk,—pan ɔl we a kɔnfɛs se bɔku glori de insay dat, kuk na wan kayn ɔfisa we de na ship-bɔd—bɔt, sɔm kayn we, a nɔ bin ɛva fansi fɔ brok bɔd dɛn;—pan ɔl we wan tɛm dɛn bin de brok am, dɛn bin de put bɔta wit sɛns, ɛn we de jɔj sɔlt ɛn pepa, nɔbɔdi nɔ de we go tɔk wit rɛspɛkt, nɔto fɔ se wit rɛspɛkt, bɔt bɔd we dɛn dɔn brok pas mi. Na frɔm di aydɔl dotings we di ol Ijipshian dɛn bin de du pan broiled ibis ɛn roast riva ɔs, na in yu si di mami dɛn fɔ dɛn krichɔ dɛn de insay dɛn big big bek-haus dɛn we na di pyramid dɛn.

Nɔ, we a go na si, a kin go lɛk simpul selɔ, rayt bifo di mast, plumb dɔŋ insay di fɔkas, ɔp de to di kiŋ mast-hɛd. Na tru se dɛn kin ɔda mi bɔt sɔm, ɛn mek a jomp frɔm wan spar to di ɔda wan, lɛk grasshopa na May mɛdɔ. Ɛn fɔs, dis kayn tin nɔ kin fayn fɔ yu. I kin tɔch pɔsin in sɛns fɔ ɔnɔ, mɔ if yu kɔmɔt na wan ol famili we dɔn establish na di land, di Van Rensselaers, ɔ Randolphs, ɔ Hardicanutes. Ɛn pas ɔl, if jɔs bifo yu put yu an insay di tar-pɔt, yu dɔn de lod am as kɔntri skulmasta, we de mek di bɔbɔ dɛn we lɔng pas ɔl tinap fɔ fred yu. Di chenj na wan we rili, a de ashu yu, frɔm skulmasta to selɔ, ɛn i nid fɔ gɛt strɔng dɛkɔkshɔn fɔ Sɛnika ɛn di Stoik dɛn fɔ mek yu ebul fɔ grin ɛn bia am. Bɔt ivin dis kin west as tɛm de go.

Wetin bɔt am, if sɔm ol hunks dɛn na si-kapten ɔda mi fɔ tek brum ɛn swip dɔŋ di dɛk dɛn? Wetin da indignity de amount to, weighed, I min, in di skel na di Nyu Tɛstamɛnt? Yu tink se di akɛnjɛl Gebrɛl tink ɛnitin di smɔl bɔt mi, bikɔs a de obe da ol hunks de wantɛm wantɛm ɛn wit rɛspɛkt insay da patikyula instans de? Udat nɔto slev? Tel mi dat. Wɛl, so, aw di ol si-kapten dɛn kin ɔda mi fɔ tɔk bɔt—ilɛk aw dɛn kin thump ɛn pan mi bɔt, a kin satisfay fɔ no se i fayn; dat ɔlman na wan we ɔ ɔda we dɛn de sav di sem we—ilɛksɛf na fizik ɔ mɛtafizikal pɔynt fɔ si, dat na; ɛn so di yunivasal thump de pas rawnd, ɛn ɔl di an dɛn fɔ rɔb dɛnsɛf in sholda-bled, ɛn satisfay.

Agen, a kin go na si ɔltɛm as selɔ, bikɔs dɛn kin mek pɔynt fɔ pe mi fɔ mi trɔbul, bɔt dɛn nɔ kin ɛva pe pasenja dɛn wan peni we a ɛva yɛri bɔt. Bifo dat, di pasenja dɛnsɛf fɔ pe. Ɛn ɔl di difrɛns de na di wɔl bitwin fɔ pe ɛn fɔ pe. Sɔntɛm di akt fɔ pe na di tin we nɔ kɔmfyut pas ɔl we di tu tifman dɛn we bin de tif di frut gadin dɛn bin du to wi. Bɔt we dɛn de pe am ,—wetin go kɔmpia wit am? Di wok we pɔsin kin du na di siti we pɔsin kin gɛt mɔni kin rili wɔndaful, we wi tink bɔt se wi rili biliv se mɔni na di rut fɔ ɔl di bad tin dɛn we de apin na dis wɔl, ɛn nɔto so pɔsin we gɛt mɔni nɔ go ebul fɔ go na ɛvin. Ah! wi kin gladi fɔ kɔnsayn wisɛf fɔ day!

Fɔ dɔn, a kin go na si ɔltɛm as selɔ, bikɔs ɔf di fayn fayn ɛksesaiz ɛn di klin briz we de na di fɔs-kast dɛk. Bikɔs lɛk dis wɔl, ed briz de fa fawe pas briz we de kɔmɔt biɛn (dat na, if yu nɔ ɛva pwɛl di Paytagorian maksim), so fɔ di mɔs pat di Kɔmwɔd we de na di kwata-dɛk kin gɛt in atmosfɛs na sɛkɔn han frɔm di selɔ dɛn na di fɔkas. I tink se na in de blo am fɔs; bɔt nɔto so. Na di sem we aw di kɔmɔn pipul dɛn kin lid dɛn lida dɛn pan bɔku ɔda tin dɛn, di sem tɛm we di lida dɛn nɔ kin sɔprayz bɛtɛ. Bɔt na dat mek afta we a dɔn smɛl di si bɔku tɛm lɛk biznɛsman we de wok na di si, naw a fɔ tek am na mi ed fɔ go waka fɔ kech wɛl; dis di polis ɔfisa we wi nɔ de si na di Fates, we de wach mi ɔltɛm, ɛn sikrit wan de dɔg mi, ɛn inflɔws mi insay sɔm we we nɔ gɛt akɔntayn—i kin ansa bɛtɛ pas ɛni ɔda pɔsin. Ɛn, wi nɔ gɛt wan dawt fɔ se, di we aw a bin de go dis we fɔ kech wɛl, bin bi pat pan di big program na Prɔvidɛns we dɛn bin dɔn mek lɔng tɛm bifo. I kam in as wan kayn shɔt intalɔd ɛn solo bitwin mɔ bɔku pɔfɔmɛnshɔn dɛn. A tek am se dis pat pan di bil fɔ dɔn rɔn sɔntin lɛk dis:

“ Grɛnd Kɔntest Ilɛkshɔn fɔ di Prɛsidɛnshi fɔ Amɛrika. “WHALING VOYAGE NA WAN ISHMAEL NA WAN. “BLƆDI FƐTUL NA AFGHANISTAN.”

Pan ɔl we a nɔ ebul fɔ tɛl wetin mek i bin rili bi se dɛn stej manija dɛn de, di Fates, bin put mi dɔŋ fɔ dis shabby pat pan wan wɛl voyage, we ɔda pipul dɛn bin de sɛt dɔŋ fɔ magnificent pat dɛn na ay trajedi, ɛn shɔt ɛn izi pat dɛn na genteel kɔmɛdi, ɛn jolly parts in farces—pan ɔl we a nɔ ebul fɔ no wetin mek dis bin rili bi; yet, naw we a mɛmba ɔl di sikɔstɛms, a tink se a kin si smɔl insay di spring ɛn motives we dɛn bin de prez mi wit kɔni kɔni ɔnda difrɛn disgiz, indyuz mi fɔ sɛt fɔ du di pat we a bin du, apat frɔm cajoling mi insay di delusion dat na bin wan chuk we a bin disayd fɔ du we a bin gɛt fridɔm fɔ disayd fɔ du sɔntin we nɔ bin de tek wan pɔsin bɛtɛ pas ɔda pɔsin ɛn di we aw a bin de jɔj di we aw a bin de trit ɔda pipul dɛn.

Di men tin we mek dɛn du dɛn tin ya na di aidia we bin rili bɔku bɔt di big wɛl insɛf. Dis kayn big big animal we bin de sho se a nɔ izi fɔ ɔndastand ɛn we nɔ izi fɔ ɔndastand bin mek a want fɔ no mɔ. Dɔn di wayl ɛn fa fa si dɛn usay i bin de rol in ayland bɔku bɔku wan; di prɔblɛm dɛn we di wɛl kin gɛt we dɛn nɔ ebul fɔ gi, we nɔ gɛt nem; dɛn tin ya, wit ɔl di wɔndaful tin dɛn we bin atɛnd fɔ wan tawzin Patagonia tin dɛn we a bin de si ɛn sawnd, bin ɛp fɔ mek a swɛla fɔ wetin a want. Sɔntɛm, wit ɔda man dɛn, dɛn kayn tin ya nɔ bin fɔ dɔn bi tin we de mek pɔsin want fɔ du sɔntin; bɔt as fɔ mi, a de sɔfa wit wan it we go de sote go fɔ tin dɛn we de fa. A lɛk fɔ sel na si dɛn we dɛn nɔ fɔ du, ɛn land na barbarous kɔst dɛn. We a nɔ de pe atɛnshɔn to wetin gud, a kin kwik fɔ no se sɔntin de mek a fred, ɛn a kin stil de wit am—dɛn go alaw mi—bikɔs i fayn fɔ mek a gɛt padi biznɛs wit ɔl di prizina dɛn na di ples we pɔsin de lod.

So bikɔs ɔf dɛn tin ya, dɛn bin gladi fɔ di waka we dɛn bin de travul fɔ kech wɛl; di big big wata-gɛt dɛn na di wɔndaful wɔl bin swing opin, ɛn insay di wayl kɔnsep dɛn we bin de swɛ mi fɔ mi purpose, tu ɛn tu de de flot insay mi inmost sol, di wɛl prushɔn dɛn we nɔ gɛt ɛnd, ɛn, midul bɔku pan dɛn ɔl, wan grand hooded phantom, lɛk sno il we de na di ays.





CHAPTER 2. Di Kapɛt-Bag.

A put wan ɔ tu shit insay mi ol kap-bag, put am ɔnda mi an, ɛn bigin fɔ go na Kep Hɔn ɛn di Pasifik. We a lɛf di gud siti we nem ol Manhatto, a rich na Nyu Bɛdfɔd di rayt we. Na bin Satide nɛt insay Disɛmba. A bin rili fil bad we a kam fɔ no se di smɔl paket fɔ Nantucket dɔn ɔlrɛdi sel, ɛn no we fɔ rich da ples de nɔ go gi, te di nɛks Mɔnde.

As bɔku pan di yɔŋ kandidet dɛn fɔ di pen ɛn penalty dɛn we dɛn kin gɛt we dɛn de kech wɛl kin stɔp na dis sem Nyu Bɛdfɔd, frɔm de fɔ bigin dɛn voyage, i kin bi bak se mi, fɔ wan, nɔ bin gɛt ɛni aydia fɔ du dat. Bikɔs dɛn bin mek mi maynd fɔ sel insay nɔto ɔda wan pas wan Nantucket kraft, bikɔs wan fayn, bɔku bɔku tin bin de bɔt ɔltin we gɛt fɔ du wit da fambul ol ayland de, we bin rili mek a gladi. Apat frɔm dat, pan ɔl we Nyu Bɛdfɔd dɔn de let smɔl smɔl fɔ tek di biznɛs fɔ kech wɛl, ɛn pan ɔl we insay dis mata, po ol Nantucket naw de bɔku biɛn am, yet Nantucket na bin in big ɔrijinal—di Taya fɔ dis Katej;—di ples usay di fɔs day Amɛrikin wɛl bin dɔn stɔp. Usay ɔdasay pas frɔm Nantucket, dɛn aborijinial wɛlman dɛn, di Rɛd-Man dɛn, bin fɔs sally aut insay kenu fɔ gi chase to di Leviathan? Ɛn usay pas frɔm Nantucket, bak, da fɔs advantej smɔl slɔp de put bifo, pat pan am lod wit imported cobblestones—na so di stori go—fɔ trowe pan di wɛl dɛn, fɔ mek dɛn go diskɔba we dɛn dɔn nia fɔ risk wan harpuon frɔm di bowsprit ?

Naw we a gɛt wan nɛt, wan de, ɛn stil ɔda nɛt we de fala mi na Nyu Bɛdfɔd, bifo a ebul fɔ go na di pɔt we a want fɔ go, i bin bi sɔntin we a fɔ wɔri bɔt usay a fɔ it ɛn slip da tɛm de. Na bin wan nɛt we bin rili dawt, nɔto so, na bin wan nɛt we bin rili dak ɛn we bin rili bad, we bin kol bad bad wan ɛn we nɔ bin gɛt gladi at. A nɔ bin no ɛnibɔdi na di ples. Wit wɔri grapnel a bin dɔn sawnd mi poket, ɛn jɔs briŋ sɔm ​​silva pis,—So, ɛnisay we yu go, Ishmaɛl, a se to misɛf, as a tinap na di midul pan wan dray strit de sholda mi bag, ɛn kɔmpia di gloom towards the not with the darkness towards the south—ɛnisay we yu sɛns yu go dɔn fɔ lod fɔ di nɛt, mi dia Ishmael, mek shɔ se yu aks di prayz, ɛn nɔ tu patikyula.

Wit stɛp dɛn we de stɔp a waka na di strit dɛn, ɛn pas di sayn we se “The Crossed Harpoons”—bɔt i bin luk tu dia ɛn gladi de. Fɔ go bifo, frɔm di brayt rɛd winda dɛn na di “Swɔd-Fish Inn,” so faya layt bin de kɔmɔt, dat i bin tan lɛk se i dɔn mɛlt di pak sno ɛn ays we bin de bifo di os, bikɔs ɔdasay dɛn, di frɔst we bin dɔn kol bin de tik tɛn inch insay wan had, asfaltik rod,—we bin taya fɔ mi, we a bin de nak mi fut pan di flinty projɛkshɔn dɛn, bikɔs frɔm had, rimorseless service di soles of mi buts bin de insay wan most miserable plight. Tu dia ɛn gladi, bak a tink, a stɔp wan mɔnt fɔ wach di brayt brayt glare na di strit, ɛn yɛri di sawnd dɛn fɔ di tinklin glas dɛn we de insay. Bɔt go bifo, Ishmayl, a se las las; yu noh yehri? kɔmɔt bifo di domɔt; yu patch but dem de stop di we. So on ai go. Naw bay instinkt a bin de fala di strit dɛn we bin de kɛr mi go na wata, bikɔs na de, i nɔ gɛt wan dawt se, na de di inn dɛn we nɔ dia pas ɔl, if nɔto di ples dɛn we pipul dɛn kin gladi fɔ.

Dɛn kayn strit dɛn de we nɔ fayn! blɔk dɛn we blak, nɔto os, na ɔl tu an, ɛn ya ɛn de wan kandul, lɛk kandul we de muv rawnd na grev. Na dis awa na nɛt, we na di las de insay di wik, da kwata de na di tɔŋ bin pruv se i nɔ gɛt pipul dɛn. Bɔt naw a kam nia wan layt we gɛt smok we de kɔmɔt na wan bildin we lɔŋ ɛn wayd, ɛn di domɔt bin tinap opin wan we de invayt pipul dɛn. I bin gɛt wan luk we dɛn nɔ tek tɛm luk, lɛk se dɛn mek am fɔ di we aw pipul dɛn de yuz am; so, we a de go insay, di fɔs tin we a du na fɔ stɔp pan wan ashis bɔks we de na di wɔl. Ha! a bin tink se, ha, as di tin dɛn we de flay bin lɛf smɔl fɔ mek a chok mi, dɛn ashis ya kɔmɔt na da siti we dɔn pwɛl, Gɔmɔra? Bɔt “The Crossed Harpoons,” ɛn “The Sword-Fish?”—so dis, so i go mɔs bi se na di sayn fɔ “Di Trap.” Bɔt, a pik misɛf ɛn yɛri wan lawd vɔys insay, a push go bifo ɛn opin wan sɛkɔn, insay domɔt.

I bin tan lɛk se na di big Blak Palimɛnt sidɔm na Tɔfɛt. Wan ɔndrɛd blak fes dɛn bin de tɔn rawnd na dɛn row fɔ luk; ɛn biɛn, wan blak Enjɛl fɔ Dum bin de bit wan buk na wan pulpit. Na bin wan nigro chɔch; ɛn di pricha in tɛks bin de bɔt di blak we daknɛs de, ɛn di kray ɛn kray ɛn tit-gnɛsh we de de. Ha, Ismaɛl, muf mi, bak aut, Wretched ɛnjɔymɛnt na di sayn fɔ ‘Di Trap!’.

We a de go bifo, a las kam to wan kayn layt we nɔ de fa frɔm di dok dɛn, ɛn yɛri wan pɔsin we nɔ gɛt layf de krak krak na di ays; ɛn we a luk ɔp, a si wan sayn we de swing oba di domɔt we gɛt wan wayt peint pan am, we de sho smɔl smɔl wan lɔng stret jet we gɛt misti sprɛy, ɛn dɛn wɔd ya ɔnda de—“Di Spouter Inn:—Pita Kɔfin.”

Kɔfin?—Spouter?—Rather ominous in that particular connexion, thought I. Bɔt na kɔmɔn nem na Nantucket, dɛn se, ɛn a tink se dis Pita we de ya na pɔsin we kɔmɔt de. As di layt bin luk so dak, ɛn di ples, fɔ di tɛm, bin luk kwayɛt inof, ɛn di smɔl wud os we dɔn pwɛl insɛf bin tan lɛk se dɛn go dɔn kɛr am kat ya frɔm di ruins fɔ sɔm distrikt we dɔn bɔn, ɛn as di sayn we de swing bin gɛt a po-stricken sort of creak to it, a bin tink se na ya na di very spot fo cheap lodgings, en di bes of pea coffee.

Na bin wan kayn ples we nɔ bin izi fɔ ɔndastand—na wan ol os we gɛt gebul ɛnd, wan say bin tan lɛk se i nɔ ebul fɔ waka, ɛn i bin de ledɔm wit sɔri-at. I bin tinap na wan shap blak kɔna, usay da big big briz de we bin de blo Yuroklidɔn bin de kɔntinyu fɔ ala pas aw i bin de ala bɔt di po Pɔl in kraft we i bin de tos. Bɔt pan ɔl dat, Yuroklidɔn na pawaful plezant zephyr to ɛnibɔdi we de insay os, wit in fut dɛn na di hob kwayɛt wan de tost fɔ bed. Wan ol man we de rayt buk—we a gɛt di wangren kɔpi we stil de bɔt in wok dɛn—we de jɔj da big big briz de we dɛn kɔl Yuroklidon—i kin mek wɔndaful difrɛns, ilɛksɛf yu luk am frɔm wan glas winda usay di frɔst de ɔlsay na di na do, ɔ if yu de wach am frɔm da winda we nɔ gɛt sash, usay di frɔst de na di tu say dɛn, ɛn we di wayt Day na di wangren glaza.” Na tru, a bin tink, as dis pat de kam na mi maynd—ol blak-lɛta, yu de tink gud wan. Yɛs, dɛn yay ya na winda, ɛn dis bɔdi we a gɛt na di os. Wetin a pity dem no stop op di chinks en di crannies though, en thrust in likl lint ya en dea. Bɔt i dɔn let fɔ mek ɛni impɔtant tin naw. Di wan ol wɔl dɔn dɔn; di kɔpston de pan, ɛn dɛn bin kɛr di chips dɛn go wit kat wan milyɔn ia bifo dis tɛm. Po Lazarɔs de, de chat in tit dɛn agens di kɔba ston fɔ in pilo, ɛn shek in tatters wit in shiverings, i kin plɔg ɔl tu in yes wit rag, ɛn put kɔn-kɔb insay in mɔt, ɛn yet dat nɔ go kip di Yuroklidɔn we gɛt big big briz. Yuroklaydɔn! se ol Dives, insay in rɛd silk wrap—(i bin gɛt wan we rɛd wan afta dat) pooh, pooh! Dis na fayn fayn nɛt we frɔsti; aw Ɔriɔn de shayn; wetin na di nɔt layt dɛn! Mek dɛn tɔk bɔt dɛn oriental sɔma klaym dɛn we gɛt kɔnsavatori dɛn we de sote go; gi mi di ɔnɔ fɔ mek mi yon sɔmda wit mi yon kol.

Bɔt wetin Lazarɔs tink? I go ebul fɔ wam in blu an dɛn bay we i ol dɛn ɔp to di big big layt dɛn we de na di nɔt? Yu nɔ tink se Lazarɔs go lɛk fɔ de na Sumatra pas fɔ de ya? Yu nɔ tink se i go lɛk fɔ le am lɔng lɔng wan along di layn we di ekweta de; yes, una gɔd dɛn! go dɔŋ na di ol we gɛt faya insɛf, fɔ mek dis frɔst nɔ kam?

Naw, we Lazarɔs fɔ ledɔm de na di kɔba ston bifo di domɔt fɔ Dayv, dis na wɔndaful tin pas fɔ mek dɛn tay wan ays blɔk pan wan pan di Mɔluka dɛn. Yet Dives insɛf, insɛf de liv lɛk Czar na ays pales we dɛn mek wit frɔzɛn sighs, ɛn bikɔs na prɛsidɛnt fɔ wan temperance sosayti, i jɔs de drink di tepid tears of orphans.

Bɔt nɔ mɔ pan dis blub naw, wi de go a-whaling, ɛn bɔku pan dat de fɔ kam yet. Lɛ wi skrap di ays frɔm wi fut dɛn we dɔn frɔst, ɛn si us kayn ples dis “Spouter” go bi.





CHAPTER 3. Di Spouter-Inn we de na di wɔl.

We yu go insay da Spouter-Inn we gɛt gable, yu si yusɛf insay wan wayd, lɔw, strɛch ɛntrɛ wit ol-fashin wainskɔt, we de mɛmba wan pan di bulwark dɛn fɔ sɔm ol kraft we dɛn dɔn kɔndɛm. Na wan say, dɛn bin hang wan rili big ɔyl peint we dɛn bin dɔn smok gud gud wan, ɛn ɔl di we dɛn bin dɔn pwɛl, dat na di krɔslayt dɛn we nɔ ikwal we yu bin de yuz fɔ wach am, na jɔs bay we yu de stɔdi am gud gud wan ɛn we yu go fɛn am ɔltɛm, ɛn we yu tek tɛm aks di neba dɛn, dat yu go ebul fɔ ɔndastand wetin mek i du ɛni we. Such unaccountable masses of shades and shadows, dat fɔs yu bin lɛf smɔl fɔ tink se sɔm ambishɔs yɔŋ atis, insay di tɛm fɔ di Nyu Ingland hags, bin tray fɔ delineate chaos bewitched. Bɔt bay we yu tink gud wan ɛn wit ɔl yu at, ɛn tink gud wan bɔku tɛm, ɛn mɔ bay we yu trowe di smɔl winda opin to di bak pat pan di ɛntrɛ, yu kin dɔn kam fɔ no se dis kayn aidia, ilɛksɛf i wayl, nɔ go bi tin we nɔ rayt atɔl.

Bɔt wetin bin mek yu kɔnfyus ɛn kɔnfyus pas ɔl na wan lɔng, limber, portentous, blak mas we gɛt sɔntin we de flay na di sɛntrɔm pan di pikchɔ oba tri blu, dim, perpendicular layn dɛn we de flɔt insay wan yist we nɔ gɛt nem. Wan boggy, soggy, squitchy pikchɔ tru tru, inaf fɔ drɛb man we de fred fɔ distɛkt. Yet bin de wan kayn indefinite, haf-attained, unimaginable sublimity bɔt am we fayn fɔ frɔz yu to am, te yu involuntarily tek wan oth wit yusɛf fɔ fɛn ɔut wetin da wɔndaful peint de min. Ever and anon a brayt, but, alas, deceptive idea would dart you through.—Na di Blak Si insay wan midnayt gale.—Na di unnatural fɛt we di 4 primal element dɛn de fɛt.—Na wan blasted heath.—Na wan Hyperborean winta scene .—Na di brok-ap fɔ di ays-bɔnd strim fɔ Taym. Bɔt las las ɔl dɛn fansi ya bin gri fɔ da wan portentous sɔntin de we bin de midul di pikchɔ. Dat bin kam fɔ no wan tɛm, ɛn ɔl di ɔda wan dɛn bin klia. Bɔt stɔp; yu nɔ tink se i tan lɛk big big fish? ivin di big big livaytan insɛf?

Infakt, di we aw di atis bin mek am bin tan lɛk dis: na mi yon las tiori, we sɔm pat pan am bin kɔmɔt frɔm di tin dɛn we bɔku ol pipul dɛn we a bin de tɔk to bɔt di tɔpik bin dɔn gɛda. Di pikchɔ de sho wan Kep-Hɔna we de insay wan big big briz; di ship we gɛt af-af fawndeshɔn we bin de welt de wit in tri mast dɛn we dɛn dɔn brok nɔmɔ we pɔsin kin si; ɛn wan wɛl we vɛks, we i want fɔ spring klin oba di kraft, de du di big big akt fɔ tik insɛf pan di tri mast-ed dɛn.

Dɛn bin hang di ɔda wɔl na dis ɛntrɛ ɔlsay wit wan big big klɔb ɛn spia dɛn we tan lɛk het. Sɔm dɛn bin tik tik wit tit dɛn we de shayn we tan lɛk ayvri saw; ɔda wan dɛn bin gɛt knot dɛn we gɛt mɔtalman ia; ɛn wan bin tan lɛk sikl, ɛn wan big handel bin de swip rawnd lɛk di pat we wan mashin we gɛt lɔng an dɛn bin mek na di nyu gras we dɛn dɔn kɔt. Yu bin de shek shek as yu de luk, ɛn de wɔnda us monstrous kanibal ɛn savayj go ɛva go day-harvesting wit dis kayn hacking, horrifying implement. Dɛn bin miks wit dɛn tin ya, dɛn bin rɔsti ol wɛl lans ɛn hapun dɛn we ɔl bin brok ɛn nɔ fayn. Sɔm pan dɛn na bin wɛpɔn dɛn we dɛn bin de tɔk bɔt. Wit dis lans we bin de lɔng wan tɛm, we naw gɛt wayl ɛlbow, fifti ia bifo naw, Netan Swain bin kil fayvtin wɛl bitwin wan san we de kɔmɔt ɛn we di san de go dɔŋ. Ɛn da harpun de—so lɛk kɔkskru naw—dɛn bin trowe am na Javan si, ɛn wan wɛl rɔnawe wit am, sɔm ia afta dat dɛn kil am na di Kep ɔf Blankɔ. Di ɔrijinal ayɛn bin go insay nia di tel, ɛn, lɛk nidul we nɔ de rɛst we de de na man in bɔdi, i travul ful fɔti fit, ɛn las las dɛn fɛn am insay di hump.

We yu krɔs dis dak ɛntrɛ, ɛn go tru yon lɔw-arch we—we yu kɔt tru wetin insay ol tɛm go dɔn bi wan big sɛntral chimini wit faya ples ɔlsay—yu go insay di pɔblik rum. Wan ples we stil dak pas ɔl na dis, wit so lɔw ponderous bim dɛn ɔp, ɛn so ol wrinkled plank dɛn ɔnda, dat yu go ɔlmost fansi se yu trod sɔm ol kraft in kɔkpit, mɔ fɔ dis kayn ala ala nɛt, we dis ol ak we dɛn ankɔ na kɔna bin de rɔk so vɛks . Na wan say, wan lɔng tebul we smɔl, we tan lɛk shelf, we dɛn kɔba wit krak krak glas kes dɛn, we ful-ɔp wit dɔti rari tin dɛn we dɛn gɛda frɔm dis big wɔl in fa fa say dɛn. We i de projɛkt frɔm di fa angle na di rum, wan den we tan lɛk dak tin tinap—di ba—na rud we fɔ tray fɔ kɔt wan rayt wɛl in ed. I bi so, na de di big big arch bon na di wɛl in jaw tinap, so wayd, i kin lɛf smɔl fɔ lɛ kɔch drayv ɔnda am. Insay de, dɛn gɛt shelf dɛn we nɔ fayn, we dɛn rɛnj rawnd wit ol dikanta, bɔtul, flask; ɛn insay dɛn jaw dɛn de we de pwɛl kwik kwik wan, lɛk wan ɔda Jona we dɛn swɛ (we dɛn kɔl am fɔ tru), wan smɔl ol man we dɔn dray de mek pipul dɛn de fred, we, fɔ dɛn mɔni, de sɛl di wan dɛn we de wok na di bot wit dia delirium ɛn day.

Di tumbul dɛn we i de tɔn in pɔyzin insay, na tin dɛn we rili bad. Pan ɔl we tru tru silinda dɛn we nɔ gɛt—insay, di bad bad grɔn gɔglin glas dɛn bin ful-ɔp fɔ ful-ɔp dɔŋ to wan bɔt we de tif. Paralel meridian dɛn we dɛn rud wan pik insay di glas, de rawnd dɛn futpad dɛn kɔp dɛn ya. Fil to dis mak, ɛn yu chaj na jɔs wan peni; to dis wan peni mɔ; ɛn so go to di ful glas—di Kep Hɔn mɛzhɔ, we yu kin gulp fɔ wan shilin.

We a go insay di ples a si bɔku yɔŋ pipul dɛn we de wok na di si gɛda rawnd wan tebul, ɛn wan dim layt we de dayvɔs de chɛk di spɛshal wan dɛn we dɛn kɔl skrimshander . A bin luk fɔ di pɔsin we gɛt di land, ɛn we a tɛl am se a want fɔ mek dɛn gɛt rum fɔ de, a bin gɛt fɔ ansa se in os ful-ɔp—nɔto bed we nɔbɔdi nɔ de de. “Bɔt avast,” i tap in fɔɛd, “yu haint no objections fɔ sheb wan harpooneer in blankit, nɔto so? I s’pose yu de goin’ a-whalin’, so i bɛtɛ fɔ yus to da kayn tin de.”

A tɛl am se a nɔ ɛva lɛk fɔ slip tu pipul dɛn na bed; dat if a fɔ ɛva du dat, i go dipen pan udat di pɔsin we de mek di harpun go bi, ɛn if in (di pɔsin we gɛt di land) rili nɔ gɛt ɔda ples fɔ mi, ɛn di pɔsin we de mek hapun nɔ bin disayd fɔ se i nɔ gri wit am, wetin mek pas fɔ waka waka mɔ bɔt wan strenj tɔŋ pan so bita nɛt, a go bia wit di af pan ɛni sɛnt man in blankit.

“A bin tink se na so i bi. Nɔ bad; tek wan sidɔm ples. Sɔpa?—yu want fɔ it ivintɛm it? Sɔpa go rɛdi dairekt wan.”

A sidɔm pan wan ol wud sɛtul, we dɛn kɔt ɔlsay lɛk bɛnch na di Batri. Na wan ɛnd wan tar we bin de luk fɔ tin dɛn bin stil de mek am fayn mɔ wit in jak-naif, i bin de butu ɛn wok tranga wan fɔ go fa say we de bitwin in leg dɛn. I bin de tray in an pan wan ship ɔnda ful sel, bɔt i nɔ bin mek bɔku edway, a bin tink.

Las las, dɛn kɔl wi lɛk 4 ɔ 5 pan wi fɔ kam it na wan rum we de nia de. I bin kol as Ayland—nɔ gɛt faya atɔl—di pɔsin we gɛt di land bin se i nɔ go ebul fɔ pe fɔ am. Natin nɔ de pas tu talɔ kandul dɛn we nɔ fayn, ɛn ɛni wan pan dɛn de insay wan sheet we de waynd. Wi bin fain fɔ bɔt wi mɔnki jakit dɛn, ɛn ol to wi lip kɔp dɛn we gɛt ti we de bɔn wit wi finga dɛn we dɔn frɔz af. Bɔt di prays na bin di kayn we we rili impɔtant—nɔto mit ɛn pɔt nɔmɔ, bɔt dɔmplin; gud ɛvin dɛn! dumplings fɔ it di ivintɛm it! Wan yɔŋ man we wɛr grɔn bɔks kot, adrɛs insɛf to dɛn dɔmpling ya insay wan we we rili bad.

Di pɔsin we gɛt di land se: “Mi bɔy pikin, yu go gɛt di bad bad drim fɔ wan day sartainty.”

A wispa se: “Landlɔd, dat nɔto di harpooneer?”

“O, nɔ,” i se, i luk wan kayn dɛbul fani, “di harpooneer na dak kɔmpleks chap. I nɔ de ɛva it dɔmpling, i nɔ de it—i nɔ de it natin pas stik, ɛn i lɛk ’em rare.”

Mi se: “Di dɛbul we i de du, usay da harpuoneer de? I de ya?”

Di ansa na bin se: “I go de ya fɔ lɔng tɛm.”

A nɔ bin ebul fɔ ɛp, bɔt a bigin fɔ fil se a de dawt dis “dak kɔlɔ” we de mek harpun. Ɛni we aw i bi, a bin disayd se if i kam fɔ no se wi fɔ slip togɛda, i fɔ pul in klos ɛn go na bed bifo a du dat.

Sɔpa dɔn, di kɔmni go bak na di ba-rum, we, bikɔs a nɔ no wetin ɔda fɔ du wit misɛf, a disayd fɔ spɛn di ɔda pat na di ivintɛm as pɔsin we de luk pan.

Naw dɛn yɛri wan nɔys we de mek pipul dɛn nɔ gɛt wanwɔd witout. Start op, di landlord kray, “Na di Grampus in kru. I sid am ripɔt in di offing dis mɔnin; wan trip fɔ tri ia, ɛn wan ful ship. Hurrah, bɔy pikin dɛn; naw wi go gɛt di layt nyuz frɔm di Feegees.”

Dɛn yɛri wan tramp pan si but dɛn na di ɛntrɛ; dɛn bin trowe di domɔt opin, ɛn wan wayl sɛt we de wok na di si bin rol insay inof. Dɛn bin kɔba dɛn wit dɛn shaggy wach kot, ɛn wit dɛn ed we dɛn muf wit wul kɔmfɔta, ɔl bedarn ɛn ragged, ɛn dɛn biad stif wit ayskul, dɛn bin tan lɛk se na bea dɛn we kɔmɔt na Labrador de bɔn. Dɛn bin jɔs dɔn land frɔm dɛn bot, ɛn dis na di fɔs os we dɛn go insay. So, i nɔ wɔnda we dɛn mek wan stret wek fɔ di wɛl in mɔt—di ba—we i nɔ tu te, di smɔl ol Jona we bin rɔtin, we bin de du di ɔfisa de, tɔn dɛn ɔlsay. It was now about nine o'clock, and the room seeming almost supernaturally quiet after these orgies, I began to congratulate myself upon a little plan that had occurred to me just previous to the entrance of the seamen.

No man prefers to sleep two in a bed. In fact, you would a good deal rather not sleep with your own brother. I don't know how it is, but people like to be private when they are sleeping. And when it comes to sleeping with an unknown stranger, in a strange inn, in a strange town, and that stranger a harpooneer, then your objections indefinitely multiply. Nor was there any earthly reason why I as a sailor should sleep two in a bed, more than anybody else; for sailors no more sleep two in a bed at sea, than bachelor Kings do ashore. To be sure they all sleep together in one apartment, but you have your own hammock, and cover yourself with your own blanket, and sleep in your own skin.

The more I pondered over this harpooneer, the more I abominated the thought of sleeping with him. It was fair to presume that being a harpooneer, his linen or woollen, as the case might be, would not be of the tidiest, certainly none of the finest. I began to twitch all over. Besides, it was getting late, and my decent harpooneer ought to be home and going bedwards. Suppose now, he should tumble in upon me at midnight—how could I tell from what vile hole he had been coming?

“Landlord! I've changed my mind about that harpooneer.—I shan't sleep with him. I'll try the bench here.”

“Just as you please; I'm sorry I can't spare ye a tablecloth for a mattress, and it's a plaguy rough board here”—feeling of the knots and notches. “But wait a bit, Skrimshander; I've got a carpenter's plane there in the bar—wait, I say, and I'll make ye snug enough.” So saying he procured the plane; and with his old silk handkerchief first dusting the bench, vigorously set to planing away at my bed, the while grinning like an ape. The shavings flew right and left; till at last the plane-iron came bump against an indestructible knot. The landlord was near spraining his wrist, and I told him for heaven's sake to quit—the bed was soft enough to suit me, and I did not know how all the planing in the world could make eider down of a pine plank. So gathering up the shavings with another grin, and throwing them into the great stove in the middle of the room, he went about his business, and left me in a brown study.

I now took the measure of the bench, and found that it was a foot too short; but that could be mended with a chair. But it was a foot too narrow, and the other bench in the room was about four inches higher than the planed one—so there was no yoking them. I then placed the first bench lengthwise along the only clear space against the wall, leaving a little interval between, for my back to settle down in. But I soon found that there came such a draught of cold air over me from under the sill of the window, that this plan would never do at all, especially as another current from the rickety door met the one from the window, and both together formed a series of small whirlwinds in the immediate vicinity of the spot where I had thought to spend the night.

The devil fetch that harpooneer, thought I, but stop, couldn't I steal a march on him—bolt his door inside, and jump into his bed, not to be wakened by the most violent knockings? It seemed no bad idea; but upon second thoughts I dismissed it. For who could tell but what the next morning, so soon as I popped out of the room, the harpooneer might be standing in the entry, all ready to knock me down!

Still, looking round me again, and seeing no possible chance of spending a sufferable night unless in some other person's bed, I began to think that after all I might be cherishing unwarrantable prejudices against this unknown harpooneer. Thinks I, I'll wait awhile; he must be dropping in before long. I'll have a good look at him then, and perhaps we may become jolly good bedfellows after all—there's no telling.

But though the other boarders kept coming in by ones, twos, and threes, and going to bed, yet no sign of my harpooneer.

“Landlord!” said I, “what sort of a chap is he—does he always keep such late hours?” It was now hard upon twelve o'clock.

The landlord chuckled again with his lean chuckle, and seemed to be mightily tickled at something beyond my comprehension. “No,” he answered, “generally he's an early bird—airley to bed and airley to rise—yes, he's the bird what catches the worm. But to-night he went out a peddling, you see, and I don't see what on airth keeps him so late, unless, may be, he can't sell his head.”

“Can't sell his head?—What sort of a bamboozingly story is this you are telling me?” getting into a towering rage. “Do you pretend to say, landlord, that this harpooneer is actually engaged this blessed Saturday night, or rather Sunday morning, in peddling his head around this town?”

“That's precisely it,” said the landlord, “and I told him he couldn't sell it here, the market's overstocked.”

“With what?” shouted I.

“With heads to be sure; ain't there too many heads in the world?”

“I tell you what it is, landlord,” said I quite calmly, “you'd better stop spinning that yarn to me—I'm not green.”

“May be not,” taking out a stick and whittling a toothpick, “but I rayther guess you'll be done brown if that ere harpooneer hears you a slanderin' his head.”

“I'll break it for him,” said I, now flying into a passion again at this unaccountable farrago of the landlord's.

“It's broke a'ready,” said he.

“Broke,” said I—“ broke , do you mean?”

“Sartain, and that's the very reason he can't sell it, I guess.”

“Landlord,” said I, going up to him as cool as Mt. Hecla in a snow-storm—“landlord, stop whittling. You and I must understand one another, and that too without delay. I come to your house and want a bed; you tell me you can only give me half a one; that the other half belongs to a certain harpooneer. And about this harpooneer, whom I have not yet seen, you persist in telling me the most mystifying and exasperating stories tending to beget in me an uncomfortable feeling towards the man whom you design for my bedfellow—a sort of connexion, landlord, which is an intimate and confidential one in the highest degree. I now demand of you to speak out and tell me who and what this harpooneer is, and whether I shall be in all respects safe to spend the night with him. And in the first place, you will be so good as to unsay that story about selling his head, which if true I take to be good evidence that this harpooneer is stark mad, and I've no idea of sleeping with a madman; and you, sir, you I mean, landlord, you , sir, by trying to induce me to do so knowingly, would thereby render yourself liable to a criminal prosecution.”

“Wall,” said the landlord, fetching a long breath, “that's a purty long sarmon for a chap that rips a little now and then. But be easy, be easy, this here harpooneer I have been tellin' you of has just arrived from the south seas, where he bought up a lot of 'balmed New Zealand heads (great curios, you know), and he's sold all on 'em but one, and that one he's trying to sell to-night, cause to-morrow's Sunday, and it would not do to be sellin' human heads about the streets when folks is goin' to churches. He wanted to, last Sunday, but I stopped him just as he was goin' out of the door with four heads strung on a string, for all the airth like a string of inions.”

This account cleared up the otherwise unaccountable mystery, and showed that the landlord, after all, had had no idea of fooling me—but at the same time what could I think of a harpooneer who stayed out of a Saturday night clean into the holy Sabbath, engaged in such a cannibal business as selling the heads of dead idolators?

“Depend upon it, landlord, that harpooneer is a dangerous man.”

“He pays reg'lar,” was the rejoinder. “But come, it's getting dreadful late, you had better be turning flukes—it's a nice bed; Sal and me slept in that ere bed the night we were spliced. There's plenty of room for two to kick about in that bed; it's an almighty big bed that. Why, afore we give it up, Sal used to put our Sam and little Johnny in the foot of it. But I got a dreaming and sprawling about one night, and somehow, Sam got pitched on the floor, and came near breaking his arm. Arter that, Sal said it wouldn't do. Come along here, I'll give ye a glim in a jiffy;” and so saying he lighted a candle and held it towards me, offering to lead the way. But I stood irresolute; when looking at a clock in the corner, he exclaimed “I vum it's Sunday—you won't see that harpooneer to-night; he's come to anchor somewhere—come along then; do come; won't ye come?”

I considered the matter a moment, and then up stairs we went, and I was ushered into a small room, cold as a clam, and furnished, sure enough, with a prodigious bed, almost big enough indeed for any four harpooneers to sleep abreast.

“There,” said the landlord, placing the candle on a crazy old sea chest that did double duty as a wash-stand and centre table; “there, make yourself comfortable now, and good night to ye.” I turned round from eyeing the bed, but he had disappeared.

Folding back the counterpane, I stooped over the bed. Though none of the most elegant, it yet stood the scrutiny tolerably well. I then glanced round the room; and besides the bedstead and centre table, could see no other furniture belonging to the place, but a rude shelf, the four walls, and a papered fireboard representing a man striking a whale. Of things not properly belonging to the room, there was a hammock lashed up, and thrown upon the floor in one corner; also a large seaman's bag, containing the harpooneer's wardrobe, no doubt in lieu of a land trunk. Likewise, there was a parcel of outlandish bone fish hooks on the shelf over the fire-place, and a tall harpoon standing at the head of the bed.

But what is this on the chest? I took it up, and held it close to the light, and felt it, and smelt it, and tried every way possible to arrive at some satisfactory conclusion concerning it. I can compare it to nothing but a large door mat, ornamented at the edges with little tinkling tags something like the stained porcupine quills round an Indian moccasin. There was a hole or slit in the middle of this mat, as you see the same in South American ponchos. But could it be possible that any sober harpooneer would get into a door mat, and parade the streets of any Christian town in that sort of guise? I put it on, to try it, and it weighed me down like a hamper, being uncommonly shaggy and thick, and I thought a little damp, as though this mysterious harpooneer had been wearing it of a rainy day. I went up in it to a bit of glass stuck against the wall, and I never saw such a sight in my life. I tore myself out of it in such a hurry that I gave myself a kink in the neck.

I sat down on the side of the bed, and commenced thinking about this head-peddling harpooneer, and his door mat. After thinking some time on the bed-side, I got up and took off my monkey jacket, and then stood in the middle of the room thinking. I then took off my coat, and thought a little more in my shirt sleeves. But beginning to feel very cold now, half undressed as I was, and remembering what the landlord said about the harpooneer's not coming home at all that night, it being so very late, I made no more ado, but jumped out of my pantaloons and boots, and then blowing out the light tumbled into bed, and commended myself to the care of heaven.

Whether that mattress was stuffed with corn-cobs or broken crockery, there is no telling, but I rolled about a good deal, and could not sleep for a long time. At last I slid off into a light doze, and had pretty nearly made a good offing towards the land of Nod, when I heard a heavy footfall in the passage, and saw a glimmer of light come into the room from under the door.

Lord save me, thinks I, that must be the harpooneer, the infernal head-peddler. But I lay perfectly still, and resolved not to say a word till spoken to. Holding a light in one hand, and that identical New Zealand head in the other, the stranger entered the room, and without looking towards the bed, placed his candle a good way off from me on the floor in one corner, and then began working away at the knotted cords of the large bag I before spoke of as being in the room. I was all eagerness to see his face, but he kept it averted for some time while employed in unlacing the bag's mouth. This accomplished, however, he turned round—when, good heavens! what a sight! Such a face! It was of a dark, purplish, yellow colour, here and there stuck over with large blackish looking squares. Yes, it's just as I thought, he's a terrible bedfellow; he's been in a fight, got dreadfully cut, and here he is, just from the surgeon. But at that moment he chanced to turn his face so towards the light, that I plainly saw they could not be sticking-plasters at all, those black squares on his cheeks. They were stains of some sort or other. At first I knew not what to make of this; but soon an inkling of the truth occurred to me. I remembered a story of a white man—a whaleman too—who, falling among the cannibals, had been tattooed by them. I concluded that this harpooneer, in the course of his distant voyages, must have met with a similar adventure. And what is it, thought I, after all! It's only his outside; a man can be honest in any sort of skin. But then, what to make of his unearthly complexion, that part of it, I mean, lying round about, and completely independent of the squares of tattooing. To be sure, it might be nothing but a good coat of tropical tanning; but I never heard of a hot sun's tanning a white man into a purplish yellow one. However, I had never been in the South Seas; and perhaps the sun there produced these extraordinary effects upon the skin. Now, while all these ideas were passing through me like lightning, this harpooneer never noticed me at all. But, after some difficulty having opened his bag, he commenced fumbling in it, and presently pulled out a sort of tomahawk, and a seal-skin wallet with the hair on. Placing these on the old chest in the middle of the room, he then took the New Zealand head—a ghastly thing enough—and crammed it down into the bag. He now took off his hat—a new beaver hat—when I came nigh singing out with fresh surprise. There was no hair on his head—none to speak of at least—nothing but a small scalp-knot twisted up on his forehead. His bald purplish head now looked for all the world like a mildewed skull. Had not the stranger stood between me and the door, I would have bolted out of it quicker than ever I bolted a dinner.

Even as it was, I thought something of slipping out of the window, but it was the second floor back. I am no coward, but what to make of this head-peddling purple rascal altogether passed my comprehension. Ignorance is the parent of fear, and being completely nonplussed and confounded about the stranger, I confess I was now as much afraid of him as if it was the devil himself who had thus broken into my room at the dead of night. In fact, I was so afraid of him that I was not game enough just then to address him, and demand a satisfactory answer concerning what seemed inexplicable in him.

Meanwhile, he continued the business of undressing, and at last showed his chest and arms. As I live, these covered parts of him were checkered with the same squares as his face; his back, too, was all over the same dark squares; he seemed to have been in a Thirty Years' War, and just escaped from it with a sticking-plaster shirt. Still more, his very legs were marked, as if a parcel of dark green frogs were running up the trunks of young palms. It was now quite plain that he must be some abominable savage or other shipped aboard of a whaleman in the South Seas, and so landed in this Christian country. I quaked to think of it. A peddler of heads too—perhaps the heads of his own brothers. He might take a fancy to mine—heavens! look at that tomahawk!

But there was no time for shuddering, for now the savage went about something that completely fascinated my attention, and convinced me that he must indeed be a heathen. Going to his heavy grego, or wrapall, or dreadnaught, which he had previously hung on a chair, he fumbled in the pockets, and produced at length a curious little deformed image with a hunch on its back, and exactly the colour of a three days' old Congo baby. Remembering the embalmed head, at first I almost thought that this black manikin was a real baby preserved in some similar manner. But seeing that it was not at all limber, and that it glistened a good deal like polished ebony, I concluded that it must be nothing but a wooden idol, which indeed it proved to be. For now the savage goes up to the empty fire-place, and removing the papered fire-board, sets up this little hunch-backed image, like a tenpin, between the andirons. The chimney jambs and all the bricks inside were very sooty, so that I thought this fire-place made a very appropriate little shrine or chapel for his Congo idol.

I now screwed my eyes hard towards the half hidden image, feeling but ill at ease meantime—to see what was next to follow. First he takes about a double handful of shavings out of his grego pocket, and places them carefully before the idol; then laying a bit of ship biscuit on top and applying the flame from the lamp, he kindled the shavings into a sacrificial blaze. Presently, after many hasty snatches into the fire, and still hastier withdrawals of his fingers (whereby he seemed to be scorching them badly), he at last succeeded in drawing out the biscuit; then blowing off the heat and ashes a little, he made a polite offer of it to the little negro. But the little devil did not seem to fancy such dry sort of fare at all; he never moved his lips. All these strange antics were accompanied by still stranger guttural noises from the devotee, who seemed to be praying in a sing-song or else singing some pagan psalmody or other, during which his face twitched about in the most unnatural manner. At last extinguishing the fire, he took the idol up very unceremoniously, and bagged it again in his grego pocket as carelessly as if he were a sportsman bagging a dead woodcock.

All these queer proceedings increased my uncomfortableness, and seeing him now exhibiting strong symptoms of concluding his business operations, and jumping into bed with me, I thought it was high time, now or never, before the light was put out, to break the spell in which I had so long been bound.

But the interval I spent in deliberating what to say, was a fatal one. Taking up his tomahawk from the table, he examined the head of it for an instant, and then holding it to the light, with his mouth at the handle, he puffed out great clouds of tobacco smoke. The next moment the light was extinguished, and this wild cannibal, tomahawk between his teeth, sprang into bed with me. I sang out, I could not help it now; and giving a sudden grunt of astonishment he began feeling me.

Stammering out something, I knew not what, I rolled away from him against the wall, and then conjured him, whoever or whatever he might be, to keep quiet, and let me get up and light the lamp again. But his guttural responses satisfied me at once that he but ill comprehended my meaning.

“Who-e debel you?”—he at last said—“you no speak-e, dam-me, I kill-e.” And so saying the lighted tomahawk began flourishing about me in the dark.

“Landlord, for God's sake, Peter Coffin!” shouted I. “Landlord! Watch! Coffin! Angels! save me!”

“Speak-e! tell-ee me who-ee be, or dam-me, I kill-e!” again growled the cannibal, while his horrid flourishings of the tomahawk scattered the hot tobacco ashes about me till I thought my linen would get on fire. But thank heaven, at that moment the landlord came into the room light in hand, and leaping from the bed I ran up to him.

“Don't be afraid now,” said he, grinning again, “Queequeg here wouldn't harm a hair of your head.”

“Stop your grinning,” shouted I, “and why didn't you tell me that that infernal harpooneer was a cannibal?”

“I thought ye know'd it;—didn't I tell ye, he was a peddlin' heads around town?—but turn flukes again and go to sleep. Queequeg, look here—you sabbee me, I sabbee—you this man sleepe you—you sabbee?”

“Me sabbee plenty”—grunted Queequeg, puffing away at his pipe and sitting up in bed.

“You gettee in,” he added, motioning to me with his tomahawk, and throwing the clothes to one side. He really did this in not only a civil but a really kind and charitable way. I stood looking at him a moment. For all his tattooings he was on the whole a clean, comely looking cannibal. What's all this fuss I have been making about, thought I to myself—the man's a human being just as I am: he has just as much reason to fear me, as I have to be afraid of him. Better sleep with a sober cannibal than a drunken Christian.

“Landlord,” said I, “tell him to stash his tomahawk there, or pipe, or whatever you call it; tell him to stop smoking, in short, and I will turn in with him. But I don't fancy having a man smoking in bed with me. It's dangerous. Besides, I ain't insured.”

This being told to Queequeg, he at once complied, and again politely motioned me to get into bed—rolling over to one side as much as to say—“I won't touch a leg of ye.”

“Good night, landlord,” said I, “you may go.”

I turned in, and never slept better in my life.
