CHAPTER 114. Di Gilda.

We di Pequod bin de go insay di at fɔ di Japan kruiz graɔn, i nɔ tu te, ɔlman bin de mek pipul dɛn bigin fɔ fishin. Bɔku tɛm, we di wɛda nɔ kin fayn, we kin fayn, fɔ twɛlv, fayvtin, ettin, ɛn twɛnti awa na di strɛch, dɛn kin de insay di bot dɛn, dɛn kin pul, ɔ sel, ɔ padul afta di wɛl dɛn, ɔ fɔ siksti ɔ sɛvinti minit bitwin dɛn we dɛn de wet fɔ mek dɛn tɔn agens di gɔvmɛnt wit kol at; pan ɔl we wit bɔt smɔl sakrifays fɔ dɛn pen dɛn.

Dɛn kayn tɛm dɛn de, ɔnda di san we dɔn stɔp; flot ɔl di de pan smol smol, sloslo heaving swells; sidɔm na in bot, i layt lɛk birch kenu; ɛn so sociably miks wit di saf saf wef dɛnsɛf, dat lɛk hearth-stone pusi dɛn de purr agens di gunwale; dis na di tɛm dɛn we pipul dɛn kin kwayɛt lɛk drim, we pɔsin kin si di kwayɛt fayn fayn tin dɛn ɛn di brayt we di si in skin kin shayn, i kin fɔgɛt di tayga at we de pant ɔnda am; ɛn i nɔ go gri fɔ mɛmba, se dis velvet paw bɔt i de ayd wan fang we nɔ de rigrɛt.

Dis na di tɛm dɛn, we insay in wɛl-bɔt di rova ​​kin fil saful wan wan patikyula filial, kɔnfidɛns, land-layk filin to di si; dat i de si am as bɔku bɔku flawa grɔn; ɛn di ship we de fa we de sho di tɔp dɛn nɔmɔ pan in mast dɛn, i tan lɛk se i de strɛch fɔ go bifo, nɔto tru ay ay wef dɛn we de rɔl, bɔt i de tray tranga wan fɔ go bifo, nɔto tru di lɔng gras dɛn we de na wan ples we de rɔl: lɛk we di pipul dɛn we kɔmɔt na di wɛst pat na di kɔntri in ɔs dɛn jɔs de sho dɛn yes dɛn we dɛn dɔn tinap, we dɛn bɔdi we ayd bɔku bɔku wan wade tru di wɔndaful verdure.

Di vayjin vali dɛn we dɛn dɔn drɔ fɔ lɔng tɛm; di mild blu hil-sayd dɛn; as ova dɛn wan ya de tif di hush, di hum; yu kin klos fɔ swɛ se pikin dɛn we taya fɔ ple kin ledɔm slip na dɛn say dɛn ya we dɛn kin de wan, insay sɔm gladi May-tɛm, we dɛn kin kɔt di flawa dɛn na di bush. Ɛn ɔl dis miks wit yu mɔs mistik mud; so dat tru ɛn fansi, af-af mitin, de go insay, ɛn fɔm wan ɔl we nɔ gɛt wan prɔblɛm.

Ɛn dɛn kayn tin dɛn ya we bin de mek pɔsin fil fayn, ilɛksɛf na fɔ shɔt tɛm nɔmɔ, nɔ bin afɛkt Eab fɔ sɔm tɛm nɔmɔ. Bɔt if i bin tan lɛk se dɛn sikrit gold ki dɛn ya de opin in yon sikrit gold trɔs dɛn insay am, bɔt stil di briz we i blo pan dɛn bin pruv se i nɔ gɛt wan dɔti.

Oh, ples dɛn we gɛt gras! oh, ɛva vernal ɛndlɛs land skay dɛn na di sol; insay una,—pan ɔl we i dɔn dray fɔ lɔng tɛm bikɔs ɔf di dray we di dray we di wɔl gɛt,—insay una, mɔtalman kin stil rɔl, lɛk yɔŋ ɔs dɛn we wɛr nyu mɔnin klova; ɛn fɔ sɔm tɛm dɛn we nɔ de te, fil di kol dyu we de na di layf we nɔ de day pan dɛn. Would to God dis blɛsin kolm go las. Bɔt di miks, miks trɛd dɛn na layf, dɛn kin wev am wit wɔp ɛn wuf: kol we big big briz kin krɔs, wan big big briz fɔ ɛvri kol at. No stedi unretracing progress nɔ de na dis layf; wi nɔ de go bifo tru fiks gradɛshɔn, ɛn di las wan de stɔp:—tru di spɛl we i nɔ no natin we i bebi, di fet we i nɔ de tink bɔt we i smɔl, di dawt we i de dawt we i yɔŋ (di kɔmɔn tin we go apin to am), dɔn di we aw i de dawt, dɔn we i nɔ biliv, we i de rɛst las las insay di we aw man de tink gud wan fɔ rɛst if If. Bɔt wans wi dɔn go tru, wi kin tray di rawnd bak; ɛn na bebi, bɔy pikin, ɛn man, ɛn If dɛn sote go. Usay di las haba de, usay wi nɔ de pul di watawɛl igen? Insay us rapt ɛta di wɔl de sel, we di wan dɛn we taya pas ɔl nɔ go ɛva taya pan? Usay di foundling in papa ayd? Wi sol tan lɛk dɛn pikin dɛn we nɔ gɛt mama ɛn papa we dɛn mama we nɔ mared de day we dɛn de bɔn dɛn: di sikrit bɔt wi papa de na dɛn grev, ɛn wi fɔ de de fɔ lan am.

Ɛn da sem de de bak, we i bin de luk fa dɔŋ frɔm in bot in sayd insay da sem gold si de, Stabɔk bin de grɔmbul wit lɔv se:—

“Lɔv we pɔsin nɔ ebul fɔ ɔndastand, lɛk aw pɔsin we lɛk pɔsin kin si na in yɔŋ yawo in yay!—Nɔ tɛl mi bɔt yu shak dɛn we gɛt tit, ɛn di we aw yu kin kidnap pipul dɛn. Mek fet pul di tru tin kɔmɔt; lɛ fansi pul mɛmori; A de luk dip dip wan ɛn a biliv.”

Ɛn Stub, we tan lɛk fish, wit spak spak skel, jomp ɔp insay da sem gold layt de:—

“Mi na Stubb, ɛn Stubb gɛt in istri; bɔt na ya Stub tek swɛ se i dɔn de gladi ɔltɛm!”





CHAPTER 115. Di Pequod Mit Di Bachelor.

“Kam insay di bot, kam insay di bot!” di gay Bachelor in kɔmanda kray, i es wan glas ɛn wan bɔtul na di ays.

“Yu dɔn si di Wait Wɛl?” grit Eab fɔ ansa am.

"Nɔ; na in nɔmɔ dɛn yɛri bɔt am; bɔt nɔ biliv am atɔl,” ​​na so di ɔda wan tɔk wit gud kɔmɛnt. “Kam insay di bot!”

“Yu na tu damned jolly. Sail on. Yu dɔn lɔs ɛni man?”

“Nɔto inof fɔ tɔk bɔt—tu pipul dɛn na di ayland, na dat nɔmɔ;—bɔt kam insay di bot, ol at, kam wit am. A go soon tek dat blak fo yu brow. Kam along, yu go (merry's di ple); ship we ful-ɔp ɛn we de go bak na os.”

“Fɔlman rili wɔndaful!” Eab bin de grɔmbul; dɔn lawd wan se, “Yu na ful ship ɛn yu de go bak na os, yu se; wɛl, so, kɔl mi ɛmti ship, ɛn we de go na do. So go yu we, ɛn a go bi mi yon. Fɔ go bifo de! Una put ɔl di bot, ɛn kip am to di briz!”

Ɛn so, we di wan ship bin de go wit gladi at bifo di briz, di ɔda wan bin trangayes fɔ fɛt am; ɛn na so di tu bɔtul dɛn sheb; di kru na di Pequod de luk wit grev, lingering glans to di Bachelor we de go bak; bɔt di Bachelor in man dɛn nɔ ɛva lisin to dɛn yay fɔ di layfful ​​revelry we dɛn bin de insay. Ɛn as Eab, we bin de ledɔm oba di tafrail, de yay di kraft we de go na os, i tek wan smɔl vayl we gɛt sand na in poket, dɔn i luk frɔm di ship to di vayl, i bin tan lɛk se dat de briŋ tu rimot asosieshɔn dɛn togɛda, bikɔs da vayl de bin ful-ɔp wit Nantucket soundings.





CHAPTER 116. Di Wɛl we De Day.

Nɔto smɔl tɛm insay dis layf, we, na di rayt say, di wan dɛn we fɔchɔ de lɛk kin sel nia wi, wi, pan ɔl we wi ɔl kin adrop bifo, kin kech sɔm pan di briz we de rɔsh, ɛn gladi fɔ fil se wi bag sel dɛn ful-ɔp. So i bin tan lɛk se i bin de wit di Pequod. Fɔ di nɛks de afta dɛn mit di gay Bachelor, dɛn si wɛl ɛn kil 4; ɛn wan pan dɛn na Eab.

I bin de fa fawe di aftanun; ɛn we ɔl di spia dɛn we di krimsin fɛt bin dɔn: ɛn we dɛn bin de flɔt na di fayn fayn si ɛn skay we di san de go dɔŋ, di san ɛn wɛl ɔl tu stil day togɛda; dɔn, so swit ɛn so kray, so inwreathing orisons kɔl up in dat rosy air, dat i bin lɛf smɔl fɔ mek i tan lɛk se fa frɔm di dip grin kɔnvɛnt vali dɛn na di Manila ayland dɛn, di Spanish land-breeze, wantonly turned seilor, dɔn go to si, we dɛn kin kɛr dɛn wit dɛn vesper hymns ya.

Eab, we bin dɔn stɛp frɔm di wɛl, bin fil fayn bak, bɔt i bin jɔs kol to dip daknɛs, i bin sidɔm gud gud wan de wach di las we aw i bin de stɔp frɔm di bot we dɔn kwayɛt naw. Fɔ da strenj spɛktakl we dɛn kin si pan ɔl di sperm wɛl dɛn we de day—di tɔn to di san to di ed, ɛn so i de day—da strenj spɛktakl, we dɛn si fɔ da kayn ivintɛm de we nɔ gɛt wanwɔd, sɔm kayn we to Eab bin gi wan wɔndaful tin we dɛn nɔ bin no bifo.

“I tɔn ɛn tɔn am to am,—aw sloslo, bɔt aw i nɔ de chenj, in ɔmaj-rendrin ɛn invok brɔw, wit in las day muvmɛnt dɛn. Insɛf de wɔship faya; mɔs fetful, brayt, baronial vassal of the san!—Oh if dɛn yay ya we de fav tumɔs fɔ si dɛn tin ya we de fav tumɔs. Luk! na ya, fa fawe wata-lɔk; pas ɔl di hum fɔ mɔtalman jɛntri ɔ prɔblɛm; insay dɛn si dɛn ya we rili klia ɛn we nɔ de tek wan pɔsin bɛtɛ pas ɔda pɔsin; usay to tradishɔn dɛn nɔ rɔk de gi tablɛt; usay fɔ lɔng tɛm Chaina ej, di bilɔ dɛn stil de rol pan pipul dɛn we nɔ de tɔk ɛn we dɛn nɔ de tɔk to, lɛk sta dɛn we de shayn pan di say we di Nayja kɔmɔt we dɛn nɔ no; na ya bak, layf de day to di san we ful-ɔp wit fet; bɔt si! i nɔ jɔs day, day de rɔn rawnd di bɔdi, ɛn i de go ɔda say.

“O, yu dak Hindu af pan di nature, we wit bon we drawn yu bil yu seperet tron ​​somwe fo di hat of dis si we no de verda; yu na infidel, yu kwin, ɛn tu tru de tɔk to mi insay di Tayfun we de kil bɔku bɔku wan, ɛn di hushed bɛrin fɔ in afta kol. Nɔto dis yu wɛl we de go na di san dɔn tɔn in ed we de day, ɛn afta dat i dɔn go rawnd bak, we nɔ gɛt lɛsin to mi.

“O, trebly hooped ɛn wɛlded hip ɔf pawa! Oh, high aspiring, renbowed jet!—dat wan de tray, dis wan de jettest ɔl na fɔ natin! Na fɔ natin, oh wɛl, yu de luk fɔ intɛres wit yon ɔl-kwikin san, we jɔs de kɔl layf, bɔt nɔ de gi am igen. Yet yu, dak haf, de rɔk mi wit wan praud, if wan dak fet. Ɔl yu imingling dɛn we nɔ gɛt nem de flot ɔnda mi ya; A de blo wit tin dɛn we bin de liv trade, we dɛn de blo lɛk briz, bɔt wata naw.

“Dɔn ays blɔk, ays blɔk sote go, O si, we di wayl fɔl kin gɛt in wangren rɛst insay in tos dɛn we go de sote go. Dɛn bɔn am na di wɔl, bɔt stil de milk na di si; pan ɔl we il ɛn vali mek mi mama, yɔlɔ bilɔ na mi brɔda dɛn we de mɛn mi!”





CHAPTER 117. Di Wɛl Wach.

Di 4 wɛl dɛn we dɛn kil da ivintɛm de bin dɔn day fa fawe; wan, we de fa to di say we di briz de blo; wan, we nɔ de fa, to leeward; wan de bifo; wan we de biɛn. Dɛn bin briŋ dɛn las tri ya alongsay ere nɛt; bɔt dɛn nɔ bin ebul fɔ rich di wan we de blo te mɔnin; ɛn di bot we kil am bin ledɔm nia am ɔl nɛt; ɛn da bot de na Eab in yon.

Dɛn bin de trowe di waif-pol stret insay di day wɛl in spɔt-ol; ɛn di lantan we bin de hang frɔm in tap, bin de mek wan trɔbul we de shayn pan di blak, glos bak, ɛn fa fawe pan di midnayt wev dɛn, we bin de chaf di wɛl in brayt flank saful wan, lɛk sɔft sɔf na bich.

I bin tan lɛk se Eab ɛn ɔl di wan dɛn we de wok na in bot dɔn slip bɔt di Pasi; we bin kray na di bɔw, sidɔm de wach di shak dɛn, we bin de ple spɛktral wan rawnd di wɛl, ɛn tap di layt sida plang dɛn wit dɛn tel. Wan sawnd lɛk di kray we de kray insay skwadron dɛn oba Asfalt dɛn we nɔ fɔgiv gɔst dɛn na Gɔmɔra, bin de rɔn de shek shek na di ays.

Start frɔm in slip, Eab, fes to fes, si di Parsee; ɛn hoop rawnd bay di daknɛs na di nɛt dɛn bin tan lɛk di las man dɛn na wan wɔl we wata dɔn ful-ɔp. I bin tɔk se: “A dɔn drim am bak.”

“Fɔ di wan dɛn we de kɛr pipul dɛn we dɔn day? Yu nɔ tink se a dɔn tɔk, ol man, se ɔs ɔ kɔfin nɔ go bi yu yon?”

“Ɛn udat dɛn yɛri we de day na di si?”

“Bɔt a se, ol man, bifo yu day pan dis voyage, yu fɔ rili si tu hears na di si; di fɔs wan we nɔto mɔtalman an mek; ɛn di wud we wi kin si fɔ di las wan, dɛn fɔ plant am na Amɛrika.”

“Aye, aye! wan strenj sight dat, Parsee:—wan hearse ɛn in plumes we de flot oba di si wit di wef fɔ di pall-bearers. Ha! Wi nɔ go si dis kayn tin jisnɔ.”

CHAPTER 119. Di Kandul dɛn.

Klaym dɛn we wam pas ɔl bɔt dɛn de nɔs di fang dɛn we kruk pas ɔl: di tayga na Bɛngal de kray na tik dɛn we gɛt spays we gɛt grɔn we nɔ de stɔp. As in the hurricane that sweeps the plain, men fly the neighborhood of some lone, gigantic elm, whose very height and strength but render it so much the more unsafe, because so much the more a mark for thunderbolts; so at those last words of Ahab's many of the mariners did run from him in a terror of dismay.

CHAPTER 123. The Musket.

During the most violent shocks of the Typhoon, the man at the Pequod's jaw-bone tiller had several times been reelingly hurled to the deck by its spasmodic motions, even though preventer tackles had been attached to it—for they were slack—because some play to the tiller was indispensable.

In a severe gale like this, while the ship is but a tossed shuttlecock to the blast, it is by no means uncommon to see the needles in the compasses, at intervals, go round and round. It was thus with the Pequod's; at almost every shock the helmsman had not failed to notice the whirling velocity with which they revolved upon the cards; it is a sight that hardly anyone can behold without some sort of unwonted emotion.

Some hours after midnight, the Typhoon abated so much, that through the strenuous exertions of Starbuck and Stubb—one engaged forward and the other aft—the shivered remnants of the jib and fore and main-top-sails were cut adrift from the spars, and went eddying away to leeward, like the feathers of an albatross, which sometimes are cast to the winds when that storm-tossed bird is on the wing.

The three corresponding new sails were now bent and reefed, and a storm-trysail was set further aft; so that the ship soon went through the water with some precision again; and the course—for the present, East-south-east—which he was to steer, if practicable, was once more given to the helmsman. For during the violence of the gale, he had only steered according to its vicissitudes. But as he was now bringing the ship as near her course as possible, watching the compass meanwhile, lo! a good sign! the wind seemed coming round astern; aye, the foul breeze became fair!

Instantly the yards were squared, to the lively song of “ Ho! the fair wind! oh-ye-ho, cheerly men! ” the crew singing for joy, that so promising an event should so soon have falsified the evil portents preceding it.

In compliance with the standing order of his commander—to report immediately, and at any one of the twenty-four hours, any decided change in the affairs of the deck,—Starbuck had no sooner trimmed the yards to the breeze—however reluctantly and gloomily,—than he mechanically went below to apprise Captain Ahab of the circumstance.

Ere knocking at his state-room, he involuntarily paused before it a moment. The cabin lamp—taking long swings this way and that—was burning fitfully, and casting fitful shadows upon the old man's bolted door,—a thin one, with fixed blinds inserted, in place of upper panels. The isolated subterraneousness of the cabin made a certain humming silence to reign there, though it was hooped round by all the roar of the elements. The loaded muskets in the rack were shiningly revealed, as they stood upright against the forward bulkhead. Starbuck was an honest, upright man; but out of Starbuck's heart, at that instant when he saw the muskets, there strangely evolved an evil thought; but so blent with its neutral or good accompaniments that for the instant he hardly knew it for itself.
