CAPÍTULO 1. Asoxacións.

Chámame Ismael. Hai uns anos, sen importar canto tempo, precisamente, tendo pouco ou ningún diñeiro no meu bolso, e nada especial que me interesase na costa, pensei en navegar un pouco e ver a parte acuática do mundo. É unha forma que teño de expulsar o bazo e regular a circulación. Sempre que me atopo cada vez máis sombrío pola boca; sempre que é un novembro húmido e chuvioso na miña alma; sempre que me atopo involuntariamente facendo unha pausa ante os almacéns de cadaleitos e subindo á parte traseira de cada funeral que coñezo; e, sobre todo, sempre que os meus hipopótamos me dominan tanto, que esixe un forte principio moral que me impida saír á rúa deliberadamente e botarlle metódicamente o sombreiro á xente; como podo. Este é o meu substituto de pistola e pelota. Cun florecemento filosófico Catón lánzase sobre a súa espada; Levo tranquilamente ao barco. Non hai nada sorprendente nisto. Se o soubesen, case todos os homes na súa titulación, algún tempo ou outro, aprecian case os mesmos sentimentos cara ao océano ca min.

Agora está a túa cidade insular dos Manhattoes, rodeada de peiraos como illas indias por arrecifes de coral: o comercio enródea co seu surf. A dereita e a esquerda, as rúas lévanche cara á auga. O seu centro extremo é a batería, onde esa nobre toupa é lavada polas ondas, e arrefriada polas brisas, que unhas horas antes estaban fóra da vista da terra. Mira as multitudes de observadores da auga alí.

Circumambula a cidade dunha tarde de sábado soñadora. Vaia desde Corlears Hook ata Coenties Slip, e desde alí, por Whitehall, cara ao norte. Que ves? - Publicados como sentinelas silenciosas por toda a cidade, están miles e miles de homes mortais fixados en ensoñacións oceánicas. Algúns apoiados contra os spiles; algúns sentados sobre os peiraos; algúns mirando sobre os baluartes dos barcos procedentes de China; algúns no alto no aparello, como se esforzasen por conseguir unha mirada aínda mellor cara ao mar. Pero estes son todos terratenentes; dos días da semana reprimidos en listóns e xeso, atados a mostradores, cravados en bancos, pegados a escritorios. Como é entón isto? Os campos verdes desapareceron? Que fan aquí?

Pero mira! aquí chegan máis multitudes, camiñando en liña recta cara á auga e aparentemente destinadas a mergullarse. Estraño! Nada os contentará senón o límite máis extremo da terra; merodear baixo o sombrío sotavento dos almacéns de aí non será suficiente. Non. Deben achegarse á auga o máis posible sen caer. E alí están, millas deles, leguas. Todos os habitantes do interior veñen de carrís e rúas, rúas e avenidas: norte, leste, sur e oeste. Con todo, aquí todos se unen. Dime, a virtude magnética das agullas dos compás de todos aqueles barcos atraeos ata alí?

Unha vez máis. Di que estás no país; nalgunha terra alta de lagos. Toma case calquera camiño que queiras, e dez a un lévate abaixo nun val e déixate alí xunto a un estanque no regato. Hai maxia nel. Deixa que o máis distraído dos homes estea mergullado nas súas ensoñacións máis profundas: pon a ese home sobre as súas pernas, pon os pés en marcha, e conducirá infaliblemente á auga, se hai auga en toda esa rexión. Se algunha vez tes sede no gran deserto americano, proba este experimento, se a túa caravana ten un profesor de metafísica. Si, como todo o mundo sabe, a meditación e a auga están casadas para sempre.

Pero aquí hai un artista. Desexa pintarche a paisaxe romántica máis soñadora, sombría, tranquila e encantadora de todo o val do Saco. Cal é o elemento principal que emprega? Alí están as súas árbores, cada unha cun tronco oco, coma se dentro estivesen un eremita e un crucifixo; e aquí dorme o seu prado, e alí dorme o seu gando; e daquela casiña sae un fume sonoro. No fondo dos bosques afastados, ventos dun xeito labirinto, chegando a esporóns superpostos de montañas bañadas polo azul dos seus outeiros. Pero aínda que a imaxe estea así en transo, e aínda que este piñeiro axita os seus suspiros como follas sobre a cabeza deste pastor, todos foron vans, a non ser que o ollo do pastor estivese fixado na corrente máxica ante el. Vaia visitar as Praderas en xuño, cando durante decenas de quilómetros vadeas ata os xeonllos entre os lirios tigre, cal é o único encanto que quere? Auga, non hai unha pinga de auga alí! Se o Niágara fose unha catarata de area, percorrerías os teus mil quilómetros para velo? Por que o pobre poeta de Tennessee, ao recibir de súpeto dous puñados de prata, deliberou se comprarlle un abrigo, que lamentablemente necesitaba, ou investir o seu diñeiro nunha viaxe peonil a Rockaway Beach? Por que case todos os nenos robustos e saudables cunha alma sana e robusta están tolos nalgún momento por ir ao mar? Por que na súa primeira viaxe como pasaxeiro, vostede mesmo sentiu unha vibración tan mística cando lle dixeron por primeira vez que vostede e o seu barco estaban agora fóra da vista da terra? Por que os vellos persas mantiveron o mar santo? Por que os gregos lle deron unha divindade separada e irmán propio de Xove? Seguramente todo isto non carece de sentido. E aínda máis profundo o significado daquela historia de Narciso, que por non comprender a atormentadora e suave imaxe que vía na fonte, mergullouse nela e morreu afogado. Pero esa mesma imaxe, nós mesmos vemos en todos os ríos e océanos. É a imaxe da pantasma inagarrable da vida; e esta é a clave de todo.

Agora ben, cando digo que teño o costume de ir ao mar cada vez que empezo a facerme nebuloso cos ollos e empezo a estar demasiado consciente dos meus pulmóns, non quero que se deduza que eu vou ao mar. un pasaxeiro. Para ir como pasaxeiro é preciso ter unha bolsa, e unha bolsa non é máis que un trapo a non ser que teñas algo. Ademais, os pasaxeiros adoecen de mar -se rixen -non dormen de noites- non se divirten moito, en xeral;-non, eu nunca vou de pasaxeiro; nin, aínda que son algo salgado, vou nunca ao mar como comodoro, ou capitán ou cociñeiro. Abandono a gloria e distinción de tales cargos aos que lles gustan. Pola miña parte, abomino todos os traballos, probas e tribulacións honradas e respectables de todo tipo. É todo o que podo facer para coidarme, sen ocuparme de barcos, barcas, bergantíns, goletas, e que non. E en canto a ir de cociñeiro -aínda que confeso que hai unha gloria considerable, sendo un cociñeiro unha especie de oficial a bordo do barco-, porén, dalgún xeito, nunca me apeteceu facer as aves; salgado e pementado, non hai quen fale máis respetuosamente, por non dicir reverencialmente, dunha galiña á brasa ca eu. A partir dos ídolos idolátricos dos antigos exipcios sobre ibis asado e cabalo de río asado, ves as momias desas criaturas nas súas enormes panaderías, as pirámides.

Non, cando vou ao mar, vou como un simple mariñeiro, xusto antes do mastro, abaixo no castelo de proa, alí arriba ata a cabeceira real. Certo que prefiren encargarme dalgúns, e facerme saltar de espada en espada, coma un saltón nun prado de maio. E ao principio, este tipo de cousas son bastante desagradables. Toca o sentido da honra, sobre todo se procedes dunha vella familia establecida na terra, os Van Rensselaer, ou Randolph ou os Hardicanutes. E máis que todo, se só antes de meter a man na pota de alcatrán, estiveches como mestre de escola rural, facendo que os rapaces máis altos te asombraran. A transición é intensa, asegúrovos, de mestre de escola a mariñeiro, e require unha forte decocción de Séneca e os estoicos para poder sorrir e soportar. Pero mesmo isto desaparece co tempo.

Que pasa, se uns vellos anciáns de capitán de mar me mandan coller unha vasoira e varrer as cubertas? A que supón esa indignidade, ponderada, quero dicir, na balanza do Novo Testamento? Cres que o arcanxo Gabriel pensa algo menos de min, porque eu obedezo con prontitude e respeto a ese vello cacho nese caso concreto? Quen non é escravo? Dime iso. Pois ben, por moito que me ordenen os vellos capitáns de mar, por moito que me dean a puñetazos, teño a satisfacción de saber que está ben; que todos os demais son servidos dun xeito ou doutro máis ou menos do mesmo xeito, xa sexa desde un punto de vista físico ou metafísico, é dicir; e así pasa o golpe universal, e todas as mans deben fregar os omóplatos entre si e contentarse.

De novo, eu sempre vou ao mar como mariñeiro, porque eles se preocupan por pagarme polos meus problemas, mentres que nunca lles pagan aos pasaxeiros nin un céntimo do que eu oín falar. Pola contra, os propios pasaxeiros deben pagar. E hai toda a diferenza do mundo entre pagar e ser pagado. O feito de pagar é quizais a imposición máis incómoda que nos supuxeron os dous ladróns da horta. Pero ser pagado , que se comparará con iso? A actividade urbana coa que un home recibe diñeiro é realmente marabillosa, tendo en conta que cremos tan sinceramente que o diñeiro é a raíz de todos os males terrestres, e que de ningún xeito pode entrar no ceo un home aforado. Ah! que alegre nos entregamos á perdición!

Finalmente, sempre vou ao mar como mariñeiro, polo exercicio saudable e o aire puro da cuberta do castelo de proa. Pois, como neste mundo, os ventos de cabeza son moito máis frecuentes que os de popa (é dicir, se nunca se viola a máxima pitagórica), así que, na súa maior parte, o comodoro da cuberta recibe a súa atmosfera de segunda man dos mariñeiros. no castelo de proa. Pensa que o respira primeiro; pero non así. Do mesmo xeito que o común leva aos seus líderes noutras moitas cousas, ao tempo que os dirixentes pouco o sospeitan. Pero por iso foi que, despois de ter cheirado repetidamente o mar como un mariñeiro mercante, agora tiña que pensar na miña cabeza para facer unha viaxe baleeira; este é o oficial de policía invisible dos Fates, que ten a constante vixilancia de min e me persigue en segredo e inflúe en min dalgún xeito inexplicable: pode responder mellor que ninguén. E, sen dúbida, o meu ir a esta viaxe baleeira, formou parte do gran programa da Providencia que foi elaborado hai moito tempo. Chegou como unha especie de breve interludio e solo entre actuacións máis extensas. Supoño que esta parte da factura debe ter algo así:

" Gran disputada elección para a presidencia dos Estados Unidos. “VIAXE CAZA DA BALEA POR UN ISMAEL. "BATALLA SANGUENTA EN AFGANISTÁN".

Aínda que non podo dicir por que foi exactamente o que eses directores de escena, os Fates, puxéronme para esta lamentable parte dunha viaxe baleeira, cando outros foron destinados a partes magníficas en grandes traxedias, e partes curtas e fáciles en comedias elegantes, e partes alegres en farsas, aínda que non podo dicir por que foi isto exactamente; con todo, agora que recordo todas as circunstancias, creo que podo ver un pouco as fontes e os motivos que, sendo astutamente presentados para min baixo varios disfraces, inducíronme a realizar o papel que fixen, ademais de enganarme coa ilusión de que foi unha elección resultante do meu propio libre albedrío imparcial e dun xuízo discriminatorio.

O principal destes motivos foi a idea esmagadora da propia balea. Un monstro tan portentoso e misterioso espertou toda a miña curiosidade. Despois os mares salvaxes e afastados onde rolou a súa illa a granel; os perigos non entregables e sen nome da balea; estes, con todas as marabillas que acompañan a mil vistas e sons patagónicos, axudáronme a facerme chegar ao meu desexo. Con outros homes, quizais, tales cousas non serían incentivos; pero en canto a min, estou atormentado cunha coceira eterna polas cousas remotas. Encántame navegar por mares prohibidos e desembarcar en costas bárbaras. Sen ignorar o que é bo, estou pronto para percibir un horror, e aínda podería ser social con el -deixáranme-, xa que é bo estar en boas relacións con todos os reclusos do lugar onde se aloxa.

Por estas cousas, pois, a viaxe baleeira era benvida; as grandes compuertas do mundo das marabillas abríronse, e nas salvaxes presuncións que me influenciaron para o meu propósito, alí flotaron dous e dous no máis íntimo da miña alma, interminables procesións da balea e, na maioría delas, unha gran fantasma encapuchado, coma un outeiro de neve no aire.





CAPÍTULO 2. A Alfombra-Bolsa.

Metei unha camisa ou dúas na miña vella bolsa de moqueta, meteuna debaixo do brazo e marchei cara ao cabo de Hornos e ao Pacífico. Saíndo da boa cidade do vello Manhattan, cheguei debidamente a New Bedford. Foi un sábado pola noite de decembro. Decepcionoume moito ao saber que o pequeno paquete para Nantucket xa navegara e que ningún xeito de chegar a ese lugar me ofrecería ata o luns seguinte.

Como a maioría dos mozos candidatos ás penas e penas da caza da balea paran neste mesmo New Bedford para emprender a súa viaxe, tamén se pode relacionar que eu, por exemplo, non tiña idea de facelo. Porque a miña mente estaba decidida a navegar en nada máis que unha embarcación de Nantucket, porque había algo bonito e bullicioso en todo o relacionado con aquela famosa illa vella, que me gustou asombrosamente. Ademais, aínda que recentemente New Bedford foi monopolizando gradualmente o negocio da caza de baleas, e aínda que neste asunto a pobre vella Nantucket está agora moi atrás dela, Nantucket foi o seu gran orixinal, o Tire desta Cartago, o lugar onde o primeiro estadounidense morto. balea quedou varada. Onde máis que desde Nantucket saíron por primeira vez aqueles baleeiros aborixes, os Homes Vermellos, en canoas para perseguir ao Leviatán? E onde, senón tamén desde Nantucket, saíu aquela primeira balandra aventureira, en parte cargada de adoquíns importados -así conta a historia- para tirarlles ás baleas, para descubrir cando estaban o suficientemente preto como para arriscar un arpón do bauprés. ?

Agora, tendo unha noite, un día e outra noite máis seguindo ante min en New Bedford, antes de que puidese embarcar cara ao meu porto destinado, tornouse unha cuestión de preocupación por onde debía comer e durmir mentres tanto. Foi unha noite moi escura e lúgubre, de aspecto moi dubidoso, moi fría e sen alegría. Non coñecía a ninguén no lugar. Coas ansiosas garfias sonara no peto e só saquei unhas poucas pezas de prata. Así que, onde queira que vaias, Ismael, dixen para min, mentres estaba no medio dunha rúa lúgubre cargando o meu bolso e comparando penumbra cara ao norte coa escuridade cara ao sur; onde queira que poidas, na túa sabedoría, concluír a pasar a noite, meu querido Ismael, asegúrate de preguntar polo prezo e non sexas demasiado particular.

Con pasos vacilantes pasei polas rúas e pasei o letreiro de "Os arpóns cruzados", pero alí parecía demasiado caro e alegre. Máis adiante, das fiestras vermellas brillantes da "Posada do Peixe Espada", saían raios tan fervorosos, que parecía que derretiran a neve e o xeo acumulados de diante da casa, pois en todas partes a xeada conxelada estaba a dez centímetros de espesor. un pavimento duro e asfáltico, bastante canso para min, cando batei o meu pé contra as proxeccións de pedernal, porque por un servizo duro e implacable as solas das miñas botas estaban nunha situación miserable. Demasiado caro e alegre, pensei de novo, facendo unha pausa un momento para observar o resplandor amplo da rúa e escoitar os sons dos lentes tintineando dentro. Pero segue, Ismael, dixen por fin; non escoitas? afastarse de diante da porta; as túas botas remendadas están parando o camiño. Así que fun. Agora seguía por instinto as rúas que me levaban cara á auga, pois alí, sen dúbida, estaban as pousadas máis baratas, se non as máis alegres.

Que rúas tan tristes! bloques de negrura, non casas, a un e outro lado, e aquí e alí unha vela, como unha vela que se move nunha tumba. A esta hora da noite, do último día da semana, aquel barrio da cidade estaba case deserto. Pero axiña cheguei a unha luz fumegante que procedía dun edificio baixo e ancho, cuxa porta estaba aberta de xeito invitante. Tiña un aspecto descoidado, coma se fose destinado aos usos do público; así que, entrando, o primeiro que fixen foi tropezar cun freixo no alpendre. Ha! pensei eu, ja, mentres as partículas voadoras case me atragan, son estas cinzas daquela cidade destruída, Gomorra? Pero "Os arpóns cruzados" e "O peixe espada?", este debe ser o sinal de "A trampa". Non obstante, erguínme e escoitando unha voz forte dentro, empurrei e abrín unha segunda porta interior.

Parecía o gran Parlamento Negro sentado en Tophet. Cen caras negras volvéronse nas súas filas para mirar; e máis aló, un anxo negro da fatalidade batía un libro nun púlpito. Era unha igrexa negra; e o texto do predicador trataba da negrura das tebras, e dos choros, lamentos e rechinar de dentes alí. Ha, Ismael, murmurei eu, retrocedendo, Desgraciado entretemento ante o sinal de 'A Trampa!'

Seguindo adiante, por fin cheguei a unha especie de luz tenue non lonxe dos peiraos, e escoitei un chirrido desesperado no aire; e mirando cara arriba, viu un letreiro balanceado sobre a porta cunha pintura branca enriba, representando débilmente un alto chorro recto de chorro brumoso, e estas palabras debaixo: "The Spouter Inn: Peter Coffin".

Ataúde? -¿Spouter? -Bastante sinistro nesa conexión en particular, pensei. Pero é un nome común en Nantucket, din, e supoño que este Peter aquí é un emigrante de alí. Como a luz parecía tan tenue, e o lugar, para o momento, parecía o suficientemente tranquilo, e a casiña de madeira en ruínas parecía como se puidese ser transportada aquí das ruínas dalgún barrio queimado, e como o letreiro balanceado tiña un Unha especie de crujido de pobreza, pensei que aquí era o lugar para aloxamentos baratos e o mellor do café de chícharos.

Era unha especie de lugar raro: unha vella casa cun extremo a dúas augas, por un lado paralítico e inclinada tristemente. Estaba nun recuncho agudo e sombrío, onde aquel vento tempestuoso Euroclydon mantivo un ouveo peor que nunca sobre a embarcación lanzada do pobre Paul. Euroclydon, con todo, é un céfiro poderoso e agradable para calquera que está dentro das portas, cos pés sobre a vitrocerámica brindando tranquilamente pola cama. "A xulgar ese vento tempestuoso chamado Euroclidon", di un vello escritor, de cuxas obras posúo a única copia que se conserva, "fai unha diferenza marabillosa, se o miras desde unha fiestra de cristal onde a xeada está toda sobre o fondo. fóra, ou se o observas desde esa ventá sen guinda, onde a xeada está a ambos os dous lados, e da que a morte é o único vidrieiro. É certo, pensei, mentres se me ocorreu esta pasaxe: vella letra negra, ben razoas. Si, estes ollos son fiestras, e este meu corpo é a casa. Que mágoa que non detiveran as fendas e as grietas, e meteren un pouco de pelusa aquí e alí. Pero é demasiado tarde para facer melloras agora. O universo está rematado; o copestone está aceso, e as fichas foron retiradas hai un millón de anos. O pobre Lázaro alí, casteando os dentes contra o bordillo para a súa almofada, e sacudindo os seus farrapos cos seus estremecimientos, podería tapar as dúas orellas con farrapos e meterlle unha mazorca de millo na boca, pero iso non impediría tempestuoso Euroclydon. Euroclydon! di o vello Dives, no seu envoltorio de seda vermella... (despois tivo un máis vermello) pooh, pooh! Que boa noite de xeada; como brilla Orión; que auroras boreais! Que falen dos seus climas orientais de verán de conservatorios eternos; dáme o privilexio de facer o meu propio verán coas miñas propias brasas.

Pero que pensa Lázaro? Poderá quentar as súas mans azuis levantándoas cara á gran aurora boreal? Non preferiría Lázaro estar en Sumatra que aquí? Non preferiría deitalo lonxitudinalmente ao longo da liña do ecuador? si, deuses! baixar ao propio pozo ardente, para evitar esta xeada?

Agora, que Lázaro quede varado alí na beiravía ante a porta de Dives, isto é máis marabilloso que que un iceberg estea amarrado nunha das Molucas. Con todo Dives, tamén vive como un Zar nun palacio de xeo feito de suspiros xeados, e sendo presidente dunha sociedade de temperanza, só bebe as tépedas bágoas dos orfos.

Pero agora xa non se fan nada, imos cazar baleas, e aínda queda moito por vir. Raspemos o xeo dos nosos pés xeados e vexamos que clase de lugar pode ser este "Spouter".





CAPÍTULO 3. O Spouter-Inn.

Ao entrar nese Spouter-Inn, rematado a dúas augas, atopácheste nunha entrada ampla, baixa e desfasada, con revestimentos anticuados, que lembraban a un dos baluartes dalgún vello oficio condenado. Nun lado colgaba unha pintura ao óleo moi grande, tan ben afumada e desfigurada en todos os sentidos, que, nas desiguais luces cruzadas polas que a mirabas, só era un estudo dilixente e unha serie de visitas sistemáticas a ela, e unha coidadosa indagación dos veciños. que de calquera xeito podería chegar a comprender o seu propósito. Tan inexplicables masas de sombras e sombras, que ao principio case pensabas que algún artista novo e ambicioso, na época das bruxas de Nova Inglaterra, se esforzara por delinear o caos embruxado. Pero a forza de moita e seria contemplación, e de meditacións a miúdo repetidas, e especialmente abrindo a pequena fiestra cara ao fondo da entrada, finalmente chegas á conclusión de que tal idea, por salvaxe que sexa, pode non ser totalmente inxustificada.

Pero o que máis te confundiu e confundiu foi unha longa, áxil, portentosa e negra masa de algo flotando no centro da imaxe sobre tres liñas azuis, tenues e perpendiculares flotando nun fermento sen nome. Unha imaxe encharcada, empapada e irregular, o suficiente para distraer a un home nervioso. Con todo, había unha especie de sublimidade indefinida, medio alcanzada e inimaxinable que te conxelaba bastante, ata que involuntariamente xurou contigo mesmo para descubrir o que significaba aquela marabillosa pintura. De vez en cando unha idea brillante, pero, por desgraza, enganosa te lanzaría.-É o Mar Negro nun vendaval de medianoite.-É o combate antinatural dos catro elementos primixenios.-É unha breza maldita.-É unha escena invernal hiperbórea. .-É a ruptura da corrente de xeo do Tempo. Pero ao fin todas estas fantasías cederon a aquel algo portentoso no medio da imaxe. Iso unha vez descuberto, e todo o resto foi claro. Pero para; non ten un lixeiro parecido cun peixe xigantesco? ata o propio gran leviatán?

De feito, o deseño do artista parecía este: unha teoría final propia, en parte baseada nas opinións agregadas de moitas persoas maiores coas que conversei sobre o tema. A imaxe representa a un cabo de Hornos nun gran furacán; o buque medio afundido que se abalanzaba alí cos seus tres mastros desmantelados só visibles; e unha balea exasperada, coa intención de saltar limpo sobre a embarcación, está no enorme acto de empalar-se nas tres cabezas de mastro.

A parede oposta desta entrada estaba colgada por todas partes cunha serie pagana de porras e lanzas monstruosas. Algúns tiñan uns dentes relucentes que semellaban serras de marfil; outros estaban ateigados de nós de cabelo humano; e un tiña forma de fouce, cun vasto mango varrindo como o segmento feito na herba recién segada por unha cortadora de brazo longo. Estremeceches mentres mirabas, e preguntácheste que monstruoso caníbal e salvaxe poderían facer unha colleita de morte cun instrumento tan arrepiante e arrepiante. Mesturados con estes había lanzas baleeiras oxidadas e arpóns todos rotos e deformados. Algunhas eran armas de historia. It was now about nine o'clock, and the room seeming almost supernaturally quiet after these orgies, I began to congratulate myself upon a little plan that had occurred to me just previous to the entrance of the seamen.

No man prefers to sleep two in a bed. In fact, you would a good deal rather not sleep with your own brother. I don't know how it is, but people like to be private when they are sleeping. And when it comes to sleeping with an unknown stranger, in a strange inn, in a strange town, and that stranger a harpooneer, then your objections indefinitely multiply. Nor was there any earthly reason why I as a sailor should sleep two in a bed, more than anybody else; for sailors no more sleep two in a bed at sea, than bachelor Kings do ashore. To be sure they all sleep together in one apartment, but you have your own hammock, and cover yourself with your own blanket, and sleep in your own skin.

The more I pondered over this harpooneer, the more I abominated the thought of sleeping with him. It was fair to presume that being a harpooneer, his linen or woollen, as the case might be, would not be of the tidiest, certainly none of the finest. I began to twitch all over. Besides, it was getting late, and my decent harpooneer ought to be home and going bedwards. Suppose now, he should tumble in upon me at midnight—how could I tell from what vile hole he had been coming?

“Landlord! I've changed my mind about that harpooneer.—I shan't sleep with him. I'll try the bench here.”

“Just as you please; I'm sorry I can't spare ye a tablecloth for a mattress, and it's a plaguy rough board here”—feeling of the knots and notches. “But wait a bit, Skrimshander; I've got a carpenter's plane there in the bar—wait, I say, and I'll make ye snug enough.” So saying he procured the plane; and with his old silk handkerchief first dusting the bench, vigorously set to planing away at my bed, the while grinning like an ape. The shavings flew right and left; till at last the plane-iron came bump against an indestructible knot. The landlord was near spraining his wrist, and I told him for heaven's sake to quit—the bed was soft enough to suit me, and I did not know how all the planing in the world could make eider down of a pine plank. So gathering up the shavings with another grin, and throwing them into the great stove in the middle of the room, he went about his business, and left me in a brown study.

I now took the measure of the bench, and found that it was a foot too short; but that could be mended with a chair. But it was a foot too narrow, and the other bench in the room was about four inches higher than the planed one—so there was no yoking them. I then placed the first bench lengthwise along the only clear space against the wall, leaving a little interval between, for my back to settle down in. But I soon found that there came such a draught of cold air over me from under the sill of the window, that this plan would never do at all, especially as another current from the rickety door met the one from the window, and both together formed a series of small whirlwinds in the immediate vicinity of the spot where I had thought to spend the night.

The devil fetch that harpooneer, thought I, but stop, couldn't I steal a march on him—bolt his door inside, and jump into his bed, not to be wakened by the most violent knockings? It seemed no bad idea; but upon second thoughts I dismissed it. For who could tell but what the next morning, so soon as I popped out of the room, the harpooneer might be standing in the entry, all ready to knock me down!

Still, looking round me again, and seeing no possible chance of spending a sufferable night unless in some other person's bed, I began to think that after all I might be cherishing unwarrantable prejudices against this unknown harpooneer. Thinks I, I'll wait awhile; he must be dropping in before long. I'll have a good look at him then, and perhaps we may become jolly good bedfellows after all—there's no telling.

But though the other boarders kept coming in by ones, twos, and threes, and going to bed, yet no sign of my harpooneer.

“Landlord!” said I, “what sort of a chap is he—does he always keep such late hours?” It was now hard upon twelve o'clock.

The landlord chuckled again with his lean chuckle, and seemed to be mightily tickled at something beyond my comprehension. “No,” he answered, “generally he's an early bird—airley to bed and airley to rise—yes, he's the bird what catches the worm. But to-night he went out a peddling, you see, and I don't see what on airth keeps him so late, unless, may be, he can't sell his head.”

“Can't sell his head?—What sort of a bamboozingly story is this you are telling me?” getting into a towering rage. “Do you pretend to say, landlord, that this harpooneer is actually engaged this blessed Saturday night, or rather Sunday morning, in peddling his head around this town?”

“That's precisely it,” said the landlord, “and I told him he couldn't sell it here, the market's overstocked.”

“With what?” shouted I.

“With heads to be sure; ain't there too many heads in the world?”

“I tell you what it is, landlord,” said I quite calmly, “you'd better stop spinning that yarn to me—I'm not green.”

“May be not,” taking out a stick and whittling a toothpick, “but I rayther guess you'll be done brown if that ere harpooneer hears you a slanderin' his head.”

“I'll break it for him,” said I, now flying into a passion again at this unaccountable farrago of the landlord's.

“It's broke a'ready,” said he.

“Broke,” said I—“ broke , do you mean?”

“Sartain, and that's the very reason he can't sell it, I guess.”

“Landlord,” said I, going up to him as cool as Mt. Hecla in a snow-storm—“landlord, stop whittling. You and I must understand one another, and that too without delay. I come to your house and want a bed; you tell me you can only give me half a one; that the other half belongs to a certain harpooneer. And about this harpooneer, whom I have not yet seen, you persist in telling me the most mystifying and exasperating stories tending to beget in me an uncomfortable feeling towards the man whom you design for my bedfellow—a sort of connexion, landlord, which is an intimate and confidential one in the highest degree. I now demand of you to speak out and tell me who and what this harpooneer is, and whether I shall be in all respects safe to spend the night with him. And in the first place, you will be so good as to unsay that story about selling his head, which if true I take to be good evidence that this harpooneer is stark mad, and I've no idea of sleeping with a madman; and you, sir, you I mean, landlord, you , sir, by trying to induce me to do so knowingly, would thereby render yourself liable to a criminal prosecution.”

“Wall,” said the landlord, fetching a long breath, “that's a purty long sarmon for a chap that rips a little now and then. But be easy, be easy, this here harpooneer I have been tellin' you of has just arrived from the south seas, where he bought up a lot of 'balmed New Zealand heads (great curios, you know), and he's sold all on 'em but one, and that one he's trying to sell to-night, cause to-morrow's Sunday, and it would not do to be sellin' human heads about the streets when folks is goin' to churches. He wanted to, last Sunday, but I stopped him just as he was goin' out of the door with four heads strung on a string, for all the airth like a string of inions.”

This account cleared up the otherwise unaccountable mystery, and showed that the landlord, after all, had had no idea of fooling me—but at the same time what could I think of a harpooneer who stayed out of a Saturday night clean into the holy Sabbath, engaged in such a cannibal business as selling the heads of dead idolators?

“Depend upon it, landlord, that harpooneer is a dangerous man.”

“He pays reg'lar,” was the rejoinder. “But come, it's getting dreadful late, you had better be turning flukes—it's a nice bed; Sal and me slept in that ere bed the night we were spliced. There's plenty of room for two to kick about in that bed; it's an almighty big bed that. Why, afore we give it up, Sal used to put our Sam and little Johnny in the foot of it. But I got a dreaming and sprawling about one night, and somehow, Sam got pitched on the floor, and came near breaking his arm. Arter that, Sal said it wouldn't do. Come along here, I'll give ye a glim in a jiffy;” and so saying he lighted a candle and held it towards me, offering to lead the way. But I stood irresolute; when looking at a clock in the corner, he exclaimed “I vum it's Sunday—you won't see that harpooneer to-night; he's come to anchor somewhere—come along then; do come; won't ye come?”

I considered the matter a moment, and then up stairs we went, and I was ushered into a small room, cold as a clam, and furnished, sure enough, with a prodigious bed, almost big enough indeed for any four harpooneers to sleep abreast.

“There,” said the landlord, placing the candle on a crazy old sea chest that did double duty as a wash-stand and centre table; “there, make yourself comfortable now, and good night to ye.” I turned round from eyeing the bed, but he had disappeared.

Folding back the counterpane, I stooped over the bed. Though none of the most elegant, it yet stood the scrutiny tolerably well. I then glanced round the room; and besides the bedstead and centre table, could see no other furniture belonging to the place, but a rude shelf, the four walls, and a papered fireboard representing a man striking a whale. Of things not properly belonging to the room, there was a hammock lashed up, and thrown upon the floor in one corner; also a large seaman's bag, containing the harpooneer's wardrobe, no doubt in lieu of a land trunk. Likewise, there was a parcel of outlandish bone fish hooks on the shelf over the fire-place, and a tall harpoon standing at the head of the bed.

But what is this on the chest? I took it up, and held it close to the light, and felt it, and smelt it, and tried every way possible to arrive at some satisfactory conclusion concerning it. I can compare it to nothing but a large door mat, ornamented at the edges with little tinkling tags something like the stained porcupine quills round an Indian moccasin. There was a hole or slit in the middle of this mat, as you see the same in South American ponchos. But could it be possible that any sober harpooneer would get into a door mat, and parade the streets of any Christian town in that sort of guise? I put it on, to try it, and it weighed me down like a hamper, being uncommonly shaggy and thick, and I thought a little damp, as though this mysterious harpooneer had been wearing it of a rainy day. I went up in it to a bit of glass stuck against the wall, and I never saw such a sight in my life. I tore myself out of it in such a hurry that I gave myself a kink in the neck.

I sat down on the side of the bed, and commenced thinking about this head-peddling harpooneer, and his door mat. After thinking some time on the bed-side, I got up and took off my monkey jacket, and then stood in the middle of the room thinking. I then took off my coat, and thought a little more in my shirt sleeves. But beginning to feel very cold now, half undressed as I was, and remembering what the landlord said about the harpooneer's not coming home at all that night, it being so very late, I made no more ado, but jumped out of my pantaloons and boots, and then blowing out the light tumbled into bed, and commended myself to the care of heaven.

Whether that mattress was stuffed with corn-cobs or broken crockery, there is no telling, but I rolled about a good deal, and could not sleep for a long time. At last I slid off into a light doze, and had pretty nearly made a good offing towards the land of Nod, when I heard a heavy footfall in the passage, and saw a glimmer of light come into the room from under the door.

Lord save me, thinks I, that must be the harpooneer, the infernal head-peddler. But I lay perfectly still, and resolved not to say a word till spoken to. Holding a light in one hand, and that identical New Zealand head in the other, the stranger entered the room, and without looking towards the bed, placed his candle a good way off from me on the floor in one corner, and then began working away at the knotted cords of the large bag I before spoke of as being in the room. I was all eagerness to see his face, but he kept it averted for some time while employed in unlacing the bag's mouth. This accomplished, however, he turned round—when, good heavens! what a sight! Such a face! It was of a dark, purplish, yellow colour, here and there stuck over with large blackish looking squares. Yes, it's just as I thought, he's a terrible bedfellow; he's been in a fight, got dreadfully cut, and here he is, just from the surgeon. But at that moment he chanced to turn his face so towards the light, that I plainly saw they could not be sticking-plasters at all, those black squares on his cheeks. They were stains of some sort or other. At first I knew not what to make of this; but soon an inkling of the truth occurred to me. I remembered a story of a white man—a whaleman too—who, falling among the cannibals, had been tattooed by them. I concluded that this harpooneer, in the course of his distant voyages, must have met with a similar adventure. And what is it, thought I, after all! It's only his outside; a man can be honest in any sort of skin. But then, what to make of his unearthly complexion, that part of it, I mean, lying round about, and completely independent of the squares of tattooing. To be sure, it might be nothing but a good coat of tropical tanning; but I never heard of a hot sun's tanning a white man into a purplish yellow one. However, I had never been in the South Seas; and perhaps the sun there produced these extraordinary effects upon the skin. Now, while all these ideas were passing through me like lightning, this harpooneer never noticed me at all. But, after some difficulty having opened his bag, he commenced fumbling in it, and presently pulled out a sort of tomahawk, and a seal-skin wallet with the hair on. Placing these on the old chest in the middle of the room, he then took the New Zealand head—a ghastly thing enough—and crammed it down into the bag. He now took off his hat—a new beaver hat—when I came nigh singing out with fresh surprise. There was no hair on his head—none to speak of at least—nothing but a small scalp-knot twisted up on his forehead. His bald purplish head now looked for all the world like a mildewed skull. Had not the stranger stood between me and the door, I would have bolted out of it quicker than ever I bolted a dinner.

Even as it was, I thought something of slipping out of the window, but it was the second floor back. I am no coward, but what to make of this head-peddling purple rascal altogether passed my comprehension. Ignorance is the parent of fear, and being completely nonplussed and confounded about the stranger, I confess I was now as much afraid of him as if it was the devil himself who had thus broken into my room at the dead of night. In fact, I was so afraid of him that I was not game enough just then to address him, and demand a satisfactory answer concerning what seemed inexplicable in him.

Meanwhile, he continued the business of undressing, and at last showed his chest and arms. As I live, these covered parts of him were checkered with the same squares as his face; his back, too, was all over the same dark squares; he seemed to have been in a Thirty Years' War, and just escaped from it with a sticking-plaster shirt. Still more, his very legs were marked, as if a parcel of dark green frogs were running up the trunks of young palms. It was now quite plain that he must be some abominable savage or other shipped aboard of a whaleman in the South Seas, and so landed in this Christian country. I quaked to think of it. A peddler of heads too—perhaps the heads of his own brothers. He might take a fancy to mine—heavens! look at that tomahawk!

But there was no time for shuddering, for now the savage went about something that completely fascinated my attention, and convinced me that he must indeed be a heathen. Going to his heavy grego, or wrapall, or dreadnaught, which he had previously hung on a chair, he fumbled in the pockets, and produced at length a curious little deformed image with a hunch on its back, and exactly the colour of a three days' old Congo baby. Remembering the embalmed head, at first I almost thought that this black manikin was a real baby preserved in some similar manner. But seeing that it was not at all limber, and that it glistened a good deal like polished ebony, I concluded that it must be nothing but a wooden idol, which indeed it proved to be. For now the savage goes up to the empty fire-place, and removing the papered fire-board, sets up this little hunch-backed image, like a tenpin, between the andirons. The chimney jambs and all the bricks inside were very sooty, so that I thought this fire-place made a very appropriate little shrine or chapel for his Congo idol.

I now screwed my eyes hard towards the half hidden image, feeling but ill at ease meantime—to see what was next to follow. First he takes about a double handful of shavings out of his grego pocket, and places them carefully before the idol; then laying a bit of ship biscuit on top and applying the flame from the lamp, he kindled the shavings into a sacrificial blaze. Presently, after many hasty snatches into the fire, and still hastier withdrawals of his fingers (whereby he seemed to be scorching them badly), he at last succeeded in drawing out the biscuit; then blowing off the heat and ashes a little, he made a polite offer of it to the little negro. But the little devil did not seem to fancy such dry sort of fare at all; he never moved his lips. All these strange antics were accompanied by still stranger guttural noises from the devotee, who seemed to be praying in a sing-song or else singing some pagan psalmody or other, during which his face twitched about in the most unnatural manner. At last extinguishing the fire, he took the idol up very unceremoniously, and bagged it again in his grego pocket as carelessly as if he were a sportsman bagging a dead woodcock.

All these queer proceedings increased my uncomfortableness, and seeing him now exhibiting strong symptoms of concluding his business operations, and jumping into bed with me, I thought it was high time, now or never, before the light was put out, to break the spell in which I had so long been bound.

But the interval I spent in deliberating what to say, was a fatal one. Taking up his tomahawk from the table, he examined the head of it for an instant, and then holding it to the light, with his mouth at the handle, he puffed out great clouds of tobacco smoke. The next moment the light was extinguished, and this wild cannibal, tomahawk between his teeth, sprang into bed with me. I sang out, I could not help it now; and giving a sudden grunt of astonishment he began feeling me.

Stammering out something, I knew not what, I rolled away from him against the wall, and then conjured him, whoever or whatever he might be, to keep quiet, and let me get up and light the lamp again. But his guttural responses satisfied me at once that he but ill comprehended my meaning.

“Who-e debel you?”—he at last said—“you no speak-e, dam-me, I kill-e.” And so saying the lighted tomahawk began flourishing about me in the dark.

“Landlord, for God's sake, Peter Coffin!” shouted I. “Landlord! Watch! Coffin! Angels! save me!”

“Speak-e! tell-ee me who-ee be, or dam-me, I kill-e!” again growled the cannibal, while his horrid flourishings of the tomahawk scattered the hot tobacco ashes about me till I thought my linen would get on fire. But thank heaven, at that moment the landlord came into the room light in hand, and leaping from the bed I ran up to him.

“Don't be afraid now,” said he, grinning again, “Queequeg here wouldn't harm a hair of your head.”

“Stop your grinning,” shouted I, “and why didn't you tell me that that infernal harpooneer was a cannibal?”

“I thought ye know'd it;—didn't I tell ye, he was a peddlin' heads around town?—but turn flukes again and go to sleep. Queequeg, look here—you sabbee me, I sabbee—you this man sleepe you—you sabbee?”

“Me sabbee plenty”—grunted Queequeg, puffing away at his pipe and sitting up in bed.

“You gettee in,” he added, motioning to me with his tomahawk, and throwing the clothes to one side. He really did this in not only a civil but a really kind and charitable way. I stood looking at him a moment. For all his tattooings he was on the whole a clean, comely looking cannibal. What's all this fuss I have been making about, thought I to myself—the man's a human being just as I am: he has just as much reason to fear me, as I have to be afraid of him. Better sleep with a sober cannibal than a drunken Christian.

“Landlord,” said I, “tell him to stash his tomahawk there, or pipe, or whatever you call it; tell him to stop smoking, in short, and I will turn in with him. But I don't fancy having a man smoking in bed with me. It's dangerous. Besides, I ain't insured.”

This being told to Queequeg, he at once complied, and again politely motioned me to get into bed—rolling over to one side as much as to say—“I won't touch a leg of ye.”

“Good night, landlord,” said I, “you may go.”

I turned in, and never slept better in my life.
